> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The constraint on a good market view is almost never too little information; it is too much low-quality information drowning out the few inputs that would actually change a decision.
>
> - Rank every source by tier: primary data > primary sources > expert synthesis > commentary > social noise. Spend most of your time at the base.
> - Filter every input through one question: *would this change a position I hold or a probability I believe?* If no, it is noise, however interesting.
> - More sources do not mean more accuracy. Calibration peaks at roughly five to seven high-signal sources and degrades as redundant, conflicting inputs crowd out interpretation time.
> - The one number to remember: 30 noise-driven trades a year at \$125 of friction each is \$3,750 you pay annually for the privilege of reacting to headlines.

At 7:00 a.m. two traders sit down with the same account, the same instruments, and the same market. The first opens a phone already buzzing with 200 overnight headlines: a war escalation, a CEO resignation, an unsourced "sources say" about the central bank, four breathless takes on an overnight crypto move, a chart someone drew with three arrows on it, and a dozen "this changes everything" threads. By 8:30 he has read all of it, formed seven half-opinions, abandoned five of them, placed two trades on the ones that felt most urgent, and feels informed, busy, and vaguely anxious. He could not tell you what is actually scheduled for today.

The second trader opens a one-page checklist. She reads the economic calendar (CPI at 8:30, a Fed speaker at noon), the actual text of last night's central-bank statement rather than the recap of it, the closing levels and breadth from yesterday, one trusted synthesis note, and the overnight move in the two instruments she actually has positions in. Twenty minutes. She has placed no trades, because nothing on her list changed a position she holds or a probability she believes. She knows exactly what the day's one real event is and what she will do if the number surprises.

By the open, who has the clearer view? The first trader has more information and a worse decision. He has confused *activity* with *analysis* and *interesting* with *actionable*. This post is about closing that gap. It builds a deliberate information diet: a small set of high-signal sources, a tier system, and a single filter that decides what earns your attention. The thesis is blunt — most news is engineered for engagement, not for decisions, and a professional's edge is increasingly in what they refuse to read.

## Foundations: what an information diet actually is

An **information diet** is the deliberate, written choice of what you let into your decision process — which sources, how often, at what depth — and, just as importantly, what you refuse. The word *diet* is exact. You are not trying to consume the most calories; you are trying to consume the right nutrients and stop. A trader who reads everything is the analytical equivalent of someone who eats whatever is in front of them: bloated, sluggish, and worse off than if they had skipped most of it.

Three terms anchor the whole discussion, and we define them from zero.

**Signal** is information that changes a decision. Concretely: a piece of input is signal *for you* if, after receiving it, you would size a position differently, hold a different probability for some outcome, or change what you are watching for next. Signal is personal and position-dependent. A surprise jobs number is signal to a rates trader with a duration position and pure noise to a long-term equity holder who will not touch the portfolio for a year.

**Noise** is everything else: information that is interesting, alarming, entertaining, or true but that does not change any decision you would make. Critically, noise is not the same as *falsehood*. Most noise is perfectly accurate. "Stock X fell 2% today" is true and almost always noise. The danger of noise is not that it lies; it is that it competes for the attention and reaction budget that signal needs, and it tempts you to act when the correct action is nothing.

The trap is that noise *feels* like signal in the moment. A dramatic, true, surprising headline triggers the same alertness whether or not it changes anything you would do. Your nervous system cannot tell the difference between "important to the world" and "relevant to my book" — it just registers *salient* and floods you with the urge to respond. The whole discipline of an information diet is installing a deliberate gate between that salience reflex and your decisions, so that the urge to act is checked against a question rather than obeyed automatically. Without the gate, salience wins, and salience is exactly what noise is optimized to maximize.

**Decision-relevance** is the property that separates the two. It is the filter the entire diet is built around, and we will formalize it in its own section. For now: an input is decision-relevant if it would move a position size or a probability. If it would not, it does not belong in your day, no matter how dramatic it is.

There is a fourth idea that makes the diet necessary in the first place: **the base rate of news**. On any given day, the overwhelming majority of what crosses your screen is already reflected in prices, irrelevant to your time horizon, or both. Markets aggregate the views of millions of participants continuously; by the time a development is a headline, the people whose job is to trade it have usually already traded it. This is the uncomfortable starting point. You are not the first to see most news. The default assumption for any headline should be *"this is probably already priced or irrelevant to me"*, and the burden of proof is on the headline to earn your attention — not the other way around. We link out to the mechanics of what *priced in* means in [What's Priced In: The Question Behind Every Trade](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/whats-priced-in-the-question-behind-every-trade) and to why only *surprises* move markets in [The Reaction Function](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-reaction-function-why-the-same-number-moves-differently).

To make the base rate concrete: of the roughly 200 headlines our 7 a.m. doomscroller read, how many would change a decision for a typical book? On a quiet day, often zero. On a scheduled-data day, perhaps one or two — the print and its surprise. On a genuinely eventful day, maybe a handful. The decision-relevant fraction of the daily feed is almost always in the low single-digit percentages, and frequently it is nil. Internalizing that ratio reframes the whole exercise. You are not panning a stream that is mostly gold for the occasional pebble; you are panning a stream that is mostly silt for the rare fleck of gold, and the more silt you process the more likely you are to mistake a shiny pebble for the real thing. The base rate is why the burden of proof must sit on the input, and why a healthy diet feels, from the inside, like ignoring almost everything.

### The cost of information is real and it compounds

People treat information as free because it is cheap to acquire. It is not free; it is just that the costs are deferred and hidden. There are three of them.

The first is the **attention cost**. Attention is a fixed daily budget. Every minute spent reading a noise headline is a minute not spent interpreting a signal one. The trader doomscrolling 200 headlines is not better informed than the one reading five primary sources — he is *worse* informed, because he has spread a fixed attention budget across mostly-empty inputs and has none left for the few that matter.

The second is the **reaction cost**. Information that crosses your screen creates pressure to act. The more inputs, the more pressure, the more trades. Most of those trades are not edges; they are reactions to noise, and each one costs commission, spread, slippage, and tax. This is the overtrading tax, and it is large enough that we devote a worked example and a chart to it below.

The third is the **calibration cost**, the subtlest of the three. Beyond a small number of high-quality sources, adding inputs makes your decisions *worse*, not better. More inputs mean more conflicting opinions, more spurious correlations your brain will pattern-match onto, and more confidence without more accuracy. A famous body of research on expert judgment found that giving forecasters more information raised their *confidence* sharply while leaving their *accuracy* flat — the dangerous combination. We will see this as a curve later: accuracy rises, peaks, and then degrades as the source count climbs.

These three costs interact, and the interaction is vicious. More inputs eat attention, which leaves less for interpretation, which lowers calibration, which makes your reactions worse, which means the extra trades the reaction cost generates are not just frictional but *negative-expectancy*. A bloated diet does not merely cost you the friction on bad trades; it makes the trades themselves worse by starving the thinking that would have caught them. The costs compound on each other the way a poor diet of food does: each bad input makes the next decision a little worse, and the effects accumulate into a chronically degraded process rather than a single bad day.

The job, then, is not to maximize information. It is to maximize *decision-relevant information per unit of attention* and to ruthlessly cut the rest. Everything that follows is machinery for doing that. The two big levers are *which sources you let in* — handled by the tier pyramid — and *whether any given input earns your attention right now* — handled by the decision-relevance filter. Master both and the costs above shrink toward their floor; neglect either and they balloon.

## The source tier pyramid

The first piece of machinery is a hierarchy of sources by how close they sit to the raw fact and how high their signal-to-noise ratio is. The pyramid in the cover figure is the whole system on one page; we will walk it from the base up. The rule is simple: **prefer the lowest tier that answers your question, and treat each higher tier as progressively more contaminated with interpretation, lag, and agenda.**

**Tier 1 — Primary data.** The print itself. The actual CPI release from the statistics agency, the closing price, the volume, the order book, the economic calendar that tells you what is scheduled and when. This is the bedrock. It has no spin because it is not yet interpreted — it *is* the fact. Primary data is where a disciplined diet spends most of its time, because everything above it is a derivative of it, and derivatives lose information. When the jobs number prints, the number and the revisions to prior months are Tier 1; everything written about them in the next hour is a higher tier.

**Tier 2 — Primary sources.** The original text of a decision or disclosure, before anyone summarizes it. The full FOMC statement and the transcript of the press conference, not the recap. The 10-K filing, not the article about the 10-K. The earnings call transcript, not the three-bullet "key takeaways". Tier 2 is one step removed from raw data but still un-spun: it is the actor speaking in their own words. The gap between Tier 2 and Tier 3 is where most retail disadvantage lives, because reading the actual statement takes fifteen minutes and most people read the recap instead — and the recap is where someone else's interpretation has already been baked in.

**Tier 3 — Expert synthesis.** Genuinely skilled people connecting Tiers 1 and 2 into a coherent read: a good sell-side economist's note, a research desk's framework, an analyst who shows their work and states what would change their mind. This tier is valuable *when the synthesizer is good and transparent* — it saves you time and adds frameworks you would not have built alone. It is dangerous when you cannot see the reasoning, because then you are importing a conclusion you cannot audit. Keep a very small number of Tier 3 sources, and only ones who show their work.

**Tier 4 — Commentary and media.** Opinion, financial media, the bulk of the daily news flow. This tier is mostly recycled lower-tier content with a narrative wrapped around it, often lagging the price by hours and written to be read, not to be acted on. Some of it is fine; most of it is filler. The defining feature of Tier 4 is that it tells you what *already happened* and invents a reason for it after the fact. "Stocks fell on rate fears" is a Tier 4 sentence: the move came first, the explanation was reverse-engineered.

**Tier 5 — Social and rumor.** Unverified flow, anonymous "sources say", hot takes, threads, and the engagement-optimized churn of social feeds. This tier is where signal-to-noise is lowest and the incentive structure is most hostile to you: the content exists to capture attention, not to inform a decision. Occasionally a genuine primary source posts here first, but you cannot tell signal from noise in real time, and acting on Tier 5 before it is corroborated by a lower tier is how accounts get whipsawed. We work the dollar cost of doing exactly that below.

The pyramid's shape is the point. It is wide at the base because primary data is where your time should go, and it narrows toward the top because the higher tiers deserve progressively less of your attention. Most people invert it — they spend hours at Tiers 4 and 5 and minutes, if any, at Tiers 1 and 2. The fix is to invert the inversion.

A useful test for any input is to ask *how many hands has this passed through?* Tier 1 has passed through zero — it is the fact. Tier 2 has passed through one — the actor stating it. Tier 3 has passed through two — a competent interpreter reading Tiers 1 and 2. By Tier 4 the input has been through several rounds of summarizing, framing, and headline-writing, each of which strips information and adds spin. By Tier 5 the chain of provenance is often untraceable: you cannot say who originated the claim or whether it traces back to any primary source at all. Every hand an input passes through is an opportunity for information to be lost and for an agenda to be inserted, and the further up the pyramid you read, the more of both you are absorbing without knowing it. The discipline is to keep asking yourself which tier you are actually reading and to drop down a tier whenever the question matters — when in doubt, read the source, not the story about the source.

There is one more reason the base matters disproportionately: the lower tiers are where your *independent* edge can live. Everyone has access to the Tier 4 narrative; it is, by construction, the consensus interpretation. The trader who reads the actual statement, builds their own read of the components, and forms a view that diverges from the recap is the one with a differentiated position. Read only the higher tiers and you will, at best, hold the consensus view — and the consensus view is already in the price. Differentiation requires going to the source and doing the interpretation yourself, which is precisely the work the higher tiers tempt you to skip.

#### Worked example: the recap tax on a \$100,000 macro book

You run a \$100,000 macro book with a 2-year Treasury position sized at \$30,000 notional. An FOMC statement drops at 2:00 p.m. Trader A reads the actual statement and the dot plot (Tier 2) in eleven minutes and sees that the language on "additional firming" was *retained*, not softened — a hawkish hold. Trader B reads a Tier 4 recap headlined "Fed holds rates steady" and concludes, reasonably from that headline, that nothing changed.

Both have the same position. Trader A trims the rates exposure by half into the hawkish language before the press conference reprices it, locking in a position consistent with what the *primary source* actually said. Trader B, reading only the recap, holds and watches the 2-year yield jump 12 basis points over the next hour as the market digests the retained firming language. On \$30,000 of 2-year notional with a duration near 1.9, a 12 bp adverse move is roughly \$30,000 × 1.9 × 0.0012 = \$68 of mark-to-market loss — small in absolute terms, but Trader A avoided it and is now positioned correctly while Trader B is offside and confused, having read *about* the event instead of reading the event. The eleven minutes at Tier 2 were the highest-paid eleven minutes of the day. **Reading the source instead of the recap is not slower; it is the only read that lets you act before the recap-readers do.**

## The decision-relevance filter

The tier system tells you *where* information sits. The decision-relevance filter tells you whether any given input — from any tier — earns your attention right now. This is the single most important habit in the whole post, and it is one question:

> Would this change a position I hold or a probability I believe?

If the answer is no, the input is noise *for you, today*, and it gets dropped. Not bookmarked, not "kept in mind", not allowed to generate a low-grade hum of anxiety. Dropped. The filter is harsh on purpose, because the default human response to information is to feel that it *must* matter — and that default is what overloads the diet.

![Decision relevance filter routing inputs to keep or drop](/imgs/blogs/building-your-information-diet-signal-versus-noise-2.png)

The figure traces the routing. A new input — a headline, a print, a post — arrives and hits the gate: *changes a decision?* If no, it is interesting-not-actionable and gets ignored. If yes, a second question sorts it: is it actually new and relevant, or is it something you already knew and had already priced into your view? Genuinely new-and-relevant inputs route to one of two places. If they are fast-decaying and demand action now (a surprise print that moves a position), you **act or resize and update the view**. If they are slow-burn inputs that feed a longer thesis (a structural data point), you **log them** for the thesis without trading on them today. Everything else — already priced, stale, or merely interesting — drops to the red boxes.

Two distinctions inside the filter do most of the work.

The first is **interesting versus actionable**. A vast amount of true, important, fascinating information is not actionable for you. A war in a region you have no exposure to is interesting and, for your book, not actionable. A CEO's resignation at a company you do not hold and would not trade is interesting and not actionable. The skill is feeling the pull of "but this is *important*" and answering "important to the world, yes; relevant to my decisions, no" — and then moving on without guilt. Importance and actionability are different axes, and conflating them is the single most common way smart people drown themselves in noise.

The second is **already-priced versus new**. Even genuinely relevant information is often already in the price. If everyone knows a rate cut is coming and it is fully discounted, the cut itself is not a tradeable surprise — the *deviation* from expectations is. This is why the filter routes "known" inputs to the drop pile even when they are relevant: you cannot trade information that the market has already absorbed. The mechanics of measuring what the market already believes are in [Mapping the Consensus](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/mapping-the-consensus-what-does-the-market-already-believe) and [Consensus, Expectations, and Priced-In](/blog/trading/event-trading/consensus-expectations-and-priced-in).

A subtle failure mode hides in this second distinction: *re-counting the same input.* A piece of news breaks, gets reported, gets recapped, gets analyzed, gets discussed, and gets re-shared, and each appearance feels like a fresh input even though it is the same fact passing through more hands. A trader who has not gated for "already known" will react to the original report, then feel the urge to react again to the recap, and again to the analysis, treating one event as three reasons to do something. The filter's "new + relevant?" gate is partly a duplicate-detector: once you have priced an input into your view, every subsequent re-statement of it is noise by definition, no matter how many times it crosses your screen or how authoritative the latest packaging looks. One fact, one update — not one fact, one update per outlet that covers it.

The filter is also not a one-time pass; it is a standing posture. The same headline can be noise on Monday and signal on Thursday if your positions change, and a structural input you logged-but-did-not-trade in March can become actionable in June when the thesis matures or the price finally moves to where it matters. Running the filter well means re-asking the question as your book and the market evolve, not stamping each input "noise" or "signal" forever. What stays constant is the question itself — *would this change a position or a probability?* — applied freshly to your current state every time.

#### Worked example: applying the filter to a morning of headlines

You hold three positions: \$40,000 long a broad equity index, \$20,000 long gold, and \$15,000 short a single tech name. Five headlines hit before the open. Run each through the gate.

- *"Overnight: European gas prices spike 8%."* You have no European energy exposure. Interesting, not actionable. **Drop.**
- *"Fed's preferred inflation gauge due Friday, consensus 2.8%."* Relevant to your equity and gold positions, but it is three days away and the consensus is already known — already priced as a base case. **Log** it as the week's key event; do not trade on it now.
- *"Analyst downgrades your short tech name on weak guidance."* Directly relevant to a \$15,000 position, genuinely new, and it strengthens your existing short thesis. **Act**: this is signal. You decide whether to add or to take partial profit on the move.
- *"Crypto token you don't hold rallies 15%."* Not a position, not on your watchlist. **Drop.**
- *"Unconfirmed report of a geopolitical incident; no primary source."* Could matter to gold, but it is Tier 5 and unverified. **Log to watch** for corroboration from a lower tier; do not trade the rumor.

Five headlines, one action, one log, one watch, two drops. If you had treated all five as equally urgent and traded each, you would have placed perhaps four trades — three of them noise — at maybe \$45 round-trip cost plus slippage, call it \$125 each, for \$375 of pure friction to react to information that did not change your decisions. The filter saved you \$375 in a single morning and, more importantly, kept your attention on the one headline that actually mattered. **The filter is not about reading less; it is about acting only on the inputs that change a decision.**

## Information half-life: why timing your inputs matters

Not all signal lasts the same length of time. Every input has a **half-life** — a horizon over which it stays decision-relevant before the market fully absorbs it and it decays into background. Matching your reading to the half-life of what you trade is half the battle, because reading a fast-decaying input late is useless and reading a slow-burn input obsessively is wasteful.

![Information half-life by input type on a log scale of hours](/imgs/blogs/building-your-information-diet-signal-versus-noise-3.png)

The chart sorts input types by how long they stay relevant, on a log scale because the range is enormous — from minutes to a year. At the bottom, an **algo headline reaction** has a half-life measured in minutes: by the time you read the headline, machines have already traded it and the edge is gone. An **intraday rumor** lasts a couple of hours at most. A **CPI or NFP print** is decision-relevant for hours — long enough to position into the reaction, but fully priced by the next session. An **FOMC statement** carries for a few days as the market re-rates the policy path. **Earnings guidance** shifts a company's narrative for a couple of weeks. A **sector regime shift** plays out over months. And a **structural thesis input** — a demographic trend, a durable change in a country's terms of trade, a multi-year capex cycle — stays relevant for the better part of a year.

The practical consequence is that your reading cadence should match the half-life of your edge. If you trade structural theses, the daily headline churn is almost entirely below your half-life and therefore almost entirely noise — you should be reading slow inputs slowly, not fast inputs fast. If you trade event reactions, you live at the short end and need the calendar and the primary release in real time, but you should ignore the month-long commentary that accumulates afterward. The mismatch — a long-horizon investor doomscrolling minute-by-minute, or a day trader reading year-old structural essays for an edge today — is one of the commonest forms of self-inflicted noise.

The half-life also tells you something the price alone hides: *whether you are even in time to use an input.* The dashed "1 day" line on the chart is a rough boundary between inputs a human can act on and inputs that are gone before you finish reading. Anything well below that line — the algo reaction, the intraday rumor in its first minutes — is information you are, by construction, late to, because the participants whose half-life it matches have already traded it. Reading those inputs and feeling the urge to chase is reading expired food and feeling hungry. The inputs above the line — the FOMC path re-rating over days, the guidance shift over weeks, the regime shift over months — are the ones where a thoughtful human still has time to form a better view than the consensus, because the market has not finished digesting them. A good diet skews your attention toward the inputs above the line and treats the ones below it as confirmation of what already happened rather than opportunities to act.

#### Worked example: the opportunity cost of two hours a day on noise

Suppose you spend two hours every trading day reading Tier 4 and Tier 5 content — the recaps, the takes, the feeds — for inputs whose half-life is mostly already expired by the time you read them. There are about 250 trading days a year, so that is 500 hours annually.

Put a value on your time. If your trading and analysis effort is worth even a modest \$50 an hour — well below what a serious analyst's time is worth — those 500 hours represent \$25,000 of opportunity cost per year, spent consuming information below its half-life. Redirect just half of it — 250 hours — into reading primary sources, building theses, and journaling decisions, and you have reclaimed \$12,500 of effective value annually and almost certainly improved your calibration at the same time. The two hours of noise feel productive because they feel busy, but they are the most expensive habit in the diet. **Time spent reading inputs whose half-life has already expired is pure cost with no decision-relevance to show for it.**

## The over-information trap

The most counterintuitive idea in this post is that more sources make you worse, not better, past a small threshold. People resist this because it contradicts the deep assumption that information is good and more is better. For *decisions under uncertainty*, that assumption is wrong beyond a handful of high-signal inputs.

![Decision accuracy peaking then degrading as source count rises](/imgs/blogs/building-your-information-diet-signal-versus-noise-4.png)

The curve tells the story. As you add your first few high-signal sources — the calendar, the primary release, one or two good synthesizers — decision accuracy climbs steeply. You are filling in the genuinely useful inputs. Accuracy peaks at roughly five to seven well-chosen sources, the green "signal zone". Then it bends over and declines. Each marginal source past the peak is increasingly redundant (it repeats what you already have), increasingly conflicting (it gives you a contradictory take you now have to reconcile), and increasingly costly (it eats the attention you need to *interpret* what you have). The curve degrades into the red zone: more inputs, worse calibration.

Three mechanisms drive the decline. First, **redundancy**: the tenth macro commentator is mostly restating the first three, so you gain confidence without gaining information — the dangerous mismatch. Second, **conflict and noise-fitting**: more inputs mean more spurious patterns, and the human brain is a relentless pattern-matcher that will manufacture a narrative from random conflicting data. Third, **interpretation starvation**: the binding constraint on a good decision is not how much you read but how well you think about what you read, and every marginal source steals from thinking time.

The fix follows directly from the curve: cap your source count near the peak. Pick five to seven high-signal sources, master them, and add a new one only by *replacing* an existing one, never by accumulation. The discipline is subtractive. An information diet that only ever grows is not a diet.

The confidence-accuracy gap deserves a closer look, because it is the part of the curve that actually hurts you. As you pile on sources, your subjective sense of being well-informed keeps climbing — you have read so much, surely you know more. But the curve says your accuracy has already topped out and started falling. The result is a widening gap between how confident you feel and how right you actually are, and overconfidence is expensive: it makes you size positions too large, hold losers too long because you are "sure", and dismiss disconfirming evidence. A trader who reads three sources and knows they have read three sources is better calibrated — both more accurate and more appropriately humble — than one who has read thirty and feels invincible. The cruel part is that the thirty-source trader cannot feel the problem from the inside; the extra reading produces exactly the sensation of competence that masks the degraded judgment.

This is also why the fix has to be a *rule*, not a feeling. You cannot rely on noticing in the moment that you have crossed the peak, because crossing the peak feels great. You have to pre-commit to a source cap and enforce it mechanically — a hard count, audited on a schedule, with replacement-only additions — exactly the way you would pre-commit to a position size limit rather than trusting yourself to feel when a position has gotten too big. The discipline lives in the rule, not in your in-the-moment judgment, because your in-the-moment judgment is the thing the over-information trap corrupts.

#### Worked example: the overtrading tax of a bloated diet

A bloated diet does not just lower calibration — it directly generates trades. Suppose the noise in your feed prompts 30 extra trades a year that you would not have made on a disciplined diet: reactions to headlines, rumors you chased, "this changes everything" moves that changed nothing.

Price the friction. Each round-trip costs roughly \$45 in commissions and spread, plus slippage on a \$100,000 account that on these impulsive, poorly-timed trades runs around \$80, for total friction near \$125 per round-trip. Thirty trades a year is 30 × \$125 = \$3,750 annually — a 3.75% drag on a \$100,000 account, paid not for any edge but purely for the privilege of reacting to noise.

![Overtrading tax accumulating over five years on a hundred thousand dollar account](/imgs/blogs/building-your-information-diet-signal-versus-noise-5.png)

The chart accumulates it. The red area is the cumulative friction paid: \$3,750 after one year, \$11,250 after three, \$18,750 after five. The amber dashed line is the same dollars compounded at a modest 7% — \$21,565 over five years — which is what that money would have been worth had it stayed in your account instead of leaking out as friction. Five years of a noise-driven diet is a \$20,000-plus hole on a \$100,000 account, and that ignores the realized losses on the bad trades themselves; this is only the *friction*. **The overtrading tax is the price of an undisciplined information diet, and it compounds against you exactly the way returns are supposed to compound for you.** The psychology behind why noise pulls us into these trades is in [Trading Psychology and the Execution Gap](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/trading-psychology-and-the-execution-gap).

## Building your personal source list by asset and horizon

A diet is not vague advice; it is a concrete, written list of named sources mapped to what you trade and over what horizon. Two people trading different things should have different diets, and the same person trading on different horizons should read different inputs. The organizing grid is asset class on one axis and horizon on the other.

![Sample weekly information diet by horizon and asset class](/imgs/blogs/building-your-information-diet-signal-versus-noise-6.png)

The figure is a sample diet laid out as horizon (rows) by asset (columns). Read it as a template to adapt, not a prescription.

The **daily row** is deliberately small — a 15-minute read — and it lives at Tiers 1 and 2. For macro and rates: the economic calendar and the Treasury curve. For equities: the index level and market breadth. For FX and crypto: the dollar index, your key cross, and on-chain flows for crypto positions. This row exists to answer one question — *did anything happen that changes a position I hold today?* — and nothing more. It is short by design, because a long daily read is a noise generator.

The **weekly row** is a deeper 60-minute session, mostly Tier 2 and selective Tier 3. For macro: the full Fed statement text and positioning data such as the COT report. For equities: earnings call transcripts and sector flows. For FX and crypto: central-bank minutes and funding rates. This is where you update the medium-horizon view, the stuff with a half-life of days to weeks.

The **structural row** is monthly and slow, reaching into longer primary sources and the occasional deep Tier 3 framework. For macro: the policy regime and the debt cycle. For equities: margins, capex, and the long filings. For FX and crypto: the balance of payments, reserve trends, and protocol roadmaps. These inputs have half-lives measured in months and deserve to be read slowly and rarely. Reading them daily adds nothing; reading them never leaves you blindsided by regime changes.

The grid does three things at once. It makes your reading *match the half-life* of what you trade. It *caps* the daily input count near the calibration peak. And it makes the diet *auditable* — you can look at the list and ask whether each source is still earning its slot, and replace rather than accumulate. The companion to this grid is the cadence that runs it, which is the subject of [The Daily and Weekly Process](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/the-daily-and-weekly-process-a-repeatable-reading-routine).

## Handling rumors and unverified flow

The hardest input to handle is the rumor: the unconfirmed report, the anonymous flow, the Tier 5 post that *might* be the first sign of something real or might be nothing. Rumors are seductive because they promise the one thing every trader wants — to be early. They are dangerous for the same reason: acting early on something false is how you get whipsawed, and you cannot tell true from false in real time.

The discipline is a graduated response. A rumor is not "trade it" or "ignore it"; it is **log it to watch and demand corroboration from a lower tier before acting**. When an unconfirmed report crosses your feed, you note it, identify what lower-tier confirmation would validate it (an official statement, a filing, a confirmed primary source), and you wait for that confirmation before risking capital. If the confirmation comes, you act — slightly later than the rumor-chasers, but on something real. If it does not come, you have lost nothing but a few minutes of attention.

The objection is always the same: *but if I wait for confirmation, I miss the move.* This is true, and it is the right trade-off, for two reasons. First, the move you "miss" by waiting is precisely the move that reverses violently when the rumor is denied — the gap that traps the chasers. You are not missing a clean profit; you are declining to step in front of a coin-flip with a fat left tail. Second, the math is asymmetric. If you act on rumors and they are right half the time, you capture some upside on the winners but you eat the full whipsaw on the losers, and the whipsaws are larger because a denied rumor overshoots back through the start. Over many rumors, the expected value of trading them raw is negative for almost everyone, because the losers are bigger than the winners and the friction is paid on all of them. Waiting for the primary source converts a negative-expectancy reflex into a positive-expectancy discipline — you trade only the rumors that turn real, and you skip the friction and the whipsaw on the ones that do not.

There is a narrow exception worth naming so the rule does not become dogma. If you have a genuine, verifiable edge in a specific rumor — you can independently corroborate it from a primary source faster than the market, or you have structural reason to assign it high probability — then it is no longer a raw rumor; it has effectively dropped to a lower tier for you, and you can act. But that is rare, it requires real work to establish, and it is emphatically not the situation of the trader staring at an anonymous post and feeling the pull. For that trader, the rule holds without exception: log it, watch for the tier you can verify, and do not trade the rumor raw.

The cost of getting this wrong is concrete, so we make it concrete.

#### Worked example: a \$5,000 loss from acting on an unverified rumor

A Tier 5 post claims, citing "people familiar with the matter", that a mid-cap company you follow is about to be acquired at a large premium. The stock is jumping. You have a \$25,000 account and the fear of missing out is acute. You buy \$10,000 of the stock at \$52, up from \$48, on the rumor alone.

Two hours later the company issues an official statement (Tier 2) denying any deal. The stock collapses back to \$46 — below where it started, because the rumor-chasers are now trapped and dumping. You sell at \$46.50 in a panic. Your loss is (\$52 − \$46.50) / \$52 × \$10,000 = roughly \$1,058 on the position, plus you over-traded the exit and the re-entry temptation; realistic all-in damage on a chased rumor like this, including a follow-on revenge trade, runs to \$5,000 — a 20% hit on a \$25,000 account, all of it for acting before the primary source spoke.

Now run the disciplined alternative. The same rumor crosses your feed. You log it and identify the corroborating input: an official company statement or a regulatory filing. You wait. Two hours later the denial comes. You acted on nothing, risked nothing, lost nothing. The cost of waiting for the primary source was a few minutes; the cost of not waiting was \$5,000. **The rumor is never worth the primary source it is impersonating — wait for the tier you can act on.**

## Common misconceptions

**"Read everything — you never know what will matter."** This is the founding error, and the over-information curve refutes it directly. You *do* know what will matter, with high probability: inputs that change a position or a probability, which are a tiny fraction of the flow. Reading everything does not protect you from missing the rare important thing; it guarantees you will miss it, because it will be buried under 200 unimportant things and your attention will be exhausted. Coverage is not the goal. Decision-relevance is.

**"Faster is an edge."** For a small number of high-frequency professionals with co-located servers and microsecond latency, speed is an edge. For everyone else, speed is a trap. By the time a human reads a headline, the machines have traded it — the algo-headline-reaction half-life is minutes. Chasing speed puts you in a race you cannot win and trains you to react before you interpret. The durable edge for a human is *interpretation*: reading the primary source carefully and forming a better view of what it means, which is a game machines are far worse at. Trade the half-life you can actually win, not the one that is already gone.

**"More sources mean more accuracy."** No — calibration peaks near five to seven sources and degrades past it. More sources past the peak add redundancy, conflict, and confidence-without-accuracy. The accurate diet is small and deliberate, not large and comprehensive. If you find yourself adding a source, ask which existing one it replaces.

**"News moves markets."** Almost right, and the gap is everything. *Surprises* move markets — the deviation between what happened and what was expected. News that confirms the consensus is already priced and moves nothing; the same number can move the market up, down, or not at all depending on what was expected. This is why two CPI prints of identical level can produce opposite reactions, and it is the single most important correction to the naive "news → move" model. The full treatment is in [The Surprise, Not the Level](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/the-surprise-not-the-level-betas-to-data-surprises) and [The Reaction Function](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-reaction-function-why-the-same-number-moves-differently).

**"If I'm anxious about the news, I'm taking it seriously."** Anxiety is a signal that you are consuming too much noise, not that you are being responsible. A well-built diet should make you *calmer*, because you know what you are watching for and you have pre-decided what would change your mind. Chronic news anxiety is a symptom of a diet that has no filter.

## How it plays out in real markets

### Information overload around a 2022 CPI day

Through 2022, every CPI release was the market's main event, and the days around them were a study in noise. A trader running a bloated diet on, say, the October 2022 print (released November 10, 2022, which came in *below* expectations and triggered one of the largest single-day equity rallies in years) would have spent the preceding week drowning. Dozens of takes on what the number "had" to be, anonymous whispers about the components, threads predicting catastrophe, recaps of recaps. By the time the number printed, the bloated-diet trader had formed and abandoned a dozen views and had no clean read on the one thing that mattered: *what was the consensus, and what is the surprise relative to it?*

The disciplined trader's diet that week was three things. The economic calendar (CPI, date and time, and the consensus number — Tier 1). The previous print and its revisions (Tier 1). One synthesis note framing the key components to watch (Tier 3). That is it. When the number came in soft, the disciplined trader already knew the consensus, instantly saw the downside surprise, and had pre-decided the action. The bloated trader saw a violent rally, did not know whether it was justified, and chased it late. Same event, same information available, opposite outcomes — the difference was entirely the diet. The lesson 2022 hammered home repeatedly: on a scheduled-event day, the only inputs that matter are the calendar, the consensus, and the primary release — everything else is noise dressed as preparation.

Notice what the bloated diet did to the bloated trader's *probability estimate* going into the print. Having read a week of doom-laden takes, his prior was skewed toward a hot number and a sell-off — not because the data pointed that way but because the loudest, most-shared content was the most alarming, and alarm travels further on social feeds than calm does. His diet had quietly biased his expectations toward catastrophe, so when the soft number printed he was doubly wrong-footed: positioned for the opposite outcome and emotionally primed to disbelieve the rally. The disciplined trader, anchored on the actual consensus number rather than the loudest takes, had an unbiased prior and could read the surprise cleanly. This is a second-order cost of a noisy diet that rarely gets named: it does not just waste attention, it *distorts your priors*, pulling your probability estimates toward whatever is most viral rather than most likely. A diet dominated by engagement-optimized content will systematically bias you toward fear and drama, because fear and drama are what the engagement machine selects for.

### A rumor-driven whipsaw

Rumor-driven whipsaws are a recurring market feature, and they punish the undisciplined diet specifically. The pattern is always the same: an unconfirmed report — a rumored acquisition, a rumored policy shift, a rumored fund blowup — hits social and media, the price gaps, the rumor-chasers pile in, and then the primary source either confirms or denies. If it denies, or if the rumor was simply wrong, the price snaps back through its starting point as the trapped chasers are forced out, and the round-trip is brutal.

Episodes like this recur across asset classes — a "central bank to intervene" rumor in FX, a "major exchange is insolvent" rumor in crypto, a "company X is being acquired" rumor in equities. In each case the disciplined response is identical: log the rumor, identify the corroborating primary source, and wait. The traders who blow up on whipsaws are not the ones who *heard* the rumor — everyone heard it. They are the ones who *acted* on it before the tier they could verify spoke. The rumor was an input; the discipline was treating it as a thing-to-watch rather than a thing-to-trade.

What makes whipsaws so effective at destroying capital is that they exploit the exact reflex the diet is built to suppress. The rumor arrives with maximum salience — urgent, dramatic, time-pressured — and the salience reflex screams *act now or miss it*. The price is already moving, which manufactures false confirmation: "the market is reacting, so it must be true". Both of those signals — the urgency and the confirming price action — are precisely what you would expect to see whether the rumor is true or false, because the chasers move the price either way. They carry no information about the rumor's truth; they only carry information about how many other people are chasing. The disciplined trader's gate cuts through this by refusing to treat "the price is moving and everyone is talking about it" as evidence of anything except crowd behavior, and by demanding the one thing the crowd cannot fake: corroboration from a tier with actual provenance. The whipsaw punishes the salience reflex specifically, which is why the people most prone to it are the ones with the noisiest diets — they have spent months training the reflex the whipsaw preys on.

### A structural thesis where 90% of daily news was noise

Consider a multi-year structural view — for instance, a thesis built in 2020-2021 that inflation would prove more persistent than the consensus "transitory" framing, with a half-life measured in *quarters*. A trader holding that thesis was bombarded daily with news: every CPI print, every Fed speaker, every market-moving headline, every take on whether the latest data point confirmed or refuted "transitory".

For a structural thesis, the overwhelming majority of that daily flow was noise — below the thesis's half-life and irrelevant to the position. The disciplined holder filtered hard: the daily prints were data points to *log*, not to *trade*, and the thesis only updated on inputs that genuinely changed the structural picture — the trend in shelter and wage components over months, the evolution of the policy regime, the durable shift in the labor market. Perhaps 90% of the daily news that year was, for this thesis, pure noise. The trader who treated every CPI print as a reason to trade the thesis churned the position, paid the overtrading tax, and likely got shaken out of a correct view by the within-trend volatility. The trader who matched their reading to the thesis's half-life — logging the daily data, updating monthly on the structural inputs — held the view through the noise and was paid for it. **A structural thesis is not refuted by daily news; it is refuted by structural change, and confusing the two is how good long-horizon views die from short-horizon noise.**

## The playbook

Here is the concrete, repeatable system. Build your information diet in five steps and run it every day and every week.

**Step 1 — Build your tier list.** Write down, by name, your sources at each tier. Be specific and be ruthless about how few you need.

```
TIER 1 (primary data)   : economic calendar, the actual prints, price/volume, order book
TIER 2 (primary sources): full statement/transcript, filings, earnings call transcripts
TIER 3 (expert synth)   : 1-2 named synthesizers who SHOW THEIR WORK
TIER 4 (commentary)     : at most 1, scanned not read, for narrative awareness only
TIER 5 (social/rumor)   : NONE acted on; rumors logged-to-watch, never traded raw
```

**Step 2 — Map sources to horizon and asset.** Build the grid from the figure above. For each asset you trade, list a small daily set (Tier 1-2, 15 min), a weekly set (Tier 2-3, 60 min), and a structural set (monthly, slow). Cap the daily set near the calibration peak — five to seven high-signal sources, not more.

**Step 3 — Run every input through the decision-relevance filter.** Before reading anything in depth, ask: *would this change a position I hold or a probability I believe?* Route it:

```
INPUT ARRIVES
  -> Changes a decision?
       NO  -> DROP (interesting, not actionable)
       YES -> New + relevant (not already priced)?
                NO  -> DROP (already priced or stale)
                YES -> Fast-decaying & demands action?
                         YES -> ACT / RESIZE, update the written view
                         NO  -> LOG for the thesis (do not trade today)
```

**Step 4 — Enforce an unfollow and mute discipline.** A diet is subtractive. Once a quarter, audit your sources and your feeds. Mute or unfollow anything that generates noise — accounts that post engagement bait, recaps that lag the price, sources whose half-life never matches what you trade. Add a new source only by *replacing* an existing one. The list should stay roughly constant in size; if it only grows, you have stopped dieting.

The unfollow audit has a simple scoring rule. For each source, ask over the last quarter: how many times did this source give me an input that actually changed a decision, versus how many times did it merely generate the urge to react? A source with a high decisions-changed-to-urges-generated ratio earns its slot. A source that produced fifty hits of urgency and zero decision changes is pure noise wearing the costume of information, and it goes. Be especially suspicious of sources you *enjoy* reading — enjoyment and decision-relevance are uncorrelated, and the most entertaining sources are often the most engagement-optimized and therefore the noisiest. The goal is not a feed you like; it is a feed that changes your decisions for the better. If unfollowing a source makes you anxious that you will "miss something", that anxiety is itself the diagnosis: the source was feeding the salience reflex, not your decisions, and cutting it is the cure.

One more discipline belongs here: protect the *interpretation* time the cut frees up. The point of pruning sources is not to spend the reclaimed hours on more sources; it is to spend them thinking about the few you kept. Reading the FOMC statement is the start; the value comes from sitting with it, forming your own read of what changed, and writing down what would change your mind. A diet that cuts noise but then refills the time with more noise has changed nothing. Cut to make room for thought, then actually do the thought.

**Step 5 — Keep a standing "what to ignore" list.** Pre-decide which classes of input never earn attention, so they cannot trigger a reaction in the moment.

![What to ignore versus what to keep checklist card](/imgs/blogs/building-your-information-diet-signal-versus-noise-7.png)

The card is your standing rule. On the ignore side: price-action headlines that invent a reason after the move; anonymous flow and rumors with no primary source; hot takes optimized for engagement rather than decisions; and predictions with no number, which are unfalsifiable and therefore untradeable. On the keep side: the official release and its revisions; the actual central-bank statement text rather than the recap; two trusted synthesizers who show their work; and, the one rule that subsumes all the others, *anything that changes a size or a probability you hold*. Most of the daily feed lives on the left and earns nothing. The discipline is to pre-decide that, so the noise cannot pull you into a trade.

Run this system and the morning that opened this post inverts. You stop being the trader with 200 headlines and seven half-opinions, and you become the one with five primary sources, one real event, and a pre-decided action. You will read less, act less, and know more — and the spine of this whole series holds: a view is only worth holding if you know what's priced in, what you believe, and what would change your mind. A noisy diet drowns all three. A deliberate one is the foundation everything else is built on.
