> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A view you cannot write on one page is not clear yet. The trade-idea note is the artifact that forces that clarity and creates accountability: one dated page with thesis, what's priced vs your variant, evidence, catalyst, expression, size, invalidation, and markers to watch.
>
> - **Writing is the test.** The discipline of fitting a view onto one page exposes the holes — a missing invalidation, a vague catalyst, a thesis that is really three theses — *before* you have money on the line, when fixing them is free.
> - **The note is a pre-commitment device.** It is written at the calmest moment you will ever have about the trade, so the calm version of you can overrule the emotional version of you later. The dated record is what you grade your judgment against.
> - **Nine fields, one page.** Thesis sentence; what's priced in vs your variant; key evidence; catalyst + timeline; expression + instrument; size + dollar risk; invalidation; markers to watch; conviction tier. Each field is a question that, left unanswered, becomes a loss.
> - The one rule: **if it does not fit on one page, the view is not finished — and if there is no invalidation line, it is not a trade, it is a hope.**

A trader I will call the desk veteran has thirty open positions and not a single note. Ask him about any one of them and the good answers come fast: this is the one where the new plant ramps in Q3, here is exactly where I am wrong, the print that tells me lands on the 22nd. But somewhere around position number twelve the answers go soft. *That one… I think it was a chart thing. Or someone on a call mentioned it. It's down, so I'm watching it.* By position twenty he is guessing about his own book. He cannot tell you why he owns half of these names, what would make him sell, or what he is even waiting for. They are not trades anymore. They are residue — ideas that came in clear and decayed into a vague feeling that he is "in something" for a reason he can no longer reconstruct.

Now picture the same thirty positions in a different trader's hands, except this one keeps a one-page note for every single one. Ask her about position number twelve and she opens a file: *Thesis — packaging margins inflect on input-cost rollover, market is modeling flat. Priced in: no margin expansion. My variant: 200bps by Q4. Catalyst: Q3 print, Oct 22. Invalidation: gross margin prints below last year, or stock closes below \$38. Size: 1% risk, \$1,000, 250 shares. Marker to watch: weekly pulp price index.* She knows the thesis, the line where she is wrong, and the one number she is watching — for *all thirty*, because she wrote each one down on the day she put it on, while it was still clear in her head.

The difference between those two traders is not intelligence, information, or even discipline in the abstract. It is a single artifact: the **trade-idea note**. This post is about that artifact — what it is, the nine fields it contains, how to write each field so it actually does its job, and why the act of writing is itself the thing that turns a fuzzy hunch into a tradeable, accountable, gradeable view. The entire series has been about *forming* a view: reading the lenses, finding the variant, knowing what is priced in, sizing the bet. The note is where all of that lands on one page, gets dated, and becomes something you can be held to.

![One page trade idea note template with nine fields from thesis to markers](/imgs/blogs/the-trade-idea-note-a-one-page-template-1.png)

The figure above is the artifact itself — the one-page template, nine fields, the thing this whole post teaches you to fill before every trade. Read it top to bottom and it is the anatomy of a complete view: a one-sentence thesis at the top (green, because it is the asset you are building); the priced-in-versus-variant gap that is the source of edge; the evidence and catalyst that justify it; the expression, size, and dollar risk that make it a position; the invalidation (red, the line where it dies) and the markers you watch in between; and a conviction tier that ties it back to size. Every field is load-bearing. Leave one blank and the note tells you exactly where your thinking is not finished yet.

## Foundations: what a trade-idea note actually is

Start with the definition, because the word "note" makes it sound casual and it is anything but.

A **trade-idea note** is a single dated page, written *before* you enter a position, that states your complete view in a fixed set of fields: what you believe, why, what would prove you wrong, how you are expressing it, how much you are risking, and what you are watching. It is not a journal entry written after the fact. It is not a research report. It is the artifact that converts a view in your head — which is always vaguer than it feels — into an explicit, falsifiable, sized, dated object that exists outside your head where it can be checked, graded, and, crucially, used to overrule you later.

Three words in that definition do the heavy lifting, and each one corresponds to a job the note performs.

**Single page** is the forcing function. The constraint is the point — more on this below, but the short version is that a view which sprawls across five pages has not been distilled into a view yet; it is still a pile of inputs. One page forces the synthesis the series keeps insisting on: collapsing everything you know into a claim you can act on. (That collapse is its own skill — [from data to a read](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/from-data-to-a-read-collapsing-everything-into-one-sentence) is about getting to the one sentence the note opens with.)

**Before you enter** is the accountability function. A note written *before* is a pre-commitment: the calm, analytical version of you records, in cold blood, what you believe and what would change your mind, so that the anxious, hopeful, sunk-cost version of you — the one who will actually be holding the position when it moves against you — has something to obey. A note written *after* is a story. It can only rationalize what already happened. The whole power of the note is in the tense.

**Fixed fields** is the completeness function. A free-form paragraph lets you skip the parts you have not thought about — and the parts you skip are exactly the ones that cost you money. A template with nine named slots makes the gaps visible: an empty "invalidation" field is a flashing red light that says *you do not yet know when you are wrong, do not enter.* The fields are not bureaucracy; they are a checklist of the questions every complete view must answer, and a blank field is a question you are about to bet money on without answering.

### Why writing forces clarity

There is a specific, almost physical reason writing sharpens a view, and it is the single most important idea in this post: **a view in your head can be vague without you noticing; a view on paper cannot.**

In your head, a thesis can coast on a warm feeling of conviction without ever resolving its details. You "know" the stock is cheap. You "have a sense" the catalyst is coming. You are "pretty sure" you would get out if it broke down. None of those is a sentence. They are moods wearing the costume of analysis, and the costume holds up perfectly as long as you never try to write them down. The moment you put a pen to "my invalidation is ___," the mood collapses, because the blank demands a *number* and a mood does not have a number. You discover, in the act of writing, that you do not actually know where you are wrong — and that discovery, made at your desk for free, is the one you would otherwise have made in the market for \$11,000.

This is why the note is not a record of your thinking — it is the *mechanism* of your thinking. The fields interrogate you. "What's priced in?" forces you to articulate the consensus you are betting against, which most traders never do explicitly. "What's the catalyst?" forces you to name what makes you right *on a timeline*, which separates a trade from a permanent opinion. "What's your invalidation?" forces you to pre-commit to being wrong, which is the hardest sentence in the craft to write and the most valuable. You do not write the note because you already have the view; you write the note *to get* the view, and the friction of the blank fields is the work doing itself.

### The accountability function: a dated record to grade

The second job of the note is to create a record you can grade. This is what separates a trader who improves from one who merely accumulates years.

Without a dated note, your memory of your own decisions is a liar. After a trade closes, you remember a version of your reasoning edited by the outcome. If it won, you remember being more certain than you were; if it lost, you remember "always having doubts." This is hindsight bias, and it makes learning from your own track record nearly impossible, because the record has been silently rewritten to match what happened. You cannot improve a process you cannot see, and your unaided memory does not let you see it.

A dated note, written before the outcome existed, is immune to this. Six months later you can open it and read exactly what you believed, why, and what you said would prove you wrong — in your own words, untouched by what then occurred. Now you can grade the *decision*, not the *outcome*. Was the thesis right and the sizing wrong? Was the invalidation well-chosen but ignored? Did the catalyst land and the market not care, meaning your "priced in" read was off? Each of those is a different lesson, and you can only extract it if you have a faithful, dated record of the prior view to compare against the result. The note is the input to the [decision journal](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/the-decision-journal-the-habit-that-compounds) — the habit that compounds your judgment over years — and there is nothing to journal if there was never a note.

### How it differs from a research report

People conflate the trade-idea note with a research report, and the conflation produces bad versions of both. They are different artifacts with different jobs.

A **research report** is for *building* the view: it is the thirty-page workbook with the model, the channel checks, the comp tables, the management-call transcripts. It is comprehensive by design — its job is to leave no stone unturned, and length is a virtue because completeness is the goal. You may well produce one. But it is *upstream* of the note.

A **trade-idea note** is for *acting* on the view: it is the one-page distillation of what the report concluded, in the specific form an action requires. Its job is the opposite of comprehensive — it is *decisive*. It throws away ninety-five percent of the report and keeps only the part that bears on the decision: the claim, the edge, the trigger, the line where you are wrong, the size. The report answers "what is true about this company?" The note answers "what am I doing about it, how much, and when do I stop?" A report with no note is research that never became a position. A note with no report is a guess. You want both, in that order, and you must never let the report's habit of completeness leak into the note, because a complete note is a contradiction in terms.

### The one-page discipline

Why one page, specifically? Because the page length is doing analytical work, not formatting work.

The constraint forces *prioritization under scarcity*, which is the same skill the market demands. When you only have one page, you cannot include the seventh-most-important reason; you have to decide which two pieces of evidence actually move you, and that decision is the analysis. A view that "needs" five pages has not identified its own load-bearing claim — it is hiding the weakness of any single argument behind the volume of all of them. The one-page limit is a filter that catches that weakness. If your real reason fits in a sentence and your note is five pages, four-and-three-quarter pages are padding you are using to feel thorough. If your real reason genuinely does not fit on a page, you have either a portfolio of trades masquerading as one, or a view too entangled to act on — and either way the page tells you something true.

There is a practical version of this too. A one-page note can be *re-read in thirty seconds* when a position is moving and you have to decide fast. A five-page report cannot be consulted in the heat of a 9% gap-down; by the time you have re-read it, the decision is moot. The note has to be short enough to function as an operating manual under pressure, and one page is roughly the size of a thing you can re-internalize in a single anxious glance. The discipline is not about saving paper. It is about producing an artifact that is actually usable at the only moment that matters — when the position is hurting and you need to remember, fast, why you are in it and where you said you would get out.

One more property of the one-page form is worth naming, because it is the reason the note scales to a whole book. A trader with thirty one-page notes has a *portfolio* he can review in fifteen minutes — flip through thirty pages, confirm each thesis is intact, check each invalidation has not been hit, glance at each marker. A trader with thirty five-page reports has fifteen *hours* of reading he will never do, which is the same as having no review at all. The one-page constraint is what makes per-position discipline survive contact with a real book of positions; the moment the note balloons, the review stops happening, and the positions decay back into the residue of the desk veteran in the opening. Brevity is not a nicety here — it is what makes the practice sustainable across more than two or three open ideas at once.

## The nine fields, one at a time

The template has nine fields. We will walk each one, define what belongs in it, and — more importantly — describe what a *good* version of that field looks like versus the weak version that passes as filled but does no work. The fields are ordered to mirror how a view actually forms: belief first, then edge, then justification, then mechanism, then position, then risk.

![The nine fields of a trade idea note from thesis sentence to conviction tier](/imgs/blogs/the-trade-idea-note-a-one-page-template-2.png)

The figure shows the nine fields as the note lays them out, color-coded by what they do: the thesis and variant in green and blue (the view and its edge), the catalyst and markers in amber (timing and what you watch), the invalidation in red (the line where it dies), and size as the number the whole thing produces. Hold this map in mind as we take the fields one at a time, because each field's quality is what the whole note's quality reduces to.

### Field 1 — The one-sentence thesis

The first field is a single sentence stating what you believe and expect to happen. Not a paragraph. One sentence, with a subject, a claim, and ideally a magnitude and direction.

A **good thesis sentence** is falsifiable and specific: *"Packaging-co's gross margin inflects up by ~200bps over the next two quarters as input costs roll over, and the market is modeling flat margins."* You can argue with that. It names a thing (gross margin), a magnitude (200bps), a mechanism (input-cost rollover), a timeframe (two quarters), and the consensus it disagrees with (flat). It is a claim that the world can prove false.

A **weak thesis sentence** is a mood: *"I like this stock here, looks cheap, good setup."* That cannot be wrong because it does not say anything. "Cheap" relative to what? "Good setup" by whose measure, resolving when? A thesis you cannot falsify is a thesis you cannot grade, size, or invalidate — which means the other eight fields have nothing to attach to. This is why the thesis is field one: it is the spine, and the entire technique of getting to a clean one-sentence claim is the subject of [structuring a thesis](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/structuring-a-thesis-claim-evidence-and-catalyst). If you cannot write field one as a real sentence, stop — there is no trade yet.

### Field 2 — What's priced in vs your variant

The second field is the source of edge, and it is the field amateurs skip entirely. Two lines: what the market currently believes (the consensus, the priced-in expectation), and where your view differs (your variant perception).

This field exists because **you only get paid for being right in a way the market is wrong.** Being right in a way everyone already agrees with earns you nothing — it is already in the price. So the note forces you to state the consensus explicitly — *"market is modeling flat margins, sell-side estimates cluster around 22% gross margin"* — and then state your departure from it — *"I think 24% by Q4 because the pulp index has already rolled and the lag is two quarters."* The gap between those two lines *is* your edge. If you cannot fill this field — if you cannot articulate what the market believes that you think is wrong — then you do not have a variant view, you have the consensus, and there is no edge to harvest. (Variant perception is the whole ballgame; the mechanics of mapping consensus and finding the gap are covered across the series, and the priced-in question specifically in [consensus expectations and priced-in](/blog/trading/event-trading/consensus-expectations-and-priced-in).)

### Field 3 — Key evidence

The third field is the *short* list of what actually supports the variant — two or three pieces, not twenty. This is where the one-page discipline bites hardest, because your research report has twenty reasons and the note has room for the two that move you.

Good evidence is specific and recent: *"Pulp price index down 18% over six months (mechanism for the cost rollover); company's own Q2 call flagged 'input-cost tailwinds into H2'; closest comp already showed the margin inflection last quarter."* Each item is a concrete, checkable fact that bears directly on the thesis. Weak evidence is a vibe-pile: *"strong management, good industry, technicals look constructive, sentiment improving."* None of those is load-bearing for *this specific margin call.* The discipline of listing only two or three forces you to identify which facts actually drive the view versus which are decoration you accumulated to feel confident. If your top three pieces of evidence are weak, the note shows you that the conviction was borrowed from volume, not strength.

### Field 4 — Catalyst and timeline

The fourth field names what makes the market come around to your view, and *when.* A view without a catalyst is a permanent opinion, and a permanent opinion is not a trade — it is a way to have your capital tied up indefinitely in something that is "right" but never pays.

A good catalyst is a specific, dated event: *"Q3 earnings print, October 22, where the margin inflection first becomes visible in the segment data."* It tells you both what resolves the uncertainty and roughly when, which is what lets you size the trade against a timeframe and set a time stop. A weak catalyst is open-ended: *"eventually the market will notice."* That is not a catalyst; it is a hope with no clock, and "cheap can stay cheap for years" is precisely the trap it walks you into. (Why timing is the hard part of the whole game, and how to think about catalysts, is [catalysts and timing](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/catalysts-and-timing-why-cheap-can-stay-cheap-for-years).) The catalyst field is also what makes the trade *gradeable*: when October 22 comes, you find out, which means you have a date on which to check your variant against reality.

### Field 5 — Expression and instrument

The fifth field is *how* you are putting the view on. The same thesis can be expressed many ways, and the note forces you to choose and justify one: long the stock outright, a call spread, a pairs trade against a comp, a position sized for a specific catalyst with options that expire just after it.

This field matters because the expression has to match the thesis's shape. A view with a sharp dated catalyst and a defined downside might be best expressed as a call spread expiring just after the print — you pay a known premium, you cap the cost of being wrong, and you concentrate the bet on the window where you expect to be proven right. A view about a slow structural mispricing with a fuzzy timeline is a terrible fit for short-dated options (theta will bleed you out before the thesis plays) and a fine fit for owning the stock. The note's expression field forces the question *does my instrument match my catalyst's timeline and my thesis's shape?* — a question that, unasked, is how traders end up right on the view and wrong on the vehicle. (Choosing the right vehicle, including when to express a view without a directional bet, is the subject of [choosing the instrument](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/choosing-the-instrument-to-express-your-thesis).)

### Field 6 — Size and dollar risk

The sixth field turns the view into a position: how many shares or contracts, and — the number that actually matters — how many dollars you lose if you are wrong. This field is *not* the notional value of the position; it is the dollar risk, the loss you take if the invalidation fires.

The note demands this be an explicit number, computed, not felt: *"1% risk on a \$100,000 account = \$1,000; invalidation \$4 below entry; therefore 250 shares, \$12,500 notional, \$1,000 at risk."* Writing the dollar risk down does two things. First, it makes you do the arithmetic that converts conviction into a share count, which prevents sizing by enthusiasm. Second, it creates a number you can sum across your whole book to see your total open risk. A trader who writes \$1,000 risk on thirty notes knows his portfolio heat at a glance; a trader with no notes is leveraged to an amount he cannot name. (The full machinery of converting a view into an exact size — risk-per-trade, fractional Kelly, volatility-targeting, the heat cap — is [from conviction to size](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/from-conviction-to-size-the-bet-sizing-bridge); the note is where that machinery's output gets recorded.)

### Field 7 — Invalidation

The seventh field is the most important sentence on the page, and the one most often left blank: the specific, observable condition that, if it happens, means the thesis is broken and you exit. Not "if it goes down a lot." A condition with a number.

A good invalidation is concrete and tied to the thesis: *"gross margin prints flat-or-down in Q3 (the thesis is the inflection, so no inflection = no thesis), OR the stock closes below \$38 (violating the base that defines my entry risk)."* Notice it has two flavors — a *fundamental* invalidation (the thesis-driver itself failing) and a *price* invalidation (a level that caps the loss regardless). A complete note names at least one, and ideally both. A weak or missing invalidation is the single most expensive omission in trading: without it, a position that goes against you has no defined exit, the line in your head drifts down with the price, and a \$1,000 loss becomes an \$11,000 bag. The note's invalidation field is the pre-commitment that ends that story before it starts, which is exactly why it must be written *before* entry, in cold blood. (The full treatment — the four kinds of invalidation, why it is not the same as a stop-loss, and how to write one you cannot move — is [what would change my mind](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/what-would-change-my-mind-defining-invalidation-upfront).)

### Field 8 — Markers to watch

The eighth field lists the handful of things you will monitor *between* entry and resolution — the leading indicators that tell you the thesis is on track or quietly breaking, before the catalyst itself arrives. The invalidation is the line where you exit; the markers are the dials you watch on the way there.

For the margin thesis, the markers might be: *"weekly pulp price index (the mechanism — if it reverses, the cost tailwind is gone); competitor margin prints (early read on the industry-wide inflection); company pre-announcements or guidance updates."* These are not exit triggers by themselves; they are the dashboard that tells you whether your thesis is aging well or rotting, so you are not blindsided at the catalyst. A note with good markers turns "watching it" from a vague anxiety into a specific, finite checklist — three numbers you glance at weekly — which is exactly how the disciplined trader in the opening can hold thirty positions without losing track of any of them.

### Field 9 — Conviction tier

The ninth field is a single label that ties the whole note back to size: how sure are you, on a coarse scale (say, low / medium / high, or a 1–5 tier), and the field forces you to be honest about it rather than treating every idea as a five-alarm conviction. The tier maps to a size multiplier, so a high-conviction note gets the full risk unit and a low-conviction "starter" note gets a fraction of it.

The point of a *coarse* tier is that you cannot actually distinguish 71% confidence from 76%, and pretending you can is false precision. But you can absolutely tell a "this is my best idea of the quarter" from a "interesting, worth a small probe," and the conviction tier captures that real, usable distinction. It also creates accountability of a subtle kind: when you grade the notes later, you can check whether your *high-conviction* ideas actually outperformed your *low-conviction* ones. If they did not — if your "high conviction" trades did no better than your tentative ones — then your sense of conviction is uncalibrated, and that is one of the most valuable things a stack of dated notes can ever teach you. (How to measure and calibrate conviction, rather than just assert it, is [measuring conviction](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/measuring-conviction-how-sure-are-you-really); the note records the output.)

The conviction tier is also the field that prevents a subtle and expensive failure: treating every idea as a maximum-conviction idea. A trader who marks every note "high conviction" has destroyed the information the field carries — if everything is a five, then nothing is, and the tier no longer modulates size, which means his best ideas and his idle probes get the same capital. That is backwards. The whole reason to grade conviction is so that capital flows toward the views you are most sure of and trickles toward the speculative ones, and a tier that is always "high" cannot do that. The honesty the field demands is the same honesty the invalidation field demands: it asks you to admit, on paper, something the warm feeling of being in a trade would rather you not — here, that this particular idea is a *maybe*, not a conviction, and should be sized like one.

### Mapping the fields to the process

The nine fields are not an arbitrary list; each one is the *output* of a step in the analytical process the rest of this series teaches. Reading the fields as a checklist is useful, but reading them as the *terminal artifacts* of a process is what shows you why the note is the natural endpoint of forming a view rather than a separate chore bolted on afterward.

![Mapping note fields to the analytical process steps that produce each one](/imgs/blogs/the-trade-idea-note-a-one-page-template-4.png)

The figure maps each field to the process step that produces it. *Reading the lenses* and synthesizing into one sentence produces the **thesis** (field 1). *Mapping the consensus* and finding the variant produces the **priced-in/variant** field (field 2). *Building the case* produces the **evidence** (field 3). *Locating the catalyst* on a timeline produces the **catalyst** field (field 4). *Choosing the expression* produces field 5. *Sizing the bet* — risk-per-trade through the invalidation distance — produces the **size and dollar risk** (field 6) and depends on the **invalidation** (field 7). *Defining what would change your mind* produces field 7 directly. The **markers** (field 8) come from the thesis's mechanism — the leading indicators that the driver is intact. And **conviction** (field 9) is the calibrated output of the whole synthesis, which sets the size multiplier in field 6. Seen this way, the note is not extra work after the analysis; it is the analysis, written in the form that an action can use. Every field has a step upstream of it, and a blank field means you skipped the step.

## The note as a pre-commitment device

Step back from the fields and look at what the note *does* as an object, because its deepest function is psychological, not informational.

The fundamental problem the note solves is that **the person who decides to enter a trade and the person who has to manage it are not in the same emotional state.** You enter calm, analytical, detached — you have not lost anything yet, the position is theoretical, you can think clearly. You *manage* the trade anxious, hopeful, attached, and increasingly desperate as it moves against you. The calm version of you is a far better decision-maker than the anxious version, and the entire value of writing the note *before* entry is that it lets the calm version *bind* the anxious version. The invalidation line written at \$50, in cold blood, is the calm trader reaching forward in time to grab the anxious trader's hand at \$45 and say: *you already decided this. Sell.*

This is the same logic as Odysseus tying himself to the mast before the sirens. He knew that the version of himself who heard the song would make a catastrophic decision, so the version who had not yet heard it removed his future self's ability to act on it. The note is your mast. You write the invalidation while you can still think, so that when the position is screaming at you and every instinct says "average down, it's even cheaper now," you have a piece of paper in your own handwriting that overrules the instinct. Without the note, the anxious version of you is in sole charge at the worst possible moment, and the anxious version of you is the one who turns a stop into a magnet and a trade into a bag.

The pre-commitment works because of an asymmetry in *when* the two versions of you exist. The calm analyst exists only once per trade — at the moment of entry, before any money is at stake — and then he is gone, replaced for the entire life of the position by the emotional manager. If the calm analyst does not write anything down, his judgment evaporates the instant the trade goes live, and the emotional manager runs the position with no adult supervision. The note is the only way the calm analyst's judgment *persists* into the period when it is needed most. This is also why the note must be written in the *imperative* where it counts — the invalidation should read "exit if X," an instruction, not "I might consider trimming if things look bad," a suggestion the emotional manager can negotiate away. A pre-commitment that can be renegotiated in the moment is not a pre-commitment; it is a wish. The whole art of the invalidation field is writing it in language tight enough that the panicking version of you cannot find a loophole, because he will look for one, and he is clever, and he has all day.

![A vague note before drifts into a bag while a sharp note after exits on principle](/imgs/blogs/the-trade-idea-note-a-one-page-template-3.png)

The before-and-after makes the mechanism concrete. On the left, the vague note: a thesis that is a mood, no priced-in line, no real invalidation. When the position falls, there is nothing written to obey, so the trader improvises — the mental stop drifts, the loss compounds, the position becomes a bag held on pain. On the right, the sharp note: a falsifiable thesis, a numbered invalidation, a sized position. When the same fall happens, the invalidation fires, the trader exits on principle, and the loss is the small bounded number he signed up for weeks ago. Same trade, same drawdown — the only difference is whether a pre-committed page was there to overrule the panic.

## Worked examples: filling the note for real

Theory is cheap. Here is the note doing its job, in dollars.

#### Worked example: a complete filled note with size, invalidation distance, and EV

Here is a fully filled note for a real-style trade, every field populated, with the numbers that make it a position.

- **Thesis:** Packaging-co's gross margin inflects up ~200bps over two quarters as input costs roll over; the market is modeling flat margins.
- **Priced in / variant:** Sell-side clusters at ~22% gross margin (flat). My variant: ~24% by Q4, because the pulp index already rolled and the lag is ~2 quarters.
- **Evidence:** Pulp index −18% over 6 months; Q2 call flagged "input-cost tailwinds into H2"; closest comp already inflected last quarter.
- **Catalyst:** Q3 print, October 22 — the segment margin first becomes visible.
- **Expression:** Long the common stock (slow structural rollover, multi-quarter — wrong shape for short-dated options).
- **Size:** 1% risk on a \$100,000 account = **\$1,000 risk.** Entry \$50, invalidation \$46, so invalidation distance = **\$4/share.** Shares = \$1,000 ÷ \$4 = **250 shares**, **\$12,500 notional.**
- **Invalidation:** Gross margin prints flat-or-down in Q3 (thesis dead), OR stock closes below \$46 (base broken).
- **Markers:** Weekly pulp index; competitor margin prints; any company pre-announcement.
- **Conviction:** High (best idea of the quarter) → full 1% unit.

Now the expected value, written right onto the note so the bet is honest. Suppose the inflection plays out: stock to \$62 (a \$12 gain, \$3,000 on 250 shares), which I put at 55%. The thesis fails partially — margins flat, stock drifts to \$46 invalidation (\$4 loss, \$1,000): 30%. Tail risk — a sector shock takes it through the stop to \$42 before I exit (\$8 effective loss, \$2,000): 15%.

$$\text{EV} = (0.55 \times \$3{,}000) + (0.30 \times -\$1{,}000) + (0.15 \times -\$2{,}000) = \$1{,}650 - \$300 - \$300 = \$1{,}050$$

The note carries a **+\$1,050 expected value** against \$1,000 of defined risk — a favorable bet, and I can see *why* it is favorable because the page forced me to lay out the probabilities and payoffs. The intuition: the note does not just describe the trade, it lets me check that the bet is positive-EV before I take it, which is the difference between a wager and a guess.

#### Worked example: the vague note vs the sharp note and the execution difference

Two traders, same stock at \$50, same \$100,000 account. Trader A's "note" is one line: *"Like this here, cheap, good story, buying some."* Trader B's note is the filled one above.

Trader A buys "some" — he eyeballs it and puts in \$20,000 (400 shares) because he is "confident." No invalidation written. The stock falls to \$46. He feels fine — *cheaper now.* It falls to \$42. His paper loss is 400 × \$8 = **\$3,200**, and he has no written line, so he holds, telling himself the thesis is "more compelling." At \$36 the loss is 400 × \$14 = **\$5,600**, and he finally capitulates near the lows on pain. He lost \$5,600 — 5.6% of his account — on a trade he never sized or bounded.

Trader B sized at \$1,000 risk with a \$46 invalidation, so she owns 250 shares. The stock closes below \$46. Her invalidation fires; she exits on principle, losing exactly 250 × \$4 = **\$1,000** — 1% of her account. Same stock, same move. Trader A lost \$5,600; Trader B lost \$1,000. The \$4,600 difference is not skill at picking stocks — they picked the *same* stock. It is the difference between a vague note and a sharp one, paid out in dollars. The sharp note bounded the loss to the number she chose; the vague note let the loss be whatever the market decided.

#### Worked example: the note catching a missing invalidation before entry

Here is the note doing its most valuable work — *before* a single dollar is committed. A trader drafts a note for a biotech long ahead of a Phase 3 readout. He fills the thesis (drug works, stock doubles), the catalyst (data due in 6 weeks), the evidence (strong Phase 2), the conviction (high). He goes to fill **size** and writes "1% risk, \$1,000." Then he hits the **invalidation** field and stops cold, because the honest answer is: *if the trial fails, this stock gaps from \$30 to \$8 overnight, and there is no \$46 to stop at — the loss is not \$4 a share, it is \$22 a share, and it happens while I am asleep.*

The note has just told him something the warm conviction never would: his "1% risk" was a fiction. At \$30 with a binary readout, buying \$1,000-of-risk worth of stock the normal way (333 shares with a \$3 stop) does *nothing* — a failed trial blows through any stop instantly to \$8, making his real risk 333 × \$22 = **\$7,326**, not \$1,000. The invalidation field exposed that the position cannot be risk-controlled with a stop at all. The fix the note forces: either express it with defined-risk options (buy calls, where the max loss *is* the premium, a real \$1,000 cap), or size the stock to the *gap* (\$1,000 ÷ \$22 ≈ 45 shares) and accept the tiny position. Either way, the note caught a \$6,300 sizing error before entry, for the price of taking the invalidation field seriously. The intuition: the empty field is not a formality — it is the trade telling you its real risk before the market does.

#### Worked example: sizing 1% risk on a \$100,000 account, written into the note

The size field is where the note refuses to let you hand-wave. Take the \$100,000 account and the 1% rule, and watch the invalidation distance — written one field above — drive the share count, all of it recorded on the page so it can be checked and summed.

| Invalidation distance | Shares = \$1,000 ÷ dist. | Notional | Dollar risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| \$2.00 (tight base) | 500 | \$25,000 | \$1,000 |
| \$4.00 (the example) | 250 | \$12,500 | \$1,000 |
| \$8.00 (wide, volatile) | 125 | \$6,250 | \$1,000 |

Every row risks exactly **\$1,000** — 1% of the account — yet the position notional swings 4×, driven entirely by where the invalidation sits. The note records both numbers (distance *and* dollar risk) precisely so this dependency is explicit: a trader who only wrote "buy \$10,000 worth" would be silently risking \$400 on a tight stop and \$2,000 on a wide one, with no record that his risk was floating. By writing "invalidation \$4, risk \$1,000, 250 shares," the note pins the loss and lets the size fall out as arithmetic. The intuition: the note makes the dollar risk the constant you choose and the position size the consequence — never the other way around.

![Bar chart of position notional held at constant 1000 dollar risk across stop distances](/imgs/blogs/the-trade-idea-note-a-one-page-template-6.png)

The chart plots the same three rows: the dollar risk is the flat amber line at \$1,000 — identical for every trade — while the blue bars (the notional you actually deploy) shrink as the invalidation distance widens. Holding risk constant is the discipline; the size adjusts to honor it. The note is what records both so you can see, across thirty positions, that your risk really is constant even when your position sizes are not.

## The note → review loop

The note's value compounds because it feeds a loop. A note is not a one-time document you file and forget; it is the first half of a cycle whose second half is grading it against the outcome and feeding the lesson back into the next note.

![The note to review loop from write through entry monitor outcome and grade back to next note](/imgs/blogs/the-trade-idea-note-a-one-page-template-5.png)

The loop runs: you *write* the note (forcing clarity); you *enter* on it (the note is the operating manual); you *monitor* against the markers and invalidation (the note is the dashboard); the trade *resolves* at the catalyst; you *grade* the decision against the dated note (was the thesis right? the sizing? the invalidation respected?); and the lesson feeds into the *next* note. The loop is what turns a stack of dated pages into a *track record of judgment* — and crucially, it grades the decision, not just the P&L. A losing trade with a sound, well-followed note is a *good decision* with a bad outcome (the EV was positive, the dice came up wrong), and you should make that decision again. A winning trade with a vague, unfollowed note is a *bad decision* with a lucky outcome, and you should not. Only the dated note lets you tell those four cases apart, which is why the note is the precursor to the [decision journal](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/the-decision-journal-the-habit-that-compounds) and why the loop, run over hundreds of trades, is how judgment actually improves.

## Common misconceptions

A few beliefs keep traders from writing notes, and each one is wrong in an instructive way.

### "Notes are bureaucracy"

The complaint is that writing a note is administrative overhead that slows you down and adds nothing the trade itself does not. This inverts what is happening. The note is not paperwork *about* the analysis; the note *is* the analysis, performed in its final, decision-ready form. The friction you feel writing it — the resistance when you hit the invalidation field and realize you do not have an answer — is not bureaucracy slowing you down; it is the thinking you skipped, showing up to be done. A trader who finds notes "bureaucratic" usually means he finds it uncomfortable to discover, in writing, that his views are vaguer than they felt. That discomfort is the value, not the cost. The bureaucracy framing is the part of you that wants to keep trading on moods making an excuse to avoid the test.

### "I keep it in my head"

This is the most seductive misconception because it feels true — you *do* have the thesis in your head, and writing it down feels redundant. But a view in your head is exactly the thing that can be vague without your noticing, and "I keep it in my head" is precisely how you end up at position number twelve unable to say why you own it. The head is where invalidations drift, where conviction silently inflates after the fact, where the catalyst quietly becomes "eventually." Everything the note protects against happens *in the head;* moving the view out of the head onto the page is the entire mechanism. Worse, a view in your head leaves no dated record, so you cannot grade it — which means the trader who "keeps it in his head" not only manages each trade worse but never improves, because his track record is unobservable even to himself.

### "Longer is more thorough"

Many people, especially analytically strong ones, equate length with rigor — a five-page note must be more careful than a one-page note. The opposite is usually true. Length is most often a symptom of *not having found the load-bearing claim:* the trader writes everything because he cannot tell what matters, and the page count is the unresolved analysis spilling onto paper. A one-page note that names the two pieces of evidence that actually move the view is more rigorous, not less, because it required the prioritization the five-pager dodged. Thoroughness in the *research* (the upstream report) is a virtue; thoroughness in the *note* is a failure to distill. The note's job is decisiveness under a constraint, and a note that cannot honor the constraint is telling you the view is not finished.

### "The note is for after, not before"

Some treat the note as a journaling exercise — something you write up after the trade to record what happened. A post-trade note is a fine *input to grading*, but it is a fundamentally weaker artifact than a pre-trade note, because it cannot do the two most valuable jobs: it cannot force clarity *before* you commit capital (the holes you would have caught are already paid for), and it cannot serve as a pre-commitment device (there is nothing to bind the anxious manager-version of you, because the note did not exist while the trade was live). The tense is load-bearing. A note written before entry is a contract with your future self; a note written after is a story about your past self. You want the contract. Write it before.

## How it plays out in real markets

The discipline is easiest to see against episodes where the *absence* of a note was the whole problem.

### The crowded long with no invalidation, 2021–2022

Through 2021, a generation of traders held high-multiple growth names — the unprofitable software and consumer-tech complex — on a thesis that was rarely written down because it did not need to be: *number go up.* There was no priced-in line (they never asked what was already in a stock trading at 30× sales), no invalidation (what print, what level, would have proven the thesis wrong?), and no catalyst beyond "more of the same." When the regime turned in late 2021 and rates began climbing into 2022, those names fell 60–80%. Traders without notes had no written line to obey, so they held the whole way down, averaging into "even cheaper" positions, because the thing that would have stopped them — a pre-committed invalidation written while they could still think — was never on paper. A one-line note with an honest invalidation field ("exit if it closes below the pre-2021 base, or if the 10-year yield breaks 2%") would have forced the question that the euphoria suppressed: *what would prove me wrong?* The absence of the note was not incidental to the blowup; it was the mechanism.

### The right thesis, wrong size, 2020 oil

In April 2020, some traders correctly reasoned that crude was absurdly cheap and would recover. The thesis was *right* — oil did recover over the following year. But many expressed it through the front-month futures or the popular oil ETF without a note that forced them to confront the *expression* and *invalidation* fields, and the front month went *negative* on April 20, 2020 — a contango and storage dynamic that a note's "expression / instrument" field would have flagged as a mismatch between the view (structural recovery, multi-month) and the vehicle (a front-month contract with a brutal roll). A note that asked "does my instrument match my thesis's timeline?" would have steered the same correct view into longer-dated exposure and survived to collect on it. The traders who blew up were not wrong about oil. They were wrong about the vehicle, and the field that catches that — expression — is one they never filled.

### The note that saved the trade, FOMC 2022

A disciplined macro trader going into a 2022 FOMC wrote a complete note: thesis (the Fed will out-hawk a market that is under-pricing the terminal rate), priced-in (market pricing a dovish pivot, fed funds futures implying cuts by year-end), variant (no cuts, higher-for-longer), catalyst (this FOMC and the presser), invalidation (*exit if the dot plot shows cuts or the chair signals a pause* — a specific, observable condition), size (1% risk, defined). When the meeting came and the chair delivered exactly the hawkish surprise the note predicted, the trader did not have to *decide* anything in the chaos of the print — the note had already decided, and he simply executed the position he had written. And had the chair gone the other way, the invalidation field gave him an instant, unemotional exit. The note's value here was not that the thesis was right; it was that the trader's behavior *in the moment* was governed by a page written in calm, not by the adrenaline of the print. That is the pre-commitment function doing exactly its job. (Why the same surprise moves different assets differently — the read the note's thesis field has to get right — is [the reaction function](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-reaction-function-why-the-same-number-moves-differently).)

## The playbook: the one-page template you copy and fill

Here is the artifact, reduced to the template you copy and fill before every trade. Print it, keep it in a file, fill all nine fields before you commit a dollar. An empty field is not an inconvenience — it is the trade telling you which part of your thinking is not finished.

![Trade idea note checklist card with nine fields and the rule that an empty field blocks entry](/imgs/blogs/the-trade-idea-note-a-one-page-template-7.png)

The card above is the operating version: nine fields, the question each one answers, and the rule at the bottom — *no field is blank before entry.* Use it as the checklist that gates every position.

The template, field by field, with the question each field must answer:

1. **Date & ticker.** When you wrote it and what it is on. The date is what makes the note gradeable later.
2. **Thesis (one sentence).** *What do I believe will happen, specifically and falsifiably?* If you cannot write one sentence with a magnitude and a direction, there is no trade yet.
3. **Priced in vs variant (two lines).** *What does the market believe, and where exactly do I differ?* The gap is your edge. No gap, no trade.
4. **Key evidence (2–3 items).** *What are the two or three facts that actually move me?* Not twenty. The ones that are load-bearing.
5. **Catalyst & timeline.** *What makes the market come around, and when?* A specific dated event. No catalyst means a permanent opinion, not a trade.
6. **Expression & instrument.** *How am I putting this on, and does the vehicle match the thesis's timeline and shape?*
7. **Size & dollar risk.** *How many shares, and how many dollars do I lose if the invalidation fires?* Computed, not felt: risk-per-trade ÷ invalidation distance = share count.
8. **Invalidation.** *What specific, observable condition means I am wrong, and I exit?* The most important line on the page. A number, not a mood.
9. **Markers to watch.** *What handful of indicators tells me the thesis is on track or rotting between now and the catalyst?*
10. **Conviction tier.** *How sure am I, on a coarse scale, and what size multiplier does that justify?*

The rules that make the template work:

- **Write it before you enter.** Always. The tense is the whole point — a pre-trade note is a contract with your future self; a post-trade note is just a story.
- **No blank fields.** An empty field is a flashing light. Empty invalidation = do not enter. Empty priced-in = you have the consensus, not an edge. Empty catalyst = you have an opinion, not a trade.
- **One page, hard.** If it does not fit, you have not distilled the view — or you have a portfolio of trades pretending to be one. Cut to the load-bearing claim.
- **Keep the note when the trade closes.** The note is the input to the [decision journal](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/the-decision-journal-the-habit-that-compounds). Grade the *decision* — thesis, sizing, invalidation discipline — not the P&L, and feed the lesson into the next note.
- **One number to remember:** the **dollar risk** in field 7. It is the loss you chose, summed across your book it is your portfolio heat, and it is the single number a trader with notes can name and a trader without notes cannot.

The note will not make your views right. Nothing does that reliably. What it does is guarantee that every view you act on is *clear* (because it fit on one page), *bounded* (because the invalidation field is filled), *sized* (because the dollar risk is computed), and *gradeable* (because it is dated). Those four properties are the difference between the trader with thirty positions and no idea why he owns half of them, and the trader who can open any one of thirty notes and tell you the thesis, the line where she is wrong, and the one number she is watching. The note is one page. It is the cheapest, highest-return page in the entire process — and it is the one almost nobody writes.
