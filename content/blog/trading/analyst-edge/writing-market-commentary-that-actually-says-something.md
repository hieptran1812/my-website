> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Most market commentary is a recap dressed as insight ("stocks fell on profit-taking"). Real commentary makes a **falsifiable claim**, states **what is priced in versus your own view**, and names **what would change your mind**. The discipline is climbing the *description → analysis → call* ladder until your note says something a desk could act on.
>
> - **Description is not analysis, and analysis is not a call.** "S&P fell 1.4%" is a fact anyone can verify. "The selloff is positioning-driven, not fundamental" is a claim. "I'd fade it, and I'm wrong if breadth keeps deteriorating" is a call. Only the last one is worth reading.
> - The single test for every sentence you write: **could a future observation prove it wrong?** If nothing could refute it, you wrote a horoscope, not a view.
> - A regular note is a **forcing function**: it makes you form a daily view and stamps it with a date so you build an honest, scoreable track record — including the weeks you correctly write *"no edge, staying flat."*
> - The one rule to remember: **if your note would read the same no matter what the market did next, it said nothing.**

## Two notes, same day

It is a Tuesday afternoon in October. The S&P 500 has fallen 1.4%, the ten-year yield is up 8 basis points, and the VIX has jumped from 14 to 19. Two analysts at two different desks sit down to write the note that goes out to their distribution list before the close. Same tape, same data feed, same hour.

The first note reads: *"Markets were volatile today amid ongoing uncertainty. Equities sold off on profit-taking as investors weighed mixed signals, while Treasury yields rose. Sentiment remains cautious heading into a busy data week. We continue to monitor developments closely."* It is grammatical, it is calm, it sounds professional, and it is completely empty. Read it again and ask: what does this person actually *believe*? What would they *do*? Nothing in those four sentences could be proven wrong tomorrow, because nothing in them is a claim. It would have read identically if the market had rallied 1.4% — just swap "sold off" for "rallied." This is commentary as wallpaper.

The second note reads: *"Today's selloff is positioning-driven, not fundamental. Breadth held up (only 55% of the S&P is down, advancers in defensives), credit spreads barely moved, and the move is concentrated in the most-crowded momentum names — this looks like de-grossing, not a growth scare. I'd fade it: I'm adding to S&P longs here around 5,180, sized to risk 1R. I'm wrong if breadth keeps deteriorating into Friday — if the advance-decline line makes a new low while price holds, the de-grossing thesis is dead and I'm out."* That note makes a claim, tells you what the writer believes versus what the tape is saying, says exactly what they're doing about it, and stamps a condition under which they admit they were wrong. You can disagree with it. You can score it on Friday. It is worth reading precisely *because* it can be wrong.

This post is about writing the second note instead of the first. The skill is not prose; it is judgment made visible. We will build the ladder that takes you from describing what happened to stating a falsifiable call, kill the hindsight-narrative habit that makes most commentary worthless, and give you a template for a weekly note that forces a real view and leaves a track record behind it.

![Three-rung stack from description to analysis to a falsifiable call](/imgs/blogs/writing-market-commentary-that-actually-says-something-1.png)

The figure above is the whole post in one picture. At the base sits **description** — verifiable, true, and edge-free. In the middle sits **analysis** — a claim about *why* and *what it means*. At the top sits the **call** — what you would do and what would prove you wrong. Most notes never leave the bottom rung. The entire job of a market note is to climb.

## Foundations: what market commentary actually is

Before we can write a good note, we need to define the thing precisely, because "market commentary" gets used to cover everything from a Bloomberg headline to a hedge-fund letter, and the looseness is exactly where empty writing hides.

**Market commentary** is any written communication whose purpose is to convey a *view* on a market — where it is, where it's going, and what to do about it. The key word is *view*. A price feed is not commentary; it's data. A list of the day's headlines is not commentary; it's a newsfeed. Commentary is the layer on top that says: *here is what I make of all this*. The moment your writing stops conveying what you make of it and starts merely re-stating what happened, it has stopped being commentary and become a recap with a byline.

**A view** — the thing commentary is supposed to deliver — is a falsifiable claim about the market that you would act on. "Falsifiable" is the load-bearing word and it comes straight from the scientific method: a claim is falsifiable if there is some future observation that would prove it wrong. "The selloff is positioning-driven" is falsifiable — if credit blows out and breadth collapses, it was fundamental after all, and you were wrong. "Markets are uncertain" is *not* falsifiable, because no observation could ever contradict it; markets are always, in some sense, uncertain. A view you cannot be wrong about is not a view. It's a mood.

### The description → analysis → call ladder

Every sentence of market writing sits on one of three rungs, and learning to tell them apart is most of the craft.

**Description** is *what happened*. "The S&P fell 1.4%; the ten-year rose 8bp; the VIX went from 14 to 19." Description is verifiable — you can check it against the tape — and it is essential context. But it carries no edge, because anyone with a screen has the same facts. If your note is all description, you have written a transcript of the day, and the reader could have gotten it from the ticker.

**Analysis** is *why it happened and what it means*. "The move is concentrated in crowded momentum names while defensives held, which looks like de-grossing rather than a growth scare." Analysis is where claims live. It is not verifiable the way description is — reasonable people will disagree about *why* — and that disagreement is the point. Analysis is the first rung that carries information beyond the tape, because it is *your interpretation*, and interpretations can be right or wrong.

**The call** is *what you would do, and what would change your mind*. "I'd fade it, long S&P at 5,180 risking 1R, wrong if breadth keeps deteriorating into Friday." The call converts the analysis into an action and binds it to an exit. It is the top rung because it is the most accountable: it names a direction, a level, a size, and a kill-switch. A call you can't act on is just analysis with extra adjectives.

The discipline of good commentary is climbing this ladder *all the way up* — and the discipline of honest commentary is admitting which rung you actually reached. Sometimes the honest answer is "I have description and analysis but no call this week," and writing that down is itself a finding. We'll come back to it.

### Recap versus view, and why most commentary is empty

A **recap** stops at description. A **view** reaches the call. The difference is not length or vocabulary — plenty of recaps are 800 words of polished prose — it is whether the writing contains a claim that could be wrong.

Most commentary is empty for two structural reasons. The first is **hindsight narrative**: the writer sees the market moved, reaches for whatever headline is lying around, and asserts the move happened *because* of it. "Stocks fell on profit-taking." But "profit-taking" is not an observed cause; it is a phrase you attach to any decline you can't otherwise explain. The narrative is fitted to the move *after* the move, which is why it always sounds plausible and never predicts anything. The second reason is the **absence of a falsifiable claim**: the writer protects themselves by never saying anything that could be checked. "We remain cautious." Cautious about what, by when, and what would make you not-cautious? If those questions have no answer, the sentence is decorative.

### Why write a regular note at all

If empty commentary is so easy to produce, why write a note at all? Because the *discipline of writing it* is the point, independent of who reads it.

A regular note — daily or weekly — is a **forcing function**. It forces you to form a view on a schedule, whether or not you feel like one is obvious. The market does not wait for you to feel confident; the note makes you put a stake in the ground every day. Second, a *dated* note builds a **track record**. The single most valuable thing about writing down "I'd fade this, wrong below 5,120, by Friday" is that on Friday you can check whether you were right. Over a quarter, those dated calls become an honest scorecard of your judgment — something almost no trader actually keeps. Third, the note is a *thinking tool*: the act of compressing your read into sentences exposes exactly where the read is thin, the same way [collapsing everything into one sentence](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/from-data-to-a-read-collapsing-everything-into-one-sentence) does. If you can't write the "because" clause, you don't have a variant. If you can't write the "until" clause, you don't have an exit. The blank space on the page is a diagnostic.

There is a fourth, subtler payoff: a regular note **slows the reflex to react**. Without a note, every headline that crosses the tape is a fresh impulse — a reason to do something, right now. The trader who has already written down "my read is X, I'm wrong if Y, I act on the jobs report Friday" has a pre-committed frame to filter every intraday headline through: does this headline change X or trigger Y? If not, it's noise, and the note has already decided to ignore it. The note converts a stream of impulses into a small number of pre-defined decisions. That is the same mechanism behind a written routine — see [the daily and weekly process](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/the-daily-and-weekly-process-a-repeatable-reading-routine) — and it is most of why disciplined writers overtrade less than undisciplined ones who consume the same information.

Note also what the note is *not*. It is not a prediction in the fortune-teller sense — you are not claiming to know what happens. It is a *conditional*: given what I see, here is my best bet, sized to be survivable if I'm wrong, with an explicit exit. The distinction matters because it kills the false comfort of "I was right" and "I was wrong" as binary verdicts. A good call can lose money (the low-probability path showed up) and a bad call can make money (you got lucky on a coin-flip you shouldn't have taken). The note lets you grade the *decision*, not just the *outcome* — and over enough dated notes, the decisions are what compound.

## The three levels, in depth

Let us go rung by rung, because the most common failure in commentary is mistaking one level for the one above it.

### Level 1 — description: necessary, never sufficient

Description anchors the reader in reality. It tells them the facts you're reacting to so your claim has a referent. A note with no description floats — the reader doesn't know which selloff you mean. So you do need it. The rule is: **description earns its place only as the setup for a claim.** One or two crisp sentences of "here's what moved" is context; six paragraphs of "here's what moved" is a recap pretending to be a note.

The tell that you're stuck on rung one is that every sentence could be checked against the tape and none of them could be argued with. If a reader who disagrees with you has nothing to disagree *with*, you haven't said anything yet.

### Level 2 — analysis: the first rung that carries edge

Analysis answers *why* and *what it means*, and this is where the work is. The dangerous thing about analysis is that it is easy to *simulate*: you can write a sentence that has the grammatical shape of analysis ("the selloff reflects growing investor concern about the path of rates") while containing no actual claim. That sentence asserts that investors are concerned about rates — which is unfalsifiable, because some investors are always concerned about something. Real analysis names a *specific, checkable* interpretation: "the selloff is concentrated in momentum and absent in credit, which is the signature of de-grossing, not a macro repricing." Now there's something to be wrong about. If credit *had* moved, your interpretation would be in trouble — and that vulnerability is what makes it analysis.

The bridge from description to analysis is the question *"so what?"* You wrote "the VIX went from 14 to 19." So what? "So options are now pricing a daily move of about 1.2% versus 0.9% last week, and the bulk of that came in the front of the curve — the market is pricing event risk into this Thursday's CPI, not a regime change." That second sentence interprets the fact, ties it to a date, and makes a claim about *what kind* of fear it is. That's the climb from rung one to rung two.

There is a useful discipline for pressure-testing your own analysis before you publish it: ask whether the *opposite* reading is also defensible from the same facts. If "the move is de-grossing, not a growth scare" is your analysis, the opposite is "the move is the start of a growth scare that hasn't hit credit yet." If *both* readings fit the data equally well, you don't have analysis — you have a coin flip dressed in interpretation, and your honest note should say the data is ambiguous. But if your reading is genuinely better supported — credit's calm, breadth held, the move is concentrated in exactly the crowded names that de-gross first — then you can name *why* yours wins and the opposite loses, and that "why" is the content of the analysis. The discipline is not to suppress the counter-reading; it's to confront it on the page and explain what tips the balance. A note that never mentions the strongest opposing interpretation is usually one that couldn't survive it.

The other thing that separates real analysis from its grammatical imitation is **specificity about magnitude**. "Rates are concerning" is not analysis; "the two-year is up 3bp, which is far too little to justify a 3.3% repricing of long-duration tech, so the move isn't really about rates" is. The number does the work: it lets the reader check whether your interpretation is *proportionate* to the move you're explaining. Vague analysis hides behind directional language precisely because directional language can never be shown to be disproportionate. The moment you attach a magnitude, you've made a claim that the size of the cause matches the size of the effect — and that claim can be wrong, which is exactly what makes it analysis.

### Level 3 — the call: what you'd do and what changes it

The call is where most writers flinch, because it is where you become accountable. A complete call has four parts:

1. **A direction or position.** Long, short, flat, long-this-versus-short-that.
2. **A level or entry.** "Around 5,180," not "at current levels."
3. **A size or risk.** "Risking 1R," "a 2% position," "half my usual." This is what connects the note to [the bet-sizing bridge](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/from-conviction-to-size-the-bet-sizing-bridge): a call without a size is an opinion, not a position.
4. **An invalidation.** The observable, ideally dated, trigger that proves you wrong and gets you out. This is the part that separates a call from a hope, and it deserves its own treatment — see [defining invalidation upfront](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/what-would-change-my-mind-defining-invalidation-upfront).

Notice that the call is the *most* falsifiable rung. Description is true by construction; analysis can be argued; but the call can be *scored* — on Friday, you either faded the selloff and it worked or it didn't, and the invalidation either triggered or it didn't. That scoreability is precisely why it's worth writing, and precisely why it's the rung writers avoid.

A subtle point about the call: it does not have to be a *trade you are personally putting on*. The call can be a hypothetical — "if I were running this book, I'd be a buyer here" — and it is still fully accountable, because the hypothetical is dated and scoreable just like a real position. This matters for analysts who write commentary but don't trade the book themselves; the discipline of the falsifiable call applies to them identically. What is *not* allowed is the escape hatch of "I'm constructive but it depends on the data," which sounds like a call and is actually a refusal to make one. "Constructive" with no level, no size, and no invalidation is rung-two analysis wearing rung-three clothing. The test is the same as always: a week later, could a reader score it without asking you what you meant? If the answer is no, you stopped at analysis and dressed it up.

One more thing the call needs that writers routinely omit: a **conviction or size that varies**. If every call you write is "I'd put on a normal-sized position," you're not actually conveying conviction — you're conveying a habit. The whole point of stating a size is that it scales with how strong the variant is, which is the bridge from a view to a position covered in [from conviction to size](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/from-conviction-to-size-the-bet-sizing-bridge). A note that says "this is my highest-conviction idea of the quarter, double size" carries information that a note saying "I like it here" does not. Let the size do some of the talking.

The before-and-after above shows the same trading day written two ways. On the left, the empty recap: volatility, profit-taking, no claim, no level, reads the same whatever happens next. On the right, the note with a view: a positioning claim, a fade at a level, a breadth-based invalidation, an implied trade, and a sentence you can score by Friday. #### Worked example: an empty recap rewritten into a note with a real claim

Start with the empty version a junior analyst actually wrote: *"Equities declined today amid risk-off sentiment. Tech led the move lower on rate concerns. We remain neutral and await further clarity."* Let's rebuild it into something with a dollar stake behind it.

First, **description** (one sentence, earning its place): "The Nasdaq fell 2.1% versus the S&P's 1.4%, with the entire gap in mega-cap tech; semis (SOX) were down 3.3%." Now there's a specific fact to react to.

Second, **analysis** (the "so what"): "But two-year yields rose only 3bp — not enough to justify a 3.3% repricing of long-duration tech on rates alone. The move tracks a single broker's options expiry and the unwind of a popular call-overwrite trade. This reads as a mechanical de-risking in the most-crowded names, not a fundamental rate scare."

Third, **the call with a dollar stake**: "I'm fading the semis underperformance. I'll buy \$50,000 of SOX exposure here, with a stop if SOX makes a new closing low below today's, which would cost me about \$2,500 (5% of the position). If the de-grossing thesis is right and semis snap back to even half the gap versus the S&P by Friday, that's roughly a 4% move, or \$2,000 — call it a 0.8R-to-1R structure, which I'd only take because the catalyst (expiry) is dated and passes tomorrow."

The rewrite is the same number of words as the empty version, but now there is a \$50,000 position, a \$2,500 defined risk, a dated catalyst, and an invalidation. A reader can disagree ("I think it *is* rates") and a scorekeeper can check it on Friday. That is the entire difference between a recap and a note: the recap risked nothing and said nothing; the note staked \$2,500 on a claim. The intuition: **a note becomes worth reading the moment it would cost the writer money to be wrong.**

## Killing the hindsight-narrative trap

The single biggest reason market commentary is worthless is the **hindsight narrative**: the reflex to explain a move that already happened by attaching a cause to it after the fact. "Stocks fell *because* of profit-taking." "Bonds rallied *because* of safe-haven demand." "The dollar weakened *because* of dovish Fed expectations." These sentences feel like analysis. They are not. They are stories fitted to outcomes.

![Flow from a market move through borrowed headlines to a post-hoc story splitting into a trap and a disciplined fix](/imgs/blogs/writing-market-commentary-that-actually-says-something-4.png)

The diagram above traces the trap. The move comes *first*. Then the writer looks around for an available headline — there are always several, pointing in opposite directions — and picks whichever one fits the direction the market happened to go. That choice gets written up as "X fell *because* Y," with a confident tone that masks the fact that no observation could refute it. The fix, on the green branch, is to invert the order: name the *tell you checked first*, before the move resolved, and state what would prove your read wrong.

Here is the mechanical reason hindsight narrative is empty. On any given day, the financial press publishes dozens of headlines, several of which point in opposite directions: "inflation concerns linger" sits next to "growth optimism builds." When the market goes down, the writer reaches for the bearish headline and writes "stocks fell on inflation concerns." When it goes *up* the next day, the same writer reaches for the bullish headline and writes "stocks rose on growth optimism." Neither sentence predicted anything; both were *selected after the outcome was known* from a menu of always-available stories. A "because" you only choose after seeing the move is not a cause — it's a caption.

The test for whether you're writing hindsight narrative is brutal and simple: **could you have written this sentence, with the same conviction, before the move happened?** If on Monday morning you would have said "I expect stocks to fall today on inflation concerns" — and put a trade on it — then writing "stocks fell on inflation concerns" on Monday afternoon is honest analysis confirmed. But if you only reached for "inflation concerns" *after* watching stocks fall, you are narrating, not analyzing. The cure is to do your interpreting *forward*: state what you think is driving the tape and what observation would confirm or refute it, *before* you know how the day ends. This is the same forward discipline that [the reaction function](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-reaction-function-why-the-same-number-moves-differently) demands — you decide in advance how a given print should move the market, so you can tell a confirmation from a coincidence.

#### Worked example: the hindsight-narrative trap shown with a concrete day

Take a real-feeling day. CPI prints at 8:30 a.m. slightly *below* expectations (a soft number, normally bullish for stocks). Stocks open up 0.8%. By 2 p.m. they're down 1.1%. By the close they finish down 0.4%. Three different directional swings in one session. Now watch how the hindsight narrative writes itself:

- The 8:30 pop: "Stocks rallied on the soft CPI print, easing rate fears." (Plausible.)
- The midday fade: "Stocks reversed as investors faded the knee-jerk rally and refocused on growth concerns." (Also plausible — and it directly *contradicts* the first story, since "easing rate fears" should not cause a selloff.)
- The close: "Stocks ended modestly lower in choppy, indecisive trade." (The catch-all.)

Each sentence is internally coherent and each was selected *after* the relevant move. Together they explain everything and predict nothing. A reader who acted on the 8:30 narrative ("rate fears easing, buy") got run over by 2 p.m. The honest version, written *forward* at 8:31 a.m., is: "CPI came in soft, which historically lifts stocks, but positioning into this print was already very long and the soft surprise was small (2bp on core). My read is the bullish reaction is already mostly priced — I would NOT chase the 0.8% pop, and I'd be a buyer only on a flush back toward yesterday's close. I'm wrong on the 'priced-in' call if we hold the gains into the afternoon, which would say real money is still adding."

Put a dollar frame on it. The chaser who bought \$20,000 of index exposure at the 8:30 high and stopped out at the 2 p.m. low ate roughly the full 1.9% intraday swing — about a \$380 loss — on a story that was a caption, not a forecast. The forward writer, who declined to chase and only scaled in near the prior close, sat out the round trip entirely; if they bought \$20,000 at the afternoon low and held to the close, they were up about 0.7% on the bounce, roughly \$140. Same day, same data — but one wrote a narrative and lost \$380, the other wrote a falsifiable forward read and made \$140. The intuition: **a "because" you can only write after the move is worth nothing; a "what's priced and what would change it" you write before the move is worth money.**

## Writing a falsifiable weekly note

The daily note keeps you honest in real time, but the **weekly note** is where the real thinking happens, because it has room for a full thesis. A good weekly note is not a longer recap — it is a structured argument that ends in a dated, scoreable call. The structure matters more than the prose, so here it is as a fixed set of fields.

![Six-field weekly note grid with read debate priced variant call and invalidation](/imgs/blogs/writing-market-commentary-that-actually-says-something-3.png)

The grid above is the skeleton of a useful note. **The read** is one sentence compressing your net view. **The key debate** is the single question the week turns on. **What is priced** is the market's current belief — the number you have to beat. **Your variant** is where and why you differ. **The call** is the trade it implies, with a size. **The invalidation** is the dated, observable trigger that says you were wrong. Every field is a separate test, and a missing field is a hole in the thinking, not just a hole in the document.

The reason this structure works is that it *separates the rungs of the ladder* and forces you to fill each one. You cannot accidentally write six paragraphs of description, because there's no field for it beyond "the read." You cannot hide from the call, because there's a field for it. And — critically — you cannot skip the invalidation, because the empty field stares back at you.

The most important field, and the one beginners skip, is **what is priced**. A view only has edge if it differs from what the market already believes; otherwise you're just agreeing with the price and calling it analysis. This is the central question of the whole craft — see [what's priced in](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/whats-priced-in-the-question-behind-every-trade) for how to read it — and your note has to state it explicitly. "The market is pricing two cuts by year-end" is the benchmark; "I think they cut three times" is your variant; the *gap* between them is your edge. If your view and the priced view are the same, you have nothing to write.

The **key debate** field is the one that gives the note a spine, and it's worth dwelling on because writers skip it and then wonder why their note rambles. Every week, there is one question the market is actually arguing about — is the selloff fundamental or technical, is the Fed done or pausing, is the earnings miss company-specific or a sector tell. Naming that question explicitly does two things. It *focuses* the note: every other field is now in service of taking a side on that one debate, rather than commenting on everything at once. And it *reveals the trade structure*: the way to express a view is usually to bet on your answer to the key debate, which is why the call field flows naturally from it. A note with no named debate tends to sprawl across five unrelated observations, none of which adds up to a position — because there was no single question organizing them.

The field *order* is deliberate, and it enforces the ladder. You write **what's priced before your variant** so that you're forced to establish the benchmark before claiming to beat it — this is the discipline that stops you from mistaking "I agree with the consensus" for "I have a view." You write **the variant before the call** so the trade is *derived* from a disagreement rather than reverse-engineered to justify a position you already wanted. And you write **the invalidation last, but before you trade**, so that the exit is defined while you're still being honest, not improvised later when you're losing money and motivated to move the goalposts. Filling the fields out of order — call first, justification after — is how analysts produce notes that are really just rationalizations of a trade they'd already decided to put on.

#### Worked example: a weekly note with a falsifiable, dated call and the dollar trade it implies

Here is a complete weekly note filled out against the structure, with a position behind it. The week is a Fed week.

> **The read:** Risk is fragile but not broken; the bigger risk this week is a hawkish surprise from the Fed, which is under-priced.
>
> **The key debate:** Does the Fed signal it's done cutting (hawkish), or leave the door open (dovish)? The whole week turns on the dot plot and the press conference tone.
>
> **What's priced:** Fed funds futures price ~85% odds of a hold this meeting and ~1.7 cuts over the next four meetings. The market is positioned for "patient and dovish" — equity vol is low (VIX 13) and rate vol (MOVE) is near the year's lows. Translation: nobody is hedged for hawkish.
>
> **My variant:** I think the dots come down by less than the market expects and the Chair pushes back on the easing priced for next year. The disinflation data has stalled for two prints and the labor market is firmer than the dovish narrative assumes. My probability of a hawkish-relative-to-priced outcome is ~45%, versus what I read as ~25% priced.
>
> **The call:** Buy 2-week S&P puts (or short a small amount of index) as a hedge into the meeting. Concretely: I'll spend \$3,000 on puts on a \$100,000 book — a 3% premium budget — positioned to pay roughly \$12,000 (4-to-1) if the S&P drops 3% on a hawkish surprise. This is a *positive-expected-value hedge given my variant*: at my 45% odds of the hawkish path paying ~4x and the 55% path losing the premium, EV ≈ 0.45 × \$12,000 − 0.55 × \$3,000 ≈ +\$3,750 on the \$3,000 stake.
>
> **The invalidation:** I'm wrong if the dots come down two or more cuts for next year AND the Chair declines to push back on market pricing — in which case the dovish path is confirmed, my puts expire worthless, and I've spent \$3,000 to learn the market read it better than I did. I let the puts go; I do not double down. Decision point: Wednesday 2:30 p.m., at the press conference.

Notice every property of a real note. It states what's priced (85% hold, 1.7 cuts, low vol). It states a variant with a probability gap (45% vs 25%). It implies a specific \$3,000 trade with a defined payoff. It has a dated invalidation (Wednesday 2:30, dots + tone). And on Thursday morning, you can *score it*: either the Fed surprised hawkish and the hedge paid, or it didn't and you lost the premium you'd budgeted. The intuition: **a weekly note is falsifiable when, a week later, an honest reader can tell whether you were right without asking you what you "really meant."**

## Building a track record from dated notes

The reason to stamp every note with a date and a falsifiable call is that, over time, those notes become a **track record** — an honest, externally-checkable history of your judgment. This is the single most underrated payoff of the discipline. Almost no trader keeps one, which is why almost no trader actually knows how good their reads are; they remember the wins vividly and the losses vaguely, and conclude they're better than they are.

![Cumulative track record built one dated note at a time over twelve weeks](/imgs/blogs/writing-market-commentary-that-actually-says-something-6.png)

The chart above shows what a track record looks like when you build it one dated note at a time. Each week is a note; each note that made a call is scored in **R** (the risk-multiple framework from [expected value](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/expected-value-the-only-math-a-view-really-needs)): a winning call books +R, a losing call books −1R, and a week you honestly said "no edge, staying flat" books 0R — a flat segment on the curve. Over twelve weeks the record climbs to +10R, but look at *where* the value comes from: not from never being wrong (there are three red losing calls), but from making bets only when there was a real variant and booking zero on the weeks there wasn't.

A track record built this way does three things no after-the-fact memory can. First, it **calibrates** you — over enough notes, you learn whether your "70% confident" calls actually come in around 70% of the time, which is the entire subject of the forward-linked [keeping score on your own forecasts](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/calibration-keeping-score-on-your-own-forecasts). Second, it **separates skill from luck** — a single good call could be noise, but a curve that grinds up over forty dated notes is signal. Third, it **disciplines you in real time**: knowing that today's note goes into the permanent record makes you less willing to dash off an empty recap, because the empty recap is itself a data point that says "I had no view this week and pretended otherwise."

The track record can be **public or private**, and the choice has consequences. A *public* record — a substack, a research note distributed to clients, posts with timestamps — is maximally honest because you cannot quietly edit the losers; the cost is that it disciplines what you're willing to commit to, sometimes toward blandness. A *private* record — a dated journal only you see — lets you be braver and more specific, but only works if you are ruthlessly honest about scoring it. The worst of both worlds, and the most common, is the *implicit* record: no dates, no scores, just a general sense that you're "usually right." That sense is almost always inflated. The fix is the same either way: date everything, write a falsifiable call, and score it on the date you said.

#### Worked example: the compounding value of a dated track record on a real account

Suppose you run a \$100,000 book and you write one weekly note with a call, sized to risk 1R = \$2,000 (2% of the book) per call. Over a quarter (about 12 weeks), the track record on the chart above plays out: 9 weeks you made a call, 3 weeks you wrote "no edge, staying flat."

Of the 9 calls, say 5 won an average of +2R (+\$4,000 each = +\$20,000), and 4 lost −1R (−\$2,000 each = −\$8,000). Net from the calls: +\$12,000. The 3 no-edge weeks: \$0. Quarter total: **+\$12,000 on the \$100,000 book, or +12%**, with a worst single loss of \$2,000 and zero loss on a third of the weeks.

Now run the *counterfactual* of a writer with no track-record discipline — someone who feels they should "always have a view" and forces a call every single week. The 9 genuine calls play out the same (+\$12,000). But on the 3 no-edge weeks, instead of staying flat, they manufacture a low-conviction trade. Low-conviction trades are, by definition, near coin-flips with costs — say they average −0.4R each after fees and slippage (−\$800 × 3 = −\$2,400). Quarter total: \$12,000 − \$2,400 = **+\$9,600**. The forced-view writer gave up **\$2,400** — a fifth of the quarter's gains — purely to avoid writing "no edge" three times. The intuition: **the discipline of a dated track record doesn't just measure your edge; the act of keeping it honestly, including the zeros, protects the dollars your edge earns.**

## The discipline of writing "no edge this week"

The hardest sentence to write in market commentary is *"I have no edge this week; I'm staying flat."* It feels like a failure — like you didn't do the job. It is the opposite. It is the most professional sentence in the genre.

Here is why. Edge is not continuous. The market does not hand you a high-conviction variant every week; most weeks the price is roughly fair, the consensus is roughly right, and you have no genuine disagreement that the data supports. On those weeks, the honest read *is* "no edge." A writer who manufactures a view anyway — because the note has to say *something* — is doing the worst thing a commentator can do: converting "I don't know" into a confident-sounding call, then trading on it. That is how low-conviction trades, the ones with no real variant behind them, leak money week after week.

Writing "no edge" does three things. It **protects capital**, as the worked example just showed — the no-trade is often the highest-EV decision available, because the alternative is a near-coin-flip minus costs. It **protects credibility** — a record that includes honest passes is far more believable than one that claims a strong view every single week, because nobody is right that often and readers know it. And it **protects calibration** — if you only ever publish strong views, you can never learn that your strong views are over-confident, because you've censored all the weeks you should have stood aside. The note that says "no edge" is not an absence of analysis; it is the *conclusion* of analysis that honestly found no variant.

The bar for "no edge" should be specific, not lazy. "No edge" does not mean "I didn't do the work." It means: *I did the work, I mapped what's priced, I looked for a variant, and I don't have one this week that the data supports.* The note should still show the work — "here's what's priced, here's the debate, and here's why I don't have a differentiated view on it" — and then conclude flat. That is a complete, falsifiable note: it's falsifiable in the sense that if a clear catalyst emerges mid-week, you'll have said in advance you were waiting for exactly that.

#### Worked example: a "no edge" note that protects the dollars

It's a quiet August week. No Fed meeting, no CPI, earnings season mostly over, the S&P drifting in a 1% range for ten days. You sit down to write the weekly note and you're tempted, as always, to find a trade. Here's the honest version:

> **The read:** Range-bound and low-information; no catalyst until the jobs report a week out.
>
> **What's priced:** Vol is at the year's lows (VIX 12.5), which is *correct* given the empty calendar — there's nothing to move it. Rate expectations are stable. Nothing looks mispriced to me.
>
> **My variant:** None. I looked at positioning (neutral), seasonality (mixed), and the technicals (mid-range, no setup). I do not have a differentiated view this week.
>
> **The call:** No new risk. I hold existing positions and stay flat on new ideas. I'll re-engage on the jobs report next Friday, where I *do* expect a setup.
>
> **The invalidation of the flat stance:** If realized vol picks up or a real catalyst emerges before Friday — a surprise data point, a geopolitical headline that actually moves credit — I revisit. Absent that, flat is the position.

Now the dollars. Suppose your typical forced "something" trade in a dead week is a small mean-reversion play, \$30,000 of exposure betting the range holds. In a *truly* dead week these often work (the range does hold) — but the edge is tiny and the tail is ugly: if a surprise headline breaks the range, you can lose 3–4% in a day. Over many such weeks, the expected value of forcing the trade is roughly: 80% chance of a small +0.5% win (+\$150) and 20% chance of a −3% loss (−\$900), for an EV of 0.8 × \$150 − 0.2 × \$900 = \$120 − \$180 = **−\$60 per dead week**, before costs. Writing "no edge, staying flat" instead books \$0 — which is, in expectation, **\$60 richer per dead week**, plus you avoided the fat-tail risk entirely (the [asymmetry of losses](/blog/trading/risk-management/the-asymmetry-of-losses-why-a-50-percent-loss-needs-a-100-percent-gain) means that 3% loss costs more to recover than the string of small wins gained). Over a dozen dead weeks a year, the honest "no edge" note is worth several hundred dollars and one avoided blow-up. The intuition: **on a week with no variant, the highest-value thing you can write is the truth, because forcing a view turns a flat zero into a negative-EV coin flip.**

## Common misconceptions

A few beliefs about commentary are so common, and so wrong, that they deserve to be corrected one at a time.

**"Commentary means summarizing the news."** No — that's a newsfeed, and the reader already has one. Summarizing the news is rung-one description with the serial numbers filed off. The job of commentary is to add the layer the news *doesn't* have: what you make of it, what you'd do, and what would change your mind. If your note could be replaced by a list of the day's headlines, you wrote a summary, not commentary. The value you add is precisely the part that *isn't* in the news.

**"A confident tone equals insight."** This is the most dangerous myth, because it rewards exactly the wrong thing. Confidence is a *style*; insight is a *claim that could be wrong*. "Markets will remain volatile as uncertainty persists" is delivered with total confidence and contains zero insight, because nothing could refute it. Meanwhile "I'd fade this selloff and I'm wrong below 5,120" is delivered with explicit vulnerability — it names its own failure condition — and contains all the insight. Readers, and especially writers, confuse the felt-confidence of a sentence with its information content. The discipline is to notice that the *most* falsifiable sentence — the one most willing to be wrong — usually carries the most edge. This is the same trap that [trading psychology](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/trading-psychology-and-the-execution-gap) warns about: conviction is a feeling, not evidence.

**"Explaining why the market moved is analysis."** Usually it's hindsight narrative, as we spent a whole section establishing. The test is whether you could have written the explanation, with the same conviction, *before* the move. If the "because" was selected from a menu of always-available headlines after you saw the direction, it's a caption, not analysis. Real analysis is *forward*: it names the driver and the confirming observation before the outcome is known.

**"I should always have a strong view."** No — you should always have an *honest* view, which is sometimes "no edge." The pressure to always sound decisive is what produces the worst commentary: forced calls on weeks with no variant, manufactured confidence to fill the space. The strongest commentators are *more* willing to say "I don't have a differentiated view here" than weak ones, not less, because they're protecting the credibility and the capital that their occasional high-conviction calls depend on. A note that's flat 30% of the time and sharp the other 70% beats a note that's confident 100% of the time and right 52%.

**"More detail makes a better note."** Detail is not the same as edge. A note can be five paragraphs of meticulous description and still say nothing, while a three-sentence note with a falsifiable call says everything. The reader does not need every data point you looked at; they need your *net* of them — the read, the variant, the call. Bury the call under detail and you've written a research dump, not a view. This is the [indicator trap](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/the-indicator-trap) in prose form: more inputs displayed is not more signal delivered.

## How it plays out in real markets

The patterns above are not theory; they show up on identifiable days, and the difference between empty and useful commentary on those days was worth real money to the people reading it.

**The 2018 "Volmageddon" — February 5, 2018.** The VIX more than doubled in a day and a handful of short-volatility ETPs were destroyed. The empty commentary that evening: "Markets plunged on volatility concerns; investors fled to safety." That sentence is circular — *of course* a vol spike involves "volatility concerns." The useful forward read, written by people who'd been watching the short-vol crowding for weeks, was structurally different: "The move is mechanical, not fundamental — the short-vol ETPs have to buy VIX futures into the close to rebalance, and the position is enormous and crowded; this is a forced-buyer feedback loop, and it will overshoot and then mean-revert once the rebalance is done." That note made a falsifiable claim (mechanical, not macro), implied a trade (fade the vol spike *after* the rebalance, not during), and had an invalidation (if credit and breadth deteriorate fundamentally, it's not just a vol-product unwind). Within days the S&P recovered most of the drop — the mechanical read was right, and it was right *because* it was forward and falsifiable, not a "because" attached after the fact.

**The October 2022 CPI print.** Headline CPI came in *hotter* than expected — a number that "should" have sent stocks down. The S&P opened down about 2%, then reversed and closed up over 2.5%, one of the largest intraday reversals on record. The hindsight narratives were a disaster: the morning note said "stocks fell on hot inflation," the afternoon note said "stocks rallied on... peak inflation hopes?" — directly contradicting itself within hours. The useful note, written *forward* at 8:31 a.m., would have said: "Positioning into this print was extremely short and hedged; a hot number is the *expected* outcome the market is leaning toward, so the surprise that moves us is whether the reaction matches the setup. If we open down 2% and *don't* keep falling, the short base will have to cover and we squeeze." That's a falsifiable, forward read of [the reaction function](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-reaction-function-why-the-same-number-moves-differently) — it said *in advance* what observation (failure to keep falling) would flip the call from short to squeeze. The writer who had that framing made money on the reversal; the writer narrating headlines lost on both sides of it.

**The 2020 COVID crash and the March 23 bottom.** Through late February and March 2020, empty commentary recapped the crash daily ("stocks fell again on virus fears") — true, useless, and identical every day. The useful weekly note in late March made a dated, falsifiable call: "The selling is now indiscriminate and forced — even Treasuries and gold are being sold for cash, which is the signature of a liquidation, not a fundamental repricing. The Fed has just announced unlimited QE and credit facilities. My read: the liquidation low is in or near; I'm a buyer of risk here, scaling in. I'm wrong if credit spreads make new wides *after* the Fed backstop — that would say the backstop failed." The S&P bottomed on March 23, the day after the Fed's announcement. The point is not that the writer nailed the exact low — it's that the note was *falsifiable and forward*: it named the tell (forced liquidation, everything sold for cash), the catalyst (Fed backstop), and the invalidation (credit making new wides post-backstop). A recap could never have produced that call, because a recap only ever describes the day that already happened.

The thread through all three: on the days that mattered most, the empty commentary contradicted itself within hours or read identically for weeks, while the useful commentary made a forward, falsifiable claim with a named invalidation — and that's exactly what let the reader act, and what let everyone score whether the writer was right.

## The playbook

Here is the repeatable process. Run it on every note before you publish — daily or weekly — and your commentary will say something.

**The weekly-note template.** Fill all six fields. A blank field is a hole in the thinking, not a formatting choice.

1. **The read** — one sentence compressing your net view. If you can't say it in one sentence, you haven't finished thinking; you've finished collecting.
2. **The key debate** — the single question the week or the move turns on. Name it explicitly so the note has a spine.
3. **What's priced** — what the market already believes, stated as a number or a benchmark you have to beat. If you skip this, you can't know whether you have a variant.
4. **Your variant** — where and why you differ from what's priced, with a probability or magnitude. No variant, no edge — write "no edge" and stop here.
5. **The call** — the trade it implies: direction, level, size (in R or dollars). A call without a size is an opinion.
6. **The invalidation** — the dated, observable trigger that proves you wrong and gets you out. Write it before you put the trade on, never after.

**The falsifiable-claim test.** Before you publish any sentence that's meant to be analysis, run it through four questions. If it fails any one, it's still a recap.

![Four-row test card pairing failing claims with passing falsifiable claims](/imgs/blogs/writing-market-commentary-that-actually-says-something-5.png)

The card above is the test in one picture: a real claim names an asset and direction, states a variant that differs from consensus, names what would change it, and carries a date or a level so it can be scored. A claim that fails any column — "markets were volatile," "I think it goes up," "until I change my mind," "eventually" — is a horoscope, not a view.

1. **Asset and direction?** Does the sentence name a specific market and which way? ("Long S&P," not "markets are uncertain.")
2. **A variant?** Does it differ from what's priced, with a reason? ("Sellers are forced, not fundamental," not "I think it goes up.")
3. **What changes it?** Could a future observation refute it? ("Wrong if breadth keeps falling," not "until I change my mind.")
4. **A date or level?** Can it be scored, and by when? ("Below 5,120 by Friday," not "eventually.")

**The hindsight-narrative check.** For every "because" you're tempted to write, ask: *could I have written this, with this conviction, before the move?* If the "because" was chosen from a menu of headlines after you saw the direction, delete it and write the forward version — what you think is driving it and what would confirm or refute that read.

**The full commentary checklist.** Before you hit send, the note clears six gates or it stays a recap.

![Six-gate commentary checklist card](/imgs/blogs/writing-market-commentary-that-actually-says-something-7.png)

1. **A claim, not a recap** — does one sentence say what you *believe*, not just what *happened*?
2. **Priced vs. my view** — have you stated what the market believes AND where you differ?
3. **Falsifiable** — could a future observation prove you wrong? If not, it's a horoscope.
4. **Dated and leveled** — is there a horizon and a price or trigger, so it can be scored?
5. **No hindsight "because"** — did you check the tell *first*, not fit a story to the move after?
6. **Honest "no edge"** — if you have no variant, did you say so and stay flat?

Run that checklist on the empty note we started with — "markets were volatile amid uncertainty" — and it fails every gate: no claim, no priced-vs-view, not falsifiable, no date, pure hindsight, dishonest about the absence of edge. Run it on the second note — "the selloff is positioning-driven; I'd fade it, wrong if breadth keeps deteriorating" — and it passes every one. That is the entire difference, and it is learnable. Write the note that would cost you money to be wrong, date it, score it, and over a quarter you'll have something almost no commentator has: a record that proves your reads say something.
