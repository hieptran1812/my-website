> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A tradeable thesis has three load-bearing parts: a **claim** (specific, with direction + magnitude + horizon, and falsifiable), the **evidence** behind it (weighted by quality, including the facts that argue against you), and a **catalyst** (a dated, observable event with a mechanism that closes the gap). Miss any one and you have a hope, not a trade.
>
> - "Undervalued" is not a thesis — it is a claim with no catalyst, and cheap can stay cheap for years while you bleed carry.
> - Evidence is ranked on a ladder: primary data outweighs direct inference, which outweighs analogy, which outweighs narrative. Quality and independence beat quantity — ten correlated narrative points are worth less than one primary data point.
> - The catalyst is the "why now": the dated, observable mechanism that forces the market to re-price. Without it, you are right but uncompensated.
> - The one rule to remember: **write the claim, ladder the evidence, name the catalyst with a date — then attack the weakest link before the market does.**

## Two theses on the same stock

It is a research morning and two analysts have written up the same mid-cap industrial, NorthStar, currently trading around \$50. Both are bullish. Both will pitch it at the same meeting. And only one of them owns a thesis.

The first note reads: *"NorthStar is a great company trading below intrinsic value. Strong management, durable franchise, clean balance sheet. The market is mispricing the quality of this business. We see meaningful upside as the value is recognized over time. Buy."* It is the kind of note that sounds like research and survives every meeting, because there is nothing in it that can ever be wrong. "Great company" — by what measure, against which competitors? "Below intrinsic value" — whose estimate of intrinsic value, and by how much? "Recognized over time" — which time, and recognized *because* of what? You cannot size this note. You cannot say when you would admit it failed. If NorthStar drifts sideways for two years, the author will tell you the thesis is "still intact, just early," and they will be technically correct, because the note carefully committed to nothing that could be checked.

The second note reads: *"NorthStar free cash flow per share inflects roughly +40%, from about \$3.00 to about \$4.20, by the Q3 print in late October, as the new Monterrey plant ramps to full utilization. The Street is mispricing this because consensus models still carry the old 18% segment margin; the new line runs structurally higher. The catalyst is the Q3 earnings report, dated, where the new plant margin first hits the income statement and forces analysts to re-model. I am wrong if Q3 segment margin prints flat — that says the ramp slipped or the margin thesis is broken. I am putting \$20,000 of risk behind it via the stock, sized so the invalidation costs me about that."*

Read both again. The first note feels safe. The second note feels exposed — it names a number (\$4.20), a date (late October), a mechanism (the plant ramp hitting reported margin), a reason it is mispriced (the Street modeling the old margin), and a kill switch (flat Q3 margin). Every clause maps to a decision a risk manager could act on this morning. That is the whole difference. The first note is a hope wearing the costume of analysis. The second is a thesis — a *structured, falsifiable, sizeable object* — and this post is the manual for building one.

## Foundations: the three load-bearing components

A thesis is not a paragraph of nice sentences. It is a small machine with three parts that have to fit together, and the job of this section is to define each part precisely before we build any of them. A process term defined sloppily produces a thesis built sloppily, so we go from zero.

### The claim, the evidence, the catalyst — and how they chain

The **claim** is what you believe will happen, stated precisely enough to be sized and graded. A good claim is not "NorthStar goes up." It is "NorthStar free cash flow per share goes from \$3.00 to \$4.20 by the Q3 print in late October." It carries a *direction* (up), a *magnitude* (+40%, to \$4.20), and a *horizon* (by late October). And it is *falsifiable*: there is a specific observable — the Q3 free-cash-flow number — that could prove it wrong. The claim is the thing you would be graded on if someone audited you in November.

The **evidence** is the set of facts that justify the claim — but not all facts are equal, which is the whole subtlety. Evidence is *weighted by quality*: a number in an SEC filing is worth more than a sell-side analyst's inference, which is worth more than an analogy to a prior cycle, which is worth more than a narrative. And good evidence-gathering deliberately includes *disconfirming* evidence: the facts that argue against your claim, sought out on purpose, because the facts you ignore are the ones that take your money.

The **catalyst** is the dated, observable mechanism that *closes the gap* between the current price and where your claim says it should be. It answers the question "why now?" Without a catalyst, you can be completely right about value and still make no money, because nothing forces the market to agree with you on any particular date. The catalyst for the NorthStar thesis is the Q3 print: a specific, scheduled event where the new margin first appears in reported numbers and forces analysts to update their models.

These three chain. The claim *names a gap* between your view and what is priced. The evidence *supports the existence of that gap* — it is the reason you think you are right and the market is wrong. And the catalyst is the *event that closes the gap into profit* on a known clock. A thesis missing the claim is just a feeling. Missing the evidence, it is a guess. Missing the catalyst, it is a value trap. All three, chained, is a trade.

It is worth being explicit about the *order* in which these components do their work, because the order is not arbitrary. The claim comes first because it defines what you are even talking about — without a precise claim, there is nothing for the evidence to support or the catalyst to resolve. The evidence comes second because it is the answer to "why do you believe the claim?" — it is the work behind the assertion. The catalyst comes last in construction but is often the *first* thing a good analyst sanity-checks, because if there is no plausible dated mechanism to close the gap, there is no point gathering evidence for a claim that will never pay. Many experienced analysts run the chain in two passes: forward to build it (claim, then evidence, then catalyst), and backward to test it (does the catalyst actually resolve the gap the claim names, and does the evidence actually support *that* claim and not a vaguely related one?).

There is a fourth element that is not a component of the thesis but its *output*: the **expression** — the specific instrument and size you use to put the view on. The chain produces it: the claim's magnitude and horizon, combined with the evidence quality, set the size; the invalidation sets the stop; the catalyst's date sets when you grade. We treat expression as the terminal node of the pipeline rather than a fourth component, because a thesis is complete the moment you can express it — and a thesis you cannot express is one whose claim, evidence, or catalyst is still too vague to act on.

### Claim versus hope

The line between a claim and a hope is falsifiability, and it is worth being brutal about. A **claim** is a statement that some observable fact could prove wrong. A **hope** is a statement arranged — usually unconsciously — so that no observation could ever refute it. "NorthStar is undervalued" is a hope: there is no price, on any date, that the author would accept as proof they were wrong, because "undervalued" is not pinned to a number or a deadline. "NorthStar trades to \$70 by year-end, and I'm wrong below \$45" is a claim: two specific, observable events would settle it.

Every word that makes a claim more falsifiable also makes it more *uncomfortable*, which is exactly why hopes are so common. To turn the hope into a claim you have to name a magnitude you might miss, a date by which you'll be measured, and a level at which you'll admit defeat. Each of those is a way to be specifically, publicly wrong. The discipline of writing a real claim is the discipline of accepting accountability you could have dodged. (This series leans on this idea repeatedly; the upfront-invalidation version of it gets its own treatment in [what would change my mind: defining invalidation upfront](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/what-would-change-my-mind-defining-invalidation-upfront).)

A useful test for whether you are holding a claim or a hope: ask yourself, *"If a colleague disagreed, what single fact would settle it between us?"* For a claim, the answer is immediate — "the Q3 free-cash-flow number." For a hope, there is no such fact, because "undervalued" and "fairly valued" are not settled by any observation; they are settled by argument, and argument never settles. The presence of a fact that could end the disagreement is the operational signature of a claim. If no such fact exists, you and your colleague can debate the name forever and never be proven right or wrong — which is comfortable, and worthless.

### The evidence ladder

Not all evidence is equal, and the single biggest analytical error is treating it as if it were — stacking up ten weak points and feeling ten times as confident. Evidence sits on a **ladder** of quality, and where a fact sits determines how much weight it can bear.

![Evidence quality runs from primary data at the wide base up to narrative at the narrow top](/imgs/blogs/structuring-a-thesis-claim-evidence-and-catalyst-2.png)

From the bottom (strongest, load-bearing) to the top (weakest):

- **Primary data** (weight ≈ 1.0) — the number itself, first-hand: the actual filing, the actual transaction, the actual print. NorthStar's 10-Q showing capex spent on the Monterrey line is primary. There is no inference between you and the fact.
- **Corroborated data** (weight ≈ 0.8) — two independent reads that agree. Supplier purchase-order data *and* port import records both showing NorthStar ramping volume. Each could be noisy alone; agreement from independent sources is hard to fake.
- **Direct inference** (weight ≈ 0.6) — something logically implied by a disclosed number. Management guided to "full utilization by Q3," and you infer the margin that implies. The inference is sound but it is your inference, not their commitment.
- **Analogy** (weight ≈ 0.3) — "this rhymes with the 2016 plant ramp at a peer." Analogies generate hypotheses; they do not confirm them. The current case can differ in the one variable that matters.
- **Narrative** (weight ≈ 0.1) — a story, theme, or vibe: "industrials are coming back," "this is an AI play." Narrative is where most bad theses get all their weight. It feels like evidence because it is *coherent*, but coherence is not confirmation.

The rule the ladder enforces: **weight by quality and independence, never by count.** One primary data point outweighs ten narrative points, because the ten narrative points are usually the same story restated, not ten independent confirmations.

A second subtlety: the ladder rung depends on the *distance between you and the fact*, not on how confident the source sounds. A management team guiding to "full utilization by Q3" sounds authoritative, but it is still direct inference, not primary data, because the margin you care about is *implied* by the guidance rather than disclosed — and managements are optimists with an incentive to guide high. A sell-side analyst's price target sounds like analysis, but it is usually narrative or analogy wearing a spreadsheet, because the target is an opinion derived from the same consensus you are trying to beat. Climb down the ladder by asking, for each fact, "how many inferential steps sit between this fact and the number in my claim?" Zero steps is primary. One sound step is direct inference. Several steps, or a step that rests on "it worked last time," is analogy. No verifiable step at all — just a story that feels right — is narrative.

The ladder also explains why *original* primary research is so valuable and so rare. If your strongest evidence is a 10-Q line everyone can read, you have an edge only in *interpretation*, not in *information* — and interpretation edges are thin because everyone is staring at the same filing. The analyst who has the supplier purchase-order data, or who counted trucks at the plant, or who surveyed the distributors, owns primary evidence the rest of the market does not, and that is where a real informational gap comes from. The ladder is not just a weighting scheme; it is a map of where edge is scarce.

### What makes a catalyst real

A **catalyst** is not "good things will happen." A real catalyst has three properties, and a candidate that misses any one is not a catalyst:

1. **Dated** — it happens on or by a knowable date. "The Q3 print in late October" is dated. "Eventually the market notices" is not.
2. **Observable** — when it happens, you and everyone else can see it. An earnings number is observable. "Sentiment improving" is not — you cannot point to the moment it occurred.
3. **Has a mechanism** — there is a specific causal reason it forces a re-price. The mechanism for the NorthStar catalyst is precise: the Q3 income statement prints the new plant margin, which is *higher than the margin consensus models carry*, so analysts must raise estimates, and higher estimates lift the price. No hand-waving about "recognition."

![Different catalyst types resolve a thesis on clocks from days to multiple quarters](/imgs/blogs/structuring-a-thesis-claim-evidence-and-catalyst-4.png)

Catalysts come in types, and the type determines the clock you are signing up for. A scheduled macro print (CPI, jobs, an FOMC decision) resolves in days. A quarterly earnings report resolves in weeks. A corporate action — a buyback authorization, a spin-off, an M&A close — resolves over one or two quarters. An operational ramp like a new plant resolves over two to four quarters. And a "regime re-rating" — the market simply deciding to value the whole sector differently — is open-ended, which is a polite way of saying it is not really a catalyst at all. Matching the catalyst's clock to your claim's horizon is half of getting the thesis right, and we will return to horizon mismatch as a failure mode.

## Writing the claim

With the parts defined, we build them one at a time, starting with the claim, because the claim is what the other two components serve. A loose claim cannot be rescued by good evidence or a clean catalyst; you will have marshaled facts and named a date for a statement too vague to be graded.

A tradeable claim passes four tests at once: it is **specific**, it carries a **magnitude**, it names a **horizon**, and it is **falsifiable**. Miss any one and the claim collapses back into a hope.

![A tradeable claim must be specific, carry a magnitude, name a horizon, and be falsifiable](/imgs/blogs/structuring-a-thesis-claim-evidence-and-catalyst-3.png)

**Specific** means you have named the variable, the entity, and the unit of measurement. "The company does well" names nothing. "NorthStar free cash flow per share" names a specific firm, a specific metric, and the unit it is measured in. Specificity is what lets you check the claim against a single number later, rather than arguing about whether "well" was achieved. Specificity also forces you to choose the *right* variable, which is itself analytical work: "NorthStar does well" might mean revenue, earnings, free cash flow, or the stock price, and these can diverge sharply — a company can grow revenue while free cash flow falls. Naming free-cash-flow-per-share commits you to the variable that actually drives the valuation you care about, and that commitment is where the thinking happens.

**Magnitude** means a number, not a direction. "Goes up" is a direction and it is nearly useless, because *how much* it goes up determines whether the trade is worth doing at all. A stock that is going up 3% is not worth the risk of being wrong by 20%. "Free cash flow inflects +40%, to about \$4.20" is a magnitude. Magnitude is also what lets you compute the reward side of the trade and therefore size it.

**Horizon** means a dated window, not "someday." "By the Q3 print, late October" is a horizon. "Eventually" is not. Horizon is load-bearing for a reason most people miss: it determines *what counts as noise versus signal* while you wait, and it determines *which instrument* you should use. A claim with no horizon cannot be graded, because you never know when to grade it, and a claim that can never be graded is a hope by another name.

Horizon also silently sets your tolerance for the path. A four-month claim graded on the Q3 print can survive a 15% drawdown along the way if the thesis is intact, because the grading event has not arrived; the adverse move is noise relative to the horizon. A four-day claim cannot survive that same drawdown, because four days is not enough time to recover before grading — the adverse move *is* the signal. Same direction, same magnitude, utterly different relationship to interim volatility, entirely because of the horizon. Analysts who fail to internalize this get shaken out of correct longer-horizon theses by short-horizon noise, or hold short-horizon theses long past the point the move was supposed to happen. The horizon tells you which moves to ignore and which to obey.

**Falsifiable** means there exists a specific observable that would prove you wrong. "A flat Q3 segment margin kills the thesis" is falsifiable. A claim that no fact could ever disprove — because it was written to be compatible with every outcome — is the hallmark of the hope.

#### Worked example: magnitude and horizon turn a vague claim into a sized one

Start with the hope: *"NorthStar is undervalued and should do well."* You cannot size this, because sizing needs two numbers — the reward if right and the loss if wrong — and the hope supplies neither. Put \$20,000 behind it and you are guessing.

Now add magnitude and horizon. The claim becomes: *"NorthStar trades from \$50 to \$70 (+40%) by the Q3 print in late October; I'm wrong below \$45."* Suddenly you can do arithmetic. Reward if right: (\$70 − \$50) / \$50 = +40%, or +\$8,000 on a \$20,000 position. Loss if wrong: (\$45 − \$50) / \$50 = −10%, or −\$2,000 on that position. The reward-to-risk is \$8,000 / \$2,000 = 4:1. The horizon is roughly four months, so the claim is graded on the late-October print and not before.

That single transformation — from "undervalued" to "+40% by late October, wrong below \$45" — is what converts a position you cannot rationally take into one you can size at \$20,000 with a 4:1 payoff and a known grading date. **The magnitude built the reward, the horizon built the clock, and the invalidation built the stop — and a claim without all three cannot be sized at all.**

### The two most common ways a claim fails

The first is the **vague claim**: it skips magnitude or horizon and hides behind quality language. "Great franchise, mispriced quality, value will be recognized." The fix is mechanical: force yourself to fill in the four blanks — variable, number, date, kill switch — and if you cannot, you do not yet have a claim, you have an interest in the name.

The second is **horizon laundering**: writing a short-horizon claim but treating it as a long-horizon one when it goes against you. You claim a Q3 catalyst, the catalyst passes without the inflection, and instead of taking the loss you quietly extend the horizon to "Q4, or maybe next year." This is the most expensive habit in the business, because it removes the one property — falsifiability on a fixed date — that made the claim a claim. Write the date down and hold yourself to it.

A third, subtler failure is the **multiple-of-itself claim**, where the magnitude is real but is just the current price extrapolated. "It went up 40% last year, so it goes up 40% this year" attaches a number and even a horizon, so it looks like a claim — but the magnitude is not derived from anything about the business; it is the past pasted onto the future. The test that catches it: can you state *why* the magnitude is what it is, in terms of a fundamental driver? For NorthStar, the +40% comes from a specific margin step-up flowing to free cash flow — a number you can trace. For the extrapolation, the +40% comes from nowhere but the chart. A magnitude with no fundamental derivation is a number, not a claim, even when it is precise.

## Marshaling the evidence

A claim is a bet on a gap between your view and the market's. Evidence is the reason you believe the gap is real. The job here is not to *collect* evidence — anyone can collect — but to *weight* it honestly and to deliberately go looking for the facts that would sink you.

### Weight by the ladder, not by the pile

Return to the evidence ladder. The discipline is to score each piece of evidence by where it sits, then ask whether the *high-quality* evidence alone supports the claim. If your thesis rests on the bottom two rungs — primary and corroborated data — it can bear weight. If it rests on the top two — analogy and narrative — it cannot, no matter how many points you have, because narrative points are almost never independent.

Independence is the subtle part. Ten analysts all repeating "industrials are back" is not ten pieces of evidence; it is one narrative echoed ten times. The correlation between them is near 1.0, so they add almost no information beyond the first. Genuine corroboration requires *independent sources that could have disagreed*: supplier orders and port data are independent because the supplier and the port have no reason to coordinate. When two such sources agree, the agreement is informative precisely because it was not guaranteed.

#### Worked example: scoring an evidence ladder changes the size

You hold a \$20,000 conviction in the NorthStar thesis and you are deciding how much of your intended position to actually put on. Score the evidence behind it.

Thesis A (narrative-heavy): "Industrials are coming back" (narrative, 0.1) + "feels like the 2016 ramp" (analogy, 0.3) + "management sounded confident on the call" (narrative, 0.1) + "the stock chart looks like it wants to break out" (narrative, 0.1). Sum the weights: 0.1 + 0.3 + 0.1 + 0.1 = 0.6 of "quality-weighted evidence." Four points that *feel* like a wall of support, but the wall is made of vibe.

Thesis B (data-grounded): "10-Q shows \$120M capex on the Monterrey line, on schedule" (primary, 1.0) + "supplier PO data and port import records both show volume ramping" (corroborated, 0.8) + "management guided to full utilization by Q3, which implies the higher margin" (direct inference, 0.6). Sum: 1.0 + 0.8 + 0.6 = 2.4 of quality-weighted evidence — four times Thesis A's, on three points instead of four.

If your sizing rule scales conviction with quality-weighted evidence, Thesis A's 0.6 might justify a quarter-position — \$5,000 of the \$20,000 — while Thesis B's 2.4 justifies the full \$20,000. **The same number of facts, scored by the ladder, produces a 4× difference in defensible size — because quality and independence, not count, are what evidence weight measures.**

### Actively seek disconfirming evidence

The most important evidence in any thesis is the evidence against it, and almost nobody goes looking for it. The default human move is confirmation: you have a view, so you notice the facts that support it and slide past the ones that don't. In markets this is not a quirk; it is a wealth-transfer mechanism. The disconfirming fact you skipped is exactly the fact the person on the other side of your trade is acting on.

The fix is procedural. For every thesis, write down the answer to: *"What would I expect to see if I were wrong, and do I see it?"* For the NorthStar thesis, being wrong looks like: the plant ramp slipping (check the capex pacing and any 8-K language), pricing softening so the higher utilization does not flow to margin (check channel pricing), or a demand air-pocket (check the order book). When you go looking, you find a real disconfirming point: one channel check shows distributors pushing back on price. The honest move is to *log it as a known risk inside the thesis*, not bury it. It does not necessarily kill the thesis — but it lowers your weight, tightens your invalidation, and tells you exactly what to watch.

This is also where the "who is on the other side" discipline lives: a strong thesis can usually name what the seller believes and why, rather than assuming the seller is simply wrong. (For how the counterparty's view enters your own, see [the reaction function: why the same number moves differently](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-reaction-function-why-the-same-number-moves-differently).)

There is a structured way to do this that goes beyond a vague "what if I'm wrong." Build a short **disconfirmation checklist** tied to the *mechanism* of your claim, not to its conclusion. The NorthStar claim's mechanism is "new plant runs at a structurally higher margin, and that margin flows to free cash flow." Each link in that mechanism has a way to break, and the checklist names them: (1) the plant does not reach full utilization on schedule — watch capex pacing and 8-K disclosures; (2) it reaches utilization but the higher gross margin does not appear — watch input costs and pricing; (3) margin improves but cash flow does not, because working capital absorbs it — watch receivables and inventory. Each item has an *observable* you can check before the catalyst date. The point is not to talk yourself out of the thesis; it is to know, in advance, exactly which fact would tell you the thesis is breaking, so you are not surprised by it on the print.

The psychology here is worth naming because it is the hardest part of the whole craft. You have done the work, you like the idea, and your brain is now a lawyer arguing for the position rather than a judge weighing it. Confirmation bias is not a flaw you can will away; it is the default setting. The only reliable counter is *procedure*: make the search for disconfirming evidence a required step with a written output, so that skipping it is a visible omission rather than an invisible comfort. The analysts who survive long careers are not the ones who are right most often; they are the ones who find out they are wrong *fastest*, and finding out fast starts with looking on purpose.

### Hit rate tracks evidence quality

The reason to do all this weighting is not aesthetic. Over many theses, the quality of the *top rung* of evidence behind a thesis predicts how often it resolves your way. Theses whose best evidence is narrative resolve correctly barely above a coin flip; theses grounded in primary or corroborated data resolve correctly meaningfully more often. The chart below is illustrative — the numbers come from the worked example, not a published study — but it captures the shape every analyst eventually learns the hard way.

![Hit rate of theses rises with the quality of their strongest evidence rung](/imgs/blogs/structuring-a-thesis-claim-evidence-and-catalyst-6.png)

The lesson is not "never trade on narrative." It is that a narrative-grounded thesis should be sized like a coin flip, because that is what it is, while a data-grounded thesis has earned the right to be sized up. The evidence ladder is, in the end, a position-sizing tool. ## The catalyst — what closes the gap

Here is the component most analysts skip, and skipping it is the single most expensive mistake in the craft. You can have a flawless claim and a mountain of primary evidence and still lose money for years, because being right about value is not the same as *getting paid*. What converts being right into being paid is the catalyst.

### Why "value will out" is not a plan

The folk belief is that if a stock is cheap, the market will "eventually" recognize it and you will be rewarded. The problem is the word "eventually." Markets can stay mispriced for a very long time — longer than your patience, your career, or your borrowing costs. While you wait, you are paying carry: financing costs, opportunity cost, and the slow drag of capital locked in something doing nothing. "Cheap" with no catalyst is not a trade; it is a subscription you pay into, hoping it converts before you run out of money.

Keynes captured the danger in the line every value investor has had cause to remember: the market can stay irrational longer than you can stay solvent. The catalyst is the discipline that protects you from that line. It does not require the market to *become* rational on your schedule; it identifies a specific event that *forces* the recalculation regardless of sentiment. An earnings print does not wait for the market to be in the mood — it arrives on the calendar, and the new number lands in every model whether the market wanted it or not. That is the difference between betting on a re-rating and identifying the mechanism that compels one. The first is hope; the second is a thesis.

There is also a sharper, often-missed cost to the catalyst-less value trap: while you wait, your capital is *not* in the names that do have catalysts. Even if the cheap stock eventually re-rates and you break even or make a little, you have spent eighteen months of capital that could have been deployed against three or four dated, catalyst-driven theses in sequence. The opportunity cost is not just the financing rate; it is the entire return stream you forwent by parking money in something with no clock. Cheap-with-no-catalyst does not just bleed carry — it freezes capital out of the part of the opportunity set where returns are actually being paid.

A catalyst is the answer to "why now?" — the dated, observable mechanism that *forces* the re-price rather than waiting for it. The NorthStar catalyst forces it because the Q3 print mechanically inserts the new margin into the numbers every analyst models from. There is no "recognition" required; the income statement does the work, on a date.

The deep reason a catalyst matters comes down to *who pays the carry*. When you hold a position, you are paying — in financing, in opportunity cost, in the patience your capital cannot deploy elsewhere. The market, meanwhile, pays nothing to keep ignoring you. That asymmetry is brutal: you are on a clock and the mispricing is not. A catalyst flips the asymmetry by putting the *market* on a clock — at the catalyst date, the market is forced to confront a fact it cannot interpret away, and the re-price happens on a schedule you can budget your carry against. Without a catalyst, you are betting that your patience outlasts the market's indifference, and the market's indifference is effectively infinite.

This is why professional event-driven and catalyst-driven strategies organize their entire process around the catalyst calendar rather than around valuation. A value that is cheap with a catalyst in six weeks is a trade; the identical value with no catalyst is a watchlist entry. The discount is the same; the tradeability is not. The catalyst is not a nice-to-have that improves a value thesis — it is the component that converts a valuation observation into a position with a return on a date.

#### Worked example: a catalyst-less thesis bleeds carry for 18 months

You buy \$20,000 of a genuinely cheap stock — trades at 8× earnings, half its peers — on the thesis "it's too cheap, the market will recognize it." There is no catalyst: no earnings inflection, no corporate action, no scheduled event that forces a re-rating. You are just early and patient.

Now count the carry over 18 months. If you funded the position on margin at 6% annually, the financing cost is \$20,000 × 6% × 1.5 years = \$1,800. Add the opportunity cost: a parked \$20,000 that could have earned even a modest 6% elsewhere forgoes another \$1,800 over 18 months — but to avoid double-counting against the margin cost, take the more conservative drag of roughly \$900 in opportunity cost net of what the position itself returned (it returned roughly nothing — it stayed cheap). Realistically, between financing and dead opportunity, the position bleeds on the order of \$1,800 + \$900 ≈ \$2,700 over the 18 months — and that is *before* any markdown if "cheap" gets cheaper. The stock did nothing wrong. The thesis was simply missing the one component that pays you: a dated mechanism.

**The \$2,700 is the price of the missing catalyst — you were right that it was cheap, and cheap with no catalyst still cost you about 13.5% of the position in carry while you waited for a re-rating no event was ever going to force.** This is the value trap in one number, and it is why "undervalued" is a claim, not a thesis. (For the full mechanics of why cheap can stay cheap, see the dedicated post [catalysts and timing: why cheap can stay cheap for years](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/catalysts-and-timing-why-cheap-can-stay-cheap-for-years).)

### Naming the catalyst and its timeline

A usable catalyst has a name, a date, and a mechanism, and you should be able to write all three in one line. "The Q3 earnings report, late October, prints the new plant margin and forces analysts to re-model." Compare to the non-catalyst: "the market eventually recognizes the value." The first has a name (Q3 report), a date (late October), and a mechanism (new margin into reported numbers). The second has none.

The timeline matters because it has to match the claim's horizon. If your claim is "+40% by late October" but your only real catalyst is an open-ended re-rating that might take three years, you have a horizon mismatch — the claim's clock and the catalyst's clock disagree, and you will grade yourself wrong in October on a thesis that was never going to resolve by then. Match them, or change one.

Catalysts also come in two flavors worth distinguishing: **hard catalysts** with a fixed date on the calendar (an earnings print, an FOMC decision, a scheduled product launch, a contractual debt maturity), and **soft catalysts** that are observable and mechanistic but not precisely dated (a new product gaining share, a cost program reaching scale, an activist building a position). Hard catalysts are easier to trade because the grading date is known — you can buy options that expire just after the event, you know exactly when to be in position, and you know exactly when to grade. Soft catalysts are real but require you to widen the horizon and accept more carry, because you are right about the mechanism but not about the timing. A common refinement is to anchor a soft-catalyst thesis to the nearest *hard* checkpoint — even if the full re-rating is gradual, the next earnings print is a dated moment where the soft progress becomes visible in numbers, and that print becomes your grading date.

The single best stress test for a candidate catalyst is to ask, "On the day after this event, will the consensus *number* be different than it is today?" If the answer is yes — analysts will revise their estimates because a fact forced them to — you have a real catalyst, because price follows the estimate. If the answer is "well, sentiment might improve" or "people might start to notice," you do not have a catalyst; you have a wish that the same facts will be interpreted more generously, which is not something any date forces.

### The gap and the "why now"

The deepest function of the catalyst is that it forces you to articulate the **gap** — the precise difference between what you believe and what the market is pricing. The NorthStar gap is concrete: the Street's models carry the old 18% margin; you believe the new line runs higher; the catalyst is the date that gap gets resolved on the income statement. If you cannot name what specifically the market believes that is wrong, and what dated event reveals it to be wrong, you do not have a variant view — you have a coincidence with the crowd. (This is exactly the priced-in question this whole series circles; the mechanics of measuring consensus and what is "in the price" are in [consensus, expectations, and priced-in](/blog/trading/event-trading/consensus-expectations-and-priced-in) and [the surprise, not the level: betas to data surprises](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/the-surprise-not-the-level-betas-to-data-surprises).)

## Chaining claim, evidence, and catalyst

The three components are not a checklist of independent boxes; they are links in a single chain, and a chain is only as strong as its weakest link. Chaining them means making sure each one actually connects to the next, with no gap papered over by hope.

![The claim names a gap, evidence supports the gap, and the catalyst closes the gap into profit](/imgs/blogs/structuring-a-thesis-claim-evidence-and-catalyst-7.png)

The chain runs like this. The **claim** asserts a number that differs from what the market prices — that difference is the **gap**. The **evidence** is the reason you believe your side of the gap is the right side; it has to actually support *this specific claim*, not a vaguely related one. And the **catalyst** is the dated event that closes the gap, turning your correctness into realized profit. Read across the figure: claim and "what's priced" define the gap; evidence supports the gap; the catalyst resolves it; and only then does a sized position with an invalidation follow.

The test for whether the chain holds is to walk it backward from the position. *Why this size?* Because of the reward-to-risk the claim implies. *Why believe the claim?* Because of the high-rung evidence. *Why now, and not in two years?* Because of the dated catalyst. *What breaks it?* The invalidation. If any of those questions returns a hand-wave, you have found the weak link, and that is where you should spend your remaining research time.

The figure below shows the same NorthStar thesis with all three panels filled in — not as abstract slots but as the specific numbered claim, the weighted evidence ladder with its logged disconfirming point, and the dated catalyst with its mechanism and invalidation. A finished thesis should fit in three panels like these, and if a panel is thin — the claim has no number, the evidence is all narrative, the catalyst has no date — the figure makes the gap obvious at a glance.

![One thesis built across the claim, evidence, and catalyst panels with numbers and dates](/imgs/blogs/structuring-a-thesis-claim-evidence-and-catalyst-5.png)

#### Worked example: building the complete thesis and the \$20,000 it justifies

Build the NorthStar thesis end to end, then size it.

- **Claim**: FCF/share inflects from \$3.00 to \$4.20 (+40%) by the Q3 print, late October. Implied stock move: from \$50 to roughly \$70 (+40%), assuming the multiple holds.
- **Evidence** (quality-weighted): primary 10-Q capex (1.0) + corroborated supplier/port data (0.8) + direct-inference margin from guidance (0.6) = 2.4; disconfirming: one channel check shows price pushback (logged, lowers weight slightly).
- **Catalyst**: Q3 earnings report, late October — dated, observable, mechanism is the new margin hitting reported numbers and forcing a re-model.
- **Invalidation**: flat Q3 segment margin, or stock closing below \$45.

Now size it. Reward if right: +\$8,000 (+40% on \$20,000). Loss at invalidation: the \$45 stop is −10%, so −\$2,000 on \$20,000. Reward-to-risk = 4:1. With strong (2.4) evidence and a clean dated catalyst, this clears the bar for a full intended position. So you put \$20,000 of capital to work, risking about \$2,000 to the \$45 invalidation, to make about \$8,000 if the Q3 print confirms. **Every dollar of the \$20,000 is justified by a specific link in the chain — the magnitude built the \$8,000 upside, the \$45 stop built the \$2,000 risk, the evidence earned the full size, and the dated catalyst guarantees the thesis gets graded in October rather than drifting.** (How you translate this into the actual instrument — stock, options, spread — is the expression step, covered alongside scenario construction in [base, bull, and bear: building three scenarios](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/base-bull-and-bear-building-three-scenarios).)

### Structural failure modes

Theses fail in recognizable, structural ways, and naming them lets you catch your own before the market does:

- **Vague claim** — no magnitude or horizon, so it can never be sized or graded. The thesis was a hope from the start.
- **Narrative-as-evidence** — the thesis rests on the top of the ladder. It feels supported because the story is coherent, but coherence is not confirmation, and the position is really a coin flip dressed up.
- **No catalyst** — a value trap. You may be right, but nothing dated forces the re-price, so you bleed carry while "eventually" never arrives.
- **Horizon mismatch** — the claim's clock and the catalyst's clock disagree. You grade yourself in October on a thesis whose only real catalyst was a multi-year re-rating.
- **Broken chain** — each component is individually fine, but the evidence supports a *different* claim than the one stated, or the catalyst resolves a *different* gap. The links exist but don't connect.

## Common misconceptions

**"Undervalued is a thesis."** It is not — it is a claim with no catalyst, and usually a vague claim at that. "Undervalued" tells you a direction with no magnitude, no horizon, and no dated mechanism to close the gap. As the carry example showed, you can be entirely right that something is cheap and still lose \$2,700 over 18 months waiting for a re-rating no event was ever going to force. A thesis needs the "why now," and "it's cheap" never supplies it.

**"More evidence makes a stronger thesis."** Quantity is the wrong axis. Ten correlated narrative points are one piece of evidence repeated, and they add almost nothing past the first. One primary data point — the actual filing, the actual transaction — outweighs the whole pile, because it sits at the load-bearing base of the ladder and because it is *independent* of the story you wanted to tell. Weight by quality and independence, not by count. The narrative-heavy thesis that scored 0.6 against the data-grounded thesis's 2.4 had *more* points and *less* support.

**"The story is the evidence."** A compelling narrative is the most dangerous thing in research precisely because it is compelling — it makes a thesis *feel* well-supported when the support is just internal coherence. "AI is transforming everything, this company does AI, therefore it goes up" is a story that explains any price move after the fact and predicts none before it. The story is a hypothesis to be tested against primary data, not a substitute for it. When you catch yourself most excited about how good the story sounds, that is the moment to go find the disconfirming fact.

**"I don't need a catalyst — value will out."** Value sometimes does out, but it does so on a clock you do not control, and the clock is usually longer than your carry can survive. Timing risk is not a footnote; it is the difference between a thesis that pays and a thesis that is technically correct and financially ruinous. The catalyst is what puts your correctness on a date you can survive. Without it, you are lending the market free patience and paying for the privilege.

**"My invalidation is just my stop-loss."** They are related but not identical. A stop-loss is a price level; an invalidation is a *fact* that says the thesis is broken — a flat Q3 margin, a slipped ramp, a guidance cut. Sometimes the fact arrives before the price moves, and a thesis-aware analyst exits on the broken fact rather than waiting for the stop. The invalidation belongs *inside the thesis*, written when you build the claim, not bolted on as a risk-management afterthought.

## How it plays out in real markets

### Building a complete thesis on a real-style setup

The cleanest real-world template for the NorthStar pattern is a capacity-expansion or margin-inflection story, which shows up constantly in industrials, semis, and energy. Consider the 2020–2021 episode where several chipmakers brought new fabs online: the *claim* was a specific gross-margin step-up by a named quarter; the *evidence* was primary (capex disclosures, equipment-maker order books) and corroborated (foundry utilization reports); and the *catalyst* was the earnings print where the new capacity first showed up in reported margin. Analysts who structured it that way could size it and grade it on the print date. Analysts who held the vaguer "semis are a secular growth story" version could not — they were holding a narrative, and when the cycle turned in 2022 they had no dated event to grade against, so they rode it down.

The discipline the structured version enforced was that the catalyst date *forced a decision*. When the print came and the margin step-up was confirmed, the thesis was validated and the analyst could press or take profit on a known reward-to-risk. When a different name's ramp slipped and the margin printed flat, the invalidation fired on the date, and the disciplined analyst was out — not nursing a "still early" narrative into the downturn.

The contrast with the unstructured version is instructive precisely because both analysts were, in a loose sense, "right" about the secular story. Semiconductors *were* a secular growth area; the narrative was true. But truth about the long run did not protect the narrative-holder, because the narrative had no date, no magnitude tied to a specific quarter, and no invalidation. When the cyclical downturn arrived in 2022, the structured analyst had already graded each name on its catalyst print and rotated accordingly, while the narrative-holder had nothing to grade against and simply held — the story was still "intact," after all — straight through a 40%-plus drawdown in many names. The lesson is not that the narrative was false; it is that a *true* narrative with no claim, no catalyst, and no invalidation is still untradeable, and untradeable views lose money in exactly the moments they most need to be acted on.

### An evidence-quality audit downgrades a narrative thesis

Run the audit on the kind of thesis that dominated late-2021 retail research: "Company X is the future of [sector], the TAM is enormous, management is visionary, and the stock has been a 10-bagger so it clearly has momentum." Lay each point on the ladder. "Future of the sector" — narrative (0.1). "Enormous TAM" — narrative, often a sell-side slide (0.1). "Visionary management" — narrative (0.1). "Been a 10-bagger" — that is *price*, not evidence about the business at all, and is arguably negative evidence since it raises the bar for the next move (0.0). Quality-weighted total: ~0.3, the support of a coin flip with a story attached.

A thesis that audits to 0.3 should be sized like the coin flip it is — a small, speculative position, not a core holding. The analysts who skipped this audit in 2021 and sized these names as if they were data-grounded convictions took the full force of the 2022 drawdown. The audit would not have told them *when* it would turn, but it would have told them the position deserved a quarter of the size they gave it, which is the difference between a bruise and a blow-up. (On why sizing to the quality of the edge is survival, not timidity, see [risk management: the only free lunch](/blog/trading/risk-management/risk-management-the-only-free-lunch-survival-as-a-compounding-engine).)

### A catalyst-less value trap bleeds for 18 months

The archetype here is a "cheap" stock in a structurally challenged industry — think a traditional media or bricks-and-mortar retail name trading at a low single-digit multiple through the late 2010s. The thesis was always the same: "trades at 6× earnings, half the market, too cheap to ignore." It was, by the multiple, genuinely cheap. And it kept getting cheaper, because the cheapness was the *correct* price of a business in secular decline, and there was no catalyst to re-rate it — no margin inflection, no transformative corporate action, just the hope that the market would "recognize the value."

Holders paid for that hope in carry and opportunity cost, often for far longer than 18 months, while the rare catalyst that did arrive — a dividend cut, a guidance miss — pushed the price down, not up. The structural lesson is exact: a low multiple is not a catalyst, and "the market will recognize it" is not a mechanism. Without a dated event that forces the gap closed, cheap is just a number you stare at while your capital does nothing, and "value will out" quietly becomes "value bled out." (The asymmetry that makes these slow bleeds so costly to recover from is laid out in [the asymmetry of losses](/blog/trading/risk-management/the-asymmetry-of-losses-why-a-50-percent-loss-needs-a-100-percent-gain).)

## The playbook

Here is the repeatable process — the thesis-construction worksheet. Run every name through it before it earns a dollar of risk.

**1. Write the claim, and make it pass all four tests.** Fill in the blanks explicitly:
- *Variable + entity*: which specific metric on which specific name? (NorthStar FCF/share.)
- *Direction + magnitude*: which way and how much, as a number? (+40%, to \$4.20.)
- *Horizon*: by what dated window? (The Q3 print, late October.)
- *Falsifiability*: what observable fact proves you wrong? (Flat Q3 segment margin.)
If you cannot fill all four, stop — you have an interest in the name, not a claim.

**2. Ladder the evidence, and audit it for quality and independence.** List every supporting fact and place each on the ladder (primary 1.0 / corroborated 0.8 / direct inference 0.6 / analogy 0.3 / narrative 0.1). Sum the quality weights. Then strip out the correlated narrative points and ask: does the *high-rung* evidence alone support the claim? If the thesis only holds with the narrative included, it does not hold.

**3. Actively hunt the disconfirming evidence.** Write: "If I were wrong, what would I see?" Go look for each item. Log what you find *inside* the thesis as a known risk; do not bury it. The fact you skip is the fact the other side is trading on.

**4. Name the catalyst — name, date, mechanism — and check the clock.** Write one line: "[event], [date], which forces a re-price because [mechanism]." If you cannot, you have a value trap, not a trade. Then confirm the catalyst's clock matches the claim's horizon; if they disagree, fix one.

**5. Chain it, and attack the weakest link.** Walk the chain backward from the position: *why this size? → why believe the claim? → why now? → what breaks it?* The link that returns a hand-wave is your weakest, and it is where the market will hit you. Spend your last hour of research there, not on polishing the link that is already strong.

**6. Size from the chain, write down the invalidation, and set the grading date.** The reward-to-risk from the claim and the evidence quality set the size; the invalidation sets the stop; the catalyst date sets when you grade. For NorthStar: \$20,000 of risk, +\$8,000 reward, −\$2,000 to the \$45 stop, graded on the late-October print.

The discipline this whole worksheet enforces is the spine of the series: *what do I believe, what is priced, what closes the gap, and what would prove me wrong?* A claim with a magnitude and a horizon, evidence that survives a quality audit, and a catalyst with a date and a mechanism — chained, stress-tested at the weakest link, and sized — is a thesis. Anything missing one of those three is a hope, and the market does not pay for hope. The natural next step is to pressure-test the finished thesis before you ever put it on, which is the subject of [stress-testing your thesis with a pre-mortem](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/stress-testing-your-thesis-with-a-pre-mortem).
