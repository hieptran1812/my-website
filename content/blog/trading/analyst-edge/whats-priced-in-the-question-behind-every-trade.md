> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The market price is already a forecast: the weighted consensus of everyone's view. You get paid not for being right about the world, but for being right about the gap between what happens and what was already priced in.
>
> - A price is a probability-weighted vote. To trade it, first read the forecast it encodes — then ask whether your own forecast differs.
> - Price moves on the **surprise** (actual minus expected), not on the level of the number. That is why "good news, stock falls" happens every earnings season.
> - The consensus is readable: analyst estimates, forward curves, fed funds futures, breakevens, the options-implied expected move, positioning surveys. Learn where each lives.
> - The one rule: **trade the gap, not the level.** Write your estimate next to the market's, and only put on a trade where the two genuinely disagree.

A semiconductor company reports earnings after the close. Revenue is up 28% year over year. Earnings per share come in at \$2.22 against a published Wall Street consensus of \$2.10 — a clean 6% beat. Management raises full-year guidance. Every headline that crosses the wire says the same thing: *strong quarter, beat-and-raise.* And in the after-hours session, the stock drops 8%.

If you are new to markets, this is genuinely confusing. The company did *better* than analysts expected. More money came in. The future looks brighter than it did this morning. And the price falls hard. Somebody who bought the stock that afternoon "because earnings would be good" is now staring at a screen that seems to be punishing them for being right. They were right about the world — the company really did have a good quarter — and they still lost money.

The analyst watching the same print is not confused at all. She knew that the *published* \$2.10 consensus was stale, that the buy-side "whisper number" — the figure the smart money was actually positioned for — was \$2.30, and that the stock had already rallied 15% into the quarter precisely because everyone expected a blowout. So when \$2.22 landed, she didn't see a beat. She saw a *miss against what was priced in.* The good news had already been bought. The print confirmed the optimism but fell short of the *extra* optimism baked into the price, and the only thing left to do was sell. This is the single most important lesson in markets, and it is the subject of this entire post.

![Many investor views feed a weighted vote that becomes one price then a surprise then a reaction](/imgs/blogs/whats-priced-in-the-question-behind-every-trade-1.png)

The reader who lost money skipped one question — the question behind every trade, the one this whole series is built around: *what's priced in?* Not "is this good news or bad news," but "is this better or worse than what the price already assumes?" Everything else in forming a market view — sizing it, expressing it, knowing what would prove you wrong — is downstream of getting this one thing right. So we are going to build the idea from zero: what a price actually *is*, why the surprise (not the level) moves it, where you go to read the consensus across stocks, rates, and volatility, and the concrete checklist that turns "what's priced in?" into a habit you run before every trade.

## Foundations: what "priced in" actually means

Start with the thing on your screen: a price. A share of a company quoted at \$100. A barrel of oil at \$78. The two-year Treasury yielding 4.2%. It is tempting to treat a price as a *fact* — the value of the thing, handed down by the market. It is not a fact. It is a **forecast**.

Here is the precise definition we will use for the whole post. **"Priced in"** means: *the market price already reflects the consensus expectation about the future.* When traders say a piece of news is "priced in," they mean the price has already moved to account for it before it happened — so when it actually happens, there is nothing left to react to. When they say something is "not priced in," they mean the price has *not* yet adjusted, so the event, when it lands, will move the price.

Why is a price a forecast and not a fact? Because of *who sets it and how.* A price is the level at which the marginal buyer and the marginal seller agree to trade right now. The buyer thinks it is worth at least that much; the seller thinks it is worth at most that much; they meet in the middle. But behind that single transaction sits the whole market: pension funds, hedge funds, retail traders, dealers, algorithms — millions of participants, each with a view about the future, each backing that view with capital. The price is where all of those views, *weighted by the money behind them*, net out. It is a **weighted consensus forecast.**

This is worth dwelling on, because it is the foundation everything else rests on. A pension fund that manages \$400 billion has a louder vote than a retail trader with \$4,000, because it moves more capital. A hedge fund with deep conviction and high conviction-sizing pushes the price harder than a passive index fund rebalancing on a schedule. The price is not a simple average of opinions; it is a *capital-weighted* average. The market is, in effect, a giant aggregation machine that takes everyone's forecast as an input and emits one number as an output. That number is the best collective guess the market can produce about what the asset is worth, given everything currently known.

The mechanism by which this aggregation happens is worth being concrete about, because it is what makes "priced in" a real, physical phenomenon rather than a metaphor. Suppose new information appears — a credible rumor that a company will be acquired at a premium. The first traders to see it and believe it start buying. Their buying lifts the price. As the price rises, it becomes a *signal* to other traders that something is happening, and they investigate, form their own views, and trade accordingly. The price keeps moving until it reaches a level where the marginal buyer and marginal seller are roughly indifferent — where the people who think it's worth more have finished buying and the people who think it's worth less are willing to sell. At that new level, the rumor is "priced in": the price has fully absorbed the collective best estimate of the acquisition's probability and premium. Anyone who buys *after* this point is paying for information that is already reflected in the price, which is precisely why it no longer offers an edge. The whole process — information appearing, the fastest and most confident capital moving first, the price settling at a new consensus — is the market doing its one job: turning dispersed private views into a single public number.

This also explains why the speed at which something gets priced in varies so much. Liquid, heavily-followed assets — large-cap stocks, major currency pairs, on-the-run Treasuries — price new information in seconds, because thousands of well-capitalized participants are watching. Illiquid or under-covered assets — a small-cap stock, an obscure commodity, a frontier-market bond — price information slowly and imperfectly, because fewer eyes and less capital are doing the aggregating. The degree to which something is "efficiently priced" is not a property of markets in general; it is a property of *that specific asset's* depth of coverage and capital. Your edge is far more likely to live in the slow, thin, under-covered corners than in the most-watched, most-liquid names, where the consensus is sharpest and hardest to beat.

> [!note]
> This does **not** require markets to be perfectly efficient, and you should be suspicious of anyone who says it does. The price is the *consensus* forecast, and the consensus is frequently wrong — overconfident, herd-driven, slow to update, panicked at the bottom and euphoric at the top. The claim is narrower and more useful: whatever the market collectively believes is *already in the price.* Your edge comes from disagreeing with that consensus *correctly*, not from the consensus being infallible. We will return to this in the misconceptions section, because "priced in means I can't make money" is the most damaging misreading of this whole idea.

### Consensus, your estimate, and the surprise

Now we can build the three quantities that the rest of the post turns on. Take any future event with a number attached — a company's quarterly earnings, a monthly inflation print, a central bank decision.

1. **The consensus expectation.** This is what the market, on average, thinks the number will be. For earnings it is the published analyst consensus EPS. For inflation it is the median economist forecast for the CPI print. For a rate decision it is the probability the market assigns to a cut or a hike. This is the forecast already *in the price.*

2. **Your estimate.** This is what *you* think the number will be, after doing your own work. If you have no independent estimate, you have no view — you are just along for the ride at consensus.

3. **The surprise.** This is the punchline:

   $$\text{Surprise} = \text{Actual} - \text{Expected}$$

   The surprise — also called the *forecast error* or *the delta* — is the difference between what actually happened and what the consensus had priced in. **Price reacts to the surprise, not to the actual.** A 3% inflation print is "good" or "bad" only relative to what was expected. If 3% was expected, a 3% print is a non-event; the price barely moves. If 3.4% was expected and 3.0% prints, that is a *downside surprise* — inflation came in cooler than feared — and bonds rally, even though 3.0% is still high. The level was high; the surprise was friendly. The market trades the surprise.

There is a clean way to hold all three quantities at once. The *actual* is what the world does. The *expected* is what the price assumed the world would do. The *surprise* is the gap, and the price reaction is, to a first approximation, proportional to that gap. Write it as a near-equation: the reaction equals some sensitivity (call it the *beta*) times the surprise. A stock might move roughly 4% for every \$0.10 its EPS surprises against consensus; a Treasury ETF might move 0.4% for every 0.1 percentage point of inflation surprise. The beta is the asset's reaction function; the surprise is the input. If the surprise is zero — the actual exactly matches the expected — the reaction is zero no matter how dramatic the absolute number is. This is why two traders can watch the same headline, "inflation at 3%," and have completely different, both-correct reactions: one knows it was expected (non-event) and the other doesn't (and is baffled when nothing moves).

The deepest implication of the surprise framework is that **your job is to forecast the surprise, not the number.** Forecasting that a great company will report a great quarter is worthless if everyone already expects a great quarter — your forecast and the consensus agree, the surprise is zero, and there is nothing to trade. Forecasting that a mediocre company will report a slightly-less-mediocre quarter than the market fears *is* valuable, because that is a positive surprise the price hasn't absorbed. The entire skill is reorienting your analysis away from "what will happen" and toward "what will happen *relative to what's priced.*" Those are different questions, they have different answers, and only the second one pays.

![Naive view trades the headline number while the analyst view trades the surprise actual minus expected](/imgs/blogs/whats-priced-in-the-question-behind-every-trade-2.png)

This is the surprise framework, and it is the engine of the entire post. On the left is the naive mental model: read the headline, judge whether the number is good or bad on its own terms, buy the good and sell the bad — and then sit there baffled when good news makes the price fall. On the right is the analyst's model: read what was *expected* first, compute the surprise as actual minus expected, and trade only the gap between the print and the price. The reaction is the sign and the size of the surprise, full stop. Internalize this framework and most of what happens around scheduled events stops being mysterious.

### A first worked example: the beat that wasn't

Let's put dollars on the opening story, because the math is the lesson.

#### Worked example: the 6% beat that fell 8%

You hold a \$10,000 position in the semiconductor stock at \$100 per share — 100 shares. You bought it the week before earnings because "the numbers will be good." Here is what was actually in play:

- **Published consensus EPS:** \$2.10. This is the number on the financial websites, often stale because analysts update slowly.
- **Whisper number (what was priced in):** \$2.30. The buy-side, watching channel checks and the 15% pre-earnings rally, was positioned for a blowout. The *price* reflected \$2.30, not \$2.10.
- **Actual print:** \$2.22.

Against the published \$2.10, the print is a **+\$0.12 beat** — a headline "beat." But against the \$2.30 that was *priced in*, the print is a **−\$0.08 miss.** The surprise that the market trades is the second one. The good news was already bought; the print fell short of the extra optimism in the price; the stock falls 8% to \$92.

Your \$10,000 position is now worth \$9,200. You lost \$800 — about 8% — on a quarter where the company *beat published estimates.* You were right about the world and wrong about what was priced in, and the market only pays you for the second thing.

![Beat versus whisper bar chart showing the print missed the higher number priced in](/imgs/blogs/whats-priced-in-the-question-behind-every-trade-3.png)

The chart makes the trap visible. The published consensus is the short gray bar. The whisper number — the figure the price was actually discounting — is the taller lavender bar. The actual print, in blue, sits *between* them: above the published number (the headline "beat") but below the whisper (the real, price-moving surprise). The naive trader anchored on the gray bar and saw a beat. The price was anchored on the lavender bar and saw a miss. *The number that matters is never the one in the headline; it is the gap to what was priced in.*

## Reading the consensus: where the forecast lives

If a price is a forecast, then the consensus is not hidden — it is quoted, all day, in instruments you can pull up on any terminal. Learning to *read* the embedded consensus is the core skill of this post. Every asset class advertises its own consensus in its own dialect; the analyst learns to read all of them. Here are the six most important places to look.

![Six market sources for reading the consensus across estimates options forwards rates breakevens and positioning](/imgs/blogs/whats-priced-in-the-question-behind-every-trade-6.png)

### 1. Analyst estimates (the equity consensus)

For a single stock, the consensus is the most explicit: sell-side analysts publish estimates for revenue, EPS, margins, and a price target, and data vendors aggregate them into a mean and a median. That published number is the *official* consensus. But the price often discounts something more aggressive — the **whisper number** — which is the unofficial figure the buy-side is really positioned for. The gap between the published consensus and the whisper is exactly the trap from our worked example. Before any earnings trade, you need both: the published number *and* a read on the whisper (from the pre-earnings price action, the options-implied move, and the tone of recent analyst revisions).

### 2. Forward curves (commodities, FX, equities)

A forward or futures price is the market's quoted expectation of where the spot price will be at a future date. The oil futures curve tells you what the market thinks oil will cost in three, six, twelve months. The shape matters: an upward-sloping curve (contango) and a downward-sloping one (backwardation) each encode a different consensus about future supply and demand. If you think oil will be at \$90 in six months and the six-month future is already at \$88, almost all of your view is priced in — you have \$2 of edge, not \$12.

This is the single most common mistake in commodity and currency trading: confusing a view about the *spot* price with a tradeable edge. A trader who is "bullish oil at \$78" and learns the six-month forward is already \$88 has just discovered that the market agrees with them and then some. Their bullishness is not an edge; it is a slightly *less* bullish view than the curve already embeds. The forward curve is the consensus forecast laid out across time, and your view has to be measured against the curve at the relevant horizon, not against today's spot. Whenever you catch yourself excited about a directional view, immediately go find the forward at the date your view resolves and ask: how much of this is already in the curve?

### 3. Fed funds futures and OIS (the rate-path consensus)

For interest rates, the market quotes its entire *expected path* of policy. Fed funds futures and overnight index swap (OIS) rates let you back out the probability the market assigns to a cut or a hike at each upcoming central-bank meeting, and the implied policy rate several meetings out. This is the single most important consensus in macro: it is the market's forecast of what the central bank will do, meeting by meeting, and it is already in the price of every rate-sensitive asset on the planet.

![Step chart of the market-implied policy path versus your own path with the gap shaded](/imgs/blogs/whats-priced-in-the-question-behind-every-trade-4.png)

This chart is the rate-trader's version of the whole post. The blue step line is the **market-implied path** — the sequence of policy rates the price already discounts, derived from fed funds futures. The amber dashed line is **your path** — your own forecast, in this case that the central bank cuts slower and later than the market expects. You are *not* paid for the level of rates; the level is priced. You are paid only for the **gap** between your path and the market's — here, about 50 basis points higher by the fifth meeting. If your gap is right, you make money even though the absolute direction (rates falling) is the same as consensus. The trade was never about the direction; it was about the *pace relative to what was priced.*

Notice what this rules out. If your forecast for the rate path is *identical* to the market-implied path, you have nothing to trade, even if you are supremely confident the central bank will cut — because the cuts are already in the price. A trader who says "I'm bullish bonds, the bank is going to ease" without checking the implied path is not expressing a view; they are restating the consensus and paying the spread to do so. The view only exists where the two lines diverge. Bond traders live in this gap: the entire fixed-income market is, at one level, a giant argument about whether the priced path of policy is too aggressive or too timid, and the only money to be made is by the side that's right about the *difference.*

#### Worked example: sizing the rate-path gap to a \$25,000 position

You think the central bank will cut *slower* than the market expects — the amber path in the chart, running about 50 basis points above the market-implied blue path by the fifth meeting. You want to express this with a \$25,000 position in a rate-sensitive instrument whose price falls when expected rates rise. From its characteristics, this instrument moves roughly **0.6% for every 25 basis points** that the expected rate path shifts.

Your view is that the path ends up ~50 bps higher than priced. If you're right and the market re-prices to your path:

$$\text{Expected move} = 50\text{ bps} \times \frac{0.6\%}{25\text{ bps}} = +1.2\%$$

On \$25,000, betting *against* the priced-in cuts (a position that gains when rates come in higher than expected), that is **+\$300** if your gap is realized. But here is the discipline the framework forces: if the bank cuts *exactly* as the market priced — the blue path — your surprise is zero and you make nothing, regardless of how much rates fell in absolute terms. You are not betting on the direction of rates (everyone agrees they fall); you are betting only on the 50 bp *gap* between your path and the \$25,000 of consensus already in the price. Size the position to the gap and your conviction in it, never to the strength of your feeling about the direction.

### 4. Breakevens and TIPS (the inflation consensus)

The bond market quotes its inflation forecast directly. The **breakeven inflation rate** — the yield difference between a nominal Treasury and an inflation-protected one (TIPS) of the same maturity — is the average annual inflation the market expects over that horizon. If the five-year breakeven is 2.3%, the market is collectively forecasting 2.3% average inflation over five years. When a CPI print lands, what moves breakevens is the *surprise* versus that 2.3% expectation, not the headline rate.

### 5. Implied volatility and the expected move (the options consensus)

The options market quotes a consensus about *uncertainty* — how big a move it expects. From the price of options, you can extract the **expected move**: the magnitude of the swing the market is pricing around a known event (an earnings date, a Fed meeting, an election). This is the single most useful read for event trades, and it deserves its own section below.

### 6. Positioning and sentiment surveys (the crowd consensus)

Finally, the *softer* consensus: where is the crowd already positioned? Commitments-of-Traders (CFTC) reports, fund-manager surveys, the AAII sentiment survey, prime-broker positioning data — these tell you not what the market *expects* but where it has already *bet.* This matters because extreme positioning is itself priced in: if everyone is already maximally long, there is no one left to buy, and even good news can fall flat (or trigger a "pain trade" squeeze in the opposite direction). Positioning is the consensus of *bets placed*, and it interacts with the consensus of *forecasts* in important ways.

The distinction between the forecast consensus and the positioning consensus is subtle but it pays to keep it sharp. The forecast consensus answers "what does the market expect to happen?" The positioning consensus answers "what has the market already done about it?" Usually they line up — bullish forecasts come with long positioning. But the interesting setups are where they diverge or reach an extreme. When positioning is stretched to one side, the *marginal* flow that could push the price further has already been used up, and the asymmetry flips: a market that is universally bullish and maximally long is fragile, because the only surprise left is disappointment, and there is a wall of long positions to unwind if it comes. Reading positioning is reading how much fuel is left in the tank for the consensus view — and a tank that is empty is itself a kind of contrarian signal, regardless of whether the forecast is right.

### The expected move: the options-implied range

The expected move deserves a worked example because it is the most directly tradeable read of "what's priced in" you will find. Around any scheduled event, options prices encode how big a move the market expects. The quick approximation: the price of the **at-the-money straddle** (buying one call and one put at the current price, both expiring just after the event) is roughly the expected move. If a stock trades at \$100 and the straddle costs \$5.95, the market is pricing an expected move of about ±\$6, or ±6%, over that window.

![Probability density with the priced-in expected move band shaded and the surprise tails beyond it](/imgs/blogs/whats-priced-in-the-question-behind-every-trade-5.png)

The picture is the whole concept in one frame. The straddle at \$5.95 prices an expected move of ±\$6, drawn as the amber band from \$94 to \$106. Everything *inside* that band is **already priced in** — the market expects an outcome in that range, so an outcome in that range is roughly a non-event for an options position bought at that price. The money is in the **tails**: the green regions beyond ±6%, where the actual move exceeds what was priced. A buyer of the straddle needs the stock to move *more* than ±\$6 to win; a seller needs it to move *less.* Neither is betting on direction — both are betting on whether the realized surprise lands inside or outside the priced range. This is the cleanest possible statement of "trade the gap, not the level": the level (the stock at \$100) is irrelevant; the only thing that pays is whether the move beats the priced expectation.

#### Worked example: sizing a \$25,000 position to the expected move

You have a \$25,000 directional view on the semiconductor stock into earnings — you think it goes up. The stock is \$100, and you've read the consensus the right way: the at-the-money straddle is \$5.95, so the market prices a ±6% expected move (±\$6). Here is how that changes the trade.

If you simply buy \$25,000 of stock (250 shares), the priced-in outcomes are roughly:

- **In line with the priced bull case (+6%, to \$106):** +\$1,500. But this is the move the market *already expects* if it goes up — you are being paid only the consensus amount for a consensus outcome.
- **In line with the priced bear case (−6%, to \$94):** −\$1,500.

The expected move tells you the *range you are not getting paid for.* To make money, your *real* forecast has to be a move bigger than ±6% in your direction — say +10%, to \$110, for +\$2,500. The extra \$1,000 over the priced +6% outcome is your edge; the first \$1,500 was just collecting the consensus. If your honest estimate is "up about 6%," you have **no edge** — you are paying spread and risking \$1,500 of downside to earn a move that is already in the price. The expected move converts a vague "I'm bullish" into the precise question: *is my move bigger than the ±\$6 the market already expects?* If not, the \$25,000 stays in cash.

## The level versus the surprise, and the second derivative

We have established that price moves on the surprise. There is a subtler version of the same idea that catches even experienced traders, and it is worth making explicit: prices often move on the **second derivative** — not on whether things are good, but on whether they are getting *better or worse than expected, faster or slower than expected.*

Consider a company growing revenue 40% a year. That is spectacular. But if it grew 50% last year and 45% the year before, the *rate of growth is decelerating*, and the market — which had priced continued 45%+ growth — sells the stock even as it reports a number most companies would kill for. The level (40% growth) is excellent. The first derivative (still growing) is positive. But the second derivative (the growth rate is falling) is what was *not* priced, and that is what moves the stock. "Peak growth" sell-offs in high-multiple stocks are almost always second-derivative events: the company is still doing well, just *less spectacularly than the price assumed.*

The same logic runs through macro. A recession is not bearish for stocks at the moment it is worst; it is bearish on the way *in*, when conditions are deteriorating faster than priced, and it becomes *bullish* once the rate of deterioration slows — "less bad than feared." This is why stock markets famously bottom *before* the economic data does: the market is not waiting for the economy to be good, only for it to stop getting worse faster than expected. The trough in sentiment, when the news is uniformly terrible and everyone has capitulated, is frequently the moment the surprise flips from "worse than feared" to "less bad than feared," and the price turns hard even as the headlines stay grim for months. Markets are forward-looking and forecast-relative all the way down. They are constantly re-pricing not the current state of the world but the *change in the change.* If you only ever ask "is this good or bad," you will be on the wrong side of half of these moves. The question is always relative: *good or bad versus what was priced, and accelerating or decelerating versus what was priced.*

The practical upshot for forming a view is that you should be deeply suspicious of any thesis that boils down to "this thing is good." A thing being good is necessary but nowhere near sufficient. The thesis that pays has the shape "this thing is *better than the price assumes*" or "this thing is *getting better faster than the price assumes*" or, on the short side, "this thing is *priced for a perfection it cannot sustain.*" Every one of those is a statement about the relationship between reality and the consensus baked into the price — never about reality alone. When a colleague pitches you an idea, the first question to ask is not "is the company good?" but "what does the price already assume, and how is your view different?" If they can't answer the second question, they don't have a trade; they have an opinion about the world, which the market has already heard and priced.

### Reflexivity and "buy the rumor, sell the news"

This brings us to the oldest piece of market wisdom there is: **buy the rumor, sell the news.** It sounds like a cliché. It is actually a precise, mechanical consequence of everything above.

When a positive catalyst is anticipated — a product launch, a likely rate cut, a probable acquisition — the price rises *in advance* as traders buy the rumor and price in the expected good outcome. By the time the news is officially confirmed, there is no surprise left: the good outcome was already priced. So the people who bought the rumor *sell the news* to lock in the gain, and the price falls on the confirmation of good news. The "sell the news" drop is not irrational; it is the unwind of a position that has already collected its expected payoff. The surprise at the moment of the news is roughly zero (it was expected), and the marginal flow is sellers taking profit.

This is why a fully-anticipated event so often produces a counterintuitive reaction. A central bank cuts rates exactly as the market had priced — and bonds *sell off*, because some traders had positioned for an even more dovish surprise (a bigger cut, or stronger forward guidance) that didn't materialize. The actual cut was a relief to no one because it was certain; the *absence of a bigger dovish surprise* was the real, price-moving event. The level (a cut) was priced; the surprise (no extra dovishness) was the disappointment.

There is a second-order trap hiding inside "buy the rumor, sell the news," and it is what separates traders who understand the saying from traders who can actually use it. The expectations themselves are a *moving target.* As a catalyst approaches and the rumor gets bought, the bar that the news has to clear keeps rising. A stock that was priced for a "good" quarter three weeks ago may, after a 20% run-up, now be priced for a *perfect* quarter. The consensus you read on Monday is not the consensus the price will be measured against on Friday, because the price action between now and the event is itself moving the implied expectation. This is why timing matters: the same fundamental view ("this will be a good quarter") is a great trade when expectations are low and a terrible one after expectations have ratcheted up to meet it. You are not just racing to be right about the world; you are racing the rising bar of what's priced in. The right time to own the rumor is *before* the crowd has bought it and lifted the bar — once the run-up has happened, much of the surprise has already been spent, and the asymmetry has quietly flipped against you even though your fundamental view hasn't changed at all.

This reflexive quality — prices moving on expectations, expectations moving on prices — is why "priced in" is never a static fact you can look up once. It is a live, shifting quantity that you have to re-read continuously, especially as a catalyst approaches and the crowd piles in. The discipline is to keep asking the question not once but repeatedly: *what's priced in now, after this move?* — because the answer on the day of the event can be wildly different from the answer a month before.

#### Worked example: buying a \$10,000 position into a fully-priced rate cut

The central bank meets tomorrow. Fed funds futures price a 25 basis point cut with 98% probability — it is, for all practical purposes, **fully priced.** You hold a view that rate-sensitive stocks will rally on the cut, so you buy a \$10,000 position in a rate-sensitive ETF at \$50 per share — 200 shares — the afternoon before the meeting.

The next day the bank cuts 25 bps, exactly as priced. And the ETF *falls* 2%, to \$49. Your \$10,000 position is now \$9,800 — a \$200 loss on a day the bank did precisely the dovish thing you expected.

What happened? The cut was already in the price. There was no surprise — actual minus expected was zero. But the *market's accompanying statement* leaned slightly hawkish (it signaled fewer cuts ahead than traders hoped), which *was* a surprise relative to the priced-in dovish path. The marginal flow was traders who had "bought the rumor" of an easing cycle now "selling the news" of a cut that came with a cautious message. You traded the level (a cut) when the level was 98% priced; you ignored the surprise (the guidance), which was the only thing left to move the price. The lesson costs \$200 here and a great deal more at size: **never put on a trade whose entire thesis is already in the price.** If the cut is 98% priced, there is no cut left to trade — only the surprise around it.

## Common misconceptions

This part of the craft attracts a specific set of persistent errors. Each one has cost real traders real money, and each is corrected by the same single question.

### Misconception 1: "Good news means the price goes up"

This is the original sin, the one that produces the baffled face after every earnings beat. Good news moves a price up *only if the news is better than what was priced in.* Good news that merely matches the priced-in optimism is a non-event, and good news that falls short of priced-in optimism — like our 6% beat that fell 8% — is *bearish.* The price had already moved on the *expectation* of good news. The reaction is to the surprise, and a beat can be a negative surprise. Reverse it too: terrible news can send a stock *up* if the news is less terrible than feared, which is why beaten-down stocks routinely rally on "less awful than expected" reports. The direction of the news is not the direction of the reaction. The *surprise* is.

### Misconception 2: "The number that came out is what matters"

When a CPI print or an earnings number crosses the wire, the instinct is to react to the number itself: "3.4% inflation, that's high, sell bonds." But the number in isolation is meaningless. What matters is the number *minus the expectation.* If 3.4% was the consensus and 3.4% printed, bonds shouldn't move at all — the print confirmed the forecast. If 3.6% was expected and 3.4% printed, that is a *cooler-than-expected* surprise and bonds *rally*, despite 3.4% being a high absolute number. Always read the consensus *before* the number lands. If you don't know what was expected, the actual number tells you nothing about how the market will react. The first thing a professional does ahead of any data release is not "what will the number be" but "what is the number *expected* to be" — because the surprise is the only thing that trades.

### Misconception 3: "If it's priced in, I can't make money"

This is the most damaging misreading, because it is half-true in a way that leads people to give up exactly where the edge is. Yes — if your view is *identical* to the consensus, you cannot make money from it; you would be paying the spread to agree with the price. But "priced in" does not mean "unbeatable." It means the *consensus* is in the price, and the consensus is regularly wrong. Your edge is **variant perception** — having a *better* estimate than the consensus and being right about the difference. The market prices the central bank to cut once next quarter; you, after your own work, think it cuts twice; the gap between your forecast and the priced forecast is your entire opportunity. "Priced in" is not the end of the conversation — it is the *starting line.* It tells you the bar you have to clear: to make money you must disagree with the price *and be right about the disagreement.* The whole job is finding the spots where the consensus is wrong and you can show why. (We develop this idea fully in the companion post on variant perception — see the cross-links.)

### Misconception 4: "Consensus is a single published number"

Beginners treat "the consensus" as the figure printed on a financial website. Reality is layered. There is the *published* consensus (the stale mean of sell-side estimates), the *whisper* (where the buy-side is actually positioned), and the *price-implied* consensus (what the pre-event price action and options-implied move reveal about expectations). These can differ wildly, and the price reacts to the most aggressive of them — usually the price-implied one. Reading "the consensus" means triangulating all three, not copying one number off a screen.

### Misconception 5: "A surprise always moves the price in the obvious direction"

Even the surprise itself can mislead if you ignore positioning. A bullish surprise into a market that is *already maximally long* can fall flat — there is no one left to buy — or even reverse, because the bulls who were "right" take profit. A bearish surprise into a market that is heavily short can trigger a violent squeeze *up* as shorts cover. The surprise sets the direction *all else equal*; positioning is the "all else." This is why the consensus of *forecasts* and the consensus of *bets* (positioning) must be read together. (The mechanics of how positioning amplifies or reverses a reaction are covered in the event-trading posts linked below — we don't re-derive them here.)

## How it plays out in real markets

Theory is cheap. Here is the surprise framework operating in well-known episodes, with the dynamics that the "what's priced in?" question would have flagged in advance.

### 2022 CPI prints: when the level was scary but the surprise was friendly (and vice versa)

Through 2022, US inflation was the only macro variable that mattered, and every monthly CPI print was a market-moving event. The instructive thing is that the *level* of inflation was uniformly high and frightening all year — and yet the market reaction flipped sign from month to month, entirely on the *surprise.*

Take the print released in November 2022, for October data. Headline CPI came in *below* the consensus forecast — a cooler-than-expected reading. The absolute number was still high (well above the central bank's 2% target), so on a "level" basis it was alarming. But it was *lower than priced*, a downside inflation surprise, and the reaction was explosive: equities staged one of their largest single-day rallies of the year, bonds rallied hard, the dollar fell. The market did not trade the level (high); it traded the surprise (cooler than feared).

Contrast that with a print earlier in the year that came in *hotter* than consensus. Same high level of inflation — but this time the surprise was to the upside, and stocks and bonds sold off sharply. Identical "the level is high" backdrop, opposite reaction, because the surprise had the opposite sign. Anyone trading the level was whipsawed all year; anyone trading the surprise versus consensus had a coherent, repeatable read. *The level was never the signal. The surprise was.* (For how to size a trade to the *magnitude* of a data surprise, see the macro-correlations post on surprise betas linked below.)

#### Worked example: sizing a CPI surprise to a \$25,000 bond position

You run a \$25,000 position in a Treasury ETF and you're trading the monthly CPI release. From history, you know that for this ETF, a **0.1 percentage point downside surprise** in core CPI (cooler than expected) is worth roughly a +0.4% move in the ETF on the day — that is its *surprise beta.* The consensus for tomorrow's core CPI is +0.3% month-over-month.

You do your own work and conclude there's a real chance the print comes in at +0.1% — a **0.2 percentage point downside surprise** versus the +0.3% consensus. If you're right:

$$\text{Expected move} = 0.2\text{pp surprise} \times \frac{0.4\%}{0.1\text{pp}} = +0.8\%$$

On \$25,000, that is **+\$200** if the surprise lands. Crucially, if the print comes in *at* consensus (+0.3%), the surprise is zero and the ETF barely moves — you make almost nothing, because the +0.3% was already priced. You are not betting on inflation being low (it's still positive); you are betting on it being *lower than the \$25,000 of consensus already in the price.* The size of your bet should scale to the size of your expected surprise times the surprise beta — not to how strongly you "feel" about inflation. The framework turns a feeling into \$200 of expected payoff conditional on a specific, falsifiable surprise.

### An FOMC where the cut was fully priced

We worked the dollars on this above, but the episode is worth naming as a pattern because it recurs at every central bank, every cycle. When a rate decision is "fully priced" — futures showing 95%+ probability of a specific move — the decision itself is a non-event. The action is *entirely* in the accompanying statement, the updated projections (the "dot plot"), and the press conference, because those are the only places a *surprise* relative to the priced path can come from. Traders who position into a fully-priced cut "because the bank is easing" repeatedly get caught when the cut arrives with cautious guidance and the market sells the news. The disciplined read is: *the cut is priced; the only thing I can trade is the surprise in the guidance.* If you have no edge on the guidance, you have no trade.

### An earnings beat that fell

Our opening story is not a contrived example; it is the single most common earnings-season event. A high-flying stock rallies 15–25% into a quarter as expectations ratchet up, the company posts a genuine beat against published estimates, and the stock falls because the beat missed the *whisper* and the run-up had already priced perfection. The tell is always the same and always visible in advance: a large pre-earnings rally, an elevated options-implied move, and a published consensus that the price action implies is too low. When you see all three, the bar for a positive reaction is brutally high — the company has to beat not the published number but the priced-in perfection. "What's priced in?" asked *before* the print would have flagged that the risk/reward of being long into the event was poor, regardless of how good the quarter turned out to be.

### The 2020 crash and "less bad than feared"

The COVID crash of March 2020 and the recovery that followed is the cleanest large-scale demonstration of prices trading the second derivative rather than the level. On the way down, the news was uniformly catastrophic and the market fell roughly a third in a few weeks — but it was falling not because the economic data was bad (most of it hadn't even been reported yet) but because the *rate of deterioration* was outrunning what any price had assumed. Then, while the actual economic news was still getting worse by every measure — record unemployment, collapsing output — the stock market bottomed and began one of the sharpest rallies in history. Nothing about the present was good. What had changed was that the *surprise* flipped: policy support arrived faster and larger than priced, and the deterioration, while still severe, was no longer accelerating beyond expectations. The market was trading the change in the change. A trader anchored on the level ("the economy is collapsing, sell everything") stayed short into a 50%+ rally. A trader asking "is it getting worse *faster than priced*, or is the worst rate of decline now behind us?" caught the turn while the headlines were still terrible. The level said disaster; the surprise said relief; the price followed the surprise.

## The playbook: the "what's priced in?" checklist

Here is the concrete, repeatable process — the thing to actually *do* before any trade built on a view. The discipline is mechanical, and that is the point: it converts a hunch into an accountable, sized, falsifiable bet.

![Decision card showing pull consensus locate the implied move write your estimate and trade only the gap](/imgs/blogs/whats-priced-in-the-question-behind-every-trade-7.png)

The card above is the whole routine in one frame; here it is step by step.

**Step 1 — Pull the consensus.** Before you form any opinion about where something is going, write down where the *market* thinks it is going. For a stock: the published consensus EPS/revenue, *and* a read on the whisper from the pre-event price action. For a rate trade: the fed-funds-implied path, meeting by meeting. For a macro print: the median economist forecast. For a commodity or currency: the forward curve. You cannot have a view until you know the view you are disagreeing with.

**Step 2 — Locate the implied path and the expected move.** Find the market's *forecast* and its *uncertainty.* The forward curve or the implied policy path gives you the expected trajectory; the options-implied expected move (the at-the-money straddle) gives you the range the market is pricing around the next catalyst. Now you know both what the market expects and how surprised it would be by any given outcome.

**Step 3 — Write your estimate right next to the market's.** Literally, in the same line of your notes: "Market prices core CPI +0.3%; I think +0.1%." "Forwards price oil at \$88 in six months; I think \$95." "Straddle prices a ±6% earnings move; I think it's a ±10% move and it's higher." The act of writing them side by side forces the comparison that matters and exposes the cases where you have *no* gap.

**Step 4 — Trade only the gap.** This is the decision rule. If your estimate genuinely differs from what's priced — and you can articulate *why* the consensus is wrong — you have a tradeable gap, and you size the position to the gap (and to your conviction in it). If your estimate is essentially the consensus, **you have no edge and no trade**, no matter how strongly you feel about the direction. Agreeing with the price is not a trade; it is paying the spread to hold the consensus. The green path on the card is "the gap is real, size it to the surprise"; the red path is "no gap, pass — you'd be paying to agree."

> [!tip]
> Make this a written habit, not a mental one. A one-line journal entry per trade — *priced in: X; my estimate: Y; the gap I'm trading: Y−X; what would prove me wrong: Z* — does three things at once. It forces you to read the consensus (Step 1), it forces you to have an independent estimate (Step 3), and it forces you to name the gap you're actually betting on (Step 4). Months later it also tells you, honestly, whether your gaps were real edges or just restatements of the consensus. The decision journal is where "what's priced in?" stops being a slogan and becomes a measurable discipline.

To see the four steps run end to end on a single decision, take a concrete case. You're considering a long position in a retailer ahead of its earnings. **Step 1:** you pull the consensus — published EPS of \$1.50, and you note the stock is up only 3% into the quarter, so the price action doesn't imply an aggressive whisper. **Step 2:** you locate the expected move — the at-the-money straddle prices a ±7% earnings move, and the implied volatility is in line with the stock's history, suggesting the market sees an ordinary quarter, no special drama priced. **Step 3:** you write your estimate next to the market's — your own model, built from foot-traffic data and supplier checks, says EPS lands around \$1.65, a meaningful beat the price action says is not expected. **Step 4:** you have a real gap (\$1.65 versus a priced \$1.50, into low expectations and a modest pre-earnings move), and you can say *why* (your channel data is ahead of the stale sell-side models), so you put on the trade and size it to the gap. Contrast this with the same retailer after a 25% run-up, where the straddle prices a ±12% move and the whisper is clearly above \$1.50: now even your \$1.65 estimate might be *below* what's priced, the gap is negative or gone, and Step 4 says pass. Same company, same fundamental view, opposite decision — because the only thing that changed, and the only thing that matters, is what was priced in.

The rule at the bottom of the card is the whole post compressed to one line: **you are not paid for being right about the world, only for the gap between your view and the price.** The world being good is not enough; the world being good *relative to a price that already assumed good* is what pays. A great company with a perfect price is a coin flip. A mediocre company priced for disaster can be the trade of the year. The asset is never the bet — the *gap to what's priced in* is the bet.

One honest caveat to close the playbook: reading what's priced in does not, by itself, make you money. It is necessary, not sufficient. Knowing the gap between your view and the consensus only matters if your view is *better* than the consensus often enough to overcome costs — and that is a separate, harder problem (the subject of the variant-perception post). What the "what's priced in?" discipline buys you is something more basic and more valuable than any single trade: it stops you from making the trades that have no edge at all. It filters out the positions where you are merely agreeing with the price and paying the spread to do so — which, for most traders, is the majority of the trades they would otherwise put on. Half of getting good at this is not finding more edges; it is refusing to trade where there is none. The question behind every trade is also, just as often, the question that tells you *not* to trade.

Everything else in this series builds on this foundation. Once you can read what's priced in, the next questions follow naturally: *where* does a real, repeatable gap to consensus come from (variant perception), how do you *map* the full consensus systematically before forming a view, and how do you turn a gap into a structured, falsifiable thesis with a size and a stop. But it all starts here, with the question behind every trade. Before you click buy, before you build the model, before you write the thesis — ask the only question that the price cannot answer for you: *what's already in it?*
