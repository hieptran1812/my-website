> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Synthesis ends in **compression**. After running the six lenses, the discipline is to collapse the whole board into ONE sentence of the form *"[asset/direction], because [variant driver], until [invalidation/catalyst]."* If you cannot say it in one breath, you do not have a view — you have a pile of data.
>
> - The one-sentence read is the **test** that you have actually formed a call. A vague sentence exposes exactly what is missing: no variant (no edge), no invalidation (no exit), no magnitude (no size).
> - The sentence is also the **seed**: it dictates the instrument, sets the size off the entry-to-invalidation distance, and becomes the thing you re-check every morning, word for word.
> - The canonical structure has three slots, each a separate test: the **claim** is falsifiable, the **because** is variant (differs from consensus), the **until** is monitorable (a price or a dated catalyst).
> - The one rule to remember: **if it takes a paragraph, you are still gathering — when it fits one sentence, you have a read.**

## "So what's the trade, in one sentence?"

An analyst stands at the front of a conference room with forty slides queued up. They have done the work — genuinely good work. Slide three is a clean decomposition of the labour market. Slide eleven shows positioning data sourced from three different feeds. There is a regression on slide nineteen, a scenario tree on slide twenty-six, a section on cross-asset correlations, a page of risks. The portfolio manager has been patient through all of it. When the analyst clicks to slide twenty-eight, the PM finally holds up a hand and asks one question: *"So what's the trade, in one sentence?"*

And the analyst can't answer. Not because they didn't do the work — they did more work than anyone in the room. They can talk for an hour about any single slide. But asked to compress all of it into one actionable line, they stall: *"Well, it depends — if the labour data softens then rates probably... although positioning is stretched, so... and there's the cross-asset signal which cuts the other way..."* The PM has stopped listening. That silence — the gap between forty slides of analysis and one sentence of conviction — is the difference between research and a view. The analyst has *information*. They do not yet have a *read*.

This post is about closing that gap. The skill it teaches is not gathering more inputs or running another lens; it is the final, uncomfortable act of **compression** — collapsing everything you know into a single falsifiable sentence that a desk could act on this morning. Compression is not a summary step you do at the end for the audience's convenience. It is the moment the analysis becomes a decision. The analyst who can't say it in one sentence hasn't finished thinking; they've finished *collecting*. We will build the canonical structure of that sentence, show how to compress six lenses into it, and turn it into the position, the size, and the daily re-check that follow from it.

![Six lenses funnel through a dominant driver into one falsifiable thesis sentence](/imgs/blogs/from-data-to-a-read-collapsing-everything-into-one-sentence-1.png)

The figure above is the whole post in one picture. On the left, the six lenses you ran to understand the market — macro, fundamental, positioning, technical, sentiment, valuation. They do not all get equal weight in the final call; they funnel through the *dominant driver*, the one lens doing the most work right now. And the dominant driver collapses into one sentence with three slots. That sentence is the deliverable. Everything to its left is process; the sentence itself is the product.

## Foundations: the read, the one-sentence thesis, and the elevator test

Before we build anything, we need to define the process terms precisely, because "a read" and "a view" get used loosely and the looseness is where analysts hide from the compression they're avoiding.

**The read** is the working term for the conclusion of synthesis: the single, committed call you have formed after looking at the market through every lens you trust. "What's your read on oil?" is not asking what you know about oil — it is asking what you have *concluded*, expressed as something you'd act on. A read is distinct from analysis the way a verdict is distinct from a trial. The trial can run for days; the verdict is one word. The read is the verdict.

**The one-sentence thesis** is the read written down in its canonical form. It is not a slogan or a simplification — it is the *complete* view, compressed to its load-bearing parts and nothing else. The claim that compression "loses information" gets this exactly backwards, and we will spend a misconception on it: compression loses the *noise* — the forty slides of supporting detail that informed the call but are not the call — and keeps the *decision*. A good one-sentence thesis is denser than the forty slides, not thinner, because every word in it is doing work.

**Compression**, then, is the act of going from the board to the sentence. It is hard for a specific reason: it forces you to *choose*. Forty slides let every consideration coexist; a sentence does not. To write the sentence you must decide which lens dominates, which way the net call points, and what single thing would prove you wrong. Each of those is a commitment you could avoid by staying in the slides. That is why analysts resist compression — not because it's intellectually difficult, but because it's *accountable*. The slides protect you; the sentence exposes you.

### Why compression forces clarity

Here is the mechanism that makes the one-sentence discipline so powerful: compression does not merely *describe* your view — it *interrogates* it. When you try to force everything into one sentence, the sentence pushes back wherever the analysis is thin. If you cannot name the variant — the thing you believe that the market doesn't — the "because" clause comes out empty or circular ("because I think it goes up"), and that emptiness is a finding: you have no edge, you're just agreeing with the price. If you cannot name the invalidation, the "until" clause dissolves into "until I change my mind," and that dissolution is also a finding: you've built a horoscope, a view no observation could refute. If you cannot state a magnitude, the claim is direction-only and you discover you can't size it.

The sentence, in other words, is a *diagnostic instrument*. A vague sentence is not a stylistic failure to be polished; it is a readout telling you exactly which part of your analysis hasn't been done. The analyst stalling in front of the PM wasn't bad at speaking — they were honestly reporting that the compression had failed because one of the three slots was empty. We will return to this: **a vague sentence is the symptom; the missing component is the disease.**

### The elevator test

The classic operationalisation of all this is the **elevator test**: can you state your view to a busy decision-maker in the time it takes to ride an elevator a few floors — one sentence, one breath, no preamble? The test is not about brevity for its own sake. It's a forcing function. An elevator gives you no room for "it depends" or "on the other hand." It demands the *net* of all your considerations, already netted. If you find yourself needing the whole elevator ride plus the walk to the office, you haven't compressed — you're narrating the slides.

The elevator test has a sharper cousin on a trading desk: *"if a PM asked you right now, could they put it on?"* That raises the bar from "can you say it briefly" to "is the brief version actually actionable" — does it name an asset, a direction, a size-able structure? A sentence can pass the brevity bar and still fail the action bar ("I'm constructive on risk" is short and useless). The full elevator test we'll build at the end checks both.

### The canonical structure

Every tradeable read fits one structure, and learning it is most of the battle:

> **[claim], because [variant driver], until [invalidation or catalyst].**

Three slots, each answering a different question and each independently testable:

- **[claim]** — *what, specifically, do I believe?* An asset, a direction, and a magnitude. "Long the 2-year, yield to 3.50%." This is the falsifiable bet. It must carry a level, because a level is what lets you size and grade.
- **because [variant driver]** — *why is there money in it?* The reason you differ from what's already priced. Not "because the economy is slowing" (consensus) but "because a September cut is only 60% priced and I read it at 90%" (variant). This clause is where the edge lives; an empty "because" means no edge.
- **until [invalidation or catalyst]** — *what ends the read?* The specific price or dated event that either proves you wrong or resolves the bet. "Until a daily close above 4.10%." This clause is the exit and the clock; it's what makes the view falsifiable and what sets the risk-per-unit you size from.

![The canonical read names a claim, the variant driver behind it, and the invalidation that ends it](/imgs/blogs/from-data-to-a-read-collapsing-everything-into-one-sentence-2.png)

The card above is the template you will fill for every view, with each slot's job spelled out and a filled example along the bottom. Note the failure modes printed in the gray box: a missing [variant] gives you consensus with extra steps, a missing [until] gives you a horoscope, a missing magnitude in [claim] means you can't size. The three slots are not stylistic — they map one-to-one onto the three things a position needs (an edge, an exit, a size). Drop any one and you don't have a smaller view; you have *no* view. This connects directly to the six-part anatomy of a tradeable thesis — the one sentence is that anatomy, compressed: claim and magnitude become [claim], variant perception becomes [because], invalidation and catalyst become [until], and the horizon and expression follow from them. (For the full six-part anatomy the sentence compresses, see [thesis vs forecast: the anatomy of a tradeable view](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/thesis-vs-forecast-the-anatomy-of-a-tradeable-view).)

## The compression process: from six lenses to one sentence

Compression is a process, not a flash of insight, and it has three steps. You run them in order, and each one throws away something so the next can sharpen what remains.

### Step 1 — Run the six lenses and write the one-line read from each

Before you can compress, you need something to compress. You look at the market through each of the six lenses and force *each one* to give you a single line. Not a slide — a line. Macro: "policy is on hold, liquidity neutral, growth soft but not breaking." Fundamental: "earnings revisions have stopped falling and are starting to tick up." Positioning: "funds are underweight, the pain trade is higher." Technical: "holding the 200-day, higher lows." Sentiment: "everyone's still bearish, the narrative is recession." Valuation: "cheap versus its own five-year history." Six one-liners. Already you've done a compression — each lens, which could be a presentation in itself, is now a single sentence. (For how the six lenses fit together as a framework, see [the six lenses: a framework for reading any market](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/the-six-lenses-a-framework-for-reading-any-market).)

### Step 2 — Find the dominant driver

This is the step beginners skip, and it's the one that makes compression possible. The six lenses do *not* get equal weight. At any given moment, one lens is doing most of the work — it is the **dominant driver**, the variable whose movement would change the call more than any other. In the read above, the lenses mostly agree (bullish), but the *reason* they agree, the engine of the move, is positioning: funds are offside and the pain trade is up. That's the dominant driver. The macro and valuation lenses are supporting context; the positioning lens is the cause.

Finding the dominant driver is what lets you write a *because* clause that means something. The because clause is not "because everything looks good" — that's a summary, not a driver. It's "because positioning is offside and a squeeze higher is the path of least resistance." When the lenses *disagree*, the dominant driver is even more important: it's the lens you're choosing to trust over the others, and naming it is how you resolve the conflict honestly rather than pretending the disagreement away. (On what to do when the lenses point opposite directions, see [reconciling conflicting signals: when the lenses disagree](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/reconciling-conflicting-signals-when-the-lenses-disagree).)

How do you tell which lens is dominant? Three tests, run quickly. First, the **sensitivity test**: which lens, if it moved, would change your call the most? If the positioning data flipping from "offside" to "crowded long" would flip you from bullish to flat, while the valuation lens going from "cheap" to "fair" would barely budge you, then positioning is dominant and valuation is context. The lens with the highest derivative of "call" with respect to "lens" is the driver. Second, the **catalyst test**: which lens has a dated event attached that will resolve it soon? A fundamental gap that gets settled by next week's earnings dominates a slow-burning valuation argument with no near-term referee, because the dated lens is the one that will actually move the price inside your horizon. Third, the **surprise test**: which lens reflects something the market hasn't fully absorbed? A lens where you and the consensus agree contributes context but no edge; the dominant *driver* should be a lens where you see something the price doesn't, because that's where the repricing comes from. Run all three and the dominant driver usually announces itself — it's the lens that is simultaneously the most sensitive, the most likely to be resolved by a near catalyst, and the most variant from consensus.

A warning that the dominant-driver step makes vivid: the dominant driver is *not* the lens you find most interesting or have spent the most time on. Analysts routinely make their favourite lens the *because* — the macro specialist makes it macro, the chartist makes it the technical level — regardless of which lens is actually doing the work in *this* setup. The sensitivity test is the antidote: it forces you to ask which lens, if wrong, breaks the trade, and that is rarely the one you enjoyed analysing most. A read whose *because* is your specialty rather than the setup's actual driver is a read built to flatter you, not to make money.

### Step 3 — Collapse the driver into the three-slot sentence

Now you write the sentence. The dominant driver becomes the *because*. The direction-plus-magnitude of the call becomes the *claim*. And the level or event that would say the driver is exhausted or wrong becomes the *until*. The five supporting lenses do not disappear — they're the evidence behind the claim, the reason you trust the magnitude — but they drop out of the *sentence*, because the sentence carries only the decision, not the dossier.

#### Worked example: collapsing a six-lens stock read into a \$20,000 position

Run the full process on a single name. You've analysed a large-cap tech ETF proxy, QQQ, trading at 440. Your six one-liners: macro neutral-to-supportive; fundamental — the street's models still bake in margin compression that the last two prints contradicted; positioning — systematic funds are underweight; technical — holding 430 support with higher lows; sentiment — persistently skeptical; valuation — full but not extreme. Six lenses, leaning bullish.

Now the dominant driver. The thing doing the most work is the **fundamental gap**: the street is modelling margin compression that the recent prints say isn't happening, and the Q3 earnings print is the catalyst that resolves it. That's your variant — you read margins beating where consensus reads them squeezing. Collapse it: *"Long QQQ from 440 to 480, because the street is still modelling margin compression that the last two prints contradicted and Q3 will confirm beats, until a daily close below 430 or a soft Q3 guide says I'm wrong."*

That sentence sizes itself. Risk budget: \$20,000. Entry 440, invalidation 430 — a \$10 stop, about 2.3% of the underlying. On a \$20,000 risk budget, \$20,000 ÷ 2.3% ≈ \$870,000 of QQQ exposure if you traded it linearly — but you'd more likely express it as a defined-risk call spread sized so the premium at risk is the \$20,000, capturing the 440-to-480 move with the loss capped if 430 breaks. Either way, the sentence — not a separate sizing exercise — produced the \$20,000 stake, because the \$10 distance from claim-entry to until-level *is* the risk-per-unit. **The compression didn't summarise the analysis; it manufactured the position.**

## One-sentence templates by view type

The three-slot structure is universal, but the four common *kinds* of view fill the slots differently. Knowing the templates lets you compress faster, because you recognise which kind of read you're forming and reach for its shape.

![Directional, relative-value, event, and mean-reversion reads all fit claim-because-until](/imgs/blogs/from-data-to-a-read-collapsing-everything-into-one-sentence-4.png)

The matrix above lays the four types against the three slots; read it row by row, because each row is a different way to be right.

**Directional.** The simplest: you think an asset goes up or down. *[claim]* is "long/short X to a level," *[variant]* is why you differ from the priced direction, *[until]* is a price stop. Filled: *"Long QQQ to 480, because margins beat the street's compression model, until a close below 430."* The directional read is the default and the most over-produced — it's also the one most likely to be secretly consensus, so the *because* clause has to work hardest here.

**Relative-value.** You have no view on the *market*, only on the *spread* between two things. *[claim]* is "long A, short B," *[variant]* is why the spread is mispriced, *[until]* is a spread level, not a price level. Filled: *"Long banks, short utilities, because the curve is steepening and the spread is too tight for that regime, until the spread widens beyond two standard deviations."* The relative-value read is more robust than the directional one because it's hedged against the thing you have no edge on (the broad market), and its *until* is a spread, so a market-wide selloff that hits both legs doesn't stop you out.

**Event / catalyst.** The read is about a dated event and what it reveals. *[claim]* is a position "into" the event, *[variant]* is your read on the event versus what's priced, *[until]* is the event outcome itself. Filled: *"Long the 2-year into the FOMC, because a cut is only 60% priced and I read it at 90%, until a hawkish hold pushes the yield up 10bp."* Event reads have the cleanest clock — the catalyst date *is* the deadline — and the cleanest grading: the event happens, you're right or wrong, done. (The mechanics of how an event's surprise versus consensus moves price are covered in [consensus, expectations, and priced-in](/blog/trading/event-trading/consensus-expectations-and-priced-in).)

**Mean-reversion.** You think an extreme move is overdone and will snap back. *[claim]* is "fade X at an extreme level," *[variant]* is why the move is noise rather than a regime change, *[until]* is the level holding (which would say it *wasn't* noise). Filled: *"Fade gold at +3 standard deviations, because the spike is flow-driven, not fundamental, until a new high holds for two closes."* Mean-reversion reads are the most dangerous to compress sloppily, because the *until* has to distinguish "temporary overshoot" from "the start of a new trend" — and a missing or vague *until* on a mean-reversion read is how traders short strength all the way up.

Notice what the templates share: in every type, the *until* is the slot that does the risk work, and it's specific to the type — a price for directional, a spread for relative-value, an event outcome for event, a level-holding for mean-reversion. Get the *until* wrong for the type and you'll stop out on the wrong thing.

## What a vague sentence reveals

A vague sentence is the most useful diagnostic you have, because the *shape* of the vagueness tells you exactly what part of the analysis is missing. Far from being a failure to polish prose, it's a precise readout. Here are the three diagnoses.

![A vague read hides a missing variant and a missing exit; the sharp read exposes and fills both](/imgs/blogs/from-data-to-a-read-collapsing-everything-into-one-sentence-3.png)

The before-and-after above traces one vague sentence through the rewrite that exposes its gaps. On the left, *"I'm constructive on tech into year-end"* — and line by line, every slot is empty: the claim is direction-only with no asset or level, there's no stated variant so it might be pure consensus, there's no *until* so nothing could make you flat, you can't size it because there's no entry-to-exit distance, and you can't re-check it because it can never be cleanly wrong. On the right, the same instinct compressed properly: *"Long QQQ from 440 to 480, because the street models margin compression I read as beats, until a close below 430 or a soft Q3 guide"* — every slot filled, sizeable at \$20,000, gradeable by the Q3 print date.

**No variant → the "because" is empty or circular.** If your sentence is "Long tech because I'm bullish tech," the *because* restates the *claim*. That's the readout for *you have no edge* — you haven't located anything you believe that the price doesn't. The fix is not better wording; it's going back to find the variant. If, after looking, you genuinely can't name a gap to consensus, the correct output is *no trade*, and the vague sentence just saved you from putting on a position with no edge. (This is the entire subject of [what's priced in: the question behind every trade](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/whats-priced-in-the-question-behind-every-trade) — the baseline your variant is measured against.)

**No invalidation → the "until" dissolves.** If your sentence ends "...and I'll hold until I think it's over," you have no *until*. That's the readout for *unfalsifiable* — there's no observation that would force you out, so you'll rationalise every adverse move ("just noise") and the position has no risk discipline. The fix is to name a specific price or event.

**No horizon → no instrument.** If the sentence has a claim and a because and an until but you can't say *by when*, you can't choose the expression — a three-day view and a three-year view on the same name need completely different instruments. The vague-on-horizon sentence is the readout for *you haven't decided what kind of trade this is*. The *until* usually fixes this for you: a dated catalyst sets the horizon directly; a price stop implies one through how far the level is and how fast the asset moves.

#### Worked example: a vague vs sharp sentence and the \$ difference in execution

Two analysts hold the *same* underlying opinion — tech grinds higher into year-end — and the only difference is whether they compressed it. **Analyst Vague** writes "I'm constructive on tech into year-end" and puts on \$20,000 of risk in QQQ "to express it." But with no level, no variant, and no until, the execution is a mess: there's no stop, so when QQQ dips from 440 to 432 they don't know if they're wrong; there's no target, so when it rallies to 455 they don't know whether to take profit; there's no variant, so they can't tell whether a strong jobs print helps or hurts the thesis. They end the year roughly flat on the position after whipsawing in and out — having been *directionally correct* and made nothing.

**Analyst Sharp** writes "Long QQQ from 440 to 480, because the street under-models margins and Q3 confirms beats, until a close below 430 or a soft Q3 guide." Same \$20,000. But now the execution is mechanical: the 430 stop caps the loss at a known amount; the Q3 print is the moment of grading; the 480 target is where they trim. QQQ runs to 478 by year-end and they capture roughly the full \$20,000-sized move — call it **+\$18,000** versus Analyst Vague's roughly **\$0**. Identical opinion. **The \$18,000 swing was entirely the compression — Sharp's sentence told the trade what to do every day; Vague's told it nothing.**

## From the sentence to the position

The most important property of a well-compressed read is that it is not just a description of a view — it is a *specification* for a position. The sentence dictates the instrument and the size, mechanically, with no separate step. This is the payoff for the discipline: once you've said it in one sentence, the trade builds itself.

**The sentence dictates the instrument.** The *until* clause tells you the kind of risk you're taking, which tells you the structure. An event read ("until a hawkish hold pushes yields up 10bp") wants an instrument that pays on the event and over the short horizon to it — a near-dated future or option. A directional grind ("until a close below 430," over a quarter) wants a linear or defined-risk structure that doesn't decay before the move plays out — the underlying or a call spread, not a weekly option. A relative-value read ("until the spread widens beyond two sigma") wants the *spread* traded directly — long one leg, short the other — not a directional position in either. The *until* is the instrument-selector. (On matching the instrument to the read, see the forward post [choosing the instrument to express your thesis](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/choosing-the-instrument-to-express-your-thesis).)

**The sentence dictates the size.** This is the cleaner mechanism. The distance from the *claim*'s entry to the *until*'s level is your risk-per-unit. Your risk budget divided by that distance is your position size. No invalidation, no size — which is exactly why the vague sentence that lacks an *until* can never be sized, and why compression is a prerequisite for sizing, not a separate activity.

![The same risk budget maps to wildly different instrument notionals depending on the read's per-unit risk](/imgs/blogs/from-data-to-a-read-collapsing-everything-into-one-sentence-6.png)

The chart above makes this concrete across the four view types from our matrix. Each read carries a small dollar *risk* (the red bars — the amount the sentence's *until* puts at risk) and implies a very different *instrument notional* (the blue bars, on a log scale). The directional QQQ read risks \$20,000 and, off its \$10 stop, implies on the order of \$240,000 of exposure. The event read — long the 2-year into the FOMC — risks only \$15,000 but, because the *until* is a tight 15bp move and the 2-year barely moves per basis point, implies a huge \$1,380,000 of notional to put that risk to work. Same family of risk budgets; radically different position sizes, *each one read straight off the sentence*. The blue bar is not a separate decision — it is the arithmetic consequence of the red risk divided by the *until* distance. (For the underlying sizing logic and why risk-per-trade is the unit, see [position sizing and the Kelly criterion](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/position-sizing-and-kelly-criterion).)

There is a second, subtler way the sentence sets the size — not the mechanical risk-per-unit, but the *risk budget itself*. How much of the book to put behind this read versus the next one should scale with the *width* and *strength* of the because clause. A read where your variant is wide (the market prices 60%, you read 90% — a 30-point gap) and supported by several independent lenses deserves more of the risk budget than a read where the gap is narrow and rests on one thin data point, even if both pass the elevator test. The sentence encodes this too: a strong because clause that names a large, well-evidenced gap is doing the conviction work, and conviction is what scales the dollar risk you're willing to commit. So the sentence sets size twice over: the *until* sets risk-per-unit (the mechanical part), and the *because* sets how much risk budget the read earns (the conviction part). A vague because — "because I'm bullish" — fails at both: it gives you no edge to size into *and* no basis to decide whether this read deserves \$5,000 or \$50,000 of the book.

What the sentence does *not* specify is worth stating plainly, because it's where compression has limits. The sentence fixes the asset, direction, magnitude, variant, exit, and (through the until) the horizon and instrument family. It does *not* fix the precise option strikes, the entry tactics (one clip or scaled in), or the exact contract count — those are execution details that live in the trade-idea note, not the read. The read is the *what* and the *why*; the note is the *how*. Confusing the two is a common error in the other direction: an analyst who tries to cram the strikes and the scaling plan into the sentence has un-compressed it back toward the forty slides. Keep the sentence to the decision; let the one-page note carry the execution. (The next step — expanding the sentence into the full execution spec — is [the trade-idea note: a one-page template](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/the-trade-idea-note-a-one-page-template).)

#### Worked example: a relative-value one-sentence sized to \$25,000 per leg

Take the relative-value read: *"Long banks, short utilities, because the curve is steepening and the bank-vs-utility spread is too tight for that regime, until the spread widens beyond two standard deviations against me."* The sentence specifies a *pair*, so the instrument is two legs, and the size is set per leg by the spread risk, not by either stock's own volatility.

Say the historical spread between the bank ETF and the utility ETF has a one-standard-deviation daily range of about 1.2%, so your two-sigma *until* is roughly a 2.4% adverse move in the spread. You want \$1,200 of risk on the spread per... no — let's size to a clean \$25,000 notional *per leg*, the standard for a balanced pair, and check the risk it implies. \$25,000 long banks, \$25,000 short utilities = \$50,000 gross, \$0 net market exposure (that's the point — you've hedged out the thing you have no view on). If the spread moves the 2.4% to your *until*, the loss on the \$25,000-per-leg structure is about 2.4% × \$25,000 ≈ \$600 of spread P&L — small, because the legs hedge most of the dollar move. The sentence told you everything: *long banks, short utilities* set the two legs; *the spread* set that you size to balanced notional, not directional risk; *two sigma* set the stop. **The relative-value sentence produced a market-neutral \$50,000 gross position whose risk is the spread, not the market — and that structure was implied by the words, not chosen separately.**

## Daily re-compression: does the sentence still hold, word for word?

Here is the property of the one-sentence read that most people miss: it is not a one-time deliverable. It is a daily instrument. Every morning, before you look at the position's P&L or the overnight headlines, you re-read your sentence and ask: *does it still hold, word for word?* This is **re-compression**, and it's the discipline that keeps a stale view from quietly turning into a losing position.

The reason this works is that the sentence has three independently-testable clauses, and each morning exactly one question attaches to each. *Is the [claim] still on track to its target?* *Is the [variant] still unpriced — or has the market caught up and closed my gap?* *Has the [until] been hit?* If all three still hold, you do nothing — you hold. If the *variant* has been priced in (the market figured out what you saw, the gap is gone), you've already collected the edge and you trim toward the exit even though nothing went wrong. If the *until* has tripped, you go flat, no matter how attached you are to the claim. The sentence routes the position to its action.

![Each morning the three clauses are re-checked and the broken clause routes the position to its action](/imgs/blogs/from-data-to-a-read-collapsing-everything-into-one-sentence-7.png)

The loop above is the morning routine. You re-read the same sentence and test the three clauses; whichever one breaks decides what you do — hold if all hold, trim if the variant got priced, go flat if the until tripped. The power of re-compressing the *same* sentence — rather than forming a fresh opinion each day — is that it makes you accountable to your past self. Yesterday's you wrote down exactly what you believed and exactly what would prove it wrong. Today's you, who is tempted to rationalise, must answer to that written sentence rather than to a freshly-rationalised version of it. The sentence is a contract with your former, clearer-thinking self.

There is an important asymmetry between the three clauses, and it's the part of re-compression that takes the most discipline to get right. The *until* breaking is unambiguous: the price hit your level or the catalyst resolved against you, and you go flat — there's no judgment, just execution. The *variant* pricing in is subtler and more dangerous, because it's *good* news that feels like a reason to add. When the market catches up to what you saw, your position is profitable and the temptation is to hold for more. But the *because* clause is now hollow — the gap you were paid to close has closed, and holding past that point is no longer your original read; it's a *new* directional bet with no stated edge. The discipline is to treat "the variant priced in" as a *trim* signal even though you're winning, because the thing that justified the position is gone. The most common way a good read turns into a bad trade is overstaying a *because* that has already played out — winning your way into a position you have no edge in.

Re-compression also catches the most insidious failure mode: the view that drifts. Without a written sentence, a long position slowly mutates — you entered for the margin-beat thesis, but three weeks in you're holding "because the chart looks good" and a month later "because I'm up and don't want to sell." Each drift felt reasonable. The written sentence stops the drift cold: if today's reason for holding isn't *yesterday's sentence*, the view has changed, and a changed view requires a *new* sentence and a fresh sizing decision — not a silent rationalisation of the old position. (The psychology of honouring this — and the execution gap between knowing and doing — is the subject of [trading psychology and the execution gap](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/trading-psychology-and-the-execution-gap).)

The frequency of re-compression should match the horizon, which is one more thing the *until* clause tells you. A three-day event read gets re-compressed every few hours — the clauses can break fast, and the catalyst is imminent. A three-month directional read gets re-compressed daily at the open, but you deliberately ignore intraday wiggles that don't touch the *until*, because the horizon says those are noise. A multi-year regime read gets re-compressed weekly or on data releases, and its clauses are thesis-level facts, not prices, so a 10% drawdown along the way is *not* a broken clause — only a structural change in the regime is. Re-compressing a long-horizon read against short-horizon price moves is how analysts stop themselves out of correct multi-year theses on month-two noise. The horizon sets both the instrument and the re-check cadence; read it off the *until* and re-compress at that frequency, not more.

#### Worked example: re-compression after a CPI surprise turns a \$15,000 long into a flat call

You're holding the event read: *"Long the 2-year into the FOMC, because a September cut is only 60% priced and I read it at 90%, until a hawkish hold or a 2-year close above 4.10%."* Risk: \$15,000, sized off the 15bp distance from your 3.95% entry to the 4.10% *until*, which on the 2-year future is about \$615 of risk per contract (DV01 ≈ \$41/bp), so roughly 24 contracts.

Ten days before the meeting, the CPI print lands hot — core inflation +0.5% month-over-month against a +0.3% expectation. You sit down the next morning and re-compress, clause by clause. *Claim* — still long, fine. *Variant* — this is where it breaks: the hot print just repriced the cut. Fed funds futures now show the September cut at *40%*, not the 60% you started against — the market moved *away* from your 90% read, and worse, the hot data is genuine evidence *against* the cut, so your variant isn't just priced in, it's looking wrong. *Until* — the 2-year jumps to 4.08% on the print, two basis points from your 4.10% line. The sentence no longer holds. The variant has inverted and the until is about to trip.

You don't wait for 4.10% to print to the tick. The re-compression already told you the read is broken — the *because* is no longer true — so you go flat that morning at 4.08%. Your loss is the 13bp adverse move on 24 contracts: 13 × \$41 × 24 ≈ **\$12,800**, a touch inside your \$15,000 budget. Had you *not* re-compressed — had you held the *position* without re-reading the *sentence* — you'd have ridden it through 4.10%, told yourself "the Fed will still cut eventually," and watched the 2-year run to 4.25% as the cut got fully priced out, turning a \$12,800 controlled exit into a \$25,000+ rout. **Re-compression didn't predict the CPI surprise; it gave you a written sentence whose broken clause forced the flat call the same morning the data broke it.**

## Common misconceptions

These four myths are why smart analysts keep producing forty slides and no read. Each one feels like sophistication and is actually avoidance.

### Myth 1 — "The more nuance, the better"

The belief that a more nuanced, caveated, on-the-other-hand view is a *better* view. It is usually the opposite. Past a point, nuance is not depth — it's *indecision wearing the costume of depth*. "I'm bullish, but if the labour data softens then I'm bearish, although positioning could squeeze it either way, and valuation is a wildcard" is not a sophisticated view; it's six views that cancel to zero, dressed up so no one notices there's no call. Real nuance lives in the *evidence* behind the sentence and in the *until* clause (which encodes the conditions under which you'd flip). It does not live in the claim, which must be singular and committed. The test: if your nuance makes the sentence *longer and less decisive*, it's indecision; if it makes the *until* clause *sharper*, it's genuine. The forty-slide analyst mistook the volume of considerations for the quality of the call — but the call is the *net* of the considerations, and the net is one sentence.

### Myth 2 — "I'll know it when I see it"

The belief that you don't need to pre-commit the read in words — you'll recognise the right trade when it appears, intuitively. This is how undisciplined money is lost. "I'll know it when I see it" means you have no written sentence to be accountable to, so you'll see whatever your current emotional state wants to see: a reason to add when you're greedy, a reason to hold when you're hoping, a reason to flip when you're scared. The whole value of the one-sentence read is that it's written *before* the moment of pressure, by a clearer-headed version of you. Intuition is a fine input to the synthesis; it is a terrible substitute for the written verdict. If you can't say it before you see it, you won't say it honestly after.

### Myth 3 — "A price target is a read"

The most common confusion. A bank publishes "S&P year-end target: 5,500" and it's discussed as a view. It's one-third of the *claim* slot with the rest missing — no asset structure, no variant (is 5,500 above or below where the futures already imply?), no *until* (at what level is the target wrong?), no instrument. A target is the easiest fragment of a read to produce, which is why it's the part everyone produces and the *because* and *until* everyone skips. The compression test catches this instantly: a target can't be said in the canonical structure, because it has no *because* and no *until* — it's a number, not a sentence. If your read is just a level, you have a headline, not a view.

### Myth 4 — "Compression loses information"

The objection that collapsing forty slides into one sentence throws away all your hard work. It throws away the *noise*, not the *signal*. The forty slides contained two kinds of content: the few load-bearing facts that determined the call, and the much larger volume of supporting detail, alternative scenarios, and context that *informed* the call but is not the call. Compression keeps the first and drops the second — and that's a feature. The sentence is *denser* than the slides, not thinner: every word is a decision. The work isn't lost; it's *concentrated*. And critically, the dropped detail is still there in your notebook as the evidence behind the sentence — you can always re-expand the *because* into its supporting lenses when someone challenges it. Compression loses the dossier from the *verdict*; it doesn't burn the dossier. An analyst who thinks compression destroys information has confused the *trial* with the *verdict* — and only the verdict is tradeable.

### Myth 5 — "If my sentence is right, I'll make money"

The belief that a well-compressed, correct read guarantees a profit. It does not — and conflating the *read* with the *result* is how analysts mis-grade themselves. The sentence can be entirely correct and the trade can still lose money, because the sentence fixes the *what* and the *why* but not the full *how*: the entry tactics, the precise instrument, the timing of the scale-in. A correct directional read expressed through a decaying short-dated option loses on a slow grind even though the call was right; a correct event read entered too early bleeds carry before the catalyst. The sentence is necessary, not sufficient. What the sentence *does* guarantee is that you'll know *which* part failed — if the trade loses while the sentence held word-for-word to the *until*, the failure was execution, not analysis, and that's a different lesson than "my read was wrong." The compression doesn't promise profit; it promises *attributable* outcomes, which is the only thing that makes you better over a hundred trades. (On why a correct call can still lose — the four ways direction fails to convert to dollars — see [thesis vs forecast: the anatomy of a tradeable view](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/thesis-vs-forecast-the-anatomy-of-a-tradeable-view).)

## How it plays out in real markets

Three scenarios: collapsing a real multi-lens read into one sentence and its position, a vague-to-sharp rewrite under pressure, and a re-compression after a surprise broke the *until*.

### Collapsing a real 2024 rate-cut read into one sentence

Take the late-summer 2024 stretch into the September FOMC — a setup many desks actually traded. You run the six lenses. *Macro*: core inflation cooled for three straight months, unemployment ticked up to 4.3%. *Fundamental* (for rates, the data): the labour market is the variable the Fed said it now weights most. *Positioning*: rates desks are not yet fully positioned for a cut. *Technical*: the 2-year yield broke below its summer range. *Sentiment*: "soft landing" consensus, no panic. *Valuation*: the 2-year at ~3.95% looks high relative to where the policy path implies it should be if cuts come.

The dominant driver is the **gap between the labour data and what futures price**: the softening employment picture has shifted the Fed's reaction function more than the market has priced. Fed funds futures show only about a 60% probability of a September cut; you read it at 90%, because you think the market is underweighting how much the labour data moved the Fed. That gap is the variant. Collapse the whole board: *"Long the 2-year Treasury into September, because a 25bp cut is only 60% priced and I read it at 90% on the labour softening, until a daily 2-year close above 4.10% or a core CPI above 0.4% says the cut is being priced out."* Six lenses, one dominant driver, one sentence — and it's an *event* read, so the instrument is the 2-year future and the clock is the September 18 meeting. (Why the *surprise* relative to consensus, not the level, is what moves the curve: [the surprise, not the level — betas to data surprises](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/the-surprise-not-the-level-betas-to-data-surprises).)

### A vague sentence rewritten sharp, under pressure

Now the room from the opening. The analyst with forty slides is asked "what's the trade, in one sentence?" and freezes. Walk through what should have happened. The slides *contained* a read — they just hadn't compressed it. The compression is mechanical if you've done the work: find the dominant driver across the slides (say it was the positioning data on slide eleven showing funds dangerously offside), make it the *because*, attach the directional call as the *claim* and the offside-resolution level as the *until*. The frozen "well, it depends..." becomes: *"Long the index to 5,400, because systematic funds are the most underweight they've been in two years and the pain trade is up, until a daily close below 5,150 says the squeeze isn't happening."* That's the same forty slides — nothing was added — compressed into a sentence the PM can act on. The analyst didn't need more analysis; they needed to *commit*, and the sentence is the commitment device. The silence in that room wasn't a knowledge gap; it was an un-run compression.

### Re-compression after the 2022 CPI surprises broke a long

Recall the 2022 inflation regime, when CPI prints repeatedly came in hotter than consensus and repriced the entire rates and equity complex. An analyst long duration in mid-2022 might have held the read: *"Long the 10-year, because inflation is peaking and the Fed will pause by year-end, until the 10-year yield closes above 3.25%."* Reasonable in the spring. Then the June 2022 CPI printed 9.1% against an ~8.8% expectation — a hot surprise — and the next morning's re-compression broke the sentence at the *because*: inflation was visibly *not* peaking, the variant was wrong, and the 10-year blew through 3.25% and kept going toward 3.5%. The analyst who re-compressed got out at the *until* with a controlled loss; the analyst who held the *position* without re-reading the *sentence* told themselves "inflation has to peak eventually" and rode the 10-year to 4.2% by October. The lesson is the same one the discipline keeps teaching: the surprise wasn't predictable, but the *written sentence* made the response automatic — a broken *because* and a tripped *until* forced the exit, against every instinct to hold and hope. (On why the data *surprise* drives the move and how the reaction function changes the magnitude, see [the reaction function: why the same number moves differently](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-reaction-function-why-the-same-number-moves-differently).)

## The playbook

Everything above reduces to a template, a test, and a habit. Use the template to *write* the read, the test to *check* it's tradeable, and the habit to *keep* it honest.

### The one-sentence thesis template

Compress your view into exactly this shape. If a slot won't fill, that empty slot is the part of your analysis that isn't done.

```
[ASSET + DIRECTION + MAGNITUDE],
  because [WHERE I DIFFER FROM WHAT IS PRICED],
  until [A PRICE OR DATED CATALYST THAT ENDS IT].
```

Filled for the four view types:

```
Directional:     Long QQQ from 440 to 480,
                 because the street under-models margins and Q3 confirms beats,
                 until a daily close below 430.

Relative-value:  Long banks, short utilities (balanced notional),
                 because the curve steepens and the spread is too tight for that regime,
                 until the spread widens beyond two standard deviations.

Event:           Long the 2-year into the September FOMC,
                 because a cut is only 60% priced and I read it at 90%,
                 until a 2-year close above 4.10% or core CPI above 0.4%.

Mean-reversion:  Fade gold at +3 standard deviations,
                 because the spike is flow-driven, not fundamental,
                 until a new high holds for two daily closes.
```

The discipline: write the sentence *first*, before you size or trade. The sentence is the spec; the position is built from it.

### The elevator test

Before any read counts as tradeable, run these five yes/no checks. Any "no" means you have data, not a view — go compress again.

![Five yes-or-no checks decide whether your one sentence is a real read or a pile of data](/imgs/blogs/from-data-to-a-read-collapsing-everything-into-one-sentence-5.png)

1. **One breath?** Said in a single sentence, with no "and also" smuggling in a second trade.
2. **Names an asset and a direction?** Long or short something specific, with a magnitude — not "I like risk here."
3. **Has a "because" that is variant?** The reason differs from consensus. If everyone already agrees, there's no edge to collect.
4. **Has an "until" that is monitorable?** A price or catalyst you could watch tomorrow — not "until I change my mind."
5. **Could a PM act on it?** Hand it over and the desk could size, stop, and put it on this morning with no follow-up question.

Five yeses and you have a read you can trade today. One "no" tells you precisely which slot is empty — go fill it, or accept that you don't have a view yet and don't put on risk.

### The daily re-compression habit

The sentence is a daily instrument, not a one-time deliverable. Every morning, before P&L and before the news, re-read your sentence and test the three clauses:

1. **Is the [claim] still on track to its target?** If yes, the thesis is working.
2. **Is the [variant] still unpriced?** If the market has caught up and closed your gap, you've collected the edge — trim toward the exit even though nothing "went wrong."
3. **Has the [until] been hit?** If yes, go flat. No exceptions, no rationalising.

If all three hold, you hold. If a clause breaks, the broken clause dictates the action — and because you're answering to *yesterday's written sentence*, not to today's rationalisation, the action is honest. This is the entire loop: compress to form the view, size off the sentence, and re-compress every morning to keep it.

That is the analyst's edge in its most compressed form. The forty slides are the trial. The one sentence is the verdict. And the discipline — the whole discipline — is the willingness to say the verdict out loud, commit it to one falsifiable sentence, and answer to it every morning until the *until* arrives. If you can't say it in one breath, you don't have a read. You have a pile of data, and a pile of data has never once cleared a trade.
