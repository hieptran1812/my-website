> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A forecast is a directional guess with no structure ("S&P to 5,500"); it cannot be sized, stopped, or graded, so it is unfalsifiable bragging. A tradeable **view** has anatomy: a specific claim, the evidence behind it, the variant perception (where you differ from what's priced), a catalyst with a timeline, a defined invalidation, and an expression.
>
> - The six components turn an opinion into an *accountable object* — something you can put \$50,000 behind, stop at a price, and mark on a deadline.
> - Falsifiability is the dividing line. If no fact or price could ever prove you wrong, you do not have a view; you have a horoscope.
> - Horizon is load-bearing: a 3-day view and a 3-year view are graded on completely different things, and matching the expression to the horizon is half the job.
> - The one rule to remember: **if you cannot state what would make you sell, you do not have a position — you have a feeling.**

## Two notes on the same desk

It is a Monday morning on a commodities desk. Two people have written something about oil over the weekend. The first is a market pundit's note, the kind that lands in your inbox and gets quoted on television: *"We're bullish crude here. The fundamentals are tightening and we think oil has a lot of room to run. Stay long."* It sounds confident. It sounds informed. And it is completely useless to anyone who has to actually do something with money.

The second note is from an analyst on the desk. It reads, roughly: *"WTI at \$72. I think it trades to \$85 over the next three months. Why: OPEC+ just announced a deeper-than-expected production cut, US inventories are drawing faster than the five-year average, and shale capex has been flat for two years so there's no quick supply response. The forward curve is only pricing about \$74 by then — so I'm \$11 above consensus, and that gap is the trade. The catalyst is the next OPEC meeting plus the summer draw season, both inside ninety days. I'm wrong if WTI closes below \$68 on a daily basis — that would mean the draws aren't materialising and OPEC discipline is breaking. I'm expressing it with a January \$78/\$90 call spread, sized to risk \$50,000, which is about 1% of the book."*

Read those two again. The first cannot be acted on. You cannot size it — bullish how much? You cannot stop it — at what point are you wrong? You cannot grade it — if oil drifts sideways for six months, was the pundit right or wrong? It will never be settled, which is exactly why pundits speak that way: a forecast that can never be checked can never be embarrassed. The second note is a different kind of object entirely. Every clause maps to a decision. The \$85 sets the upside. The \$68 sets the exit. The ninety days sets the clock. The \$11 gap to the curve says *why there's money in it*. The call spread says *how you collect it*. You could hand that note to a risk manager and they could put the position on this morning.

That is the entire subject of this post. The difference between those two notes is the difference between a **forecast** and a **thesis** — and it is the difference between someone who talks about markets and someone who trades them. A forecast is a point on a chart someone shouted toward. A thesis is a structured, falsifiable, sizeable object with a defined anatomy. This post dissects that anatomy, component by component, and gives you the template and the test to build one yourself.

## Foundations: forecast, prediction, thesis, and view

Before we dissect anything, we need to define our terms precisely, because they get used interchangeably and that sloppiness is where the trouble starts. These are process words, not market mechanisms, so we will build each one from zero.

A **forecast** is a statement about a future value of some variable: "GDP will grow 2.1% next year," "the S&P will be at 5,500 by December," "oil is going to \$100." A forecast is a *number attached to a future date* — or sometimes not even a date, just a direction ("oil is going higher"). Crucially, a bare forecast carries no reasoning, no probability, and no consequence. It is the answer with the work erased. Banks publish year-end index targets every December; the financial press treats them as analysis, but a target with no structure behind it is just a forecast wearing a suit.

A **prediction** is a near-synonym, but in practice it carries a connotation of a *specific binary or near-binary event*: "the Fed will cut in September," "this earnings report will beat." A prediction is the kind of thing that resolves cleanly true or false on a known date. Predictions are more falsifiable than vague forecasts simply because they tend to have a settlement event baked in. But a prediction is still not a view — knowing the Fed will cut tells you nothing about whether that cut is already in the price, which is where the money actually is.

A **thesis** is the structured argument: not just *what* you think will happen, but *why*, *where you differ from the crowd*, *what would force it to resolve*, *what would prove you wrong*, and *how you'd profit if you're right*. A thesis contains a forecast as one of its parts — the claim — but wraps it in the reasoning and the risk structure that make it actionable. The word comes from academic argument: a thesis is a position you defend with evidence and are prepared to have refuted.

A **view** is the working term traders use for a thesis they actually hold and are willing to act on. "What's your view on rates?" is a request for a thesis, not a forecast. A well-formed view *is* a thesis; the words are nearly interchangeable on a desk. We will use **thesis** for the structured object and **view** for the act of holding one. (For why *forming a view at all* is the analyst's real job — not gathering more inputs — see [the analyst's edge: why forming a view is the real job](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/the-analysts-edge-why-forming-a-view-is-the-real-job).)

### The role of time horizon

Every one of these statements is incomplete without a **time horizon** — the window over which the claim is supposed to come true. "Oil is going higher" is meaningless until you say *by when*. Higher by Friday? By year-end? Over the next decade? Those are three completely different claims requiring three completely different trades, and a statement that doesn't specify the horizon has quietly refused to commit to any of them.

Horizon does more than scope the claim. It determines *how the claim is graded*, *what counts as noise versus signal*, and *which instrument you should use to express it*. A 3-day view that the CPI print will spike yields is graded on one event. A 3-year view that we are entering a higher-rate regime is graded on a path that will include many adverse months. Same direction, utterly different objects. We give horizon its own section later because it is the single most underrated component of a view.

### Falsifiability, applied to markets

The philosopher Karl Popper argued that what separates science from pseudo-science is **falsifiability**: a scientific claim is one that could, in principle, be proven wrong by an observation. "All swans are white" is scientific — one black swan refutes it. "Everything happens for a reason" is not — no observation could ever contradict it, so it explains everything and predicts nothing.

The same blade cuts cleanly through market opinions. A claim is tradeable only if it is falsifiable: there must exist some future price or fact that would prove it wrong. "I'm bullish oil" is unfalsifiable — there is no price of oil, on any date, that the pundit would accept as proof they were wrong, because they never committed to a level, a date, or an exit. Like "everything happens for a reason," it is compatible with every outcome, which is exactly why it is worthless. "WTI below \$68 on a daily close means I'm wrong" is falsifiable — there is a specific, observable event that refutes it. That single property — *can this be proven wrong?* — is the dividing line between a horoscope and a view.

![Statements range from unfalsifiable horoscopes to fully falsifiable views with a price, a date, and an exit](/imgs/blogs/thesis-vs-forecast-the-anatomy-of-a-tradeable-view-3.png)

The axis above is the mental model for the whole post. On the far left sit the horoscopes — "I'm bullish oil" — committing to nothing, refutable by nothing. As you move right, the statements acquire structure: a vague clock ("higher this year"), then a level ("to \$85"), and finally a level *and* a date *and* an exit ("\$85 by December, wrong below \$68"). Only the right end of that axis is tradeable. Everything to its left feels like a view but is, on inspection, a statement that has carefully arranged to never be wrong.

### What makes a statement tradeable

Pulling these threads together: a statement is **tradeable** when it can be (1) *sized* — you can put a specific dollar amount of risk behind it; (2) *stopped* — there's a defined point at which you exit because you were wrong; and (3) *graded* — on a known horizon, you can say afterward whether it worked. Sizing requires a level (how far is the target, how far is the stop). Stopping requires an invalidation. Grading requires a horizon. A forecast supplies none of these. A thesis supplies all three. That is the whole game, and the rest of this post is the construction manual.

### Why forecasts are everywhere and theses are rare

If theses are so much more useful, why does the world produce ten thousand forecasts for every one thesis? Because the *incentives* favour forecasts, and understanding that is part of inoculating yourself against producing them. A forecast is cheap to make and impossible to be held to. A pundit who says "I'm bullish oil" and is later asked about a 10% selloff can always reply "I meant longer-term," or "the thesis is intact, just early," or simply move on to the next forecast — there was never a level, a date, or an exit to be measured against. The vagueness is not laziness; it is *self-protection*. An unfalsifiable claim is a claim that can never embarrass its author, and most public market commentary is optimised for the author's reputation, not the listener's P&L.

A thesis, by contrast, is *expensive*. It requires you to state a magnitude you might miss, name a date by which you'll be graded, and write down in advance the price at which you'll admit you were wrong. Every one of those is a way to be publicly, specifically incorrect. The discipline of writing a thesis is precisely the discipline of *accepting accountability you could have avoided*. That is why a written thesis is rare and valuable: it is the analyst voluntarily choosing the harder, more falsifiable, more embarrassing version of their own opinion — because that is the only version that can be sized and that can compound. The first sign of a serious analyst is not the quality of their forecasts; it is that they bother to convert forecasts into theses at all when the world would happily let them get away with the cheaper version.

There is a second, subtler reason theses are rare: a forecast feels *complete* the moment you have the number, while a thesis is obviously unfinished until all six parts are filled. The brain rewards the closure of "S&P to 5,500" — it feels like the analysis is done. The five missing components don't announce themselves; nothing prompts you to ask "but where do I differ, and what would prove me wrong?" The template and the test at the end of this post exist to supply that prompt mechanically, so the missing five-sixths can't hide behind the satisfying completeness of the number.

## The six components of a thesis

A thesis has six load-bearing parts. Drop any one and the structure stops standing: it reverts to a forecast you cannot size, stop, or grade. Each component answers one specific question. We will take them one at a time, and we will keep returning to the same running example — a view on oil and, later, a view on rates — so the parts assemble into a whole.

### Component 1 — The claim: *what, specifically, do I believe will happen?*

The claim is the forecast at the heart of the thesis: the specific, falsifiable statement of what you expect. The discipline here is *specificity*. "Oil higher" is not a claim; "WTI from \$72 to \$85 over three months" is. The claim needs a *direction*, a *magnitude*, and (implicitly, from the horizon) a *deadline*. Without magnitude you can't size; without a deadline you can't grade.

A good claim is also *singular*. "Oil up, the dollar down, and tech to lead" is three claims masquerading as one, and when you try to trade it you'll find the three legs fight each other. State one thing. If you have three views, write three theses and let each stand or fall on its own.

#### Worked example: the cost of a vague claim

Suppose your claim is "oil higher" and you put on a \$50,000 position. Three months later WTI is at \$73.50, up \$1.50 from \$72. Were you right? You were *directionally* right — it went up. But +2% over three months is roughly the cost of carry; you've made almost nothing after financing and you tied up \$50,000 of risk capital to do it. Had your claim been "WTI to \$85," that same \$73.50 is a clear *miss* — you're nowhere near target, the thesis isn't working, and you should be questioning it. The vague claim let a non-result masquerade as a win. The specific claim ("to \$85") would have told you, at \$73.50, that \$1.50 of a needed \$13 move had arrived — about 12% of the way there with two-thirds of the clock gone. **A claim without a magnitude lets you grade yourself a winner for a move that earned nothing.**

### Component 2 — The evidence: *why do I believe it?*

The evidence is the *case* — the lenses that, when you synthesise them, point the same way. For the oil view: OPEC+ cutting supply, inventories drawing below the five-year average, flat shale capex limiting the supply response. Evidence is what separates a thesis from a hunch. Crucially, evidence should be the synthesis of *multiple independent lenses* (supply, demand, positioning, technicals), not one fact repeated three ways. If your three pieces of evidence are "OPEC cut, OPEC is disciplined, and OPEC won't add barrels," that's one fact wearing three hats, and one piece of contrary news kills all three at once.

The evidence also has to be *load-bearing for the magnitude*, not just the direction. Lots of evidence supports "oil up a bit." Far less supports "oil up \$13 in ninety days." Be honest about whether your evidence justifies the size of the claim or just its sign. (On building the evidentiary case rigorously — and tying it to a catalyst — see [structuring a thesis: claim, evidence, and catalyst](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/structuring-a-thesis-claim-evidence-and-catalyst).)

### Component 3 — The variant perception: *where do I differ from what's already priced?*

This is the component that beginners skip and professionals obsess over. **Variant perception** is the gap between what *you* believe and what the *market* has already priced in. It is, quite literally, where the money is. If you believe oil goes to \$85 and the forward curve already prices \$85, you have no edge — you agree with the market, and agreeing with the market pays nothing. The trade exists only because you think \$85 and the curve thinks \$74. That \$11 gap is your variant perception, and it is the engine of the entire position.

A thesis without a variant perception is just consensus with extra steps. You can have flawless evidence and a precise claim, but if the market already shares your view, there's no repricing to capture. The first question a sharp risk manager asks is not "why do you think oil goes up?" but "what do you know or believe that the price doesn't already reflect?" (This is the entire subject of [variant perception: where real edge comes from](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/variant-perception-where-real-edge-comes-from), and it connects directly to [what's priced in — the question behind every trade](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/whats-priced-in-the-question-behind-every-trade). The mechanism by which consensus forms and gets embedded in price is covered in [consensus expectations and priced-in](/blog/trading/event-trading/consensus-expectations-and-priced-in).)

#### Worked example: edge lives in the gap, not the level

Two analysts both forecast WTI at \$85. Analyst A notes the curve already prices \$84 — a \$1 gap. Analyst B notes the curve prices \$74 — an \$11 gap. Both have the *same forecast*, but they have radically different *views*, because a view is a forecast relative to what's priced. Say each puts \$50,000 of risk behind a position that pays \$1 for every \$1 of "edge" that converts. If the curve is right and oil ends at the curve's level, Analyst A loses a small amount (their \$1 of claimed edge was noise) while Analyst B loses the full \$11 of claimed edge — but if *they're* right and oil prints \$85, Analyst B collects roughly eleven times what Analyst A does on the same stake. The forecast was identical; the tradeable view was eleven times larger for B. **Your edge is never the level you forecast — it is the distance between your level and the market's.**

### Component 4 — The catalyst and timeline: *what forces the gap to close, and by when?*

You can be right about the destination and still lose money if there's no force pushing the market there inside your holding period. A **catalyst** is the event or process that converts your variant perception into a price move: an OPEC meeting, an earnings report, a Fed decision, a seasonal inventory draw, a refinancing wall. The **timeline** is when that catalyst fires and how long the repricing should take.

The catalyst answers the most dangerous objection to any thesis: "you may be right, but the market can stay wrong longer than you can stay solvent." If your only answer to "why now?" is "it's cheap," you have a value observation, not a trade — cheap things stay cheap until something makes them un-cheap. The catalyst is that something. The timeline turns the catalyst into a deadline, and the deadline is what lets you grade the view and, just as importantly, what determines the *carry cost* of waiting (more on that below).

It helps to separate two kinds of catalyst, because they demand different expressions. A **scheduled catalyst** is on the calendar: an FOMC date, an earnings release, an OPEC meeting, a CPI print, an index rebalance, a bond maturity. You know exactly when the question gets answered, which means you can match the expiry of an option or the holding period of a position to the date precisely. Scheduled catalysts also tend to be partly *priced* — the market knows they're coming and has positioned for them — so the trade is usually about the *surprise* relative to consensus, not the event itself. An **unscheduled catalyst** is a process you expect to play out without a fixed date: an inventory draw that should accelerate "over the summer," a credit cycle that "turns sometime next year," a margin squeeze that builds until something breaks. Unscheduled catalysts are harder to express because you can't pin the timing, so you need an instrument that doesn't decay against you while you wait — the underlying itself, or a long-dated structure, rather than a near-dated option. The worst trades pair an unscheduled catalyst with a short-dated, decaying expression: you're right about the process, but the clock on the instrument runs out before the process completes. Naming which kind of catalyst you have is the first step to choosing an expression that survives until the catalyst fires.

#### Worked example: a destination with no catalyst bleeds carry

You believe a beaten-down stock at \$40 is worth \$60 — a clean \$20, 50% upside. But there's no catalyst: no earnings re-rating, no buyback, no sector rotation in sight. You buy \$50,000 of it anyway. The stock does nothing for a year, then finally re-rates to \$60 in month fourteen. You were *right* — but you held dead money for fourteen months. If your cost of capital (or the return on the trades you couldn't make because this one tied up the capital) is 8% a year, that idle \$50,000 cost you roughly \$4,700 in opportunity over fourteen months, and you white-knuckled through every drawdown with no idea whether the thesis was working or broken, because nothing was supposed to happen on any particular date. A catalyst with a timeline would have told you: if the re-rating hasn't started by the Q3 print, the thesis is stalling — recycle the capital. **A right destination with no catalyst is a savings account with volatility; the catalyst is what you're actually paid for getting right.**

### Component 5 — The invalidation: *what would prove me wrong?*

The invalidation is the pre-committed answer to "what would change my mind?" — a specific price, level, or fact that, if it occurs, means the thesis is wrong and you exit. For the oil view it was "a daily close below \$68." This is the component that separates an accountable view from an opinion, because it is the one that *costs you something* to state. Naming your invalidation in advance means admitting, in writing, the conditions under which you'll be wrong — and then honouring them when they arrive.

Defining invalidation upfront does two things. First, it makes the view falsifiable in the Popperian sense — without it, the view is a horoscope. Second, it is what lets you *size* the position: the distance from your entry to your invalidation is your risk per unit, and your risk budget divided by that distance is your position size. No invalidation, no sizing — which is why a forecast can never be sized. (Defining it well, and the psychology of honouring it, is the whole of [what would change my mind: defining invalidation upfront](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/what-would-change-my-mind-defining-invalidation-upfront).)

#### Worked example: what the missing invalidation costs

You're long oil from \$72, \$50,000 at risk, no invalidation defined. Oil falls to \$70, then \$68, then \$65. At each step, without a pre-committed line, you tell yourself a fresh story: at \$70 it's "just noise"; at \$68 it's "a buying opportunity, average down"; at \$65 it's "I can't sell *now*, it's too low." You ride it to \$60 and finally capitulate, down \$16.7% on the underlying — call it a \$58,000 realised loss after the averaging-down doubled your size at \$68. Now run the same trade *with* the invalidation: "wrong below \$68 on a daily close." Oil closes at \$67.50; you're out. Your loss is the \$72-to-\$68 move on the original size — roughly \$14,000 — and you've freed the capital and your attention. The invalidation didn't just cap the loss at \$14,000 instead of \$58,000; it removed the moment-by-moment improvisation that turned a wrong trade into a disaster. **The invalidation is the cheapest \$44,000 you'll ever not lose.**

### Component 6 — The expression: *what instrument pays me if I'm right?*

The expression is the actual trade — the instrument, structure, and size you use to convert the view into P&L. The same view can be expressed a dozen ways, and the expression can make a correct view lose money or a marginal view print. Long the future? Long a call spread? Short a put? Long the equity of an oil producer? Each has a different payoff shape, a different cost of carry, a different sensitivity to *timing*, and a different breakeven.

The expression must match the *horizon* and the *shape* of the claim. A view that "oil grinds to \$85 over three months" is badly expressed through a weekly option that decays to zero before the move plays out, and well expressed through a longer-dated call spread or the future itself. A view that "oil spikes on the OPEC headline next Thursday" is the opposite: the weekly option is right and the slow future is wrong. Choosing the expression is where the thesis meets market microstructure — bid/ask, financing, theta, margin — and it is where many technically-correct views go to die.

The expression also encodes a second decision that the claim alone doesn't specify: the *payoff shape* you want. Long the underlying or the future gives you a linear, symmetric payoff — you make and lose dollar-for-dollar with the move, with no decay but full downside to your stop. A long call spread gives you a defined, capped payoff — you cannot lose more than the premium and you cannot make more than the spread width, but you're immune to a whipsaw through your stop, since options don't get margin-called out on a spike. Selling a put expresses the view as "I'll be paid to be wrong slowly" — you collect premium and only lose if the move is large and adverse, which suits a view that's really about *what won't happen* (oil won't crash) rather than *what will*. The same directional claim, expressed three ways, produces three completely different relationships between the move you forecast and the dollars you collect. The art is matching the payoff shape to the *confidence profile* of your view: high conviction in a specific level favours the capped, cheap call spread; high conviction in direction but uncertainty about magnitude favours the linear future; a view that's mostly about a tail not happening favours the premium-selling structure. Pick the shape deliberately; defaulting to "just buy a call because it's exciting" is how the trader in our autopsy lost money on a correct forecast.

#### Worked example: building the \$50,000 oil position

Take the complete oil thesis and size it properly. Claim: WTI \$72 to \$85. Invalidation: daily close below \$68. Risk budget: \$50,000, which is 1% of a \$5,000,000 book. The distance from entry (\$72) to invalidation (\$68) is \$4. WTI futures are 1,000 barrels per contract, so each contract risks \$4,000 to the stop. \$50,000 ÷ \$4,000 ≈ 12 contracts. If you're right and oil reaches \$85, that's \$13 × 1,000 × 12 = \$156,000 of profit — a roughly 3:1 reward-to-risk on the \$50,000 stake, before any leverage cost. Alternatively, express it as a January \$78/\$90 call spread: max risk is the premium paid (say \$3.50 wide × the contracts you buy to cap loss at \$50,000), and the payoff is capped at the \$90 strike but cheaper to hold and immune to a stop-running spike below \$68 that you'd have ridden out anyway. Same view, two expressions, two different risk profiles — and the choice depends on whether you fear a whipsaw through your stop more than you fear paying option premium. **The view sets the direction; the expression sets what actually lands in the P&L.**

## Horizon-matching: a 3-day view and a 3-year view are different objects

We flagged horizon as underrated. Here is why it deserves its own section: the horizon doesn't just scope the claim, it changes *what you grade*, *what counts as being wrong*, and *which expression is even valid*. Get the horizon-expression match wrong and a correct view becomes a losing trade.

![The grading criterion — catalyst, level, or thesis path — is set by the view's stated time horizon](/imgs/blogs/thesis-vs-forecast-the-anatomy-of-a-tradeable-view-4.png)

Read the matrix above row by row. A **3-day view** lives or dies on a single catalyst — one print, one headline. Its level is a tight band (roughly the options-implied expected move, the at-the-money straddle); its invalidation is binary (the stop is hit, you're done); and you grade it on one question: *did the event move the price the way I said?* Noise and signal are nearly the same thing on a 3-day horizon, so the expression must be something that pays on the event before it decays — a short-dated option, a tight futures position.

A **3-month view** lives on a *sequence* of two or three catalysts; its target is a zone rather than a point; it must survive intra-trend wiggles without stopping out; and you grade it on *did the trend play out?* A **3-year view** is a bet on a *regime change* — a structurally higher-rate world, a secular commodity bull. Its "level" is a fair-value range; large drawdowns along the way are *normal and expected*, not invalidations; and you grade it on *did the thesis path hold?* — did the regime actually shift, regardless of the path's bumps. The invalidation for a 3-year view can't be a price wiggle, or you'll be stopped out of a correct thesis on month-two noise; it has to be a *thesis-level* fact ("the Fed pivots back to easing and inflation rolls over for good").

#### Worked example: horizon mismatch costing carry

You have a genuinely good 3-month view: oil grinds from \$72 to \$85 over the quarter. But you express it with *weekly* call options because they're cheap and offer huge leverage. Each week you spend, say, \$2,000 in premium on weeklies that mostly expire worthless because the grind is slow — \$13 over ninety days is about \$1 a week, not enough to overcome the strike distance and theta on a weekly. Over thirteen weeks you bleed roughly \$26,000 in premium chasing a move that, in the underlying, was a clean win. When oil finally hits \$85, your *current* weekly is in the money for maybe \$8,000 — so you're down ~\$18,000 on a thesis that was *completely correct*. Now express the identical view through the future or a single quarterly call spread: you capture the \$13 move with no weekly decay, turning the same correct view into a five-figure gain. **The view was right; the horizon-expression mismatch converted a winner into a loser, and the entire \$44,000 swing was carry, not analysis.**

### A view is not a position

One more distinction the matrix forces into view: a **view** is the belief; a **position** is the view expressed, sized, and live in the book. They are not the same, and conflating them is a classic error. You can hold a view and have *no* position — because the expression is bad, the risk/reward is poor at current prices, or the catalyst is too far off to justify the carry. "I'm bullish oil but I'm not long" is a perfectly coherent, professional sentence: the view is bullish, the position is flat, because the entry isn't there yet.

Equally, you can be *forced out of a position while keeping the view*. Your invalidation hits, you exit — but the *thesis* might still be intact if the invalidation was a stop-loss for risk control rather than a refutation of the argument. A disciplined trader distinguishes "I was wrong" (the thesis broke) from "I was stopped" (the risk limit triggered, the thesis may still be alive). The position is the thing in the book; the view is the thing in your head; and a tradeable view is precisely the bridge that lets you convert one into the other and back.

This is also why the same analyst can hold *several* views simultaneously while running only one or two *positions*. Views are free to hold; positions consume risk budget. A book with eight live views and two positions is not indecisive — it is an analyst who has correctly judged that only two of the eight currently clear the tradeability bar (a present catalyst, an entry near the invalidation, a clean expression) while the other six are watchlist items awaiting their moment. The anatomy is what lets you triage: a view with a catalyst three days out and a tight stop earns capital today; the same view with a catalyst three months out and no entry edge waits. Confusing "I have a view" with "I should have a position" is how books get crowded with marginal trades that each looked reasonable in isolation and collectively bled the account.

### How the anatomy feeds sizing and conviction

The six components don't just make a view *tradeable* — they make it *sizeable in proportion to conviction*, which is the next link in the chain. Two of the components set the size mechanically: the entry-to-invalidation distance gives you risk-per-unit, and your risk budget divided by that distance gives you the position size. But the *risk budget itself* — how much of the book to put behind this view versus the next one — should scale with two other components: the *width of the variant perception* (a bigger gap to consensus, if you're right, is a bigger payoff) and the *strength of the evidence* (more independent confirming lenses justify more conviction). A view where you differ from the market by \$11 and have four independent reads supporting it deserves more of the risk budget than a view where you differ by \$1 with a single thin data point — even though both might pass the six-question test. The anatomy is thus not only a yes/no gate on tradeability; it is the raw material for *how much* to trade. A thesis with a wide, well-evidenced variant perception and a tight invalidation is the ideal object: large potential payoff, small defined risk, high conviction — exactly the asymmetry you want to size up into. The forecast, having none of these parts, cannot tell you whether to risk \$5,000 or \$500,000; it can only tell you which way you're pointing, and which way you're pointing was never the hard part.

## Common misconceptions

These four myths are the ones that keep otherwise-smart people producing forecasts and calling them analysis. Each one feels true and each one is a trap.

### Myth 1 — "A price target is a thesis"

The most common confusion. A bank publishes "year-end S&P target: 5,500" and it gets discussed as a view. It is not. A price target is a *claim* — component one of six — with the other five missing. There's no stated variant perception (is 5,500 above or below where the futures already imply?), no catalyst (what gets us there?), no invalidation (at what level is the target wrong?), and no expression (how would you even trade an index point estimate?). A target is the easiest 1/6th of a thesis to produce, which is exactly why it's the part everyone produces and the five hard parts everyone skips. The fix: every time you see or write a target, ask the other five questions. If you can't answer them, you have a headline, not a view.

### Myth 2 — "More precise is better"

Beginners think precision signals rigour: "WTI to \$84.37" sounds more analytical than "WTI to the mid-80s." It is the opposite. False precision is a tell that the analyst is confusing the *output of a model* with the *resolution of their knowledge*. Markets are not knowable to the cent; a thesis that claims \$84.37 is either lying about its confidence or has mistaken arithmetic for insight. The right resolution is the one your evidence actually supports — usually a *zone* (\$83–\$87), with the precision concentrated where it matters: the invalidation (which must be a specific, monitorable level) and the size (which must be an exact dollar amount). Be vague where you're uncertain (the target) and precise where you act (the stop and the size). Precision in the wrong place is a costume, not a virtue.

### Myth 3 — "If it went up, I was right"

This is the unfalsifiability trap restated as self-grading. If your view was "oil up" and oil went up a dollar, you bank the win and your track record looks great. But a view graded only on direction is a view that can almost never lose — markets go up *or* down, so a 50/50 directional guess is "right" half the time by coin flip, and a permabull is "right" in every bull market while quietly destroyed by the magnitude and timing they never specified. Being right requires hitting the *magnitude* within the *horizon* and *making money on the expression*. The "right forecast, lost money" trade (next section) is the proof: you can be directionally correct and still lose, which means direction alone was never the thing you were grading. The fix is built into the anatomy — a specific claim and a defined horizon make "right" mean something a coin flip can't fake. (On why the simple win-rate scoreboard is a liar, see [expectancy: why win-rate lies](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/expectancy-why-win-rate-lies).)

### Myth 4 — "Horizon doesn't matter, I'll just hold"

The belief that you can form a view without committing to a horizon and "let the trade work." But as the horizon-matching section showed, the horizon determines what counts as noise, what counts as invalidation, and which expression is valid. A view with no horizon quietly defaults to "whenever it's convenient to declare victory" — which is unfalsifiable bragging by another route. Worse, "I'll just hold" usually means holding a *short-horizon expression* (a near-dated option, a leveraged position with carry) on a *long-horizon view*, which bleeds you out before the thesis plays. Commit to the horizon up front; it is not a detail, it is the spine that the catalyst, invalidation, and expression all hang from.

## How it plays out in real markets

Two worked scenarios. First, we turn a vague forecast into a full thesis around a real macro setup. Second, we dissect a forecast that "came true" and still lost money — the most instructive trade in this entire post.

### From "I think rates are going down" to a tradeable view

Take a setup like the late-summer 2024 stretch into the September FOMC. The starting opinion is the kind of thing you hear constantly: *"I think rates are going down."* That is a forecast — direction only, no magnitude, no horizon, no edge, no exit. Untradeable. Let's run it through the six components and watch it become a position.

**Claim.** Sharpen the direction into a specific, falsifiable statement: *the Fed cuts 25bp at the September meeting, and the 2-year Treasury yield falls roughly 25bp to around 3.50% over the following month.* Now there's a level and a deadline.

**Evidence.** Synthesise the lenses: core CPI has cooled for three consecutive months; the unemployment rate has ticked up to 4.3%, triggering recession-indicator chatter; the Fed's own Dot Plot signals cuts ahead; and Fed speakers have turned audibly dovish. Four independent reads, all pointing the same way — not one fact repeated.

**Variant perception.** Here's the edge. Fed funds futures price only about a 60% probability of a cut at that meeting. You think it's closer to 90% — you believe the market is underweighting how much the softening labour data has shifted the Fed's reaction function. *That gap — your 90% versus the market's 60% — is the trade.* If the market already priced 90%, there'd be nothing to capture. (The mechanism by which the same data moves the curve more when positioning is offside is the surprise, not the level — see [the surprise, not the level: betas to data surprises](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/the-surprise-not-the-level-betas-to-data-surprises).)

**Catalyst and timeline.** The catalyst is concrete and dated: the August CPI print (about ten days before the meeting) and the September 18 FOMC decision itself. The timeline is tight — the whole thing resolves within about six weeks. You're not hoping rates "drift lower someday"; you have two scheduled events that force the question.

**Invalidation.** Pre-commit to what proves you wrong: *if August core CPI prints above 0.4% month-over-month, or the 2-year yield closes above 4.10%, the cut is being priced out and the thesis is broken — exit.* That's a specific, monitorable line, and the distance from your entry to it is what sizes the trade.

**Expression.** Long 2-year Treasury futures, sized so that the move to your 4.10% invalidation costs about \$50,000 — roughly 1% of a \$5,000,000 book. The 2-year is the cleanest expression because it's the maturity most sensitive to the near-term policy path you're betting on; a 10-year would dilute your view with term-premium and growth noise you have no edge on.

![The same six components, filled with a real rate-cut view, stack into one accountable tradeable object](/imgs/blogs/thesis-vs-forecast-the-anatomy-of-a-tradeable-view-7.png)

That stack is the whole point. The starting opinion — "rates are going down" — was a feeling. The finished object is a thing you can hand to a risk desk: a sized position with a stop, a clock, and a written reason that names exactly where you differ from the market and exactly what would prove you wrong. Notice that nothing in the construction required predicting the *unknowable* better than anyone else. It required *structuring* a knowable disagreement (90% vs 60%) into something you could size and grade. That is the analyst's edge — not clairvoyance, but the discipline to turn a vague directional feeling into a falsifiable, sizeable view.

#### Worked example: the rate view, sized and graded

The 2-year yield is around 3.95% at entry; your invalidation is a close above 4.10% — 15bp of room. On 2-year Treasury futures, a 1bp move is roughly \$41 per contract (DV01 ≈ \$41 on the \$200,000-notional contract). So 15bp of adverse move ≈ \$615 of risk per contract. \$50,000 ÷ \$615 ≈ 81 contracts. If you're right and the yield falls the ~45bp from 3.95% to 3.50%, that's 45 × \$41 × 81 ≈ \$149,000 of profit — close to 3:1 on the \$50,000 stake. And the grading is unambiguous: by mid-October you'll know whether the Fed cut, whether the 2-year hit 3.50%, and whether the futures-implied odds repriced toward your 90%. There is no version of "well, rates sort of drifted, so I guess I was right." **The thesis made itself accountable: a known stake, a known stop, a known deadline, and a known scoreboard.**

### The forecast that came true and still lost money

Now the most instructive case. A trader in early 2023 forecasts, correctly, that crude oil will rise meaningfully over the year — call it +18% on the underlying. The *forecast* is right. The *trade* loses money. Here is how, and it is a story about everything other than direction.

The trader expresses the view through near-dated, out-of-the-money call options — cheap, high-leverage, exciting. They enter early, in the depths of a Q1 drawdown when oil is actually falling, because "it's going up this year and I want to be positioned." Three things then conspire. First, **timing**: oil drops further before it rises, and the early entry means the position is deep underwater for months while the near-dated options decay. Second, **expression**: the out-of-the-money calls they keep rolling expire worthless again and again, because the move, when it comes, is a grind, not a spike — each option dies before the underlying reaches the strike. Third, **friction**: the constant rolling racks up bid/ask spread and commissions. By the time oil is up 18% on the year, the trader is *down* on the trade. The forecast came true. The account shrank.

![Decomposition of a correct +18 percent oil forecast into the timing, expression, and friction losses that turned it into a realized loss](/imgs/blogs/thesis-vs-forecast-the-anatomy-of-a-tradeable-view-5.png)

The decomposition above is the autopsy. A clean expression of the *correct* directional view — just being long the underlying or a long-dated call spread sized to the same stake — would have captured roughly +\$9,000 on a \$50,000 commitment. Instead, timing decay (entering early into a drawdown, holding through theta) erased about \$6,500; the wrong expression (out-of-the-money near-dated calls expiring worthless) cost another \$8,000; and roll and friction took a further \$1,700. The directional gain that *existed in the market* was real and positive. Every dollar that vanished, vanished in the four components that aren't direction: the timing, the expression, the horizon match, and the carry. The trader had a correct forecast and no thesis — no defined horizon to dictate the right instrument, no invalidation to stop the bleed, no sizing discipline. Direction was the only thing they got right, and direction, it turns out, was worth +\$9,000 that the missing five components gave back twice over.

Generalise the lesson, because this trade is not a freak — it is the *default* outcome of trading a forecast instead of a thesis. There are four distinct ways a correct direction fails to convert into dollars, and a forecast addresses none of them. **Timing**: you can be right about the destination and wrong about the path, entering before the move and getting shaken or decayed out before it arrives — the horizon and the catalyst exist precisely to time the entry. **Expression**: you can pick an instrument whose payoff shape or decay profile is hostile to your move — a near-dated option against a slow grind — which only the horizon-matched expression fixes. **Sizing**: you can be right and still get carried out by a position too large to survive the adverse path, or too small to matter when it works — which the entry-to-invalidation distance sizes correctly. **Carry**: you can be right but pay so much in financing, roll, and spread while you wait that the move, when it comes, doesn't cover the toll — which a catalyst with a near deadline minimises. A forecast hands you a 50%-ish shot at the *easy* variable, direction, and leaves all four *hard* variables to chance. A thesis nails down all four in advance. That is why "right forecast, lost money" is not a tragedy of bad luck; it is the predictable result of trading the one part of the problem that was never the hard part.

#### Worked example: same forecast, two outcomes

Hold the forecast fixed at "+18% on crude this year" and vary only the thesis structure, each on a \$50,000 stake. **Trader A (forecast only):** near-dated OTM calls, early entry, constant rolling. Realised P&L ≈ **−\$7,200**, as decomposed above. **Trader B (full thesis):** same direction, but a stated 12-month horizon dictates a long-dated (January) call spread or simply the future; a defined invalidation (a sustained break of the year's opening range) prevents the panicked early-drawdown additions; and the size is set once. Trader B captures the bulk of the +18% — roughly **+\$9,000** clean, less modest spread cost, call it **+\$8,300**. Identical forecast. A **\$15,500 swing** in realised P&L, entirely attributable to the five components beyond direction. **The forecast was the cheap, shared part; the thesis structure was the entire difference between losing money and making it.**

## The playbook

Everything above reduces to a template and a test. Use the template to *build* a view; use the test to *check* whether what you've built is tradeable yet. Run both before any position goes on.

### The thesis-anatomy template (fill in the blanks)

Write your view as these six lines. If you can't fill a line, that's the line that isn't ready — and an unready thesis is a forecast in disguise.

```
CLAIM:        I expect ________ to move from ____ to ____ (direction + magnitude).
HORIZON:      over the next ________ (days / weeks / months / years).
EVIDENCE:     because (1) ________, (2) ________, (3) ________  (independent lenses).
VARIANT:      the market currently prices ________; I differ because ________.
              (the gap between those two is the edge)
CATALYST:     the gap closes when ________ happens, expected by ________ (a date).
INVALIDATION: I am wrong if ________ (a specific price / level / fact). I exit then.
EXPRESSION:   I express this via ________ , risking $______, sized off the
              entry-to-invalidation distance (= risk budget / per-unit risk).
```

Filled in for the rate view, that becomes: *Claim — 2-year yield from 3.95% to 3.50%. Horizon — six weeks. Evidence — cooling core CPI, rising unemployment, dovish Dots, dovish speakers. Variant — futures price 60% odds of a cut; I price 90%. Catalyst — the Aug CPI and the Sep 18 FOMC. Invalidation — a 2-year close above 4.10% or core CPI above 0.4% m/m. Expression — long 2-year futures, \$50,000 of risk, ~81 contracts.* Every blank filled; every blank an accountable commitment.

### The 60-second tradeability test

Before you size anything, run these six yes/no questions. They map one-to-one onto the anatomy. If *any* answer is no, you do not yet have a position — you have a forecast that needs work or a pass.

![Six yes-or-no questions decide whether a view can be sized, stopped, and graded today](/imgs/blogs/thesis-vs-forecast-the-anatomy-of-a-tradeable-view-6.png)

1. **Is the claim specific?** A direction *and* a magnitude (a level), not just "higher."
2. **Do I differ from what's priced?** Can I name the consensus and state my gap to it? (No gap, no edge.)
3. **Is there a catalyst and a deadline?** A dated event that forces the gap to close — not "eventually."
4. **Have I defined invalidation?** A specific price or fact that proves me wrong and triggers an exit.
5. **Can I express it cleanly?** An instrument whose horizon matches my view's horizon.
6. **Can I size and grade it?** A dollar stake the stop defines, and a date by which I'll mark it.

Six yeses and you have a position you can put on this morning. One no, and you've found exactly which component is missing — go fill it, or admit you have a forecast and don't trade it. The discipline is not to *always* get six yeses; it's to *never confuse* a four-yes forecast for a six-yes view. A four-yes idea isn't a failure; it's a watchlist item with two pieces of homework left. The mistake is putting risk behind it as if the homework were done.

![A forecast states a number; a thesis states a number, a gap, a clock, and an exit](/imgs/blogs/thesis-vs-forecast-the-anatomy-of-a-tradeable-view-2.png)

The before-and-after above is the whole post on one page. On the left, a forecast: a claim and nothing else, every other component blank, untradeable by construction. On the right, the same instinct built into a thesis: a claim with a magnitude, a stated edge against the curve, a dated catalyst, a hard invalidation, and a sized expression. Both started as "I'm bullish oil." Only the right-hand version can be put on, stopped, and graded. The pundit and the analyst from the opening had the same opinion; the analyst just did the five-sixths of the work that makes an opinion into a trade.

That work is the analyst's edge. Not the forecast — the forecast is the cheap, shared part that everyone has. The edge is the structure: the variant perception that locates the money, the catalyst that schedules it, the invalidation that bounds the loss, the horizon that picks the instrument, and the sizing that makes it survivable. Build the anatomy, run the test, and you stop producing horoscopes and start holding positions.

There is a compounding benefit that's easy to miss. A forecast, because it can never be cleanly graded, can never teach you anything — six months later you can't say whether it worked or why, so the next forecast is no better than the last. A thesis is the opposite: because it commits to a magnitude, a deadline, and an invalidation, it *settles*, and a settled view is a data point. Over a hundred theses you can ask which component most often broke — was it usually the variant perception (you weren't actually differing from the market), the catalyst (you were early), the expression (you kept the right view in the wrong instrument)? That diagnostic is impossible with forecasts and routine with theses. The anatomy is therefore not just a gate for *this* trade; it is the unit of learning that makes you a better analyst over a career. You cannot improve at a craft whose outputs you cannot grade, and the only market outputs you can grade are theses. The spine of this whole series — *what's priced in versus what I believe, and what would change my mind* — is exactly the set of questions a thesis answers and a forecast dodges.

So here is the one habit that forces all of it: before any trade, write down what would make you sell. If you can't, you don't have a position — you have a feeling, and feelings don't clear.
