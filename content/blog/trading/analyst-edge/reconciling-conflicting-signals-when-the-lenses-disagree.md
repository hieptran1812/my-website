> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The hard part of synthesis is not when the lenses agree; it is when they disagree. Macro says sell, the chart says buy, positioning says the crowd is already short. Amateurs cherry-pick the lens that confirms what they want. Pros resolve the conflict on purpose.
>
> - **Classify the conflict first.** A *noise conflict* is two lenses acting on different horizons — both can be right. A *regime conflict* is the world changing — one lens is about to be wrong. Treat them completely differently.
> - **Match the lens to your holding period.** A bearish one-year macro view and a bullish one-week technical view are not actually in conflict. Resolve most "conflicts" by asking which lens governs *your* horizon.
> - **Find the dominant driver.** One question: "what single thing decides this trade?" The lens that owns that thing leads; the others are context.
> - **Sometimes disagreement IS the signal.** Price up while breadth, momentum, and positioning roll over is the anatomy of a turn — fade it, small, with defined risk.
> - **The one rule:** when you genuinely cannot resolve the conflict, size *down* or stand aside. Forcing a full-size trade through unresolved conflict is paying full variance for a coin-flip edge. Standing aside is a position.

You are long a stock, and you are long for a good reason. The fundamentals are excellent — revenue accelerating, margins expanding, a clean balance sheet, a product cycle that has another two years to run. The fundamental lens is flashing green and you believe it. But this morning two other lenses lit up red. The positioning data came out and the stock is now one of the most crowded longs in its sector — every fast-money fund owns it, retail flows are euphoric, and the put/call ratio says nobody is hedged. And the chart, which had been grinding higher for months, just broke its trendline on heavy volume for the first time since the move began.

Three lenses. Two of them are telling you to sell, or at least to get smaller. One of them — the one that got you into the trade, the one you have the most conviction in — is telling you to hold. What do you do?

The instinct, and it is a powerful one, is to reach for the lens you already trust and let it outvote the others. "The fundamentals are what matter in the end; positioning and charts are noise." Or maybe you average it out — two-to-one bearish, so you trim a third and call it disciplined. Both of those answers are wrong, or rather, both of them are *guesses dressed up as process*. The right answer is not "go with your favorite," and it is not "average them." The right answer is a specific procedure that tells you *which kind* of conflict you are looking at, *which lens* gets to lead given your horizon, and *whether* the conflict can be resolved at all — because sometimes the honest answer is that it can't, and that has its own correct response.

![Decision tree branching from lenses disagree through noise conflict and regime conflict to pick lead lens, fade crowd, or cut size and stand aside](/imgs/blogs/reconciling-conflicting-signals-when-the-lenses-disagree-1.png)

This post is the synthesis step at its hardest. In [the six lenses framework](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/the-six-lenses-a-framework-for-reading-any-market) we built the habit of reading any market through six independent views — macro, fundamental, technical, valuation, positioning, and sentiment. Most of the time those lenses point roughly the same way and synthesis is easy. The skill that actually pays is what you do on the days they *don't*. That is the day this post is about.

## Foundations: what a signal conflict actually is

Let us define the terms precisely, because the whole procedure depends on the distinctions.

A **lens** is one independent way of reading a market — a self-contained method that produces a directional read (bullish, bearish, or neutral) and a confidence. Macro reads the growth-and-liquidity backdrop. Fundamental reads the cash flows and the business. Technical reads price and trend. Valuation reads how much you pay for those cash flows. Positioning reads who already owns the trade and how heavily. Sentiment reads the mood and the narrative. Each lens is "independent" in the sense that it draws on a different data source and a different mechanism — and that independence is exactly why they can disagree.

A **signal conflict** is the state where two or more of those lenses point in opposite directions at the same time. Macro bearish, technical bullish. Fundamental bullish, positioning bearish. Valuation says expensive, momentum says higher. A conflict is not a malfunction. It is the *normal* state of markets at most interesting moments, because the thing that makes a moment interesting is precisely that the forces are not yet aligned.

Here is the foundational claim, and everything downstream rests on it: **conflict happens because the lenses act on different horizons and through different mechanisms.** A macro recession call is a statement about the next twelve months. A technical oversold bounce is a statement about the next ten days. A valuation read is a statement about the next several years. A positioning read is a statement about the next few weeks of forced flow. When a one-year bearish view and a two-week bullish view sit in the same row of your notebook, they *look* like a contradiction — but they are answers to different questions. They are not actually fighting. The reader who treats them as a contradiction is the reader who has confused the horizon of the lens with the horizon of the trade.

### Why confirmation bias makes this lethal

The reason conflict resolution is hard has very little to do with the market and almost everything to do with you. When the lenses disagree, you are handed a menu of conclusions, and human beings reliably pick the item from that menu that confirms what they already want to be true. You are long; the fundamental lens says hold; so you find the fundamental lens "most reliable" *today*, having found it irrelevant last week when it disagreed with a trade you liked for technical reasons. This is **confirmation bias**, and signal conflict is its natural habitat, because conflict is exactly the situation that gives you the raw material — multiple valid-looking lenses — to construct whatever conclusion you had already decided on.

The tell is inconsistency across time. If you find yourself trusting the macro lens when it agrees with your position and dismissing it as "long-term, doesn't matter for this trade" when it disagrees, you are not weighting lenses; you are rationalizing a position. The entire value of a *process* for conflict resolution is that it commits you to a rule before you know which way the rule points — so that you cannot quietly let your favorite lens win every argument it happens to be in.

### Noise conflict versus regime conflict

The single most important distinction in this whole post is the kind of conflict you are facing, because the two kinds demand opposite responses.

A **noise conflict** is a disagreement that exists only because the lenses are measuring different horizons or different timeframes — and *both readings are correct*. The macro lens is right that the economy is slowing over the next year. The technical lens is right that the asset is oversold and due a bounce over the next two weeks. There is no contradiction to resolve; there is only a horizon to match. You resolve a noise conflict by figuring out which lens governs *your* holding period and acting on that one, while filing the others as context for a different trade.

A **regime conflict** is a disagreement that exists because the world is in the process of *changing* — and one of the lenses is about to be proven wrong. The macro backdrop has shifted (a central bank pivoted, a war started, a credit event hit) but one of your lenses is still reading the old regime. Here both readings cannot be right. One of them is stale. The job is not to match horizons; the job is to figure out which lens is reading the new world and which is reading the old one, and to weight the trade toward the one that is current. A regime conflict is dangerous precisely because the stale lens often looks the most confident — trends and fundamentals have inertia, and they keep flashing green right up until the regime that supported them is gone.

The first fork in the decision tree, then, is always the same question: *is this noise (different horizons, both right) or regime (the world is changing, one is wrong)?* Get that wrong and every step after it is wrong too.

## The conflict-resolution decision tree

Here is the full procedure, the tree in the cover figure rendered as steps you run in order. Each step either resolves the conflict or hands it to the next step.

**Step 1 — Classify: noise or regime?** Ask whether the disagreeing lenses are simply on different clocks (noise) or whether something has structurally changed that makes one lens stale (regime). The diagnostic for regime: has a *driver* of one of the lenses actually moved? If the macro lens turned bearish because the yield curve inverted and credit spreads are widening, that is a real driver change — regime. If the macro lens is "bearish" only because it has been bearish for a year while price kept rising, that is not a new driver; that is a stale lens and the conflict is noise (or worse, the macro lens is simply wrong on this horizon).

**Step 2 — Match the horizon.** If it is a noise conflict, the resolution is almost mechanical: which lens governs the horizon of the trade you are actually putting on? A swing trader held for two weeks lets the technical lens lead and treats the one-year macro call as a reason to keep the position small and the stop tight, not as a reason to skip the trade. A position investor held for two years lets the macro and valuation lenses lead and treats the two-week oversold bounce as noise. The horizon of the trade selects the lens. Most "conflicts" dissolve completely at this step.

**Step 3 — Find the dominant driver.** If matching the horizon did not resolve it — because the lenses genuinely disagree *on your horizon* — then ask the dominant-driver question: "what single thing decides whether this trade works?" There is almost always one. For a stock into earnings, the dominant driver is the print versus expectations. For a rate-sensitive trade into an FOMC, it is the policy surprise. For a crowded momentum name, it is whether the marginal buyer is exhausted. Whichever lens reads that one thing best is the **lead lens**; the others become context that sizes and frames the trade rather than deciding its direction.

**Step 4 — Resolve, or admit you can't.** With the dominant driver identified, you land in one of three places:

- **Pick the lead lens** and trade its direction, sized for the fact that other lenses disagree (smaller than full conviction, because dissent is information).
- **Fade the crowd** — if the dominant driver turns out to be *positioning*, and the conflict is "good fundamentals but euphoric, exhausted crowd," the lead lens is positioning and the trade is to fade the consensus, not to follow the fundamentals.
- **Cut size or stand aside** — if you cannot honestly say which lens leads, the conflict is unresolved, and the correct response is to size *down* hard or to take no position at all. An unresolved conflict is the market telling you it does not have a tradeable edge for you right now.

The rest of the post is each of these steps in depth, with the dollar math that makes them real.

## Horizon reconciliation: most conflicts are not conflicts

Start here because this step alone dissolves the majority of the disagreements that torment people. The error underneath most "conflicting signals" panic is comparing two lenses without first asking what horizon each one is speaking about.

![Timeline of one asset showing a bullish one-week technical read near now and a bearish one-year macro read at the far end, both valid on their own clocks](/imgs/blogs/reconciling-conflicting-signals-when-the-lenses-disagree-3.png)

The figure shows a single asset on a single timeline. Near "now," the technical lens reads bullish: the asset is oversold, sentiment is washed out, and a mean-reversion bounce is the highest-probability path over the next one to two weeks. Far out at "+1 year," the macro lens reads bearish: the economy is decelerating, earnings estimates are going to be cut, and the most likely path over the next twelve months is lower. A reader who stacks "technical: bullish" next to "macro: bearish" in a single column declares a conflict and freezes. But there is no conflict on the page. There are two correct statements about two different time windows. The asset can absolutely rally 8% over two weeks *and* be 20% lower in a year. Both lenses are right. They are answering different questions.

The resolution is to **match the lens to your holding period** and let that lens lead, demoting the others to context:

- If you are putting on a **two-week swing trade**, the technical lens leads. You buy the oversold bounce. The bearish one-year macro call does not veto the trade; it *frames* it — it is the reason you keep the size modest, take profit at the technical target rather than getting greedy, and do not marry the position. Macro is your context, not your signal.
- If you are building a **two-year core position**, the macro and valuation lenses lead. The two-week oversold bounce is irrelevant noise; you would not even put it in your notebook except as a possible *entry timing* tool. You might use the technical bounce to get a better entry price on a position you are going to hold through the macro storm, but it does not change the direction of the trade.

The lens does not change; your horizon changes which lens is in charge.

#### Worked example: a \$20,000 position resolved by holding period

You have \$20,000 you want to put to work in an index ETF. Your two lenses conflict. The **macro lens** is bearish: leading indicators are rolling over, the curve is inverted, and your honest twelve-month base case is the index 15% lower. The **technical lens** is bullish: the index just put in a higher low on a positive momentum divergence after a 12% pullback, and the two-week setup points to a 6% bounce back to the declining 50-day average.

The amateur move is to call this a conflict and either (a) skip it entirely — "too murky" — or (b) trim it to a confused half-position with no plan, exposed to both the bounce and the macro downtrend with no clear horizon. Both leave money or discipline on the table.

The process move is to pick a horizon and let its lens lead. Say you decide this is a **two-week tactical trade**. The technical lens leads. You buy \$20,000 of the ETF with a defined plan: target +6% (≈ +\$1,200), stop −3% (≈ −\$600), exit in two weeks regardless. The macro lens does not veto the trade; it *constrains* it — it is why your target is the 50-day average and not "new highs," why your time stop is two weeks and not two months, and why you will not let a winner turn into a "long-term hold." If the bounce plays out you make ≈ \$1,200 on a clean two-week trade and you are flat before the macro thesis has any say. If it fails you lose ≈ \$600 and you are out. The macro bear and the technical bull never actually fought; you simply chose which clock you were trading on.

The takeaway: most "conflicting signals" are two correct readings on different horizons, and the fix is not to average them — it is to pick your horizon and let the lens that owns that horizon run the trade while the others set the guardrails.

## The dominant-driver test: which lens actually decides this?

Horizon-matching resolves noise conflicts. But some conflicts survive it: the lenses genuinely disagree *on the same horizon*. The macro lens and the positioning lens both speak to your three-month swing, and they point opposite ways. Now you need a tiebreaker that is not "my favorite lens." That tiebreaker is the dominant-driver test.

The dominant-driver test is one question, asked honestly: **"What single thing decides whether this trade works or fails?"** Force yourself to name *one* variable. Not "a combination of factors" — one. The lens that reads that variable best becomes the lead lens, and the rest become supporting context. This works because at any given moment, for any given trade, there usually is one variable that dominates the outcome, and the other lenses are second-order to it.

A few worked identifications, so the question becomes concrete:

- **A biotech into a trial readout.** Fundamentals, valuation, macro, positioning all have views. But the single thing that decides the trade is *the trial result*, which none of the lenses can read in advance — so the dominant driver is binary and unknowable, which itself is the answer: this is a coin flip, size it like a coin flip or skip it.
- **A bank stock during a credit-spread blowout.** The fundamental lens may love the bank's deposit franchise and cheap valuation. But the single thing that decides the trade over the next month is *whether credit spreads keep widening* — a macro/regime variable. Macro leads; the cheap valuation is a value trap until spreads stop widening.
- **A crowded momentum tech name after a great print.** Fundamentals are excellent and the print confirmed them. But the single thing that decides the *next move* is whether there is a marginal buyer left, or whether everyone who wants it already owns it. That is a positioning question. Positioning leads, and it may say sell into strength even though fundamentals are pristine.

Notice what the test does: it stops you from weighting lenses by how much you *like* them and forces you to weight them by *relevance to the one variable that matters now*. Your favorite lens leads only when it happens to read the dominant driver. The rest of the time it sits down.

There is a second-order subtlety that separates people who run the test mechanically from people who run it well: **the dominant driver changes over the life of a trade.** The variable that decides whether you should *enter* is rarely the variable that decides whether you should *exit*. Entering a beaten-down quality name, the dominant driver might be valuation and seller exhaustion — a positioning-and-value question. Six months later, after the rebound, the dominant driver of whether you *hold or sell* has shifted to whether the macro recovery that justified the rerating is actually arriving — a macro question. The lead lens at entry is not necessarily the lead lens at exit. This is why a position you entered correctly can become a position you are holding for the wrong reason: the driver moved and you did not re-run the test. Re-running the dominant-driver question at every major decision point — not just at entry — is what keeps the lead lens current as the trade ages.

The discipline this enforces is humility about your *reason* for being in a trade. If you cannot, today, name the single variable that decides the next move and point to the lens that reads it, you do not actually have a thesis you can act on — you have a leftover position you are rationalizing with whichever lens currently looks friendly. The dominant-driver test, asked repeatedly, is the antidote to the slow drift from "I'm long because positioning was washed out" to "I'm long because, well, I'm long."

### When the dominant driver is positioning, you fade the crowd

The most counterintuitive output of the dominant-driver test is the case where the lead lens turns out to be positioning and it tells you to do the *opposite* of the fundamentals. This is the crowded-long-into-good-news setup from the hook, and it deserves its own treatment because it is where disciplined analysts lose the most money.

When fundamentals are excellent and *everyone knows it*, the good news is already in the price and, more importantly, already in the positioning. The marginal buyer — the next person who would push the price up — has already bought. At that point the dominant driver is not "are the fundamentals good" (they are, and it's priced) but "is there anyone left to buy." When the answer is no, the fundamentally-correct bull is the one who gets hurt, because the only thing that can happen at the margin is selling. This is the mechanism behind the pain trade, which we built in detail in [mapping the consensus](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/mapping-the-consensus-what-does-the-market-already-believe): a fully-loaded consensus is fragile, not strong. For the formal microstructure of why the other side of your crowded trade is a forced seller, see [positioning and the pain trade](/blog/trading/event-trading/positioning-and-the-pain-trade).

So the resolution to "great fundamentals, euphoric positioning" is not to follow the fundamentals you have high conviction in. It is to recognize that the dominant driver is positioning, let the positioning lens lead, and either reduce hard or fade. Which brings us to the matrix of *which* lens pairs conflict and what each pairing usually means.

## The lens-disagreement matrix: what each pairing tells you

Not all conflicts are the same. The *pair* of lenses that disagree carries information about what kind of conflict you are in and therefore what to do. Here is the catalog.

![Matrix of four lens pairs mapped to usual cause, meaning, and action including match horizon, cut size, fade, and stand aside](/imgs/blogs/reconciling-conflicting-signals-when-the-lenses-disagree-2.png)

The matrix reads row by row, and each row is a different *species* of conflict:

- **Macro versus technical** is the classic noise conflict. It is almost always two different horizons talking past each other — a long-horizon macro view against a short-horizon price read. The usual cause is *different horizons*, both can be right, and the action is to match the lens to your holding period (the previous section). This row resolves cleanly more often than any other.

- **Fundamental versus positioning** is the dangerous one. The usual cause is *the crowd is loaded on good news* — fundamentals are great and everyone already owns the trade because of it. The meaning is *fragility near exhaustion*: the good news is priced and positioned. The action is to *cut size or fade*, because the dominant driver has quietly shifted from the fundamentals to whether any buyer remains. This is the row that fools good analysts, because the fundamental lens they trust is genuinely correct — it just stopped being the thing that decides the next move.

- **Technical versus breadth** is the turn-signal row. When the index (price) is making new highs but breadth — the number of individual stocks participating — is rolling over, the usual cause is that *fewer and fewer stocks are holding the index up*. The meaning is *a turn is brewing*: the move is narrowing, which is the classic late-stage topping pattern. The action is to *fade with tight risk* — the divergence is itself the signal, which we develop next.

- **Macro versus macro-priced** is the no-edge row. Your macro lens is bearish, but the market has *already priced* the bearish macro. The usual cause is that *the bad news is in the price*; the meaning is *there is no edge left* even though your directional read is correct; and the action is to *stand aside*. Being right about the macro is worthless if the price already reflects it — a point made precisely in [the surprise, not the level](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/the-surprise-not-the-level-betas-to-data-surprises): markets move on the gap between the outcome and what was priced, not on the level of the outcome.

The matrix is the lookup table for "I have a conflict between lens A and lens B — what species is this?" Find the row, read the usual cause, and you have a head start on whether to match horizon, cut size, fade, or stand aside.

#### Worked example: cutting a \$20,000 long to \$10,000 under conflict, and the EV math

You are long \$20,000 of a momentum tech name. Your fundamental lens still loves it. But you run the matrix and you are squarely in the *fundamental-versus-positioning* row: the stock is one of the most crowded longs in its sector, retail flows are euphoric, and it just had a great print that the stock *sold off* on — the classic "good news, no buyers left" tell. The dominant driver is no longer the fundamentals; it is positioning, and positioning says exhausted.

Here is the expected-value math for cutting to half. Without the conflict, your full-size edge was, say, a 60% chance of +20% and a 40% chance of −10% over your horizon:

```
EV(full, no conflict) = 0.60 × (+20%) + 0.40 × (−10%)
                      = +12% − 4% = +8% on $20,000 = +$1,600
```

But the positioning conflict changes your honest probabilities. The exhausted crowd cuts your up-case probability and fattens the down-case, because a crowded long that fails to rally on good news is the setup for a flush:

```
EV(full, with conflict) = 0.45 × (+20%) + 0.55 × (−15%)
                        = +9% − 8.25% = +0.75% on $20,000 = +$150
```

Your edge has collapsed from \$1,600 to \$150 — and the *variance* has gone up (the down-case is now −15%, not −10%, and more likely). You are now being paid almost nothing to hold full exposure to a fatter tail. Cutting to \$10,000 keeps half the upside if you are wrong about the exhaustion while halving the damage if you are right about it:

```
EV(half, with conflict) = 0.45 × (+20%) + 0.55 × (−15%) on $10,000
                        = +0.75% on $10,000 = +$75
```

The EV of the half position is half the EV of the full one (+\$75 vs +\$150) — but you have also halved the dollar variance, and crucially you have halved your exposure to the −15% flush, which on full size is −\$3,000 and on half size is −\$1,500. When edge has collapsed to near zero but tail risk has grown, the correct response is to cut size: you give up a trivial \$75 of expected value to remove \$1,500 of downside exposure. That is an excellent trade.

The takeaway: a positioning conflict does not just change your direction, it collapses your edge and fattens your tail at the same time — and when edge is near zero with a growing tail, cutting size is not timid, it is the math.

## When divergence IS the signal: disagreement at the turn

Up to now we have treated conflict as a problem to resolve. But there is a category of conflict where the disagreement is not noise and not a stale lens — it is *itself the highest-quality signal you will ever get*. This is divergence at a turning point, and learning to recognize it is the difference between fearing conflict and hunting for it.

![Before and after panels showing mid-trend where price breadth and momentum agree versus a turn where price rises while breadth and momentum roll over](/imgs/blogs/reconciling-conflicting-signals-when-the-lenses-disagree-4.png)

In the middle of a healthy trend, the lenses *agree* — that is what makes it a trend. Price is making new highs, breadth is confirming (most stocks participating), and momentum is rising in step with price. The figure's left panel: everything green, everything aligned, the correct action is to trade *with* the trend. Agreement is the signature of a trend's healthy middle.

At a major top, the lenses *split on purpose*. Price is still making new highs — but breadth has started rolling over (fewer stocks making new highs even as the index does), and momentum is diverging lower (each new price high comes on weaker momentum than the last). The figure's right panel: price still amber-green but breadth and momentum red. This is not a malfunction and it is not two horizons talking past each other. It is the mechanical anatomy of a turn. **Price lags because it is the last thing to break; breadth and positioning and momentum lead because they reflect the internal participation that has already started to fail.** The divergence is the early-warning system.

The reason this works is that a market top is a *process*, not an event. The strongest stocks keep dragging the index up while the average stock has already topped. By the time price itself rolls over, the turn is well underway internally. The analyst who waits for *all* the lenses to agree on "bearish" gets the signal far too late, at the bottom of the first leg down. The analyst who recognizes that *price-up-but-internals-down is the conflict that signals the turn* gets paid for being early.

The classic real example is a price/breadth divergence: the index makes a marginal new high but the percentage of stocks above their 200-day average has been falling for months. The same shape shows up as price/momentum divergence (price higher, RSI lower) and as price/positioning divergence (price higher, but smart-money positioning already turning net short). For the honest treatment of what these technical divergences can and cannot do — they identify *conditions*, not exact timing — see [the indicator trap](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/the-indicator-trap). The discipline is to treat a divergence as a *condition that raises the probability of a turn*, sized accordingly, never as a precise sell-the-top timer.

The failure mode is equally important to understand, because it is what keeps you sizing small. Divergences fail *often*. A breadth divergence can persist for months while the index grinds higher — the famous warning that "the market can stay irrational longer than you can stay solvent" is, in large part, about divergences that took far longer to resolve than the early trader could afford to wait. A momentum divergence can reset and re-accelerate. Smart-money positioning can be early by a quarter. This is why a divergence is never a full-size, all-in fade: the *direction* of the eventual move is high-conviction, but the *timing* is low-conviction, and you size to the weaker of the two. The correct expression of a divergence trade is small, with a stop that defines the dollar damage if you are early, and ideally with a *confirmation trigger* — you start small on the divergence and add only when price itself begins to confirm (the index finally breaks its trendline, breadth thrust to the downside). The divergence gets you positioned before the crowd; the confirmation lets you press once the turn is real. Trading the divergence alone, full size, with no confirmation and no stop, is the express route to being right about the turn and broke before it arrives.

#### Worked example: a divergence-at-the-turn trade sized small with \$2,000 of defined risk

It is October 2022. The S&P 500 has been falling all year, and the lenses are a mess. The **macro lens** is still bearish — inflation is high, the Fed is hiking, recession calls are everywhere. The **sentiment lens** is washed out — the AAII survey is at multi-decade bearish extremes, everyone hates stocks. The **positioning lens** shows funds at historically low equity exposure. And the **technical lens** is throwing a *bullish* divergence: the index made a marginal new low in mid-October, but it did so on *higher* momentum than the September low (a positive RSI divergence) and on *improving* breadth (fewer stocks making new lows than at the prior low).

The lenses disagree, and most people resolved the conflict by deferring to the loudest lens — the bearish macro narrative — and stayed short or in cash. But run the process. Classify: this is a potential *turn*, and the disagreement (price at new lows, internals improving) is the divergence signature, not noise. The dominant driver of a turn is exhaustion of selling — and the sentiment/positioning lenses both screamed exhaustion (nobody left to sell), while the technical divergence confirmed the internals were turning. Three lenses (sentiment, positioning, breadth) were quietly bullish; the macro narrative was the loud, stale, already-priced lens.

So you take the trade — but *small*, because a divergence is a probability-raiser, not a certainty, and you are fading a powerful downtrend. You buy \$15,000 of the index with a stop just below the October low. The distance to your stop is ≈ 13%, but you are not risking \$15,000 — you have defined your risk at **\$2,000** by choosing your size so that the stop-out costs exactly that. The math: if your acceptable loss is \$2,000 and your stop is 13% away, your position is \$2,000 / 0.13 ≈ \$15,000 — which is how you arrive at the size, not the other way around. (Sizing from risk, not from a dollar amount you "feel like" deploying, is the bridge we build in [from conviction to size](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/from-conviction-to-size-the-bet-sizing-bridge).)

The trade worked — that October low held and became the bottom of the cycle, with the index rallying over 20% into 2023. But the point is not that it worked; the point is the *process*. You recognized that the disagreement among the lenses was the divergence anatomy of a turn, you identified that the dominant driver (seller exhaustion) was confirmed by the quiet lenses while the loud lens was stale, and you sized the trade small with defined \$2,000 risk because you were early and fading a trend. If it had failed you would have lost \$2,000 — a survivable, pre-decided amount — and learned that the turn was not yet in.

The takeaway: the most valuable conflicts are divergences at turns, where price disagrees with breadth, momentum, and positioning — but because you are early and fading a trend, you express the view small with hard-defined dollar risk, never full size.

## The conflict scorecard: making it countable

The decision tree is the logic; the scorecard is the artifact you actually fill in so the logic does not stay in your head where confirmation bias lives. The scorecard forces you to score every lens *at the same horizon*, tally the votes, run the dominant-driver and horizon checks, and land on a size.

![Grid scorecard of six lenses scored plus or minus at one horizon, a tally of plus four versus minus two, dominant driver and horizon checks, and a half size verdict](/imgs/blogs/reconciling-conflicting-signals-when-the-lenses-disagree-6.png)

The scorecard in the figure is a worked instance. Six lenses, each scored at a fixed three-month horizon as +1 (constructive), −1 (warning), or 0 (neutral). Macro +1, fundamental +1, technical +1, valuation +1 (cheap), positioning −1 (crowded), sentiment −1 (euphoric). The tally is **+4 versus −2**. A naive reader stops here and says "+4 beats −2, majority bullish, full size." That is the *go-with-the-majority* error, and the scorecard is built to stop it at the next two cells.

The **dominant-driver check** asks: is positioning the thing that decides this trade? If the trade is a crowded momentum name where the marginal-buyer question dominates, then the two −1 lenses (positioning, sentiment) are not just two votes among six — they are the *two votes that read the dominant driver*. A +4/−2 tally where the −2 owns the dominant driver is not a "majority bullish" situation; it is a "the lenses that matter most are flashing red" situation. The **horizon check** confirms all six were scored at the same three-month clock, so the tally is comparing like with like (no stale long-horizon lens inflating the count).

The verdict cell: **net conflicted → half size.** The tally is nominally bullish but the dissent sits exactly on the dominant driver, so the trade is real but compromised. You do not skip it (the constructive case is genuine and outnumbers the warnings) and you do not load it (the warnings own the variable that decides it). You take it at half conviction. The scorecard converted a vague feeling — "I'm bullish but nervous about the crowd" — into a countable structure with a sizing output.

This is the discipline the figure encodes: **score at one horizon, tally, then override the raw tally with the dominant-driver and horizon checks before you size.** The tally is an input to the decision, never the decision itself.

#### Worked example: the cost of forcing a \$15,000 trade through unresolved conflict

Sometimes the scorecard does not resolve. The tally is +3/−3, the dominant-driver question has two plausible answers, and you genuinely cannot say which lens leads. This is an *unresolved* conflict, and the temptation is to force the trade anyway — "I've done all this work, I should have a position." Here is the cost of forcing it.

When a conflict is genuinely unresolved, your honest edge is roughly zero — the constructive and warning cases are balanced and you cannot tell which dominates. Forcing a \$15,000 trade through that state means taking full variance for no expected return. Suppose the symmetric outcome on your horizon is a 50/50 between +12% and −12%:

```
EV(forced) = 0.50 × (+12%) + 0.50 × (−12%) = 0% on $15,000 = $0
```

Zero expected value — but very real variance. On any single trade you will either make \$1,800 or lose \$1,800, and over many such forced trades your expected return is zero *before costs* and *negative after costs* — every forced trade pays the bid-ask spread, commissions, and the slippage of entering and exiting. If those frictions cost you even 0.5% round-trip, that is −\$75 per forced trade, and a habit of forcing twenty unresolved trades a year is −\$1,500 of pure friction on top of full variance, in exchange for zero edge. You are paying the casino's rake to play a fair coin flip.

Now compare standing aside. Standing aside on an unresolved conflict has an EV of exactly \$0 too — but with **zero variance and zero cost**. You keep your \$15,000, you keep your capital intact for a trade where the conflict *does* resolve and the edge *is* real, and you pay nothing. Between two zero-EV choices — force the trade (zero EV, high variance, negative after costs) and stand aside (zero EV, zero variance, zero cost) — standing aside dominates. It is not even close.

The takeaway: forcing a \$15,000 trade through an unresolved conflict is buying full variance and paying real frictions for an edge of zero — strictly worse than standing aside, which delivers the same zero edge with no variance and no cost.

## Stand-aside as an active position

The hardest discipline in this entire post is the one with no chart and no entry: choosing not to trade. The reason it is hard is that doing nothing *feels* like failure — you did the analysis, you have an opinion, and not expressing it feels like wasted work. The reframe that fixes this: **standing aside is a position.** It has an entry (now), an exit (when the conflict resolves), and a profit-and-loss (the trades you correctly avoided). A trader who stands aside through an unresolved conflict and watches it resolve into a clean setup the next week has *made money on the stand-aside* — the money they did not lose forcing the murky version.

![Grid showing three responses to unresolved conflict force the trade cut size or stand aside each mapped to its outcome random edge low damage or kept capital and optionality](/imgs/blogs/reconciling-conflicting-signals-when-the-lenses-disagree-7.png)

The figure lays out the three responses to an unresolved conflict and where each one lands. **Force the trade at full size** leads to "random edge, full variance" — you are exposed to the full swing on a coin-flip, the worst of both worlds. **Cut size to a half position** leads to "stay in the game, low damage" — a reasonable middle path when you have *some* edge but not full conviction. **Stand aside to zero position** leads to "keep capital and optionality" — you preserve the capital and, more valuably, the *optionality* to deploy it when a real edge shows up.

That last word, optionality, is the key. Capital that is committed to a murky trade is not available for the clean one. The opportunity cost of forcing a bad trade is not just the variance you eat on the bad trade; it is the good trade you cannot fully size next week because your capital and your risk budget are tied up. Standing aside is how you *hold* the option to act decisively when the lenses realign. The best traders are not the ones who have a position on every conflict; they are the ones who stand aside through the murk and then bet big when the murk clears.

There is an execution-psychology dimension here too, because standing aside is the position your impatience will sabotage. The pull to "do something" with an open trading screen is enormous, and it is exactly how unresolved conflicts get forced into bad trades — a dynamic covered in [trading psychology and the execution gap](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/trading-psychology-and-the-execution-gap). The defense is to make stand-aside a *named, written* decision in your journal — "stood aside, conflict unresolved, watching for X to break the tie" — so that it is a deliberate position you are tracking, not a passive failure to act.

## Sizing down: the continuous response to conflict

Stand-aside is the extreme. Most of the time the right response to conflict is the continuous version: size *down* in proportion to how much the lenses disagree. Full conviction (all lenses aligned) earns full size; mild conflict earns a trim; severe conflict earns a deep cut; unresolvable conflict earns zero. The sizing is a dial, not a switch.

![Bar chart of position size in dollars falling as lens agreement drops from six zero at twenty thousand dollars to three three at zero dollars stand aside](/imgs/blogs/reconciling-conflicting-signals-when-the-lenses-disagree-5.png)

The chart maps net lens agreement (constructive lenses minus warning lenses) to the dollars you commit, anchored to a \$20,000 full-conviction size. When all six lenses agree (6–0), you deploy the full \$20,000. At 5–1 — one dissenting lens — you trim to \$15,000, because a single warning is information that lowers your edge a notch. At 4–2 — two dissenters, the scorecard case from earlier — you cut to \$10,000, a half position, because two warning lenses meaningfully compromise the trade. At 3–3 and below — a genuine split — you stand aside (\$0), because the edge has gone to zero and, as the forced-trade math showed, full variance for zero edge is strictly worse than no position.

The shape matters: the size does not decline linearly to some small-but-nonzero floor; it goes to *zero* at the point of genuine conflict and stays there. There is no "minimum position you always have on." The whole point of treating conflict seriously is that when you cannot resolve it, the correct exposure is none. The bars are illustrative — your own mapping might trim faster or slower — but the principle is fixed: **agreement is a dial that turns size up, and conflict is a dial that turns it down, all the way to zero.**

This is the operational link between this post and the rest of the synthesis chain. Conflict resolution does not just tell you *what* to trade; it tells you *how big*, and the size is where your read on the conflict gets expressed in dollars. The full treatment of mapping conviction to size — Kelly, fractional Kelly, the conviction-to-size bridge — lives in [from conviction to size](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/from-conviction-to-size-the-bet-sizing-bridge) and rests on first honestly [measuring your conviction](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/measuring-conviction-how-sure-are-you-really). For the survival logic underneath all of it — why cutting size under uncertainty is the free lunch that keeps you compounding — see [risk management, the only free lunch](/blog/trading/risk-management/risk-management-the-only-free-lunch-survival-as-a-compounding-engine).

## Common misconceptions

A handful of beliefs about conflicting signals are not just wrong but actively expensive. Each one is a shortcut that *feels* like process and is actually a way to avoid the real work.

**"Average the signals."** The most common error. Two bullish lenses and one bearish lens does not mean "net mildly bullish, take a two-thirds position." Lenses are not commensurable quantities you can add up — a one-year macro bear and a one-week technical bull do not average into a "neutral-ish three-day view." Averaging throws away the two things that actually matter: the *horizon* each lens speaks to and *which lens reads the dominant driver*. A single bearish lens that owns the dominant driver (positioning, on a crowded trade) outweighs three bullish lenses that don't. You resolve conflicts by structure, not by arithmetic mean.

**"Go with the majority of lenses."** A close cousin of averaging, and just as wrong. Four lenses bullish, two bearish is not "majority bullish, full size" — as the scorecard showed, if the two bearish lenses own the dominant driver, the majority is irrelevant. Lenses get votes weighted by *relevance to the variable that decides the trade*, not one-lens-one-vote. Counting lenses is a way to feel systematic while ignoring the only question that matters: which lens reads the thing that decides the outcome.

**"Conflict means stay out, always."** Over-correcting in the other direction. Some traders, having learned that forcing unresolved conflicts is bad, conclude that *any* disagreement means no trade. But that throws away the best setups in the market — divergences at turns are *built* on conflict (price up, internals down), and the crowded-long fade is *built* on conflict (great fundamentals, exhausted positioning). Conflict is not a stop sign; it is information whose meaning depends on the species (the matrix) and on which lens leads. The skill is to know *which* conflicts are opportunities and which are murk to avoid, not to flee all of them.

**"My favorite lens wins."** The deepest and most personal error, because it does not feel like an error — it feels like *conviction*. The fundamental investor who dismisses positioning as "noise" every time it disagrees, the technician who waves away macro as "not my timeframe" precisely when macro is the regime that is changing — these are not strong views, they are confirmation bias with a methodology badge. The tell, again, is inconsistency: do you weight a lens the same way when it disagrees with your position as when it agrees? If not, your "process" is a rationalization engine. The dominant-driver test exists specifically to override the favorite-lens reflex by forcing relevance, not preference, to pick the lead.

**"Disagreement among smart people means I should be confused too."** When you see famous investors taking opposite sides — one calling a bottom, another calling a crash — the instinct is paralysis. But their disagreement is usually a *horizon* disagreement (the bull is trading the quarter, the bear is positioning for the decade) or a *mandate* disagreement (different risk tolerances, different books). It is not a signal to freeze; it is a reminder to identify your *own* horizon and dominant driver and ignore the noise of people trading a different game than you are.

## How it plays out in real markets

The process is clearest in the episodes where it would have saved real money — or made it.

### Macro-versus-technical, resolved by horizon: 2020

In late March 2020, the conflict was as stark as it gets. The **macro lens** was apocalyptic — a global economy in freefall, unemployment spiking to Depression-era levels in weeks, GDP forecasts being slashed daily. The **technical and positioning lenses** were screaming the opposite by late March: a historic momentum washout, sentiment at generational extremes of fear, forced selling exhausting itself, and on March 23 a violent intraday reversal off the lows. A reader who stacked "macro: worst recession in a century" against "technicals: capitulation bottom" and called it an irresolvable conflict stayed in cash and missed one of the fastest rallies in history.

The process move was horizon-matching. The macro bear was a statement about the *real economy over the next year*. The technical-and-positioning bull was a statement about the *next several weeks of a market that had already discounted catastrophe and was now driven by forced-selling exhaustion plus an unprecedented liquidity injection*. Both were correct on their clocks. A tactical trader who matched the lens to a multi-week horizon bought the capitulation; a long-horizon investor who matched the lens to the macro added to quality positions at a discount, accepting that the path would be ugly. The conflict was noise, not regime — the dominant driver was liquidity and seller exhaustion, both of which the quiet lenses read correctly while the macro lens read a different, longer question.

### Crowded-long-into-good-fundamentals: the 2021 mega-cap top

Through 2021, the most-loved growth and mega-cap tech names had a fundamental lens that was genuinely excellent — revenue compounding, dominant franchises, the works. But by late 2021 the *fundamental-versus-positioning* conflict was flashing: these names were the most crowded longs in the market, held by every fund, with valuations stretched and the marginal buyer exhausted. The fundamental lens said hold; the positioning and valuation lenses said the crowd was loaded and the good news was fully priced.

The analyst who let their favorite lens (fundamentals) win held full size into 2022 and watched the most crowded longs lead the drawdown — the very names with the best fundamentals fell the hardest, because they had the most positioning to unwind. The analyst who ran the dominant-driver test recognized that with the fundamentals priced and the crowd exhausted, the variable that decided the *next move* was positioning, not earnings. They cut size or rotated, and they were paid for treating the conflict correctly. This is the matrix's most dangerous row playing out in real money: being right about the business and wrong about the crowd.

### Bullish divergence at the bottom: the 2022 lows

The October 2022 low — the worked example earlier — is the cleanest modern case of *disagreement as a turn signal*. The lenses split: macro and the loud narrative were bearish, while sentiment (washed out), positioning (funds at minimum exposure), and the technical internals (a higher-momentum, better-breadth low than September) were quietly bullish. The disagreement was not noise and it was not a stale lens to discount — it was the divergence anatomy of a bottom: price made a new low while the internals refused to confirm. Those who recognized the species of conflict and faded the stale-but-loud macro bear, small and with defined risk, caught the turn. Those who resolved the conflict by deferring to the loudest lens missed a 20%+ rally and re-entered far higher.

The thread across all three: the conflict was never resolved by averaging, by counting lenses, or by deferring to a favorite. It was resolved by classifying the species (noise, regime, or divergence-at-a-turn), matching the horizon, and identifying which lens read the dominant driver — and then sizing for how much genuine disagreement remained.

## The playbook

Here is the repeatable procedure. Run it every time the lenses disagree, in order, and write the output in your journal so the process — not your bias — picks the answer.

**The conflict-resolution checklist:**

1. **Classify the conflict.** Is this *noise* (the lenses are on different horizons and can both be right) or *regime* (the world is changing and one lens is stale)? Diagnostic for regime: has an actual *driver* of one lens moved (a policy pivot, a credit event, a curve inversion), or is a lens just "still bearish" from inertia? Noise → step 2 resolves it. Regime → skip to step 3 and weight toward the lens reading the new world.

2. **Match the horizon.** Name your holding period for *this* trade. Whichever lens governs that horizon leads; the others become context that sizes and frames the trade, not signals that veto it. A bearish one-year macro view does not kill a two-week technical long; it makes the long smaller and the stop tighter. Most conflicts die here.

3. **Find the dominant driver.** Ask: "what single thing decides whether this trade works?" Name *one* variable. The lens that reads that variable best is the lead lens. If the dominant driver is *positioning* on a crowded trade, the lead lens is positioning and the trade may be to *fade* the fundamentals you have conviction in.

4. **Score it at one horizon.** Fill the scorecard: score all six lenses +1 / 0 / −1 at the *same* horizon, tally, then override the raw tally with the dominant-driver and horizon checks. A +4/−2 tally where the −2 owns the dominant driver is a half-size trade, not a full-size one. The tally is an input, never the decision.

5. **Check for divergence-at-a-turn.** Is the disagreement the signature of a turn — price up while breadth, momentum, and positioning roll over (a top), or price down while internals improve and sentiment/positioning are exhausted (a bottom)? If so, the conflict *is* the signal. Fade it — but *small*, with defined dollar risk, because divergences raise probability, they do not time the exact turn.

6. **Resolve into one of three actions.** Land the trade in exactly one bucket:
   - **Pick the lead lens** and trade its direction, sized below full conviction because dissent is real information.
   - **Fade the crowd** if the dominant driver is exhausted positioning.
   - **Cut size or stand aside** if the conflict is unresolved. Size down in proportion to disagreement, all the way to zero when the tally is split and the dominant driver is ambiguous. Forcing full variance for zero edge is strictly worse than standing aside.

7. **Write the stand-aside as a position.** If you stand aside, journal it as a deliberate decision with an exit condition — "stood aside, fundamental-vs-positioning conflict unresolved, watching for the crowd to flush or for the chart to reclaim the trendline to break the tie." Stand-aside is a tracked position with a P&L (the losses you avoided and the optionality you kept), not a passive failure to act.

The one sentence to internalize: **when the lenses disagree, do not pick the one you like — classify the conflict, match it to your horizon, find the one driver that decides the trade, and let the disagreement set your size, all the way down to zero.** Synthesis is not the art of making the lenses agree. It is the discipline of acting correctly when they don't.

The next step, once you have resolved the conflict and have a directional read, is to collapse everything you know into a single tradeable sentence — the subject of [from data to a read](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/from-data-to-a-read-collapsing-everything-into-one-sentence) — and then to measure how sure you really are before you size it.
