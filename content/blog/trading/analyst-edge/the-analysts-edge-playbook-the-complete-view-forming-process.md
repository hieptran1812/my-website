> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The analyst's edge is not knowing more; it is running a complete, accountable process end to end. This is that process as one machine you execute for any market, asset, or horizon: information → synthesis → thesis → probability → conviction → size → expression → write → monitor → update → review.
>
> - The spine never changes: **what's priced in vs. what I believe — and what would change my mind?** A view that cannot be falsified, sized, and updated is a horoscope, not analysis.
> - Edge has three durable sources — informational, analytical, and **behavioral** — and the behavioral one (sizing, exiting, reviewing) is the largest and the hardest for anyone to copy. It lives in the *back* of the pipeline, which is exactly where amateurs spend the least effort.
> - Every repeatable way analysts lose money maps to **one skipped stage**. Run the whole pipeline and you have already defended against each known failure mode.
> - The one rule to remember: **write the call down — claim, probability, size, invalidation — then grade the decision separately from the outcome, and feed the lesson back in.** That accountability loop is what turns experience into compounding skill.

On the morning of 13 September 2022, two analysts read the same 8:30 a.m. release: U.S. core CPI printed **0.6%** against a roughly **0.3%** consensus — twice as hot as expected. The first analyst was the better economist by a mile. She could trace the shelter lag, owners'-equivalent rent, the used-car distortion, and the exact map from a hot core to the Fed's hike path. She read it, nodded, said "rates should go higher," and went to lunch. The second knew less economics but had a process. He saw 0.6% vs. 0.3% and asked one question — *is this priced in?* It wasn't; futures were leaning toward a slowing pace. So he wrote a view: front-end yields too low; 65% confident; risk \$2,000 of a \$100,000 account; out if the 2-year yield falls 25 bp or the next core print comes in soft. The position was on by 8:35 a.m. The S&P fell 4.3% that day and front-end yields ripped. The economist was right and made nothing. The second analyst turned a read into money.

That gap is the whole subject of this series, and this post is its capstone. The first thirty-nine posts each take one stage of the second analyst's process and go deep. This one is the complete machine — every stage in order, what connects to what, why each guard matters, and the single page you can pin above your desk and run on any market tomorrow morning. If [the opening post](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/the-analysts-edge-why-forming-a-view-is-the-real-job) told you *that* forming a view is the job, this one is the full assembly: the process the second analyst ran, drawn end to end so you can run it too.

![The complete view forming pipeline from information to review across ten stages](/imgs/blogs/the-analysts-edge-playbook-the-complete-view-forming-process-1.png)

That pipeline is the map of everything below. Notice what it is *not*: it is not "learn more macro." The mechanism — how a hot CPI lifts hike odds — sits *before* stage 1 as an input you already have. The job is the ten stages that convert that input into a position you can be held accountable to, and then feed the graded result back so the next call is sharper. Each box is a stage; each stage is a post; this page is the whole route.

## Foundations: the spine, the thesis of the series, and the shape of the machine

Before walking the stages, let me restate the two ideas the entire series hangs on, because the playbook is just these two ideas made operational.

**The thesis: judgment over inputs.** This series never re-taught mechanisms — how rates move, how options price, which sector leads a cycle. Those are *table stakes*: knowable, teachable, and widely known, which means the person on the other side of your trade knows them too. You do not get paid for owning the map everyone owns. You get paid for the meta-skill of *using* the map: looking at a market, synthesizing many lenses, forming a falsifiable view, quantifying it, sizing it, expressing it, writing it down, tracking it, and updating it. The difference between the two analysts above was not knowledge. It was process.

**The spine: what's priced in vs. what I believe — and what would change my mind?** Every post returned to this one question, and it is doing three jobs at once.

![The spine of every call shown as priced in versus my view versus the invalidation line](/imgs/blogs/the-analysts-edge-playbook-the-complete-view-forming-process-2.png)

*What's priced in* forces you to find the consensus before you opine. A price is not a fact about the world; it is the crowd's probability-weighted bet. If the 2-year yields 4.00%, the market is collectively saying it expects an average overnight rate near 4.00%. To have an edge you must believe something *different* — and know what the "different" is. *What I believe* is your variant view: the specific way your read departs from the price. Edge is not being right; it is being **differently right**. *What would change my mind* is the falsification clause amateurs skip. A view with no kill level is unfalsifiable, which means it is not a claim about reality at all — it is a story you can keep telling yourself with your account balance long after reality moved on.

The figure shows the three numbers the spine produces in the CPI example: the market prices a terminal rate of **5.00%**; my view, weighting sticky services more heavily than the curve does, is **5.45%**; the **+45 bp gap is the edge**; and the kill line is a 2-year yield drop of 25 bp or a sub-0.3% core print. A view that has only the middle number is a horoscope.

**The shape of the machine.** The pipeline is *one runnable process*, and it has a property worth stating plainly: it is a **loop**, not a line. Stage 10 (review) feeds stage 1 (information) — your graded record tells you which of your reads actually had edge and which were luck, which sharpens what you pay attention to next. The stages are also *ordered for a reason*: you cannot size (stage 5) before you have a probability (stage 4); you cannot have a probability worth anything before you have a thesis (stage 3); you cannot have a thesis before you have synthesized (stage 2) what you read (stage 1). Skipping forward is the single most common process error — sizing a position you never wrote a thesis for, or trading off a headline you never synthesized against the consensus.

One more foundational point that the whole back half of this post turns on: **where the money is actually made**. Beginners spend ninety percent of their energy on stages 1–3 — gathering information and forming an opinion — because that part *feels* like analysis. They spend almost nothing on stages 4–10. But the information edge is small and fleeting; everyone reads the same CPI. The behavioral edge — sizing correctly, cutting at the pre-defined level, not over-betting favorites, learning from your record — is large and durable. The front of the pipeline is where you feel smart. The back is where you get paid. If you spend more time deciding *whether* to put on a trade than deciding *how big* and *where you're wrong*, your effort is backwards.

This is worth stating sharply because it is the most counterintuitive lesson in the craft and the one beginners resist hardest. The mental image most people have of a great analyst is someone who *knows more* — a walking database of macro relationships, earnings models, and chart patterns. That image is wrong, and chasing it is how careers stall. The great analyst is not the one who knows the most; it is the one who *converts what they know into accountable, well-sized, well-tracked positions most reliably*. Two analysts can have identical reads of a market and one makes money while the other does not, entirely because of what happens in stages 4 through 10. The series spent thirty-nine posts on the full process precisely because the part that pays — sizing, invalidation, monitoring, review — is the part no course teaches and no one wants to practice, since it has no glamour and produces no feeling of being clever. The whole edge is hiding in the unglamorous half.

There is also a structural reason the pipeline is ordered the way it is and why running it in order matters. Each stage *constrains* the next: synthesis constrains what theses are even plausible; the thesis constrains what probability is honest; the probability and the scenarios constrain the EV; the EV and the edge constrain the size; the invalidation constrains the expression; the written record constrains what you can monitor and later review. Skip a stage and the downstream stages have nothing to constrain them, so they default to whatever feels right in the moment — which is to say, to your biases. A position sized without a probability is sized by emotion. A trade monitored without a written thesis is monitored against the P&L. A review run without a written record is run against a flattering memory. The order is not bureaucratic; it is the chain of constraints that keeps judgment honest at every step.

Now the stages, one at a time.

## The complete pipeline, stage by stage

Each stage below is one or two paragraphs and links to its deep-dive post(s). Read this section as the table of contents to the whole craft: when a stage is the one giving you trouble, the linked post is where you go to fix it.

### Stage 1 — Information diet: build the input, separate signal from noise

Garbage in, garbage view. The first stage is not "read everything"; it is the deliberate construction of an *input stream* whose signal-to-noise ratio is high enough that synthesis is possible at all. Most analysts drown: they consume a torrent of headlines, hot takes, and price-action commentary that has near-zero predictive content, then wonder why their reads feel scattered. A good information diet is curated, repeatable, and ruthlessly pruned — a small number of high-signal sources read in a fixed order, with the noise (most of financial Twitter, most sell-side narrative, most television) explicitly excluded. See [building your information diet: signal versus noise](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/building-your-information-diet-signal-versus-noise).

The diet only works if it is *executed on a cadence*. A view that you form once a quarter when you happen to feel inspired is not a process; it is a hobby. The discipline is a repeatable reading routine — a daily pass that takes twenty minutes and a weekly deeper pass — so that the inputs arrive on schedule and the synthesis muscle stays warm. See [the daily and weekly process: a repeatable reading routine](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/the-daily-and-weekly-process-a-repeatable-reading-routine). The output of stage 1 is not a view. It is a clean, dated set of observations ready to be read together.

The subtle failure at stage 1 is not reading too little; it is reading too much of the wrong thing. More information past a surprisingly low threshold does not improve a view — it degrades it, because every additional low-signal source adds noise that your brain then has to filter, and the filtering is where confirmation bias creeps in. The professional's diet is *narrower* than the amateur's, not wider: a handful of primary sources (the actual data release, the actual filing, the actual price), a small set of trusted synthesizers, and an explicit blocklist for the rest. The test for whether a source belongs in your diet is brutal and simple: over the last year, did reading it ever change a *decision*? If not, it is entertainment, and entertainment dressed as research is the most expensive habit in the business.

### Stage 2 — Synthesis: six lenses into one sentence, conflict reconciled

Synthesis is the act of reading a market through several independent lenses at once and collapsing them into one coherent read. The framework is six lenses — macro, valuation, technicals, positioning, flows, and sentiment — each of which can be read on its own and each of which catches something the others miss. See [the six lenses: a framework for reading any market](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/the-six-lenses-a-framework-for-reading-any-market). The discipline is to read *all six* before concluding, because the failure mode is to read the one lens that confirms what you already wanted to believe and stop.

Two sub-skills make synthesis real rather than a checklist. First, the lenses *will* disagree — macro says one thing, positioning says the opposite — and the analyst's job is to reconcile the conflict rather than average it away or pick the lens that is loudest. See [reconciling conflicting signals: when the lenses disagree](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/reconciling-conflicting-signals-when-the-lenses-disagree). Second, the lens most analysts under-weight is *flows and positioning* — who already owns the trade, who is offside, where the pain is. Positioning is the tell that explains why the same number moves the market differently on different days, and it is where a surprising amount of durable edge hides. See [reading flows and positioning: the tell most analysts miss](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/reading-flows-and-positioning-the-tell-most-analysts-miss), and the mechanism in [positioning and the pain trade](/blog/trading/event-trading/positioning-and-the-pain-trade). The output of stage 2 is a single sentence: the read, compressed. If you cannot say it in one sentence, you have not synthesized — you have a pile of lenses. See [from data to a read: collapsing everything into one sentence](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/from-data-to-a-read-collapsing-everything-into-one-sentence).

The reason synthesis is a distinct skill — and not just "read more lenses" — is that the lenses are not equally informative at all times, and a mechanical average of six signals throws away the most important judgment you make: *which lens is in charge right now?* In a liquidity-driven market, flows dominate and valuation is noise; in a repricing-of-fundamentals market, valuation and macro dominate and technicals are noise; into a binary catalyst, positioning dominates because the surprise relative to consensus is what moves price. The analyst's edge at stage 2 is knowing which lens is currently load-bearing and weighting accordingly, rather than treating all six as equal votes. This is exactly why the output must collapse to *one sentence* and not a six-bullet summary: the act of compression forces you to decide what actually matters, and a read you cannot compress is a read where you have not yet made that decision.

### Stage 3 — Build the thesis: claim, evidence, catalyst; scenarios; pre-mortem

A one-sentence read is not yet a tradeable thesis. A thesis has three load-bearing parts: a **claim** (what will happen), the **evidence** (the two or three facts behind it), and a **catalyst** (the dated event or condition that will force the market to reprice). The catalyst is the part beginners omit, and its absence is why cheap things stay cheap for years — without a catalyst, you are right and broke, paying carry while you wait for a repricing that has no trigger. See [structuring a thesis: claim, evidence, and catalyst](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/structuring-a-thesis-claim-evidence-and-catalyst) and [catalysts and timing: why cheap can stay cheap for years](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/catalysts-and-timing-why-cheap-can-stay-cheap-for-years).

A thesis you only state in its base case is fragile. Build it as three scenarios — base, bull, bear — each with a rough probability and a rough payoff, so you know not just what you expect but the *shape of the distribution* around it. See [base, bull and bear: building three scenarios](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/base-bull-and-bear-building-three-scenarios). Then attack it before the market does: run a **pre-mortem**, in which you assume the trade has already failed and write down why. The pre-mortem is the single cheapest defense against falling in love with a story, because it forces the bear case onto the page while you are still calm. See [stress-testing your thesis with a pre-mortem](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/stress-testing-your-thesis-with-a-pre-mortem). The output of stage 3 is a thesis that can be argued *and* attacked.

### Stage 4 — Probability, expected value, decision trees, and the invalidation line

Now you put a number on it. A view stated as a story ("I'm pretty sure rates go up") cannot be sized, compared, or scored. A view stated as a probability ("65%") can. The shift from prediction to probability is the hinge of the whole craft. See [thinking in probabilities, not predictions](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/thinking-in-probabilities-not-predictions). Once you have a probability and the scenario payoffs from stage 3, you have the only piece of math a view truly needs: **expected value**, the probability-weighted average outcome. A 55% view with a 3-to-1 payoff is a buy; a 90% view with a 1-to-4 payoff is a pass. EV, not confidence, decides. See [expected value: the only math a view really needs](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/expected-value-the-only-math-a-view-really-needs).

For event-driven views — an FOMC, an earnings print, a binary catalyst — the EV is cleanest drawn as a **decision tree**: branches for each outcome, probabilities on the branches, payoffs at the leaves, the EV at the root. See [decision trees for event-driven views](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/decision-trees-for-event-driven-views). And the non-negotiable output of stage 4 is the **invalidation level**: the specific, observable price or event, defined *before entry*, that proves you wrong and triggers the exit. This is the "what would change my mind" leg of the spine made mechanical, and it is the most important single line you will write all day. See [what would change my mind: defining invalidation upfront](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/what-would-change-my-mind-defining-invalidation-upfront).

A second-order point that separates good probability work from sloppy probability work: your probability is only as good as your *consensus estimate*, because edge is the gap between them, and the gap is twice as sensitive to error. If the true consensus probability is 50% and you read it as 45%, you will think you have a 15-point edge when you have a 10-point edge — and you will over-size by half. This is why stage 4 is not really separable from stage 2's consensus mapping: the probability you assign to your own view is meaningless in isolation; what trades is the *difference* from what the market already believes, and a careless read of the market's number quietly inflates every edge you think you have. The discipline is to write *both* numbers — the consensus probability and your probability — never just your own, so the edge is explicit and auditable rather than assumed.

### Stage 5 — Conviction, asymmetry, and the bridge from conviction to size

Conviction is your honest probability *minus* the consensus probability, weighted by how much you trust your edge — not how strongly you feel. The discipline here is to measure conviction rather than emote it, because the size you put on must be a function of a number, not a mood. See [measuring conviction: how sure are you really](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/measuring-conviction-how-sure-are-you-really). High-conviction bets are the ones where the payoff is *asymmetric*: you can lose 1 and make 4, so even a coin-flip probability is a screaming buy. Recognizing and concentrating in asymmetry is where outsized returns come from. See [asymmetry and the art of the high-conviction bet](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/asymmetry-and-the-art-of-the-high-conviction-bet) and the deeper math in [the asymmetry of losses: why a 50% loss needs a 100% gain](/blog/trading/risk-management/the-asymmetry-of-losses-why-a-50-percent-loss-needs-a-100-percent-gain).

The bridge from conviction to dollars is where most edge is won or lost. Sizing the same on every idea — the most common amateur error — guarantees that your best reads contribute no more than your worst. A fractional-Kelly-style rule scales risk to edge: more conviction and better asymmetry get more dollars, capped so a single wrong call cannot threaten the account. See [from conviction to size: the bet-sizing bridge](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/from-conviction-to-size-the-bet-sizing-bridge) and the survival logic in [risk management: the only free lunch](/blog/trading/risk-management/risk-management-the-only-free-lunch-survival-as-a-compounding-engine). The output of stage 5 is a dollar figure: exactly how much you are willing to lose on this call.

Stage 5 carries the heaviest load in the whole pipeline because it is where edge becomes *captured* rather than merely *identified*. An analyst with great reads who sizes uniformly is leaving most of the value on the table: their high-edge calls and low-edge calls contribute equally to P&L, so the portfolio's return is the *average* edge rather than the *edge-weighted* one. Differentiated sizing — bigger on the asymmetric, high-conviction, well-defended calls; smaller on the marginal ones; nothing on the ones that fail the EV test — is what turns a set of decent reads into a return. The cap matters just as much as the scaling: the upside of correct sizing is a better return, but the downside of *over*-sizing is ruin, and ruin is not a bad year, it is the end of the game. The asymmetry of losses means a single bet large enough to do real damage can erase years of compounding, so the rule is "size up to capture edge, but never so large that being wrong threatens survival." Survival first, then optimize within it.

### Stage 6 — Expression: pick the instrument that pays the view best

A correct view expressed in the wrong instrument can still lose money. Stage 6 chooses *how* to put the view on. The simplest path is a directional position in the underlying — buy the stock, short the future. But often a better expression exists. If your edge is *relative* ("Bank A is cheap versus Bank B") rather than directional, a relative-value pair strips out the market move you have no view on and isolates the bet you do. See [choosing the instrument to express your thesis](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/choosing-the-instrument-to-express-your-thesis) and [relative value: expressing a view without a directional bet](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/relative-value-expressing-a-view-without-a-directional-bet).

When your view is about *timing*, *magnitude*, or *the shape of the payoff* — "a big move soon" or "limited downside, large upside" — options let you buy exactly that shape, paying a known premium for a defined payoff. The cost is theta and the risk is paying too much for implied volatility, which is why understanding the price you pay matters. See [using options to shape the payoff of a view](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/using-options-to-shape-the-payoff-of-a-view), and remember who sells you that option in [how an options market maker thinks](/blog/trading/options-volatility/how-an-options-market-maker-thinks-the-other-side-of-your-trade). The output of stage 6 is the actual trade — instrument, direction, structure — calibrated so the invalidation level from stage 4 costs you the dollar size from stage 5, no more.

The discipline that ties stage 6 back to the rest of the pipeline is this: the expression must *match the shape of the view*, not your preference for an instrument. If your view is directional and you are confident in the level, the underlying is fine. If your view is *relative* — you are confident A beats B but have no opinion on the market — then a directional position smuggles in a market bet you never formed a view on, and you can be exactly right about A-versus-B and still lose because the whole market fell. If your view is about *the magnitude or timing of a move* rather than its direction, only an options structure captures it cleanly, and a directional position will whipsaw you out. The most common stage-6 error is expressing every view the same way (usually a directional position in the underlying) because it is familiar, which means most of your views are partly bets you never intended to make. Choosing the expression is choosing *which risks you keep and which you strip out* — and the goal is to be exposed only to the specific thing you actually have a view on.

### Stage 7 — Write it down: trade-idea note, memo, or commentary

The view is not real until it is on paper, dated, *before* the money goes on. Writing is not bureaucracy; it is the act that makes the call falsifiable and scoreable, and it is the single habit that separates analysts who compound from those who churn. Three formats serve three purposes. The **trade-idea note** is the one-page template you write for every position — claim, priced-in, probability, size, invalidation, catalyst — and it takes three minutes once it is a habit. See [the trade-idea note: a one-page template](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/the-trade-idea-note-a-one-page-template).

When the call is large or you must persuade others, the **investment memo** argues the full thesis on paper, including the bear case, so the argument survives scrutiny rather than vibes. See [the investment memo: arguing your view on paper](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/the-investment-memo-arguing-your-view-on-paper). And when you must communicate a read to clients or a team, **market commentary that actually says something** makes a falsifiable claim instead of hedging everything into mush. See [writing market commentary that actually says something](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/writing-market-commentary-that-actually-says-something). The output of stage 7 is a dated, written, falsifiable record — the raw material every later review depends on.

### Stage 8 — Monitor: watch the dashboard, separate broken from noise

A live thesis has to be watched against the specific things that would confirm or break it — not against the P&L, which is mostly noise day to day. The discipline is a **watch dashboard**: a short list of the catalysts, levels, and data points your thesis actually depends on, so you are monitoring the *drivers*, not the ticker. See [monitoring a live thesis: building your watch dashboard](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/monitoring-a-live-thesis-building-your-watch-dashboard).

The hardest call in the whole pipeline lives here: *is my thesis broken, or is this just noise?* Exit too early on noise and you bleed out of good trades by a thousand cuts; ignore a genuine break and you ride a loser past your invalidation. The resolution is the line you wrote in stage 4 — if price hits the invalidation level or a thesis-driver flips, it is broken; if neither has happened, it is noise and you hold. See [thesis broken or just noise: the hardest call you make](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/thesis-broken-or-just-noise-the-hardest-call-you-make). And when genuinely new information arrives, you update your probability like a **Bayesian** — moving your estimate in proportion to how surprising and how diagnostic the news is, rather than anchoring on your original number or over-reacting to a single print. See [updating on new information: thinking like a Bayesian](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/updating-on-new-information-thinking-like-a-bayesian).

### Stage 9 — Update the position: add, cut, or exit around the view

Monitoring tells you the state of the thesis; stage 9 is the *action* you take in response. The position is not a one-shot bet you set and forget — it is managed around the evolving view. When new evidence strengthens the thesis and the price has not yet moved to reflect it, you may **add**. When the edge has partly played out or conviction has slipped, you **cut**. When the invalidation triggers, you **exit** — fully, mechanically, without renegotiating with yourself. See [when to add, cut, or exit: managing the position around the view](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/when-to-add-cut-or-exit-managing-the-position-around-the-view).

The trap at this stage is letting the *position* drive the *view* instead of the reverse — averaging down because you are underwater, not because the thesis improved; holding because exiting would "lock in" a loss. Stage 9 done right means every add, cut, and exit traces back to a change in the written thesis from stage 7, not to your P&L or your ego. The output is a position whose size always matches your *current* conviction, not the conviction you had at entry.

There is a clean test for whether an adjustment is disciplined or emotional: would you put on this position, at this size, *if you held no position at all right now*? If you are underwater and tempted to add, ask whether — flat, with fresh eyes — you would open a position this large at today's price given today's evidence. If yes, the add is a real view and you should make it. If you only want to add because the average entry would look better, that is the sunk-cost fallacy wearing a thesis costume, and it is how a manageable loss becomes a catastrophic one. The same test disarms the urge to hold a broken trade: a position you would not *open* today is a position you should not *keep* today, regardless of what it cost you to get here.

### Stage 10 — Review: decision journal, process vs. outcome, calibration

This is the stage that converts experience into skill, and the one almost everyone skips. The foundation is a **decision journal**: a log of every call with its thesis, probability, size, and outcome, so you have a record to learn from instead of a memory that flatters you. See [the decision journal: the habit that compounds](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/the-decision-journal-the-habit-that-compounds). The journal enables the two reviews that matter.

First, **process versus outcome**: you grade the *decision* by what was knowable at the time, not by whether it made money. A well-reasoned, well-sized bet that lost to a genuine tail is a *good decision with a bad outcome* — repeat it. A reckless bet that happened to win is a *bad decision with a good outcome* — stop doing it. Judging decisions by their P&L is called *resulting*, and it is how good processes get unlearned. See [process versus outcome: judging decisions, not results](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/process-versus-outcome-judging-decisions-not-results). Second, **calibration**: over many calls, do the things you call 65% actually happen about 65% of the time? If your 70%-confidence calls hit 50%, you are overconfident and must shade every probability down — a mechanical fix that improves every future EV calculation. See [calibration: keeping score on your own forecasts](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/calibration-keeping-score-on-your-own-forecasts) and the broader lesson that [win rate lies](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/expectancy-why-win-rate-lies). The output of stage 10 is a lesson, fed back into stage 1.

### The cross-cutting guard: the behavioral traps

The ten stages are the structure; the behavioral traps are the termites that eat it from inside. They are worth treating as a parallel guard you run *across* every stage, because they do not respect the pipeline's boundaries. **Confirmation bias** corrupts stages 1–3: you read the sources that agree with you and build the thesis you already wanted. See [confirmation bias and the thesis you fall in love with](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/confirmation-bias-and-the-thesis-you-fall-in-love-with). **Narrative addiction** corrupts stage 3: a good story overrides the data, and stories are stickier than spreadsheets. See [narrative addiction: when a good story beats the data](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/narrative-addiction-when-a-good-story-beats-the-data). And **anchoring and recency** corrupt stages 4 and 8: you anchor your probability to your first number and over-weight whatever happened most recently. See [anchoring, recency, and the judgment traps that cost money](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/anchoring-recency-and-the-judgment-traps-that-cost-money). The pre-mortem, the written invalidation, and the calibration review are the three mechanical defenses; the bias awareness is the human one. Knowing the spine is theory; running it under the pressure of a live, losing position is the execution gap — see [trading psychology and the execution gap](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/trading-psychology-and-the-execution-gap).

## The three sources of durable edge

Step back from the stages and ask the harder question: where does edge actually *come from*, and why does most of it evaporate? There are exactly three sources, and they are not equally durable.

![The three sources of durable edge informational analytical and behavioral mapped to pipeline stages](/imgs/blogs/the-analysts-edge-playbook-the-complete-view-forming-process-3.png)

**Informational edge** is knowing something true that the market has not yet priced. It is real, it is the most intuitive kind of edge, and it is the *least* durable. Information leaks, gets priced, and decays — often in minutes for liquid markets. Legal informational edge for most analysts means being faster or more thorough at *public* information, not having private information. It lives in stages 1–2, and you should expect to capture it only occasionally and briefly. The mechanism of why a surprise moves markets at all — and why the *surprise* relative to consensus, not the level, is what trades — sits in [the surprise, not the level](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/the-surprise-not-the-level-betas-to-data-surprises) and [consensus expectations and priced-in](/blog/trading/event-trading/consensus-expectations-and-priced-in).

**Analytical edge** is taking the *same* public facts everyone has and synthesizing them into a better read — connecting lenses others read in isolation, spotting the positioning tell, building the cleaner decision tree. This is more durable than informational edge because synthesis is hard and most people read one lens and stop. But it is contested: the smart crowd is doing analysis too, so your analytical edge is only as good as the margin by which your synthesis beats theirs. It lives in stages 2–4.

**Behavioral edge** is the largest and most durable of the three, and it is the one almost no one works on. It is the edge of *executing the back of the pipeline well*: sizing to conviction instead of uniformly, cutting at the pre-defined invalidation instead of hoping, not over-betting your favorite ideas, writing every call down, grading process over outcome, and calibrating. This edge is durable precisely because it is *behavioral* — it requires discipline under emotional pressure, which is the one thing competitors cannot copy by reading a research note. It lives in stages 5–10. The uncomfortable implication: the part of the process that feels least like "analysis" is where the most durable money is. An analyst with a mediocre information diet and merely-decent analysis, but world-class sizing and review discipline, will out-compound a brilliant analyst who sizes uniformly and never reviews.

The reason behavioral edge does not decay the way the other two do is structural. Informational edge decays because information spreads; analytical edge decays because frameworks get taught and copied. But behavioral edge requires you to *do the uncomfortable thing in the moment* — cut the loser you love, stay small on the idea you are sure of, write the call down when you would rather not be held to it, grade a profitable trade as a bad decision. None of that can be acquired by reading; it can only be acquired by repetition under pressure, which is why it stays rare even though it is fully public knowledge. Every retail trader has *heard* "cut your losers and let your winners run" — the proverb is free — yet the behavior is vanishingly rare, because the proverb is knowledge and the edge is the doing. This is the single most hopeful fact in the whole series for an ordinary analyst: you cannot out-information a trading desk, and you may not out-analyze the best minds in your market, but you absolutely can out-*behave* them, because most of them are not even trying to, and the behavioral edge is the largest of the three.

## The accountability loop

The pipeline's feedback edge — stage 10 back to stage 1 — deserves its own treatment, because it is the engine that turns a one-time process into a *compounding* one. The loop has five moves, and the order is load-bearing.

![The accountability loop from writing a view through grading the process to a sharper next call](/imgs/blogs/the-analysts-edge-playbook-the-complete-view-forming-process-4.png)

**Write the view** first — claim, probability, size, invalidation — *before* entry, so there is a fixed record to grade against. **The outcome** then lands: the P&L, which is a noisy, single-sample signal you must resist over-reading. **Grade the process** separately from the outcome: was the decision good given what was knowable, regardless of how it turned out? **Calibrate** across many calls: when you say 65%, does it happen 65% of the time? **Improve**: fix the one weakest stage and update your checklist. The result feeds the **next view** — sharper read, better sizing, tighter exits.

The reason the loop compounds is that it grades the only thing you actually control. You do not control outcomes; markets are noisy and a good call can lose. You *do* control the quality of the decision and the calibration of the probabilities — and those are exactly what the loop measures and improves. An analyst without this loop is running open-loop: making decisions, getting noisy feedback, and unable to tell skill from luck, which means unable to improve in any direction except by accident. The loop is what makes the second analyst's *next* CPI call better than this one.

#### Worked example: the accountability loop's math on a \$100,000 account over one year

Take two analysts, each with a genuine but modest edge, each making 50 independent calls a year on a **\$100,000** account, each risking **2%** (\$2,000) per call. Suppose each call has a true expectancy of **+0.4R** — for every \$1 risked, the expected gain is \$0.40 — so each call's EV is +0.4 × \$2,000 = **+\$800**. Fifty calls: 50 × \$800 = **\$40,000** of expected edge in the year if both capture their full edge.

Analyst A runs the accountability loop. She writes every call, grades process over outcome, and calibrates quarterly. Her first calibration check shows her 70%-confidence calls hit only 55% — she is overconfident — so she shades probabilities down, which corrects her sizing (she was over-betting), and she fixes one leak: she had been exiting winners early on noise. The fixes lift her *realized* expectancy from a leaky +0.25R toward the full +0.4R over the year. Her realized edge: roughly 50 × 0.25R early, climbing to 0.4R, averaging ~0.32R → ~50 × \$640 = **+\$32,000**.

Analyst B never reviews. He has the same true edge but bleeds it three ways: uniform sizing (his best calls and worst calls both risk \$2,000, so his edge concentration is zero), early exits on noise (clipping winners), and resulting (he abandoned a good strategy after a string of unlucky losses and chased a worse one that had recently worked). His realized expectancy collapses to ~+0.05R → 50 × \$100 = **+\$5,000**, and that is *before* the deeper risk that an un-reviewed overconfidence trend leads him to over-size into a drawdown and breach his risk limits entirely. Same raw edge, same number of calls, same account. The \$27,000 gap in one year is not analysis. It is the accountability loop — and it compounds: A's process is sharper next year while B's is exactly as leaky.

## The failure-modes-to-preventing-stage map

Here is the most useful single artifact in this post for a working analyst. Every repeatable way analysts lose money maps to *one skipped stage*. Run the whole pipeline and you have already built the defense against each.

![Failure modes mapped to the pipeline stage that prevents each one](/imgs/blogs/the-analysts-edge-playbook-the-complete-view-forming-process-5.png)

Read the matrix as a diagnostic. When a call goes wrong, the post-mortem almost always points at a skipped stage, and the matrix tells you which one to reinstate.

- **No falsifier** — holding a loser forever because you never defined what "wrong" looks like. *Prevented by stage 4:* write the invalidation level before entry.
- **Wrong size** — betting favorites too big and blowing up, or sizing uniformly and capturing no edge. *Prevented by stage 5:* map conviction and asymmetry to dollars at risk, capped for survival.
- **Resulting** — judging a good decision by its bad outcome (or a bad one by its lucky win). *Prevented by stage 10:* grade the process, not the result.
- **Narrative-driven** — a compelling story overriding the data. *Prevented by stage 3:* run the pre-mortem and argue the bear case before you buy.
- **Never reviewing** — repeating the same mistake for years because there is no record. *Prevented by stage 10:* keep the decision journal and calibrate quarterly.

The deeper point: the pipeline is not an academic flowchart. It is a *catalog of expensive mistakes, each with the stage that prevents it attached*. You do not have to discover these failures the hard way with your own capital. They are known, they cluster into the same handful of categories across every market and instrument, and each one is defended by one stage you simply must not skip.

#### Worked example: a failure caught by its preventing stage

You are long a \$25,000 position in a semiconductor stock at \$100, thesis: a product cycle will beat consensus revenue at the next earnings print (the catalyst). At entry you wrote — because stage 4 forced you to — the invalidation: *exit if the stock closes below \$92 (an 8% adverse move, ~\$2,000 of risk on the position) or if guidance is cut.* Three weeks before earnings, the stock drifts to \$93 on a sector downgrade. Your gut, deep in narrative addiction, says "the downgrade is noise, my catalyst is still ahead, average down." This is the exact moment failure modes are born.

But you run the monitor stage against the dashboard, not the gut. The dashboard asks two questions: has price hit the invalidation level? *Not yet — \$93 is above \$92.* Has a thesis-driver flipped? *No — no guidance cut, the product cycle is intact.* So this is **noise**, and you hold; you do *not* average down, because stage 9 forbids letting the position drive the view. Two days later the stock closes at \$91.40 *and* a supplier warns on the product cycle — a thesis-driver has flipped. Now stage 4's line and stage 8's broken-vs-noise test both fire: you exit at the open near \$91, taking a **\$2,250** loss — close to the budgeted \$2,000, no catastrophe. Had you averaged down at \$93 into a \$50,000 position, the same break would have cost **~\$9,000** and you would still be holding, hoping. The written invalidation (stage 4) and the broken-vs-noise discipline (stage 8) turned a potential account-denting disaster into a controlled, expected loss. *That is the pipeline doing its job: not avoiding losses, but bounding them.*

## How it plays out: a compressed end-to-end run into an FOMC

The full case study lives in [case study: forming a cross-asset view into an FOMC decision](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/case-study-forming-a-cross-asset-view-into-an-fomc-decision). Here is the compressed version, every stage firing in sequence, so you can see the machine run once start to finish.

It is the week of an FOMC meeting. **Stage 1 (diet):** your curated sources flag that the prior two CPI prints surprised hot and that the labor market is still tight; you ignore the wall of television speculation. **Stage 2 (synthesis):** across six lenses — macro (sticky inflation), positioning (the market is leaning dovish, pricing cuts soon), flows (real-money has been adding duration), valuation (front-end yields look low for the inflation path), technicals (the 2-year is at support), sentiment (consensus is "the Fed is nearly done") — the one-sentence read is: *the market is pricing cuts too soon given sticky inflation.* **Stage 3 (thesis):** claim — front-end yields rise; evidence — two hot prints, tight labor, dovish positioning; catalyst — the FOMC statement and the dot plot, three days out. Base/bull/bear scenarios drawn; pre-mortem says the main risk is a genuinely dovish surprise, which you accept.

**Stage 4 (probability + EV + invalidation):** you assign **60%** to "front-end repricing higher," draw the decision tree around the dot plot, compute a positive EV because the asymmetry is good, and write the invalidation: *exit if the 2-year yield falls 20 bp from entry or the dots come in below current pricing.* **Stage 5 (conviction + size):** conviction is your 60% minus the market's ~45%, weighted by trust — a real but moderate edge, so you risk **2%** of a \$100,000 account = **\$2,000**, not your maximum. **Stage 6 (expression):** the cleanest expression is short 2-year Treasury futures sized so a 20 bp adverse move costs the budgeted \$2,000. **Stage 7 (write):** the one-page trade-idea note is dated and filed before entry. **Stage 8 (monitor):** you watch the dot plot and the 2-year yield, not the P&L. **Stage 9 (update):** the FOMC comes in hawkish-of-expectations, the dots rise, the 2-year sells off, the thesis confirms — you hold and let it work to a pre-defined target. **Stage 10 (review):** weeks later you log it: the *decision* was sound and well-sized, the calibration check confirms your 60% calls are running near 60%, and the lesson — *positioning that leans one way amplifies the surprise* — feeds back into stage 1. One loop of the machine, closed.

#### Worked example: the compressed end-to-end run, with the dollar math tied together

Take the FOMC view above and run every number through the machine on a **\$100,000** account, so you can see how stages 3 through 6 chain into a single sized position. The thesis (stage 3) is "front-end yields rise on a hawkish dot plot," with the catalyst three days out. The scenarios: **bull** (hawkish surprise, 2-year rises ~25 bp, the position makes **+\$5,000**) at probability 0.60; **bear** (dovish surprise, 2-year falls and hits the 20 bp stop, the position loses the budgeted **\$2,000**) at probability 0.40. The expected value (stage 4) is 0.60 × (+\$5,000) + 0.40 × (−\$2,000) = \$3,000 − \$800 = **+\$2,200**. Positive EV, and the asymmetry (\$5,000 up vs. \$2,000 down, a 2.5-to-1 reward-to-risk on a better-than-even probability) is what makes it a bet worth making, not the confidence alone.

Conviction (stage 5) is your 60% minus the market's ~45% priced probability = a **15-point edge**, which you trust at a moderate level, so a fractional sizing rule lands you at **2%** of the account = **\$2,000** of risk, not the 4% you would stake on a high-conviction, highly asymmetric call. The invalidation (stage 4) is a 20 bp drop in the 2-year; you size the futures position (stage 6) precisely so that a 20 bp adverse move *is* a \$2,000 loss — the stop and the size are two views of the same number. Notice the chain: the scenarios set the EV, the EV plus the edge set the conviction, the conviction sets the dollar risk, and the dollar risk plus the invalidation level set the position size. Change any one and the rest move. **A view is not "I think rates go up"; it is this whole chain of numbers, written down before entry, where a wrong call costs exactly \$2,000 and a right one makes \$5,000.** That chain is the difference between the second analyst and the first.

## Common misconceptions

The biggest mistakes in this craft are *meta-mistakes* — not errors inside a stage, but the refusal to run the stage at all. Six are worth naming, because they are the failure modes that no amount of market knowledge can fix.

**"I don't need a written process; I keep it in my head."** This is the master mistake, the one that contains all the others. An un-written process is not a process; it is a series of improvisations you will remember selectively and flatteringly. Without the written record there is no falsification, no honest review, no calibration — and therefore no compounding. The whole edge of the second analyst over the first was not knowledge; it was that he *ran a process and wrote it down*. If you take one thing from this series, take the habit of writing the call before the money goes on.

**"If I'm confident enough, I don't need an invalidation level."** Backwards. The more confident you are, the *more* you need a defined kill line, because high confidence is exactly the state in which you will rationalize holding a loser. An invalidation is not an admission of doubt; it is the structural guarantee that a wrong call costs you a bounded, pre-budgeted amount instead of an unbounded one. A view with no falsifier is unfalsifiable, and an unfalsifiable view is not analysis.

**"I made money, so it was a good call."** This is *resulting*, and it is how good processes get unlearned. A single outcome tells you almost nothing about the quality of a decision, because markets are noisy: good decisions lose all the time and bad ones win all the time, in the short run. Grade the *decision* by what was knowable at the time. If you judge by P&L, you will abandon your best strategies after unlucky streaks and double down on reckless ones after lucky wins — the precise opposite of learning.

**"The story is so compelling it has to be right."** A compelling narrative is a *warning sign*, not confirmation. Stories are stickier than data because they are easy to remember and emotionally satisfying, which is exactly why they override the spreadsheet at the worst moment. The pre-mortem exists to drag the bear case onto the page while you are still calm enough to write it. The most expensive trades are the ones where the story was too good to question.

**"My conviction is high, so I'll just size big."** Conviction is not a feeling to be sized off; it is your probability *minus the consensus*, weighted by how much you trust your edge, and then run through a sizing rule that caps any single bet for survival. "I feel strongly" is not a number. Sizing off emotion is how analysts who are *right* still blow up — they bet the account on the one they loved and met the one time it was wrong.

**"I'll review when I have time."** You will not. Review is the stage with no immediate reward — the call is closed, the P&L is booked, the dopamine is spent — which is exactly why it is the one everyone skips and the one that separates compounders from churners. Schedule it. The decision journal and the quarterly calibration check are non-negotiable, because they are the only mechanism that turns this year's experience into next year's skill.

## The playbook

This is the page to pin above your desk. Run it on any market, any asset, any horizon.

![The master one page checklist of ten questions from information to review](/imgs/blogs/the-analysts-edge-playbook-the-complete-view-forming-process-7.png)

### The one-page master checklist

Run these ten in order. If you cannot answer one, you have not finished forming the view.

1. **Diet.** What in my inputs is signal, not noise? Have I read my curated sources, not the firehose?
2. **Read.** What is the one-sentence read across all six lenses — and where do the lenses disagree?
3. **Thesis.** What is the claim, the evidence, and the *catalyst*? Does it survive a pre-mortem? Have I drawn base/bull/bear?
4. **Probability + EV + invalidation.** What is P? What is the EV given the scenario payoffs? What is the *specific level or event* that proves me wrong — written before entry?
5. **Conviction + size.** What is my probability *minus* the consensus, and how much do I trust it? How many dollars at risk does that map to, capped for survival?
6. **Expression.** Which instrument pays this view best — directional, relative value, or options — sized so the invalidation costs exactly my budgeted risk?
7. **Write.** Is the call on paper, dated, *before* the money went on — as a trade-idea note, memo, or commentary?
8. **Monitor.** Am I watching the thesis-drivers, not the P&L? Is this move my thesis breaking, or just noise?
9. **Update.** Does every add, cut, or exit trace back to a change in the written thesis — not to my P&L or my ego?
10. **Review.** Have I logged it, graded the *process* not the outcome, checked my calibration, fixed one leak, and fed the lesson back to step 1?

And cross-cutting, at every step: **am I confirming a belief I already held, falling for a good story, or anchoring on a stale number?**

### The one rule above the checklist

If the whole page is too much to hold at once, hold this: **write the call down — claim, probability, size, invalidation — then grade the decision separately from the outcome, and feed the lesson back in.** Every other stage is in service of making that one accountable loop possible.

### The 30-day plan to build the habits

You do not install ten stages at once. Build one front-of-pipeline habit and one back-of-pipeline habit per week, each with a measurable checkpoint.

![The thirty day plan to build the view forming habits week by week](/imgs/blogs/the-analysts-edge-playbook-the-complete-view-forming-process-6.png)

- **Days 1–7 — Read and synthesize.** Build the 20-minute daily routine; every day, collapse your read into one sentence across the six lenses. *Checkpoint: seven dated one-sentence reads, written.*
- **Days 8–14 — Write the thesis.** For every position (real or paper), write the claim, the probability, and the invalidation level on paper *before* entry. *Checkpoint: every trade this week has a dated note with an invalidation level — zero exceptions.*
- **Days 15–21 — Size and express.** For each thesis, compute the EV, set conviction = your P minus consensus, map it to dollars at risk, and pick the instrument. *Checkpoint: every position sized off a written EV and conviction number, not a feeling.*
- **Days 22–30 — Monitor and review.** Build the watch dashboard; on every closed call, grade process vs. outcome in the journal and plot your first calibration curve. *Checkpoint by day 30: 10+ logged calls, your first calibration curve, and one identified-and-fixed process leak.*

#### Worked example: the 30-day plan's measurable checkpoints on a \$100,000 account

You start the plan with a **\$100,000** account and a 2%-per-call risk budget (\$2,000). The point of the checkpoints is that they are *countable*, so you cannot fool yourself about whether the habit is installed. By day 7 you should have seven dated one-sentence reads in a file — if you have three, the diet habit is not built, repeat the week. By day 14, every position you opened should carry a written invalidation; audit it by counting positions and counting notes, and the two numbers must match. Suppose you opened five positions that week — you need five dated notes, each with a kill line such as "exit if the 2-year falls 20 bp." Four notes means one undisciplined trade; the gate is binary.

By day 21, each position is sized off a written EV: for a 60% call with a 3-to-1 payoff structure risking \$2,000, the written EV is 0.6 × \$6,000 − 0.4 × \$2,000 = \$3,600 − \$800 = **+\$2,800**, and that number, not your enthusiasm, justified the size. By day 30 you have 10+ closed-and-logged calls and your first calibration curve — say it shows your "70%" calls hit 11 of 18, about 61%, flagging mild overconfidence. The fix — shade 70s toward 65 — is your one identified leak. Notice what the plan delivered: not a profit number (30 days is far too short to judge a process by P&L — that would be *resulting*), but four *installed habits* and one measured leak. The P&L is the outcome; the four checkpoints are the process, and the process is the only thing 30 days can honestly grade.

### The analyst's edge as compounding skill

End where the series began. The two analysts on 13 September 2022 had the same information and opposite results. The gap was never knowledge — it was that one of them ran a complete, accountable process and the other ran his mouth. The pipeline in this post is that process, drawn whole: ten stages that convert what you already know into a sized, falsifiable, written, monitored, and *reviewed* call, with the result fed back to sharpen the next one.

The reason it is worth the discipline is that it *compounds*. A single well-run call is worth a little. But the accountability loop means each call makes the next one better-calibrated, better-sized, and better-defended — so a decade of running the pipeline does not just give you a decade of P&L; it gives you a decade of *sharpening*, and the analyst at year ten is operating on a level the year-one analyst cannot reach by working harder. Mechanisms commoditize; the people who learned them yesterday are competing against the people who will learn them tomorrow. But a calibrated, disciplined, self-correcting *process* is the one edge that does not commoditize, because it is built from the one input no one can copy from a research note: your own honest, accountable judgment, sharpened call after call after call.

And the compounding is not metaphorical. Each closed-and-graded call is one data point about your own process — one calibration sample, one confirmed-or-refuted assumption about which of your reads have edge, one chance to find and fix a leak. An analyst who logs and reviews 50 calls a year accumulates 500 such data points in a decade; an analyst who never reviews accumulates zero, no matter how many trades they make, because an un-graded trade teaches nothing. The first analyst's probabilities get sharper, their sizing tightens around their real edge, and their failure modes get caught earlier each year. The second is running in place — busy, experienced in the shallow sense of having lived through many markets, but no better calibrated than they were on day one. The pipeline's loop is the mechanism that turns *time spent in markets* into *skill*, and without it, time spent in markets is just time spent. That is the analyst's edge. Now go run it.
