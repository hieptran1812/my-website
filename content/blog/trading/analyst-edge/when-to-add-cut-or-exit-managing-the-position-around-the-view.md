> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Managing an open position is a sequence of decisions — add, hold, trim, cut, exit — and each one must be driven by the thesis and the risk budget, never by the P&L or the emotion of the moment.
>
> - **Add only when conviction rises AND the risk budget allows.** A new confirming fact plus room under your heat cap justifies pyramiding into a winner. "It's down, so I'll average in" is the opposite move and the most dangerous one on the menu.
> - **Trim or cut on thesis decay or a risk-limit breach.** When the driver weakens, when a scenario target is hit, or when your open risk runs over its cap, you take size off — and you bank partial profits on the way up rather than round-tripping the whole gain.
> - **Exit on invalidation or catalyst resolution.** When the fact that defines "I am wrong" prints, or the catalyst you were waiting for has resolved, the trade is over regardless of the P&L. Write all of this down *at entry*, so exiting is execution, not a decision under fire.
> - The one rule: **never average down a broken thesis** — a position whose story has failed gets smaller, never larger.

Two traders put on the identical winning position. Both buy a stock at \$50 on a clean earnings beat with raised guidance, both risk 1% of a \$100,000 account, both are up nicely six weeks later when the stock trades \$58. Here is where they diverge, and where their careers diverge with them. The first trader watches a second confirming data point arrive — a competitor's weak print that validates the share-gain thesis — checks that her portfolio still has risk budget to spare, and *adds* to the position, trailing her stop up so her total risk stays capped. The stock runs to \$70 and she trims it in pieces on the way, banking realized gains while letting a runner ride. She turns one good read into a position that compounded.

The second trader does the opposite of every one of those moves. At \$58 he is up \$2,000 and terrified of giving it back, so he sells the whole thing — "you never go broke taking a profit" — and watches it run to \$70 without him. Then, itching to redeploy, he buys a *different* stock that he likes less, on thinner evidence. It goes against him immediately. Rather than honoring the invalidation he never bothered to define, he tells himself it is "cheap now" and buys more to lower his average. It keeps falling. He buys more. By the time he finally capitulates, a position he intended to size at 1% has cost him 5% of his account, and the only winner he had this quarter is the one he cut for a sliver of its potential. One trader compounds; the other bleeds. The views were not the difference. The *management* was.

This post is about everything that happens *after* the position is on. The rest of this series builds the view — reading the lenses, finding the variant perception, quantifying the edge, sizing the bet. But a position is not a static object you set and forget. It is a live thing that you steer, and the quality of your steering — when you add, when you hold, when you trim, when you cut, when you walk away entirely — determines whether a good entry becomes a great trade or a round-trip, and whether a bad entry becomes a small loss or a catastrophe. The single governing idea is this: **manage the position around the thesis and the risk, never around the P&L or the feeling.** Everything below is the machinery for doing that with discipline.

![Decision tree from an open position through thesis health to add hold trim cut or exit](/imgs/blogs/when-to-add-cut-or-exit-managing-the-position-around-the-view-1.png)

The figure above is the whole post in one picture, and we will earn the right to trust every branch. You start at an open position and you read two things — the health of the thesis and the state of your risk budget — *not* the unrealized P&L. If the thesis got stronger and you have room, you add. If it is intact and nothing changed, you hold and let the winner run. If it is decaying or you have breached a risk limit, you trim. If it broke — the invalidation fired, or the driver is gone, or the catalyst has resolved — you cut or exit the whole thing. Each branch is a rule, decided in advance, executed without negotiation. The trader who bled in the opening took the wrong branch at every node because he was reading his P&L and his fear instead of the tree.

## Foundations: what position management actually is

Start with the words, because the discipline lives in keeping two ideas separate.

**Position management** is the set of decisions you make about an *existing* position — how much of it to hold, when to add to it, when to reduce it, and when to close it entirely. It is distinct from *forming the view* (the work that got you into the trade) and distinct from *execution* (the mechanics of getting filled). Once the position is on, management is the day job: you are no longer deciding whether to take the trade — you took it — you are deciding what to do with it now, and again tomorrow, and again the day after, as new information arrives and the price moves and your risk picture shifts.

The decisions form a small, fixed menu. Every moment you look at an open position, exactly one of five actions is correct:

- **Add** — increase the position size. You commit more capital and (usually) more risk to a view you already hold.
- **Hold** — do nothing. The position stays exactly as it is. This is the default and, counterintuitively, the hardest action to take, because doing nothing feels like not managing.
- **Trim** — reduce the position size partially. You take some off, banking a portion of the gain or shrinking the risk, while keeping a stake in the view.
- **Cut** — reduce the position sharply because something has gone wrong, distinct from a planned trim. A cut is a defensive reduction; a trim is an offensive one.
- **Exit** — close the position entirely. The view is over, for good or ill, and you have zero stake left.

The entire skill is choosing the right one of these five at each moment, on the right basis. And the right basis — the thing that separates the trader who compounds from the trader who bleeds — is the central distinction of this whole post.

### Managing the position versus managing the view

There are two different objects in front of you when you look at an open trade, and conflating them is the root of nearly every management error. The first object is the **view**: your thesis about what the asset is worth and why, the catalyst that will close the gap, and the fact that would prove you wrong. The second object is the **position**: the dollars you currently have at risk, the unrealized P&L, the stop level, and the share count. These are not the same thing, and they do not move together.

Managing *the view* means asking, "Is my thesis still true? Did the driver I bet on get stronger, weaker, or break? Is the catalyst still coming?" Managing *the position* means asking, "How much should I have on right now, given the answer to that first question and given my risk budget?" The correct workflow runs in exactly that order: read the view, *then* set the position to match it. A strengthening view should pull the position bigger (if risk allows); a decaying view should push it smaller; a broken view should take it to zero.

The fatal error is to skip the first object entirely and manage the position off its own P&L — to add because the trade is down ("better price"), to cut because the trade is up ("lock in the gain"), to hold a loser because selling would "realize" the loss. None of those decisions consulted the thesis at all. They are reactions to the number on the screen, and the number on the screen is the *least* informative input you have, because it tells you what already happened, not whether your thesis is still alive. We will return to this constantly: **the P&L is a consequence of your decisions, not an input to them.**

### Why P&L-driven management destroys edge

Here is the uncomfortable mechanism. Edge — the reason you have a positive expectancy at all — lives in the asymmetry between your wins and your losses, not in your win rate. (The deep version of this is in [expectancy: why win rate lies](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/expectancy-why-win-rate-lies); it matters here because it explains *why* P&L management is poison.) A positive-expectancy strategy makes money by letting the winners run large and keeping the losers small. P&L-driven management does the precise opposite: it cuts winners early (because a green number triggers the fear of giving it back) and holds losers late (because a red number triggers the hope of getting back to even). Cut your winners small and ride your losers large, and you have inverted the exact asymmetry that your edge depends on. You can have a *correct* set of views and still lose money, because the management around those views systematically truncated the gains and extended the losses.

Put it in one line: P&L-driven management trades on the emotion the number provokes — fear when green, hope when red — and those two emotions point you toward the two worst possible actions. The whole discipline of this post is a set of rules designed to override those emotions with pre-committed, thesis-driven triggers, so that the management *amplifies* your edge instead of bleeding it away.

## The five actions, and the trigger for each

Walk through the menu one action at a time. For each, the question is the same: *what, specifically, must be true for this action to be correct?* A management rule that cannot name its trigger is not a rule — it is a mood.

### Rules for ADDING

Adding is the most seductive and most dangerous action, because it can be either the best move on the board (pyramiding a confirmed winner) or the worst (averaging down a broken loser), and the two feel similar in the moment — both are "I'm buying more of something I own." The trigger is what separates them, and the trigger has two conditions that must *both* hold.

**Condition one: conviction has risen on new confirming evidence.** You do not add because the price moved. You add because a *new fact* arrived that makes your thesis more likely to be true than it was when you entered. A competitor pre-announced weakly, validating your share-gain thesis. The company guided up again, extending the runway. A macro print confirmed the regime your trade depends on. Crucially, this is new information that updates your probability of success upward — the disciplined Bayesian move covered in [updating on new information](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/updating-on-new-information-thinking-like-a-bayesian) — not just the comforting feeling that comes from a position going your way. A rising price with *no new confirming fact* is not a reason to add; it is just the market agreeing with the view you already had.

**Condition two: the risk budget has room.** Even with rising conviction, you can only add if your portfolio heat — the sum of what you would lose if every open position hit its stop — still has headroom under your cap after the add. If you are already at your 6% heat cap, a new confirming fact does not create new risk budget; you would have to free some up (by trimming another position) before you can add. Adding without checking heat is how a "high-conviction" book quietly becomes a leveraged directional bet. (The heat-cap mechanics are built in [from conviction to size: the bet-sizing bridge](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/from-conviction-to-size-the-bet-sizing-bridge).)

What adding is *not*: it is not "the stock is down, so I'll buy more at a better average price." That move adds risk to a position the market is telling you is going wrong, on the basis of *no new confirming evidence* — in fact, usually in the face of *dis*confirming evidence, since the price action is against you. We will give that error its own section, because it deserves it.

### Rules for TRIMMING

Trimming is reducing size while keeping a stake. There are four legitimate triggers, and notice that none of them is "I'm up and I'm scared."

**Trigger one: a risk-limit breach.** Your position, or your portfolio, has crossed a risk threshold you set in advance. A single position has grown (through a winning run plus an add) to where it is now 4% of your equity and your single-name cap is 3% — you trim it back to 3%. Or your total portfolio heat has crept above its cap and you need to bring it down — you trim the position that has the worst thesis-to-risk ratio. The breach is a number you defined when calm, and crossing it is a mechanical trigger to reduce, no judgment required.

**Trigger two: conviction decay.** The thesis is weaker than it was, but not broken. A driver you were counting on has softened — guidance was reiterated but not raised, a competitor matched your company's move, the macro tailwind flattened. The view is not *wrong*, but it is *less right*, and a less-right view deserves a smaller position. Trimming on decay is the symmetric opposite of adding on confirmation: as your probability of success drifts down, your size drifts down with it.

**Trigger three: hitting a scenario target.** When you built your base/bull/bear scenarios, you attached price targets to them (the discipline is in [base, bull, and bear: building three scenarios](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/base-bull-and-bear-building-three-scenarios)). When the price reaches your base-case target, a meaningful chunk of the edge you identified has been realized — the gap between price and value that justified the trade has closed. Trimming at a scenario target is taking the chips the thesis said were there. This is the offensive trim, and it is the basis of the partial-profit ladder we will build below.

**Trigger four: trimming portfolio heat to make room.** A new, better opportunity has appeared, but you are at your heat cap. To take it, you trim an existing position — ideally the one with the weakest remaining thesis or the worst reward-to-remaining-risk. This is portfolio-level management: the positions compete for a fixed risk budget, and a trim here funds an entry there.

#### Worked example: trimming on a risk-limit breach

You run a \$100,000 account with a single-name risk cap of 3% — no one position may put more than \$3,000 of equity at risk if it hits its stop. You entered a stock at \$50 risking the standard 1% (\$1,000): 250 shares with a \$46 stop, a \$4 stop distance. The thesis is confirming and the stock runs to \$60, so you pyramid twice on fresh evidence, taking the position to 600 shares and trailing your stop up to \$55 as you go. Now check the risk on the *combined* position against your cap. If the stop at \$55 triggers, the position's outcome relative to your blended entry is a gain — but suppose instead the run was sharp and your trailing stop still sits at \$52 while your blended cost is \$53.50: a stop-out would lose 600 × (\$53.50 − \$52) = **\$900** — fine, under cap. The breach you actually need to watch is *notional concentration*: 600 shares at \$60 is a **\$36,000 position**, 36% of your equity, and your single-name *exposure* cap is 25% of equity (\$25,000). You have breached the concentration limit even though the stop-loss risk is small, because a sudden gap *below* your stop (an overnight earnings miss, a halt) could lose far more than the \$900 the orderly stop implies. The rule binds: trim the position back to \$25,000 notional = roughly 417 shares, taking 183 shares off at \$60 and banking 183 × (\$60 − \$50 blended-ish entry) of realized profit in the process. You did not trim because you were scared or because the thesis weakened — the thesis is fine. You trimmed because a *risk limit you set in advance* was breached, and the limit is non-negotiable. Trimming on a breach is mechanical: when the number crosses the line, you reduce, no judgment required.

### Rules for CUTTING and EXITING

Cutting and exiting both reduce or eliminate the position because the *view* has changed, not because of the P&L. The triggers:

**Trigger one: invalidation is hit.** You defined, *before* entering, the specific fact or price level at which your thesis is wrong (the discipline is in [what would change my mind: defining invalidation upfront](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/what-would-change-my-mind-defining-invalidation-upfront)). When it prints, you exit. No renegotiation, no "let me give it one more day." The invalidation is the line you drew in cold blood; honoring it when it fires is the entire reason you drew it. This is the most important exit, because it is the one your emotion will fight hardest.

**Trigger two: the thesis driver broke.** The specific causal mechanism your trade depended on has been falsified, even if the price has not yet moved to your invalidation level. You bet on share gains; the company admitted it is *losing* share. You bet on a rate cut; the central bank signaled a hold. When the *driver* dies, the trade is dead, and you should not wait for the price to confirm what the fundamentals already told you. (Distinguishing a broken driver from ordinary noise is the hardest call in the craft, treated in [thesis broken or just noise](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/thesis-broken-or-just-noise-the-hardest-call-you-make).)

**Trigger three: the catalyst resolved.** Many trades are bets on a specific event — an earnings report, an FOMC decision, a product launch, a court ruling. Once that event has happened and been digested, the reason the trade existed is gone, regardless of whether you made or lost money. Holding a position past its catalyst is holding a position with no remaining edge — you are now just exposed to noise. Exit when the catalyst resolves and the gap it was supposed to close has closed (or failed to).

**Trigger four: a clearly better opportunity.** Capital is finite, and so is your attention and your risk budget. If a new idea has materially better expected value than a position you hold, and you cannot fund the new one without freeing capacity, exiting the weaker position to fund the stronger one is correct. The test is "clearly better" — a marginal improvement does not justify the transaction costs and the tax drag of churning; a step-change in edge does.

The common thread across all five actions is that *every trigger is a fact about the thesis or the risk, and none is a fact about the P&L.* You add on confirming evidence and budget room. You hold on an intact thesis. You trim on decay, a target, or a breach. You cut on invalidation, a broken driver, a resolved catalyst, or a better opportunity. The unrealized gain or loss is never on this list, and that absence is the whole point.

![Grid mapping thesis health states strengthening intact decaying broken to add hold trim exit](/imgs/blogs/when-to-add-cut-or-exit-managing-the-position-around-the-view-2.png)

The grid above collapses the menu into a single lookup table that you can run in seconds. Read the left column — the state of your thesis — and the action falls out. Strengthening thesis, new confirming evidence: ADD (scale up, if heat allows), because the edge grew and so should the size. Intact, story unchanged, just noise: HOLD, let it run to target, because nothing changed. Decaying, the driver weakening or risk near the limit: TRIM, cut size and bank profit, because the edge shrank and so should the size. Broken, invalidation hit or the driver gone: EXIT, close it all, because the edge is gone and the size should be zero. The position size, read across the rows, tracks thesis-health monotonically — bigger as the view confirms, smaller as it decays, zero when it breaks. That monotonic relationship *is* disciplined management. P&L-driven management breaks the monotonicity: it adds to losers (size up on a broken thesis) and cuts winners (size down on an intact one), which is the table run upside down.

## Pyramiding winners versus averaging down losers

The two actions that look most alike and behave most differently are pyramiding and averaging down. Both are "adding to an existing position." The difference is everything, and getting it straight is worth a section of its own because it is where the most account-ending damage happens.

**Pyramiding** is adding to a *winner* whose thesis is *confirming*. The price has moved your way *because the market is coming around to the view that new evidence has strengthened.* You add a smaller increment on top of your original (hence the pyramid — each tranche smaller than the last), and critically, you *trail your stop up* behind the price so that your total risk on the combined position stays inside your budget. Done right, pyramiding lets a confirmed winner become a large position without ever exposing you to more than your defined risk, because the original tranche is now protected by a stop above your entry. You are scaling into strength, on rising conviction, with risk capped.

**Averaging down** is adding to a *loser* whose price has moved *against* you, in order to lower your average cost. The seductive logic is "if I liked it at \$50, I love it at \$42." The fatal flaw is that the move against you is *information* — it is, more often than you want to admit, the market telling you something your thesis missed — and averaging down responds to that disconfirming information by *increasing* your bet. You are scaling into weakness, against the evidence, with risk expanding. A position you sized to lose 1% if wrong now stands to lose 2%, 3%, more, on the exact trade the market is voting against. And because you have anchored to "my average is now lower," you are *less* likely to honor your invalidation, not more — you have given yourself a reason to hold past the line you drew.

There is exactly one legitimate version of "adding to a position that is underwater," and it is not averaging down: it is **scaling into a planned position** where the lower price was *anticipated in the original plan* and the total intended risk was budgeted from the start. If your entry plan was "buy a third here, a third if it dips to support, a third on confirmation," and the dip to support was a *planned* tranche whose risk was already counted, then buying that second third is executing the plan, not averaging down — provided the thesis is still intact and your invalidation has not moved. The distinction is whether the add was *pre-committed and risk-budgeted* (legitimate) or *improvised to relieve the pain of a loss* (ruinous). If you are adding because it hurts to be down, you are averaging down, whatever you call it.

![Before and after comparison of averaging down a loser versus pyramiding a winner](/imgs/blogs/when-to-add-cut-or-exit-managing-the-position-around-the-view-3.png)

The before-and-after makes the divergence stark. On the left, averaging down: the thesis is broken but the price is down, so you add to lower the cost basis; your risk doubles on a failing trade; the result is a bigger loss that you are slower to admit because your anchor moved. On the right, pyramiding: the thesis is confirmed by new evidence, so you add on strength with the stop trailed up; your total risk stays inside the budget; the result is a bigger gain with the loss still capped. The two columns are the same action — "buy more" — pointed in opposite directions by a single question: *did the thesis confirm or break?* Answer that honestly first, and the add decision answers itself.

#### Worked example: averaging down a broken thesis versus pyramiding an intact one

Run both paths in dollars on a \$100,000 account that risks 1% (\$1,000) per trade. You buy a stock at \$50 with an invalidation at \$46, so your \$4 stop distance gives you 250 shares — a **\$20,000 position** risking exactly \$1,000.

**The averaging-down path.** The stock falls to \$42. Worse, the reason is a fact that *broke your thesis*: the company conceded it is losing the market share your entire trade was built on. You should exit here — the driver is gone — taking your defined loss. Instead, you tell yourself it is "cheap now" and buy another 250 shares at \$42, taking your position to **500 shares** worth roughly \$21,000 at an average cost of \$46. You have not lowered your risk; you have *doubled the share count on a broken trade* and moved your mental anchor to \$46, which makes you even less willing to sell. The stock keeps falling — broken theses tend to — to \$38, where you finally capitulate. Your loss: 500 shares × (\$46 average − \$38) = **\$4,000**, four times the \$1,000 you signed up for. The averaging-down increment is the entire reason a \$1,000 mistake became a \$4,000 one.

**The pyramiding path.** Same entry, 250 shares at \$50, \$1,000 risk. This time the stock *rises* to \$56, and it rises because a *new confirming fact* arrived — a competitor pre-announced a miss, validating your share-gain thesis and raising your probability of success. You check your heat: you have room. So you pyramid, adding 150 shares at \$56 (a smaller tranche), taking your position to **400 shares**, and you trail your stop up to \$53 — above your original \$50 entry. Now compute your *total risk on the combined position* if the trailed stop triggers: the original 250 shares lose nothing (stop at \$53 is above the \$50 entry — they bank a \$3 gain), and the new 150 shares lose (\$56 − \$53) = \$3 each, or \$450. Net, a stop-out now would *bank* roughly \$750 — \$3 × 250 minus \$3 × 150 worked through is a guaranteed profit, not a loss. You added size, your conviction was confirmed, and your downside went from a \$1,000 loss to a locked-in gain. That is the asymmetry pyramiding buys you: more size on the trades that are working, with the risk on the whole stack trailed to safety.

The two paths started identically — a 250-share position at \$50 risking \$1,000. The averaging-down path turned a \$1,000 risk into a \$4,000 loss. The pyramiding path turned it into a larger position with a *locked-in profit*. The single fork was whether the new information confirmed or broke the thesis, and whether the add respected the risk budget. Pyramid intact theses; never average down broken ones.

## Taking partial profits: the trim ladder

Holding a winner all the way to a single target and selling the whole thing at once is psychologically brutal and practically fragile — you are betting the entire gain on getting one exit exactly right, and the fear of round-tripping it makes you far more likely to cut the whole thing early out of nerves. The fix is to **scale out in tranches**, taking partial profits at predefined rungs as the price climbs. A partial-profit ladder banks realized gains along the way — which relieves the psychological pressure that causes premature full exits — while keeping a "runner" exposed to the thesis's full upside.

The mechanics are simple. You decide, at entry, the price rungs at which you will trim and how much you will take at each. A common structure is to trim a third of the *remaining* position at each rung, so you take progressively fewer shares as the price rises (you are always selling a third of what is left, not a third of the original). This keeps a meaningful runner alive even after several trims, so that if the thesis delivers its bull case, you still have exposure to it. The realized gains you bank at the lower rungs are money in the account that the market cannot take back, and that certainty is what lets you hold the runner calmly instead of grabbing the whole gain the first time the screen turns green.

The subtle benefit is psychological, and it is the entire reason the ladder works. The reason traders cut winners early is the fear of giving back an unrealized gain. A trim ladder *converts* some of the unrealized gain into realized gain at each rung, which discharges the fear in stages rather than all at once. After you have banked profit at two rungs, the runner is "house money" in your head, and you can let it run to the bull target without the nervous itch to sell that would otherwise truncate it. The ladder is a tool for managing your own psychology as much as your position — it is how you let winners run *without* requiring superhuman nerve.

![Partial profit ladder bar chart showing shares held shrinking and profit banked rising at each trim rung](/imgs/blogs/when-to-add-cut-or-exit-managing-the-position-around-the-view-4.png)

The chart shows the ladder in action on the worked example below. The blue bars are the shares you still hold, stepping down at each rung as you trim — 400 at entry, 267 after the first trim, 178 after the second, 119 after the third, and then the 119-share runner left to ride. The green bars are the cumulative profit you have banked, stepping *up* — \$1,064 after the first trim, \$2,488 after the second, \$3,904 after the third — money realized and safe. The two staircases moving in opposite directions are the whole idea: you shed risk and bank certainty as the price rises, while keeping a runner exposed to the upside that is still in front of you. By the time the runner is all that is left, you have already locked in nearly \$4,000 and you are playing the rest with the market's money.

#### Worked example: a partial-profit ladder on a 400-share winner

You hold 400 shares of a stock bought at **\$50** — a \$20,000 position. Your scenario work put the base-case target at \$58, a higher target at \$66, and a bull-case stretch at \$74, with a stretch beyond that if everything breaks right. You set a trim ladder at entry: **trim a third of the remaining position at each of \$58, \$66, and \$74, and let the final third run.**

- The stock reaches **\$58**. You trim a third of 400 = 133 shares at \$58, banking 133 × (\$58 − \$50) = **\$1,064** of realized profit. You hold 267 shares.
- It reaches **\$66**. You trim a third of 267 ≈ 89 shares at \$66, banking 89 × (\$66 − \$50) = **\$1,424** more, for **\$2,488** cumulative. You hold 178 shares.
- It reaches **\$74**. You trim a third of 178 ≈ 59 shares at \$74, banking 59 × (\$74 − \$50) = **\$1,416** more, for **\$3,904** cumulative banked. You hold a 119-share runner.
- The runner rides toward the bull case. If it reaches \$82, those 119 shares are worth an additional 119 × (\$82 − \$50) = \$3,808 of gain on top of the \$3,904 already banked — and if the thesis fails from here, you still keep the \$3,904 you locked in.

Compare this to two cruder alternatives. If you had sold all 400 shares at the first sign of green at \$58, you would have banked 400 × \$8 = \$3,200 total and *missed everything above \$58* — the classic fear-driven early full exit. If you had held all 400 to a single \$74 target and the stock had reversed to \$66 before you could sell the lot, you would have round-tripped a chunk of the gain. The ladder threaded between those failures: it banked \$3,904 with certainty *and* kept a runner for the bull case. The ladder is how you let a winner run without betting the whole gain on one perfectly-timed exit.

## The position path: a managed trade end to end

The cleanest way to see the actions working together is to watch a single position from entry to exit, marking each management decision where it happens. A real trade is not one decision; it is a sequence of them, and the sequence is driven by the thesis and the risk at each step.

![Line chart of a managed position price path with add trim and exit points marked over twenty weeks](/imgs/blogs/when-to-add-cut-or-exit-managing-the-position-around-the-view-5.png)

The chart traces a position over 20 weeks. The blue line is the price; the green-shaded band above your \$50 entry is profit territory, the red band below is loss territory, and the dashed red line is your *trailing invalidation* — the stop you ratchet up behind the price to protect the gain as it accrues. Notice that the invalidation only ever moves *up*, never down; trailing a stop higher to lock in profit is disciplined, loosening a stop to avoid taking a loss is the cardinal sin. Now read the management decisions marked on the path:

- **Week 3, ADD (pyramid).** The price has cleared \$54 on a confirming earnings beat — a new fact that strengthened the thesis — and the heat budget has room, so you add. You trail the invalidation up to \$51, above your entry, capping the combined position's risk.
- **Week 9, TRIM.** The price hits \$62, your first scenario target. You trim a third, banking realized profit, and you ratchet the trailing invalidation up again. The trade is now structurally safe — your stop sits above your average cost.
- **Week 14, TRIM again.** The price reaches \$68, the next rung. You trim another third, banking more, and trail the stop higher still, to \$63.
- **Week 18, EXIT the runner.** The earnings catalyst that the trade was built around has now resolved; the gap it was meant to close has closed. With no remaining catalyst and the thesis fully played out, you exit the runner at \$66. The trade is over — not because the P&L told you to, but because the *reason for the trade is gone.*

Every mark on that path is a thesis-or-risk decision: add on a confirming beat with budget room, trim at scenario targets while trailing the stop, exit when the catalyst resolves. At no point did the unrealized P&L drive an action. The P&L was the *result* of getting the sequence right. A trader watching the green number instead would likely have sold the whole position in the week-5 dip to \$55 (fear), or held the runner past week 18 into the post-catalyst drift (hope) — both the wrong branch of the tree, both driven by the number rather than the view.

## The exit plan, written at entry

Here is the principle that makes all of the above executable rather than aspirational: **you write the exit plan before you enter, when you are calm, and you execute it without renegotiation when the moment comes.** The reason is simple and devastating — the moment you most need to make a clean exit decision is the moment you are least capable of making one. A position screaming against you floods you with the urge to hope; a position racing in your favor floods you with the urge to grab the gain. Decisions made in those states are not analysis; they are panic and greed wearing analysis costumes. The only defense is to have decided in advance, in writing, so that the act of exiting is *execution of a prior decision* rather than a fresh decision under fire.

A complete exit plan, written at entry, specifies four things:

1. **The invalidation exit.** The specific fact or price level at which the thesis is wrong and you close, with the dollar risk it implies. "Close below \$46; thesis wrong; risk \$1,000."
2. **The target / trim ladder.** The rungs at which you will take partial profits, and how much at each. "Trim a third at \$58, a third at \$66, let the rest run."
3. **The catalyst / time stop.** The event whose resolution ends the trade, and a backstop date if the thesis simply fails to play out. "Exit if still flat two weeks after the earnings date."
4. **The add trigger.** The specific new evidence that would justify pyramiding, and the risk condition that must hold. "Add only on a fresh confirming beat, and only if heat stays under 6%."

![Exit plan card grid with invalidation exit target trim ladder catalyst time stop and add trigger](/imgs/blogs/when-to-add-cut-or-exit-managing-the-position-around-the-view-6.png)

The card above is what an exit plan looks like as an artifact you fill in at entry and keep next to the position. Four cells, each pre-committing one class of decision: the invalidation exit (red — the loss you will accept), the target/trim ladder (green — the gains you will bank), the catalyst/time stop (amber — the clock on the thesis), and the add trigger (the one condition under which the position grows). Filled in before the trade, when no money is moving and no emotion is firing, the card converts every future management decision from a judgment call into a lookup. When the price hits \$46, you do not *decide* to exit — you *execute* the line you already wrote. That difference, between deciding under fire and executing a prior decision, is most of the gap between disciplined and undisciplined trading.

#### Worked example: a written exit plan executed versus an improvised panic exit

Two traders enter the identical position: 250 shares at \$50, \$1,000 risk, invalidation at \$46, with a thesis that runs through an earnings catalyst eight weeks out.

**The written-plan trader** fills in the card at entry: invalidation \$46; trim a third at \$58 and a third at \$66; exit the runner when earnings resolve; add only on a confirming beat with heat room. Over the next weeks the stock chops — \$53, then \$48 (an ugly week that has nothing to do with her thesis, just a market-wide risk-off day), then back to \$55. At \$48, with the screen red and down \$500, her *plan* says nothing has triggered: she is above her \$46 invalidation, no thesis-marker broke, the catalyst is still coming. So she holds. The stock recovers, hits \$58, and she trims per the ladder, then \$66, then exits the runner on the catalyst. She banks roughly **\$3,900** by executing a plan she wrote in five calm minutes at entry.

**The improvised trader** wrote nothing. At the same \$48 ugly week, with no pre-defined invalidation and the screen red, his fear takes over: "I'm down \$500 and it's falling, get me out." He panic-exits at \$48 for a **\$500 loss** — on a day that had nothing to do with his thesis, two points above where his invalidation would have been if he had set one. He then watches the stock he just sold run to \$66 without him. His loss is not the \$500; it is the \$500 *plus* the \$3,900 he would have made, a \$4,400 swing, and the entire difference between the two traders is that one had written the plan and the other was improvising under the pressure of a red number. The plan did not make her smarter; it made her *consistent*, and consistency is what compounds.

## The emotion traps

The two emotions that destroy management are fear and hope, and they are dangerous precisely because they fire at the wrong times and push you toward the wrong actions. Naming them is the first step to overriding them.

**Fear cuts winners early.** When a position is in profit, the dominant emotion is the fear of giving the gain back. That fear feels like prudence — "a bird in the hand," "you never go broke taking a profit" — but it is the mechanism that systematically truncates your winners, which are the source of your entire edge. The fear is most acute right after a position turns green, which is exactly when you should be *least* inclined to sell (the thesis is confirming, not breaking). Fear-driven selling cuts the right tail off your return distribution, and the right tail is where positive expectancy lives. The defense is the trim ladder: by banking partial profits at predefined rungs, you discharge the fear in controlled stages so it does not stampede you into a full early exit.

**Hope holds losers late.** When a position is in loss, the dominant emotion is the hope of getting back to even. Hope feels like conviction — "I still believe in this," "it'll come back" — but more often it is the refusal to realize a loss masquerading as a thesis. Hope is what makes you ignore your invalidation when it fires, average down to lower your anchor, and ride a broken thesis all the way down. Hope extends the left tail of your return distribution, turning small defined losses into large undefined ones. The defense is the written invalidation: by deciding in advance the exact line at which the thesis is wrong, you give yourself a pre-committed trigger that overrides the hope when the line is crossed.

The symmetry is the cruelest part. Fear fires on green and makes you sell (cutting winners); hope fires on red and makes you hold (riding losers). Left to your emotions, you will do exactly the opposite of what edge requires: small wins, large losses, inverted asymmetry, negative expectancy on a positive-expectancy strategy. The entire architecture of pre-written triggers — invalidation, trim ladder, add condition, time stop — exists to override these two emotions at the precise moments they are strongest. (The deep treatment of the gap between knowing the right action and executing it is in [trading psychology and the execution gap](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/trading-psychology-and-the-execution-gap).)

## Common misconceptions

**"Average down to lower the cost basis."** This is the single most expensive belief on this list, and it survives because it contains a grain of arithmetic truth dressed as wisdom — yes, buying more at a lower price does lower your average cost. But your average cost is irrelevant to whether the trade is good *from here*. The market does not know or care what you paid; the only question is whether the thesis is intact going forward, and a price moving against you is usually evidence that it is *not*. Averaging down responds to disconfirming evidence by increasing your bet, on the exact trade the market is voting against, and it moves your mental anchor so you become *less* willing to honor your invalidation. The correct response to a broken thesis is a smaller position, not a larger one. Lowering your cost basis is not a strategy; it is a rationalization for refusing to be wrong.

**"Let winners run means never trim."** "Let your winners run" is sound advice aimed at the fear that cuts winners early — but it is not a prohibition on ever taking profit. Letting a winner run means *keeping a runner exposed to the upside the thesis still points to*; it does not mean refusing to bank any gains along the way or ignoring a scenario target you defined at entry. The trim ladder *is* letting winners run — it keeps a runner alive through every rung — while also banking realized certainty that funds the patience to hold that runner. The misconception turns a useful corrective into a rigid rule that has you round-trip gains you could have banked. Trim at your targets *and* keep a runner; the two are not in conflict.

**"Exit when I'm up enough."** "Up enough" is a P&L target, not a thesis target, and managing to it is exactly the fear-driven early exit that bleeds your edge. The question is never "am I up enough?" — it is "is the thesis still pointing higher, and has the catalyst resolved?" If the thesis is intact and the catalyst is still ahead, you are not "up enough," no matter how green the screen; the trade still has edge. If the catalyst has resolved and the gap has closed, you should exit even if you are "only" up a little, because the edge is gone. Anchoring to a dollar amount of gain ("I'll sell when I've made \$5,000") substitutes an arbitrary number for the thesis, and the arbitrary number will have you selling winners that are just getting started and holding through the resolution of the very catalyst you were trading.

**"Managing the position means watching the P&L."** Watching the unrealized P&L tick up and down all day is not management; it is the opposite of management, because the P&L is the input most likely to provoke the two emotions that destroy your edge. The number on the screen tells you what already happened, not whether your thesis is still alive — and staring at it trains you to react to noise. Real management watches the *thesis-markers* (the facts that confirm or break the view), the *invalidation level*, and the *catalyst clock*; the P&L is context, glanced at, never the driver. (The dashboard that does this correctly is built in [monitoring a live thesis: building your watch dashboard](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/monitoring-a-live-thesis-building-your-watch-dashboard).) If your management consists of refreshing your P&L and reacting to the color, you are not managing — you are being managed, by the number.

**"A position that's down needs a decision; a position that's up can take care of itself."** Both directions need exactly the same discipline, and the up-direction is where the subtler money is lost. A winner left unmanaged either gets cut early by fear or held past its catalyst by greed; a winner managed well gets pyramided on confirmation, trimmed at targets, and exited when the reason is gone. The asymmetry of attention — agonizing over losers while letting winners drift — is itself an edge-destroying habit, because your winners are the larger part of your return and deserve more management thought, not less. Manage the position the same way whether it is green or red: read the thesis and the risk, choose the action, ignore the number.

## How it plays out in real markets

The management rules are not academic. Watch them decide outcomes in episodes you remember.

**Holding through the March 2020 COVID drawdown — and the trader who averaged down into it.** When the market fell roughly 34% from the February 19, 2020 peak to the March 23 low, two traders held the same quality-compounder position, both convinced of the long-term thesis. The first had a written plan: her invalidation was a *fundamental* break (a real deterioration in the business), not a price level, because her thesis was a multi-year one — so a market-wide liquidation that left the business untouched did *not* trigger her exit. She held through the drawdown, her thesis intact, and her position recovered with the market and then some. The second trader had no plan and managed by P&L: as the position fell, he "averaged down" aggressively to lower his cost, on margin, with no defined invalidation. When the cascade continued, his enlarged, leveraged position generated a margin call he could not meet, and he was forced to liquidate at the March lows — the worst possible prices. Both were *right* about the businesses. The first managed the position around the thesis (no fundamental break, hold) and survived; the second managed it around the P&L and the urge to lower his anchor, and the averaging-down increment plus the leverage is precisely what destroyed him in the month neither saw coming.

**Cutting on a broken driver, February 2018 — "Volmageddon."** Traders who were short volatility going into February 5, 2018 had, for most of them, a thesis: vol stays low and they collect the carry. When the VIX more than doubled in a single session and the popular short-vol product (XIV) lost about 96% of its value overnight, the *driver of the trade — low, stable volatility — had unambiguously broken.* The traders who survived were the ones whose plan said "the trade is over the moment vol regime breaks," and who exited on the broken driver rather than hoping for a snap-back. The casualties were the ones who managed by P&L and hope: staring at a position down 90% in a night, they reasoned "it can't go lower, it has to bounce" — the textbook hope-holds-losers-late trap — and held a broken thesis into oblivion. The edge (selling vol) had been real for years; what killed people was refusing to cut when the driver died, because the loss was too large to "realize." A position whose thesis has broken gets closed, regardless of how big the loss already is — and especially when it is already big.

**Pyramiding the 2022 macro short — adding on confirmation, not on price.** Through 2022 the S&P 500 fell about 19% as the Fed hiked aggressively, and traders who were short equities or long the dollar on a "rates higher, risk lower" thesis had a year of confirming data points: hot CPI print after hot CPI print, hawkish FOMC after hawkish FOMC. The disciplined ones *pyramided* — adding to the winning position each time a *new* confirming inflation print arrived and the central bank reaffirmed the regime, trailing their stops down behind the falling price (the short-position equivalent of trailing up) so the combined risk stayed capped. They turned one correct macro read into a position that compounded across the year. The undisciplined ones did one of two worse things: cut the winning short early in one of 2022's vicious bear-market rallies (fear, on a counter-trend bounce that broke no thesis-marker), or — on the long side — averaged down into "cheap" stocks on every rally that they mistook for a bottom, riding a broken thesis lower into each fresh low. The same year that compounded the trader who added on *confirmation* bled the trader who reacted to *price*. The driver — restrictive policy — was confirmed repeatedly; managing around that confirmation, rather than around the noisy path, was the whole game.

In all three episodes the entry was almost beside the point. What separated the survivors from the casualties was whether their management — when to add, when to hold, when to cut — was driven by the state of the thesis and a pre-written plan, or by the P&L and the emotion the number provoked.

## The playbook

Here is the routine that manages an open position around the thesis instead of the P&L. Run it before you enter (to write the plan) and at every review point thereafter (to execute it).

1. **Write the exit plan at entry, before any money moves.** Fill in the four cells of the exit-plan card: the invalidation exit (the fact or level at which the thesis is wrong, and the dollar risk it implies), the target/trim ladder (the rungs at which you bank partial profits), the catalyst/time stop (the event whose resolution ends the trade, plus a backstop date), and the add trigger (the confirming evidence and heat condition that would justify pyramiding). Do this calm, in writing, so every later decision is a lookup, not a judgment under fire.

2. **At each review, read the thesis and the risk — not the P&L.** Ask the two questions in order: *Is the thesis stronger, intact, decaying, or broken?* and *Where does this position and the portfolio stand against the risk limits?* The unrealized P&L is context you glance at; it is never the input that drives the action. If you catch yourself reacting to the color of the number, stop and re-ask the two questions.

3. **Choose the action the thesis-health dictates.** Strengthening on new confirming evidence, with heat room → **ADD** (pyramid, trailing the stop up so combined risk stays capped). Intact, just noise → **HOLD** the runner to its target. Decaying, or a scenario target hit, or a risk-limit breached → **TRIM** (bank partial profit, reduce size). Invalidation hit, driver broken, or catalyst resolved → **CUT / EXIT** the whole thing.

4. **Pyramid only on confirmation; never average down on pain.** Adding to a winner whose thesis is confirming, with the stop trailed up and the risk budget respected, is the best move on the board. Adding to a loser to lower your cost basis is the worst. Before any add, ask: did a *new fact confirm the thesis*, or am I just trying to relieve the discomfort of being down? If it is the latter, you are averaging down a broken thesis — the smaller position, or the exit, is the correct move.

5. **Take partial profits on a ladder; let a runner run.** Trim a fixed fraction of the remaining position at each predefined rung, banking realized certainty that discharges the fear which otherwise cuts winners early — and keep a runner exposed to the bull case. Trailing your stop up at each trim turns the whole position structurally safe. This is how you let a winner run *without* requiring superhuman nerve.

6. **Honor the invalidation and the catalyst, every time.** When the line you drew in cold blood prints, you exit — no renegotiation, no "one more day," no moving the stop looser. When the catalyst resolves and the gap has closed, you exit even if the P&L is barely moved, because the edge is gone. The discipline is not in *having* the rules; it is in executing them at the exact moment your emotions are screaming to do otherwise.

![Position management rules checklist grid with six rules tied to thesis health and risk budget](/imgs/blogs/when-to-add-cut-or-exit-managing-the-position-around-the-view-7.png)

The checklist card is the post in operating form: add on strength (only if conviction rose and heat allows), hold on intact (noise is not news), trim on decay (a risk breach or weakening driver), exit on break (invalidation hit or catalyst resolved), never average down (a broken thesis gets smaller, never larger), and write it all at entry (pre-commit every exit before any emotion arrives). The first rule is green because it is the offensive, edge-amplifying move; the trim and exit rules carry amber and red because they are the governors that take risk off when the thesis decays or breaks. A complete management practice runs all six, in cold blood, on every position.

One closing reframe, because it is the thing to carry out of here. Forming the view is the work everyone romanticizes; managing the position is the work that actually decides whether the view pays. A correct thesis managed by fear and hope — winners cut early, losers ridden late, broken theses averaged down — produces losses on positive-expectancy trades. The same thesis managed by pre-written, thesis-driven rules — added to on confirmation, trimmed at targets, exited on invalidation — compounds. The two traders in the opening had the identical winning position. One managed it around the thesis and the risk and compounded; one managed it around the P&L and the emotion and bled. Manage the position around the view, never around the number — and write the plan before you need it, when you still can. The next step is learning to update the view itself as new information arrives, the disciplined Bayesian habit covered in [updating on new information: thinking like a Bayesian](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/updating-on-new-information-thinking-like-a-bayesian) — because every add, trim, and exit decision above is, at bottom, a re-estimate of how much you should believe.
