> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — New information arrives constantly, and the entire skill is updating your view *proportionally*: not ignoring data that contradicts you (anchoring), not flipping your whole book on one noisy print (whipsaw), but moving by an amount that matches how diagnostic the evidence actually is.
>
> - A view is a **prior** — a probability you can write as a number. New evidence has a **likelihood** — how expected it was under each scenario you hold. Together they produce a **posterior** — your moved view. That is the whole loop.
> - The size of the move depends on **diagnosticity**, not drama. Clean, on-question, surprising data that separates your scenarios moves you a lot; loud-but-uninformative noise barely moves you at all. A scary headline is not strong evidence.
> - We systematically *under-weight disconfirming evidence* — the data that says we're wrong. The discipline is to update *more* on the print that hurts, not less, because that is the print carrying the information.
> - The one rule to remember: **write the prior as a number before the data lands, ask how diagnostic the new evidence is, then move proportionally and re-mark your scenarios so they still sum to 100%.**

## Two traders, one data point, two mistakes

It is 8:30 a.m. on a CPI release day. The core inflation print drops, and it comes in soft — a clear downside surprise to consensus. Two traders on the same desk hold the same view going in: both think the Fed is more likely than not to cut rates in September, and both are long the trade that profits if cuts come. The number that just hit the tape is *evidence for their view*. And yet, watching how each of them reacts, you would never guess they were looking at the same screen.

The first trader barely moves. He has held the "cuts are coming" thesis for two months, he is convinced, and one print is one print. "Single data point," he mutters, and he does nothing — he doesn't add, he doesn't re-mark his odds, he doesn't even update the number in his head. A week later a *second* soft print lands, then a dovish speech, then a third print, and he is still sitting at the same conviction he started with, having absorbed none of the accumulating signal. When the trade finally works he is under-sized for the move he correctly anticipated, and when, two months on, the data turns and the cut gets pushed out, he is the last one to notice because he stopped updating in either direction. He **anchored**.

The second trader does the opposite. The instant the soft print prints, she flips. "Cuts are locked in," she declares, and she doubles her position, lifts every offer, and tells the desk the September cut is a done deal. She has gone from a sensible 60% to something like 95% on the strength of a single number — a number that, on its own, is genuinely consistent with the Fed *not* cutting too. Two days later a hawkish Fed official walks it back, the next employment report is hot, and the position she tripled on one print gets carried out feet first. She paid the spread to put it on, paid it again to take it off, and the round trip cost her real money on a thesis that was never actually wrong — she just sized it to a certainty the evidence never justified. She **whipsawed**.

![Prior to evidence to posterior loop showing a view moved proportionally by diagnostic data](/imgs/blogs/updating-on-new-information-thinking-like-a-bayesian-1.png)

Both traders are wrong, and they are wrong in exactly opposite directions. The anchored trader treated new information as noise to be ignored; the whipsawed trader treated it as proof to be obeyed. The correct response was neither — it was to move a *measured amount*: from 60% to maybe 72%, a real and meaningful update, but nowhere near certainty, and with the rest of the scenarios re-marked to match. That measured move is what it means to think like a Bayesian. This post is about how to do it: how to hold a view as a number, how to weigh a new data point by how much it should actually move you, and how to avoid both the stubbornness of never updating and the whipsaw of over-updating on noise. We will build the whole thing from zero, ground it in dollar-sized positions, and finish with a routine you can run on the next print that hits your tape.

## Foundations: prior, likelihood, posterior

Before any technique, we need four words to mean exactly one thing each. They get used loosely, and the looseness is where the mistakes live. These are process words — they describe how you *update a belief* — not market mechanisms, so we build each from the ground up, in order.

A **prior** is your view *before* the new piece of information arrives, expressed as a probability you can write down. "Going into this CPI print, I think the Fed cuts in September with 60% probability." That 60% is the prior. The critical discipline is that it is a *number*, not a mood. You cannot update a feeling, and you cannot tell afterward whether you moved the right amount if you never wrote down where you started. A prior is not a guess pulled from nowhere either — it is anchored on the **base rate** (how often does this kind of setup resolve this way historically?) and adjusted for the specifics of the current situation. The base rate *is* the prior in its rawest form: the starting odds before you look at anything specific to today.

A **likelihood** is the answer to a precise question: *how expected was this new piece of evidence, under each scenario I hold?* It is written as a conditional — "the probability of seeing *this data* given that *the Fed is going to cut*." Notice the direction: it is not the probability that the Fed cuts; it is the probability of the *evidence* assuming the cut is or isn't coming. A soft CPI print is *highly likely* in a world where cuts are coming (call it 70%) and *less likely but not impossible* in a world where they aren't (call it 35%) — soft prints happen sometimes even when the Fed is going to hold. The likelihood is where the evidence's *strength* lives, and it is the part everyone skips.

A **posterior** is your view *after* you fold the evidence in — the updated probability. It is what the prior becomes once the new data has done its work. The whole machinery exists to get you from the prior to the posterior honestly: 60% becomes 72%, or 51%, or whatever the evidence and its strength dictate. Tomorrow's posterior becomes the next day's prior, and the loop runs again on the next print. A view is never finished; it is a posterior that is always about to become a prior.

**Updating** is the act of moving from prior to posterior in response to evidence — and the two failure modes have names. **Anchoring** is updating *too little*: clinging to the prior, treating disconfirming data as noise, refusing to move even as evidence piles up. **Overreacting** (or whipsawing) is updating *too much*: flipping the view on a single print, treating noise as proof, lurching to near-certainty on evidence that doesn't justify it. The Bayesian sits between them and moves *proportionally* — by an amount that matches the evidence's actual strength.

### The base rate is the prior — and most people skip it

The most common way the whole loop goes wrong is at the very first step: people start from a prior that ignores the **base rate**. A base rate is the long-run frequency of an outcome across the reference class of similar situations — how often this *kind* of thing resolves this *way*, before you look at any of today's specifics. "Of all the times a profitable, growing company traded at this valuation, how often did the next two years deliver the implied growth?" "Of all the September FOMC meetings where the market priced a cut two months out, how often did the cut actually arrive?" The base rate is the gravity of the prior: it's where the number should start before the story about *this* situation pulls it around.

The failure is the **base-rate neglect** that behavioral economists have documented for fifty years: people latch onto the vivid, specific story in front of them and forget to ask how often that story actually comes true. A trader hears a compelling bull case for a stock — great product, visionary founder, huge market — and sets a prior of 80% that it triples, completely ignoring that the base rate for "stock triples in two years" is maybe 5%. The vivid case *feels* like evidence, but most of it is already reflected in the base rate of similar vivid cases, the great majority of which disappointed. The disciplined prior starts at the base rate and adjusts only for what is genuinely *different* about this case — and most cases are far less different than their stories suggest.

This matters for updating because a badly-set prior corrupts every subsequent move. If you anchor your prior on a vivid story instead of the base rate, you start at 80% when you should be at, say, 25%, and now even perfectly proportional updates leave you systematically overconfident. Getting the prior right — base rate first, specifics second — is half the battle. Mapping where the consensus prior already sits is its own skill, covered in [mapping the consensus — what does the market already believe](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/mapping-the-consensus-what-does-the-market-already-believe): the market's implied probability is often the cleanest base rate you have, and your prior should usually start there and move only where you genuinely disagree.

### Diagnosticity: how much should this evidence move me?

The single most important concept in this whole post is **diagnosticity**: how well a piece of evidence *distinguishes between the scenarios you hold*. A piece of data is diagnostic when it is much more likely under one scenario than another — when seeing it genuinely tells you which world you're in. It is *un*diagnostic when it is roughly equally likely under every scenario, because then seeing it tells you nothing about which world you're in, no matter how loud it is.

A soft CPI print that consensus did not expect is highly diagnostic: it is much more likely in the "cuts coming" world than the "no cuts" world, so it should move you a lot. A CPI print that lands exactly in line with consensus is nearly *un*diagnostic: it was equally expected under both your scenarios, so it confirms what was already priced and should move you barely at all — even though it is the same economic release that, in surprise form, would have moved you a great deal. A punchy headline ("INFLATION FEARS GRIP MARKETS") with no new number in it is almost perfectly undiagnostic: it is equally likely whatever the Fed does, because it contains no information that separates the scenarios. **Drama is not diagnosticity.** The size of your update should track how much the evidence separates your scenarios, never how alarming it sounds.

![Diagnosticity scale from highly diagnostic to nearly undiagnostic evidence](/imgs/blogs/updating-on-new-information-thinking-like-a-bayesian-3.png)

That scale is the heart of proportional updating. Evidence at the top — clean, hard, on-the-question data that the two scenarios genuinely disagree about — earns a big move in your posterior. Evidence in the middle — partial, mixed, roughly in-line — earns a small nudge. Evidence at the bottom — loud but uninformative, a single noisy tick, a print equally likely under everything you hold — earns essentially nothing. The skill of updating is, in large part, the skill of placing each new data point on this scale before you let it touch your position.

#### Worked example: a diagnostic print moves a \$20,000 position

You hold a prior that the Fed cuts in September with **60%** probability. You have expressed it as a \$20,000 long position in a rate-sensitive trade — your sizing rule says a 60% conviction view of this type gets \$20,000 of your book. Then a soft core-CPI print lands, a clear downside surprise. You judge its likelihoods: a print this soft is about **70%** likely if cuts are genuinely coming, and about **35%** likely if they're not (soft prints happen even when the Fed holds). Running the update (the arithmetic is below), your posterior lands at roughly **72%** — a move of +12 percentage points. That is a *large* update from a single print, which is correct, because the print was both surprising and diagnostic.

Now the position. Your sizing curve maps conviction to dollars; a jump from 60% to 72% conviction takes your target size from \$20,000 to about \$26,000. So you *add* \$6,000 of exposure — a meaningful increase, sized to the new posterior, not a reckless double-up to a certainty you don't hold. The intuition: a strong, surprising, on-question print is exactly the kind of evidence that *should* move you a lot — but "a lot" means +12 points and +\$6,000, not a flip to 95% and a tripled position.

![Bayesian update shown as a stacked probability bar moving from sixty to seventy-two percent](/imgs/blogs/updating-on-new-information-thinking-like-a-bayesian-2.png)

That figure is the whole update in one picture: the prior stack on the left (60% cuts / 40% holds), the posterior stack on the right (72% / 28%), and the +12-point move between them. The move is large and visible — but the bar does not go to 100%. One print, however diagnostic, almost never justifies certainty, because the complementary scenario was never impossible. Holding that 28% open is what keeps you from being the whipsawed trader.

## The practical Bayesian loop

You do not need to compute conditional probabilities in your head to update like a Bayesian. The formal arithmetic (which we will do once, below, so you trust the engine) just makes precise a loop you can run qualitatively in seconds. The loop has four moves: **write the prior, take in the evidence, judge its diagnosticity, move to the posterior.** Then a fifth that closes it for trading: **re-mark the scenarios and re-size.** Let's walk each one.

**1. Write the prior as a number.** Before the data lands — ideally the night before a known catalyst — write today's odds down. "Fed cuts in September = 60%." This step feels trivial and is the one everyone skips, and skipping it is fatal, because you cannot move what you never set. A view held as a vague lean ("I'm kind of bullish") has no anchor, so any reaction to news is unmeasured: you can't tell afterward whether you moved too much or too little because there's no "before." The number also forces honesty. Writing "60%" makes you confront that you are carrying a real 40% chance of being wrong, which is precisely the part the anchored trader pretends doesn't exist.

**2. Take in the new evidence as an event.** When the data hits, state it as a concrete event: "core CPI printed +0.2% m/m versus +0.4% consensus — a downside surprise." Not "inflation looks soft" (a conclusion) but the actual release (the evidence). The discipline of stating the raw event keeps you from skipping straight to a conclusion that smuggles in the update you're trying to compute honestly.

**3. Ask how diagnostic it is.** This is the step that determines the *size* of the move. Ask three questions. *Is it on-question?* — does this data bear directly on the thing my scenarios disagree about, or is it adjacent? *Is it clean or noisy?* — one hard release versus one volatile tick. *How surprising is it?* — evidence already priced in is not diagnostic, because it changes nothing about which world you're in. The more on-question, clean, and surprising the data, the larger the move it earns. In-line, noisy, or off-question data earns a nudge at most.

**4. Move to the posterior.** Combine the prior and the diagnosticity into an updated number. Strong-and-surprising evidence → a big move (60% to 72%). Weak, in-line, or noisy evidence → a small move (60% to 62%, or no move at all). The hard rule: **never to 0% or 100% on a single print.** A view that goes to certainty on one piece of evidence was never a probability; it was a prediction wearing a probability's clothes. Write the posterior down next to the prior so the size of the move is visible and gradeable.

**5. Re-mark the scenarios and re-size.** Your view is rarely a single binary. It is usually a set of scenarios — base, bull, bear — that sum to 100%. When evidence moves the probability of one, the mass has to come from or go to the others; probabilities are conserved. Re-balance the whole set so it still sums to 100%, then map the new probabilities to a position size and adjust. This is the step that turns an updated belief into an updated trade.

### Weighing evidence by source quality and surprise

Two pieces of evidence can point the same direction and deserve wildly different weights. The weight is set by two multipliers on top of raw diagnosticity: **source quality** and **surprise**.

**Source quality** is how much you trust the evidence to be what it claims to be. An official BLS CPI release is a high-quality source: the number is what it is. A single sell-side analyst's revised forecast is lower-quality — it is one person's opinion, often itself anchored on the same consensus you already knew. A rumor on financial Twitter is lower still. An anonymous "sources say" headline ahead of a Fed decision is lower yet, and frequently planted. The same directional signal — "the Fed is leaning dovish" — should move you a lot if it comes from the Chair's prepared remarks and almost nothing if it comes from an unsourced tweet. Weight the evidence by how much you'd trust it to survive scrutiny.

**Surprise** is how far the evidence sits from what was already expected and priced. This is the multiplier most people get exactly backwards. A blockbuster earnings beat that everyone already expected is *not* strong evidence — the market priced the beat, so the beat itself confirms nothing and the stock can even fall on it. A modest beat that nobody saw coming is *strong* evidence, because it carries genuine information: it tells you something the consensus didn't know. The general principle has its own deep treatment in [the surprise, not the level — betas to data surprises](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/the-surprise-not-the-level-betas-to-data-surprises): markets move on the *delta from expectations*, not the absolute number, and your Bayesian update should too. An in-line print, however dramatic the headline number sounds, is low-surprise and therefore low-diagnosticity. The print that should move you is the one the consensus didn't see coming.

There is a subtle trap inside the surprise multiplier: **the same number is wildly diagnostic in one context and nearly useless in another, depending on what was priced.** A 0.3% core CPI print is a *dovish surprise* when consensus was 0.4% and a *hawkish surprise* when consensus was 0.2% — the identical number moves your posterior in opposite directions depending on the prior expectation it lands against. This is why you cannot judge diagnosticity from the data alone; you have to know what was expected. The professional habit is to write down the consensus *before* the release, so that when the number lands you immediately know the surprise, and therefore the diagnosticity, rather than reacting to the absolute level. A print of "+0.3%" tells you nothing until you know whether the desk was braced for +0.4% or +0.2%.

A second refinement: **evidence from a source that has skin in being right deserves a quality discount in proportion to its incentive to mislead.** A company's own guidance is high source-quality on its operations but carries a known optimistic bias — management is incentivized to frame softly. A short-seller's report is information-rich but adversarially selected. An anonymous "officials are considering" leak in the days before a policy decision is frequently a trial balloon planted *to move you*. None of this means you ignore biased sources — they often carry real signal — but you weight the update by the source's incentive, treating a self-interested party's claim as you would a witness with a motive. The cleanest evidence is data that nobody chose to show you: the print that simply *is* what it is.

#### Worked example: a noisy signal that should barely move the view

You hold the same 60% prior on a September cut, expressed as that \$20,000 position. This time the "new information" is a single regional manufacturing survey — one of a dozen second-tier indicators — that comes in slightly soft. A headline flashes: "FACTORY GAUGE SLUMPS, RECESSION FEARS MOUNT." Your pulse ticks up. Should you update?

Run the diagnosticity check. *On-question?* Barely — one regional survey is loosely related to the national inflation and rate path your scenarios actually turn on. *Clean or noisy?* Noisy — these surveys are volatile month to month and frequently revised. *Surprising?* Mildly, but a single second-tier print being soft is well within normal monthly variation. Likelihoods: a slightly-soft regional survey is maybe **55%** likely if cuts are coming and **45%** likely if they aren't — barely different, which is the definition of undiagnostic. The honest posterior moves from 60% to about **61%** — essentially unchanged. Your position stays at \$20,000; you do not trade on it. The intuition: the headline was loud, but the evidence was nearly undiagnostic, so the correct update is almost zero — and resisting the urge to act on a scary-but-empty print is exactly the discipline that separates the Bayesian from the whipsawer.

The hardest version of this judgment — is a contrary move *real information or just noise* — gets its own full treatment in [thesis broken or just noise — the hardest call you make](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/thesis-broken-or-just-noise-the-hardest-call-you-make). The Bayesian frame is the engine underneath it: a low-diagnosticity print barely moves the posterior, so it cannot break a thesis on its own.

### We systematically under-weight disconfirming evidence

Here is the asymmetry that quietly wrecks more views than any other: when evidence confirms what we already believe, we accept it readily and update *toward* our view; when evidence disconfirms what we believe, we scrutinize it, discount it, find reasons it doesn't count, and update too little — or not at all. This is confirmation bias dressed up as rigor, and it is the precise mechanism behind the anchored trader in the opening. He didn't refuse to update on *all* evidence; he refused to update on the evidence that said he was *wrong*.

The Bayesian engine is indifferent to which direction evidence points — a likelihood ratio that favors "you're wrong" moves the posterior exactly as hard as one that favors "you're right." Your brain is not indifferent. So the correction is deliberate and counterintuitive: **when a piece of evidence cuts against your view, update at least as much as you would if it confirmed it — and probably more, because the disconfirming print is the one you're biased to discount.** The data that hurts is the data carrying the information. A confirming print mostly tells you what you already believed; a disconfirming print is the market telling you something you didn't know, which is, by definition, the higher-information event.

A practical tool for this is to define, *before* the catalyst, what evidence would move you against your view and by how much — the discipline covered in [what would change my mind — defining invalidation upfront](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/what-would-change-my-mind-defining-invalidation-upfront). If you've pre-committed that "a 0.4% core print would cut my cut-odds to 35%," then when that print lands you update mechanically instead of rationalizing it away in the heat of the moment. Pre-registering your disconfirming updates is the single best defense against the asymmetry.

#### Worked example: strong disconfirming data cuts a \$15,000 position

You are long a \$15,000 position expressing a 65% prior that a cyclical sector keeps outperforming over the next quarter — your thesis is that demand stays strong. The catalyst is the sector bellwether's earnings. It misses revenue badly, cuts forward guidance, and management explicitly flags demand softening into next quarter. This is high source-quality (audited results plus management's own forward read), highly surprising (a clean miss against consensus for a strong beat), and dead on-question (demand is the exact thing your thesis rests on). Likelihoods: an outright miss-and-cut like this is maybe **20%** likely if your "demand stays strong" thesis is right, and **75%** likely if it's wrong. That is a strongly disconfirming likelihood ratio.

The honest posterior collapses your "outperformance continues" odds from 65% to roughly **30%** — your thesis is now more likely *wrong* than right. The disciplined response is not to shrug ("it's one quarter") but to cut hard: your sizing curve maps a sub-coin-flip view to a much smaller position, so you take the \$15,000 down to about \$5,000 or out entirely, accepting the loss on the cut. The intuition: because this evidence was diagnostic *against* you, the correct move is a large downward update and a real reduction in size — and the instinct to defend the thesis and hold the full \$15,000 is exactly the disconfirming-evidence bias that turns a manageable trim into a compounding loss.

### Avoiding both stubbornness and whipsaw

The two errors are mirror images, and the Bayesian frame catches both with the same test: *did my update match the diagnosticity of the evidence?*

![Under-update anchoring versus over-update whipsaw shown side by side](/imgs/blogs/updating-on-new-information-thinking-like-a-bayesian-4.png)

On the left, the anchor: strong, diagnostic data lands that genuinely contradicts the view, and the trader treats it as noise — "it's just one print." The posterior barely budges, 60% limps to 58%, and the trader stays in a deteriorating trade while the evidence piles up against him. The error is *under-updating on high-diagnosticity evidence*. On the right, the whipsaw: a noisy, low-diagnosticity tick lands and the trader treats it as proof — "everything's changed." The posterior overshoots from 60% to 15%, the book flips, and the trader pays the spread both ways on evidence that never justified the move. The error is *over-updating on low-diagnosticity evidence*.

Both are failures of *proportion*, and the same discipline fixes both: size the update to the diagnosticity, not to your prior emotional commitment to the view. The anchored trader is emotionally committed to being right, so he under-weights the evidence that says he's wrong. The whipsawed trader is emotionally committed to *reacting*, to looking decisive, so he over-weights any evidence that lets him do something. The Bayesian is committed to neither — only to moving the posterior by the amount the evidence's strength dictates.

A useful sanity check sits between the two: **if I would update by X on this evidence in favor of my view, would I update by X against it if it pointed the other way?** If the answer is no — if you'd leap on confirming data but explain away disconfirming data — you're anchoring. If *any* data, in either direction, sends you flipping the book, you're whipsawing. The symmetric, proportional update is the only one that survives both tests.

There is a deeper structural reason these two errors are mirror images, and it explains why the same people rarely commit both. The anchorer has a prior that is *too strong* — too concentrated, too certain — so it takes an unreasonable amount of evidence to move it. The whipsawer has a prior that is *too weak* — too diffuse, too easily dislodged — so any breeze knocks it over. Calibrating how *movable* your prior should be is itself part of the skill: a prior built on years of base-rate data and a deeply-researched thesis *should* be hard to move and update slowly; a prior that is really just a fresh hunch *should* be easily revised and update fast. The error is a mismatch between how movable your prior actually is and how movable it *should* be given the evidence behind it. A well-researched view that flips on one headline was never really held; a fresh hunch defended against a wall of contrary data was never really a hunch — it became an identity.

This is why the journal matters so much. Over many updates, you can see your own pattern: do your posteriors drift too little when you're proven wrong (chronic anchoring), or do they lurch around on noise (chronic whipsaw)? Most traders have a dominant lean, and knowing yours lets you apply a deliberate correction — the chronic anchorer forces a bigger update on disconfirming prints, the chronic whipsawer imposes a "wait for two confirmations" rule before moving size. You cannot fix a bias you haven't measured, and the only way to measure it is to have written down the prior, the evidence, and the move every time.

### Updating the probability mass across scenarios

A real view is rarely one number; it is a *distribution* across scenarios — and updating means moving probability *mass* between them, because the total is conserved at 100%. When a soft print makes "cuts soon" more likely, that extra probability has to come *out* of the other scenarios. You cannot add 15 points to the base case without subtracting 15 points from somewhere else. This conservation is what keeps the update honest: it forces you to confront which scenario you're now *less* sure of, not just which one you're more sure of.

![Scenario probability mass shifting between three Fed-path scenarios after a print](/imgs/blogs/updating-on-new-information-thinking-like-a-bayesian-5.png)

That figure shows the re-marking in action across a three-scenario Fed-path view. Before the print: cuts-soon 45%, cuts-later 35%, no-cuts-this-year 20%. The soft, diagnostic print pulls 15 points *into* cuts-soon (to 60%), which has to come *out* of the other two — cuts-later drops to 28%, no-cuts drops to 12%. The bars still sum to 100% on each side, because they must. The building of these three scenarios in the first place is its own discipline, covered in [base, bull and bear — building three scenarios](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/base-bull-and-bear-building-three-scenarios); updating is what you do to them when the news arrives. The connection to the binary framing is exact: this whole subject sits downstream of [thinking in probabilities, not predictions](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/thinking-in-probabilities-not-predictions) — you can only *update* a view that was held as probabilities in the first place. A prediction ("the Fed will cut") has nothing to update; it's just right or wrong. A distribution has mass you can move.

The conservation rule also catches a sneaky error: updating one scenario up while leaving the others alone, so the total drifts above 100%. If you find your scenarios summing to 115%, you've been adding confirming evidence to your favored case without ever debiting the alternatives — a quantitative fingerprint of confirmation bias. Re-normalizing to 100% forces the debit you were avoiding.

The scenario frame also makes a subtle point about *which* scenario a piece of evidence actually informs. A single print is often diagnostic for one branch and silent on the others. A hot inflation print is strongly diagnostic against "cuts soon" but says little about *whether* the eventual path is "cuts later" versus "no cuts at all" — both of those remain on the table, and the mass leaving "cuts soon" has to be split between them according to *their* relative likelihoods, not dumped arbitrarily into the most salient one. The discipline is to ask, for each scenario, "how likely was this exact print under *this* scenario?" and let the relative likelihoods do the re-allocation. Sloppy updaters move all the mass into whichever alternative is most vivid at the moment, which is how a single hot print sometimes flips a desk straight from "imminent cut" to "hikes are back" — skipping the far more probable middle scenario entirely because it's less dramatic. The mass has to land where the likelihoods send it, not where the narrative wants it.

#### Worked example: splitting the mass across three scenarios on one print

You hold a three-scenario rate view sized as a \$20,000 position: cuts-soon 45%, cuts-later 35%, no-cuts-this-year 20%. A hot core-CPI print lands — a clear upside surprise. It is highly diagnostic *against* cuts-soon: that print is maybe 25% likely if cuts are imminent and 70% likely otherwise. So cuts-soon's mass falls hard. But the 25 points it loses can't all go to the scariest scenario; they split between cuts-later and no-cuts according to *their* likelihoods. The print is roughly equally consistent with both delayed-cut worlds, and cuts-later started with more mass, so the re-mark lands at: cuts-soon 20%, cuts-later 50%, no-cuts 30%. Note what did *not* happen — no-cuts went from 20% to only 30%, not to 60%, because the print, while hot, was not nearly enough to make "the Fed hikes again" the base case. Your position, mapped from a now-much-lower probability on the near-term dovish path, comes down from \$20,000 to about \$11,000. The intuition: a single diagnostic print re-allocated the mass, but it did so proportionally across *all three* scenarios by their likelihoods — it did not let the most dramatic alternative swallow everything, which is the whipsaw the headlines were inviting.

### A simple numeric Bayes walkthrough

You don't need this arithmetic in the heat of the moment, but doing it once builds trust in the qualitative loop. Bayes' rule, in odds form, is just: *posterior odds = prior odds × likelihood ratio.* Put plainly, you start with your prior odds, multiply by how much more likely the evidence was under your hypothesis than against it, and that product is your posterior odds. Here is the formula in probability form, where $H$ is the hypothesis ("Fed cuts in September") and $E$ is the evidence ("a soft CPI print"):

$$P(H \mid E) = \frac{P(E \mid H)\,P(H)}{P(E \mid H)\,P(H) + P(E \mid \neg H)\,P(\neg H)}$$

Now plug in the worked-example numbers, all rounded. Prior: $P(H) = 0.60$, so $P(\neg H) = 0.40$. Likelihoods: the soft print is $P(E \mid H) = 0.70$ likely if cuts are coming, and $P(E \mid \neg H) = 0.35$ likely if they aren't. The numerator — the probability of being in a cuts-coming world *and* seeing this print — is $0.60 \times 0.70 = 0.42$. The other branch — holding world *and* seeing this print — is $0.40 \times 0.35 = 0.14$. The denominator is the total probability of seeing the print at all: $0.42 + 0.14 = 0.56$. So the posterior is $0.42 / 0.56 \approx 0.75$.

![A worked Bayes calculation shown as a ratio of two joint-probability bars](/imgs/blogs/updating-on-new-information-thinking-like-a-bayesian-6.png)

That figure is the calculation made visual: Bayes is just *comparing the size of two slabs*. The green slab (0.42) is the probability of the cuts-coming world producing this print; the red slab (0.14) is the probability of the holding world producing it. The posterior is simply the green slab as a fraction of both slabs together — 0.42 out of 0.56, which is about 75%. The green slab is three times the red, so the posterior lands near three-quarters. (In the earlier figures I rounded the practitioner's move to 72%; the strict arithmetic gives 75%. The gap is the difference between exact likelihoods and the round numbers a trader actually carries in their head — and it doesn't matter, because both say "a big move, but nowhere near certainty.")

The deep lesson from the arithmetic: the posterior depends on the *ratio* of the likelihoods, not on how dramatic either one is. If the print had been equally likely under both scenarios — say 0.70 and 0.70 — the slabs would be in the same proportion as the prior, and the posterior would equal the prior: *no update*, because the evidence was undiagnostic. The whole engine reduces to "how lopsided are the two slabs?" — which is exactly the diagnosticity question, now made precise.

#### Worked example: the cost of NOT updating — anchoring into a \$6,000 loss

You hold a 65% prior that a stock keeps rising on a growth thesis, expressed as a \$20,000 position. Over three weeks, three diagnostic disconfirming signals land: a competitor reports the same end-market is softening, the company quietly cuts its hiring, and a key customer publicly switches suppliers. Each is on-question, decent source-quality, and surprising — each *should* knock several points off your odds. A Bayesian would have updated from 65% to maybe 55% after the first, to 45% after the second, to 35% after the third, trimming the position at each step.

You anchor instead. "One competitor, one hiring freeze, one customer — none of these is the company's own numbers." You hold the full \$20,000 through all three. The next earnings report confirms what the three signals foreshadowed, the stock gaps down 30%, and you take a **\$6,000 loss** on the position you should have been trimming the whole way down. Had you updated proportionally — cutting to roughly \$11,000 by the third signal — the same gap would have cost you closer to \$3,300, and you'd have sidestepped nearly half the damage. The intuition: the cost of not updating is not abstract; it is the difference between the loss you took anchored at full size and the smaller loss you'd have taken had you let the accumulating evidence move your posterior down in real time. Anchoring doesn't avoid the loss — it just maximizes it.

## Common misconceptions

**"Stick to your guns — conviction means not flip-flopping on every headline."** This conflates two different things. Refusing to whipsaw on *noise* is correct; refusing to update on *signal* is just anchoring with a flattering name. Conviction is about how much evidence it takes to move you, not about being immovable. A high-conviction Bayesian moves *slowly* on weak evidence and *fast* on strong evidence — the conviction is in the calibration of the move, not in the refusal to move. The trader who "sticks to his guns" through three diagnostic disconfirming prints isn't disciplined; he's about to take the \$6,000 loss.

**"Always react to new data — staying on top of the flow is the job."** The opposite error. Reacting to *everything* means reacting to mostly noise, because most of the flow is undiagnostic — in-line prints, second-tier surveys, recycled headlines, opinions. The job is not to react to every data point; it's to react *proportionally*, which for the large majority of data points means barely reacting at all. The trader who flips on the regional survey isn't responsive; he's about to pay the spread both ways. Filtering signal from noise is upstream of updating, and it has its own treatment in the indicator-trap problem: most indicators are noise dressed as signal — see [the indicator trap](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/the-indicator-trap).

**"Bayes needs hard math — conditional probabilities, formulas, the whole apparatus."** The formula is a *check*, not the method. The method is the four-move loop: write the prior as a number, take in the evidence, ask how diagnostic it is, move proportionally. You can run that loop qualitatively in seconds — "strong surprising print, on-question, good source → move a lot; noisy in-line tick → barely move." The arithmetic is there to build trust in the engine and to settle the occasional close call, not to be ground through on every print. Thinking like a Bayesian is a *habit of proportional updating*, not a calculation you perform.

**"A surprising headline means I should update."** Surprise in the *headline* is not the same as diagnosticity in the *evidence*. Headlines are written to be surprising — that's their job — and most of them contain no new information that separates your scenarios. The question is never "is this dramatic?" but "is this print much more likely under one of my scenarios than the others?" A calm, in-line CPI print can be more diagnostic (by confirming a priced-in path) than a screaming headline with no number behind it. Update on diagnosticity, not on drama.

**"If I was right to be 60%, a confirming print proves I should go to 90%."** No — a single confirming print, if it was already somewhat expected, barely moves you. The size of the update depends on the likelihood *ratio*, and confirming evidence that was likely under both scenarios has a ratio near 1, which means almost no move. Going from 60% to 90% on one expected confirmation is whipsaw in the *confirming* direction — the over-update people forget to guard against because it feels like being right.

**"Updating means I keep changing my mind, which means I never had a real view."** This is the identity trap, and it's why anchoring feels virtuous. Changing the *number* on your view as evidence arrives is not flip-flopping; it is the only intellectually honest thing to do, and it is precisely what a real view *is* — a posterior that responds to data. The person who "never changes their mind" hasn't got conviction; they've fused their identity to a prediction and now defend it as they would defend their reputation, which is the worst possible reason to hold a position. The Bayesian holds views *lightly enough to update* and *firmly enough not to whipsaw* — and feels no embarrassment in saying "I was 60%, the data came in, I'm now 30%, I'm cutting." Being seen to update is a sign of a working process, not a confession of having been wrong; the only real failure is the print that should have moved you and didn't.

## How it plays out in real markets

### The 2022 CPI sequence: updating a Fed-path view print by print

Through 2022, the single most-traded question in markets was the Fed's path, and it was updated, brutally, one CPI print at a time — a real-world Bayesian loop running monthly. Going into the year, the prior across most desks was that inflation was "transitory" and the Fed would tighten gently. Then the prints came: the January release (covering December) ran hot, the February and March prints ran hotter, and the June 2022 print landed at a 9.1% headline year-over-year — a genuine upside surprise to a consensus that kept expecting the peak to be "last month."

The Bayesian behavior was to update the Fed-path posterior *hard and repeatedly* as each hot, diagnostic, surprising print landed: the "gentle tightening" scenario lost mass print by print, and the "aggressive, sustained hikes" scenario gained it, until the market was pricing 75-basis-point moves it would have called unthinkable in January. The traders who did well were not the ones who called the destination in advance; they were the ones who *updated cleanly* — who let each diagnostic surprise move their posterior the amount it deserved, rather than anchoring on the transitory prior (the anchor error, which cost the "transitory" crowd dearly) or whipsawing on every intramonth wiggle.

The reverse ran through late 2023 into 2024, when softening prints diagnostically pulled mass *out* of "higher for longer" and *into* "cuts coming," and the same skill applied in the other direction. The lesson from both: a Fed-path view is never finished. It is a posterior that gets re-marked on every CPI and every jobs report, and the edge is in the *quality of the updating*, not in the original call. Monitoring those inputs as they arrive is its own discipline — see [monitoring a live thesis — building your watch dashboard](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/monitoring-a-live-thesis-building-your-watch-dashboard).

### A single noisy print that should barely move a macro thesis

Contrast the CPI sequence with a single jobs report in an otherwise quiet stretch. Suppose your prior is a 65% lean that the economy stays resilient (a "soft landing" thesis), and a monthly nonfarm payrolls print comes in 80,000 below consensus — a soft number, with a scary headline. The naive reaction is to slash the resilience odds. The Bayesian reaction is to check diagnosticity first: payrolls are *notoriously noisy*, routinely revised by 50,000–100,000 in subsequent months, and a single month's miss within that revision band is barely distinguishable between "economy resilient" and "economy slowing." The likelihood ratio is near 1, so the honest update is small — 65% to maybe 62% — and you do not flip your book on it. You wait for the *sequence*: if the next two prints also disappoint *and* survive revision, that pattern is diagnostic and earns a real update. One noisy print, however much the headline shouts, should barely move a thesis built on the broader trend.

### Strong disconfirming data that forces a cut: a single earnings night

In February 2018, and again on several earnings nights since, a market-leading stock has missed and guided down hard after a long run, and the question for anyone long it was the disconfirming-update test in its purest form. The data is maximally diagnostic *against* the bull thesis: the company's own numbers, the company's own forward guidance, a clean surprise against an expectation of a beat. The likelihood ratio is heavily lopsided toward "the thesis is wrong." The Bayesian response is a large downward update and a real cut — exactly the \$15,000-to-\$5,000 move from the earlier worked example. The traders who got hurt were the ones whose disconfirming-evidence bias kicked in: "one bad quarter, the story's intact, buy the dip" — anchoring on the prior precisely when the most diagnostic evidence they'd get all year was telling them to update. The night the company tells you its own demand is softening is not the night to discount the evidence; it is the night to move the most.

### The COVID crash: when a single piece of evidence justified a violent update

The hardest part of proportional updating is that *occasionally the proportional update is enormous* — and the discipline is recognizing the rare case where flipping hard is correct rather than whipsaw. In late February 2020, as it became clear that the novel coronavirus was spreading uncontained outside China, the evidence arriving was maximally diagnostic for a thesis nobody had been pricing: a synchronized global economic shutdown. The likelihood of "containment fails, economies lock down" given the case-count data exploding across Italy and then everywhere was overwhelming; the likelihood of "this stays a regional event" collapsed by the day. A Bayesian who had been carrying a benign 2020 prior should have updated *violently* — and the ones who did, who let the genuinely diagnostic and surprising evidence move them to near-certainty on a recession within a week, were not whipsawing. They were updating proportionally to evidence that genuinely justified a near-total revision. The anchorers — "markets always overreact to scares, buy the dip" — applied a base rate ("most scares fizzle") that was correct in general and catastrophically wrong for *this* evidence, because this evidence was diagnostic in a way the average scare is not. The lesson cuts against the rest of this post and is the most important nuance in it: proportional does not mean *small*. When the evidence is strong enough, the proportional update is a flip — and the skill is distinguishing the rare flip-justifying evidence from the far more common noise that merely *feels* like it justifies a flip.

## The playbook

Here is the routine to run on the next piece of information that hits your tape. It turns "I saw some news and felt something" into a numeric, proportional, gradeable update.

![Five-step update routine card from stating the prior to re-sizing and logging it](/imgs/blogs/updating-on-new-information-thinking-like-a-bayesian-7.png)

**1. State the prior as a number — before the data lands.** The night before a known catalyst (or right now, for the views you carry), write each view as an explicit probability: "Fed cuts in September = 60%." If it's a multi-scenario view, write all the scenarios and confirm they sum to 100%. No number, no update — you can't move what you never set, and you can't grade a move with no starting point.

**2. Pre-register what would move you, and by how much.** For your main views, write the disconfirming triggers in advance: "a 0.4% core print cuts my odds to 35%; a sub-150k payrolls *sequence* cuts them to 45%." Pre-registering the updates that hurt is your defense against the disconfirming-evidence bias — when the painful print lands, you update mechanically instead of rationalizing.

**3. When data lands, state it as an event and ask the three diagnosticity questions.** Write the raw event ("core CPI +0.2% vs +0.4% expected"), then score it: *on-question?* *clean or noisy?* *surprising versus what was priced?* The more yeses, the bigger the move it earns. A loud headline with no number scores zero — drama is not diagnosticity.

**4. Move the posterior proportionally — and write it next to the prior.** Strong + surprising + on-question → a big move. Weak, in-line, or noisy → a small move or none. Never to 0% or 100% on a single print. Writing "60% → 72%" next to each other makes the size of the move visible, so you can check it against the evidence and grade it later.

**5. Apply the symmetry test.** Ask: "would I update this much *against* my view if the evidence pointed the other way?" If you'd leap on confirming data but explain away disconfirming data, you're anchoring — increase the disconfirming update. If *any* data sends you flipping the book, you're whipsawing — shrink it. Only the symmetric, proportional move passes.

**6. Re-mark all the scenarios so they sum to 100%.** The mass you added to one scenario has to come out of the others. Re-balance base, bull, and bear and re-normalize to 100% — if they sum to 115%, you've been adding confirming evidence without debiting the alternatives, the fingerprint of confirmation bias.

**7. Map the new posterior to size, adjust, and log what moved you.** Run the new probability through your conviction-to-size rule, adjust the position, and write one line in your journal: what the evidence was, how diagnostic you judged it, and how far you moved. Later, when the question resolves, grade whether the update was the *right size* — too big (whipsaw), too small (anchor), or proportional. That grading is what calibrates your future updates, the subject of [calibration — keeping score on your own forecasts](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/calibration-keeping-score-on-your-own-forecasts).

Run that loop and you stop being either trader from the opening. You won't anchor, because step 2 pre-commits you to updating on the data that hurts. You won't whipsaw, because step 3 forces you to score diagnosticity before you move, and step 5 catches over-reactions. What you'll do instead is the only thing that actually works: hold your view as a number, weigh each new print by how much it should genuinely move you, and update by a measured, proportional, gradeable amount — which is the entire skill of turning a constant stream of new information into an accountable, tradeable view.

None of this requires you to predict the future better than the next desk. It requires only that you process the present honestly: that you start from the base rate, hold your view as a number, and let diagnostic evidence move that number by the amount it deserves — no less when the data hurts, no more when the data merely shouts. The market hands you new information every single day; the edge was never in receiving it first, it was in updating on it *right*. The next step is putting that updated posterior to work in the position itself, which is [when to add, cut, or exit — managing the position around the view](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/when-to-add-cut-or-exit-managing-the-position-around-the-view).
