> **TL;DR** — A good market view is produced by a repeatable process, not by inspiration; pros run a daily and weekly routine that reads the same lenses in the same order, updates written priors, and refuses to react to anything that is not a genuine surprise.
>
> - The daily routine has three fixed blocks: a 45-minute pre-market read, an intraday rule to act only when reality diverges from your written priors, and a post-close mark on every open thesis plus a short journal entry.
> - The weekly routine is the weekend deep-dive: reset the regime view, mark every open thesis against last week, score yourself, refill the idea pipeline, and write a one-page week-ahead plan with reaction rules before Monday.
> - You do not react to news; you react to *surprises versus your written priors*. Most of what crosses your screen confirms what you already expected and is therefore noise, however dramatic.
> - The one rule to remember: a 45-minute routine that you run every day beats a 4-hour ad-hoc scramble, because consistency removes recency bias and a written prior is the only thing that lets you tell a surprise from a headline.

At 7:00 a.m. two traders sit down with identical accounts, identical instruments, and the identical market. The first picks up a phone already buzzing with 200 overnight headlines and starts reacting. A war headline, an analyst downgrade, three takes on an overnight crypto move, an unsourced central-bank rumor, a chart with arrows on it. By the open he has formed and abandoned six half-opinions, put on two trades because they felt urgent, and feels informed and busy. By Friday his account is a tangle of small positions he can no longer explain, a few winners, a few more losers, and no memory of why he is in any of them. He has been working hard all week and has produced churn.

The second trader opens a one-page checklist instead of a feed. In forty-five minutes she reads the economic calendar (CPI is the week's one real event, Friday at 8:30), the overnight moves in the two instruments she actually holds, her key levels, who is offside, and the health of each open thesis she carries. She places no trades, because nothing on the list diverged from what she had already written down she expected. At the close she spends ten minutes marking each open thesis alive or dead and writing one paragraph about what she did and why. By Friday she has a coherent book of three positions, each with a written reason and a written line that would tell her she was wrong.

The difference between them is not intelligence, information, or even effort — the first trader worked harder. The difference is that one runs a **process** and the other improvises. This post builds that process from the ground up: the daily routine, the weekly routine, the monthly cadence, and the templates that make the whole thing survive a bad week without you. The thesis is blunt. A market view is the output of a repeatable routine that reads the same lenses in the same order and updates written priors. Routine beats IQ because it removes the two things that quietly destroy retail accounts: recency bias and inconsistency. Where you need a mechanism — how a number actually moves a market, why positioning matters — we link out rather than re-deriving it.

## Foundations: what a process actually is

A **process** is a fixed, written sequence of steps you run regardless of how you feel, what the market did yesterday, or how interesting today's headlines are. It has three defining properties, and each one is doing real work.

First, it is **the same every time**. The same lenses, read in the same order, at the same time of day. You do not decide each morning what to look at; the decision was made once, in advance, when you built the routine. This is the property that removes recency bias, and we will return to why that matters.

Second, it is **written down**. A routine that lives in your head is not a routine; it is a good intention that evaporates the moment the market gets scary. The checklist exists on paper or on a screen precisely so that on the worst morning — the one where you are down money and adrenaline is high — you can run it mechanically without trusting your judgment to remember what to check.

Third, it **ends in an output you can audit later**. Each block of the routine produces something written: a day plan, a thesis-health mark, a journal entry. The output is what lets you check, weeks later, whether the process is working — and it is what lets the process catch its own mistakes.

Contrast this with **ad-hoc reading**, which is what most people actually do. Ad-hoc reading means you open whatever is loudest, read until something feels important, react to the feeling, and stop when you run out of attention. There is no fixed order, nothing is written, and nothing is audited. It feels like analysis. It is mostly a stress response to salience — your attention is captured by whatever is most dramatic, and drama and decision-relevance are almost completely uncorrelated.

### Why a checklist beats improvisation under stress

The case for routine is not a productivity platitude; it rests on a specific, well-documented fact about human judgment. Under stress, time pressure, and emotional load — which is to say, exactly the conditions of an active trading day — human improvisation degrades sharply and predictably. We anchor on the most recent vivid input. We confuse the feeling of urgency with the importance of the decision. We forget routine checks precisely when they matter most. We act to relieve anxiety rather than because acting has positive expected value.

The famous evidence for checklists comes from outside finance. In aviation and surgery — two fields where experts under pressure were making catastrophic, avoidable errors — a dumb written checklist, read aloud in a fixed order, cut error rates dramatically. The pilots and surgeons were not unintelligent; they were highly trained experts. The checklist did not add knowledge. It removed *variance*: it guaranteed that the known, important steps happened every time, including on the bad days when the expert's working memory was overloaded and would otherwise have skipped one. The lesson transfers exactly to trading. Your edge is not in remembering to check the calendar on a calm morning; anyone does that. Your edge is in checking it on the chaotic morning when you are already down and would otherwise have forgotten.

There is a second, subtler benefit. A fixed routine produces a **fixed baseline**, and a baseline is what lets you detect a deviation. If you read the same five things every morning, then on the morning something is genuinely abnormal — overnight moves twice their usual size, a position suddenly offside, an event you had not flagged — the abnormality *jumps out* against the familiar backdrop. The improviser has no baseline, so nothing jumps out; everything is equally novel and equally salient, which means nothing is. You cannot spot an anomaly without a norm, and a routine is how you manufacture a norm.

### The cost of an unsystematic read

An unsystematic read is not merely less tidy than a routine; it is actively expensive, in four specific ways. Naming them is the foundation for everything that follows, because the routine is engineered to attack each one.

The first cost is **recency bias**. Without a fixed order, your read is dominated by whatever you saw most recently and most vividly. The overnight headline that scared you weights ten times its true importance; the slow, structural shift that actually matters gets no weight because it did not generate a dramatic moment. Your view becomes a function of the news cycle's editing rather than of the actual state of the market.

The second cost is **blindsides**. The events that hurt most are the scheduled ones you simply forgot to look up — an FOMC decision, a CPI print, an earnings release in a name you hold. These are not unknowable; they are on a public calendar. A blindside from an unscheduled shock is bad luck. A blindside from a scheduled event is a *process failure*, and it is entirely preventable by a five-minute calendar scan.

The third cost is **inconsistency**. On Monday you check positioning; on Tuesday you forget; on Wednesday you are tired and skip the whole thing. Your decisions are therefore made on different information on different days, which means your results carry a large component of noise that has nothing to do with skill. You cannot improve a process you do not run consistently, because you cannot tell whether a bad week was bad luck, a bad market, or simply the three days you skipped the routine.

The fourth cost is **overtrading**. Every input that crosses an undisciplined screen creates pressure to act. More reading, more pressure, more trades — and most of those trades are reactions to noise, each one paying commission, spread, slippage, and tax. The routine attacks this directly with a single intraday rule, examined below: you act only on a surprise relative to a written prior, which means most of the day you correctly do nothing.

## The daily routine

The daily routine is three fixed blocks, shown in the cover figure: a **pre-market read** before the open, an **intraday rule** that governs what you do while the market is live, and a **post-close mark** plus journal after the close. The whole thing takes about an hour spread across the day. We build each block in turn.

### Block 1 — the pre-market read

The pre-market read is a fixed sweep of six lenses, in a fixed order, that you run before the open. The order is deliberate: you go from what is *scheduled and external* (which you cannot change and must simply know) toward what is *personal and actionable* (your levels, your positions, your plan). The lens-sweep card below is the whole block on one page; you run it top-left to bottom-right, and each lens ends in one written line.

![Pre-market lens-sweep card with six numbered lenses from calendar to day plan](/imgs/blogs/the-daily-and-weekly-process-a-repeatable-reading-routine-4.png)

**Lens 1 — the economic calendar.** What is scheduled today, at what time, with what consensus? This is the single highest-value, lowest-effort lens, and it goes first because a scheduled event reframes everything else. If CPI prints at 8:30, then nothing you read before 8:30 is worth trading on, and you should be flat or hedged into the number. The output is one line: *today's events, their times, and the consensus the market expects*. Most days this line is short or empty, and that emptiness is itself information — a quiet day is a day to do nothing.

**Lens 2 — overnight moves.** What moved while you slept, and why? You read the actual closing and overnight levels, not the narrative wrapped around them. The point is not to absorb every move; it is to notice the ones that are *abnormal against your baseline* and the ones that touch instruments you hold. The output is one line: *what moved more than usual, and whether it changes anything I hold*.

**Lens 3 — key levels.** Where are your lines? These are the prices you decided, in advance and calmly, where you would add, trim, or stop. Writing them before the open is what stops you from inventing a convenient level in the heat of a move. The output is the levels themselves, already written: *add at X, trim at Y, stop at Z*.

**Lens 4 — positioning deltas.** Who is offside, and where is the pain? Positioning is the lens that tells you not what is true but what the market is leaning on, and therefore where a violent move could come from if that lean is wrong. You do not need exotic data; even a rough read of "everyone is long this, sentiment is euphoric" is enough to flag where a pain trade lives. We link out to the mechanism in [Positioning and the Pain Trade](/blog/trading/event-trading/positioning-and-the-pain-trade); here the routine just asks you to write one line: *where is the crowd leaning, and what would hurt it*.

**Lens 5 — thesis health.** For each open thesis you carry, is it still alive? You walk each position and ask: does the evidence still support the original reason, or has something quietly broken? This is the lens that prevents you from holding a position whose reason has evaporated. The output is a one-word mark per thesis — *alive*, *weakening*, or *dead* — which feeds the post-close mark we formalize below.

**Lens 6 — the day plan.** Given lenses 1 through 5, what will you do today, conditionally? The plan is written as if-then rules: *if CPI surprises above 3.4%, I add to the short; if it is in the expected band, I do nothing; if a level breaks, I stop.* Writing the plan before the open is the entire point — you are pre-committing your reactions while you are calm, so that the live market cannot talk you out of them.

The whole sweep takes about 45 minutes once it is a habit, and the discipline is in reading all six even on a boring morning, because the boring mornings are where you build the baseline that makes the dangerous mornings legible.

The *order* is not arbitrary, and it is worth dwelling on why. You read the external, scheduled lenses first (calendar, overnight) before the personal, actionable ones (levels, positioning, thesis health, plan) because the external state reframes how you read the personal state. If you check your key levels before you have checked the calendar, you might calmly plan to add at a support level — only to discover, too late, that a major data release is due an hour later that could blow through it. Reading the calendar first means every subsequent lens is interpreted in light of what is scheduled. The day plan comes last because it is the synthesis: it can only be written once the other five lenses have reported. A routine that reads the lenses in a random order each day loses this compounding structure and degrades back toward ad-hoc reading even though it technically covers the same ground. Order is part of the process, not a detail.

There is a failure mode worth naming here: the **lens you always skip**. Almost every trader who builds a routine quietly drops one lens — usually positioning, because it feels soft, or the day plan, because the open is approaching and writing it feels like a luxury. The skipped lens is, with grim reliability, the one that would have caught the next blindside. The defense is the forced output: if every lens must produce a written line, the missing line is visible at a glance, and you cannot skip it without noticing. A routine with optional steps is not a routine; it is a menu, and under stress you will order only the comfortable items.

#### Worked example: the 45-minute routine versus the 4-hour doomscroll

Put a dollar figure on the time itself, on a \$100,000 account. The disciplined trader spends 45 minutes pre-market, about 15 minutes on intraday checks, and 10 minutes on the post-close mark — roughly 70 to 75 minutes a day of focused, decision-relevant work. The doomscroller spends about 4 hours, or 240 minutes, of fragmented screen time, almost all of it reactive and most of it low-signal.

The naive view is that more time means more information means better decisions. The opposite is true, and the cost is concrete. Suppose the extra 165 minutes a day of churn produces, conservatively, two noise-driven trades a week — trades placed because something *felt* urgent, not because a written prior was surprised. At, say, \$120 of round-trip friction each (commission, spread, slippage, and the tax drag on short holds), that is \$240 a week, or roughly \$12,000 a year, which is **12% of the \$100,000 account** paid out in friction for trades that had no edge. The routine's 45 minutes does not just save time; it saves the \$12,000, because its intraday rule means most of those urgent feelings never become trades. Spending less time reading is what makes you richer here — the extra hours were buying you negative-expectancy activity.

### Block 2 — intraday: react to surprises, not to news

Once the market is open, the routine collapses to a single rule, and it is the most important rule in this entire post: **you act only when reality diverges from your written priors.** Not when something happens. Not when something feels urgent. Only when a thing that occurs is *different from what you wrote down you expected*.

This requires that you have written priors, which is exactly what the pre-market plan produced. A prior is a written expectation: *I expect CPI around 3.1%; I expect the market to be quiet until the print; I expect my short to be fine unless the 2-year yield breaks above my entry.* With those priors written, the live day becomes a comparison engine. CPI prints at 3.1%? That matches your prior — it is not a surprise, it is a confirmation, and confirmations are not trades. The 2-year yield drifts but stays below your line? Your thesis is intact; do nothing. The number prints at 3.5%, well outside the expected band? *Now* you have a surprise, your pre-written if-then rule fires, and you act on a plan you made while calm.

The reason this rule is so powerful is that markets only move on surprises in the first place. A number that matches expectations is already in the price; the move happened when the expectation formed, not when the number confirmed it. We link out to the full mechanism in [The Reaction Function](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-reaction-function-why-the-same-number-moves-differently) and to what "already in the price" means in [Consensus, Expectations, and Priced-In](/blog/trading/event-trading/consensus-expectations-and-priced-in). For the routine, the consequence is direct: if you find yourself wanting to trade because a number came out, ask whether the number *surprised* you relative to what you wrote this morning. If it did not, the urge is noise, and the correct action is nothing.

This is also the rule that kills overtrading. An undisciplined trader reacts to every headline, because every headline is novel to someone with no baseline. The routine's reader reacts to almost nothing, because almost everything confirms a prior that was already written and already priced. The empty space in your trading log is the routine working.

A practical subtlety: a surprise is not only an event that prints far from consensus. It is *any* divergence between reality and what you wrote down, and divergences come in three flavors that the routine treats differently. The first is a **data surprise** — a number prints outside the band you expected, and your pre-written if-then rule fires. The second is a **price surprise** — the number was in line, but the market's *reaction* to it was not, which is itself information about positioning and tells you the lean was the opposite of what you thought. We treat that second-order effect in [The Reaction Function](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-reaction-function-why-the-same-number-moves-differently); for the routine, an unexpected reaction is a surprise even when the data was not. The third is a **silence surprise** — something you expected to happen did not, or a level you expected to hold broke without a catalyst. All three are divergences from a written prior, and all three earn a look; everything else, however loud, does not. The skill the rule trains is not predicting events but *noticing the gap* between what you expected and what occurred, which is only possible if the expectation was written before the event.

One more guardrail belongs in the intraday block: a hard cap on discretionary trades per day. Even with the surprise rule, a stressed trader can rationalize a non-surprise into a surprise. A pre-committed limit — say, no more than two new discretionary positions a day except on a genuine, pre-flagged event — is a circuit breaker that catches the rationalization. If you find yourself wanting a third trade, the cap forces you to ask whether the urge is a real surprise or the overtrading reflex wearing a disguise. The cap costs you nothing on a normal day, because a normal day rarely produces even two genuine surprises, and it saves you on the day the reflex is loudest.

#### Worked example: the overtrading tax from reacting to non-surprises

Make the cost of breaking this rule explicit. Take a trader on a \$100,000 account who, lacking written priors, reacts to roughly three "events" a week that are not actually surprises — a number that came in on consensus, a headline that restated a known fact, a move that was already priced. Each reaction is a trade, and each trade costs about \$110 in total friction.

That is \$330 a week, \$1,320 a month, and about \$17,000 a year — **17% of the account** — paid for the privilege of reacting to things that, by construction, contained no new information. Worse, these are not break-even trades that merely cost friction; reacting to a non-surprise usually means buying what already moved or selling what already fell, so the trades carry negative expected value *on top of* the friction. The surprise-versus-prior rule does not improve your win rate on good trades. It does something more valuable: it deletes an entire category of guaranteed-loss trades, and on a \$100,000 account that deletion is worth roughly \$17,000 a year before you have improved a single real decision. The takeaway is blunt: the cheapest edge available to most traders is simply to stop paying the overtrading tax.

### Block 3 — the post-close mark and the journal

After the close, the routine has two short steps. The first is the **post-close mark**: for every open thesis, you write down whether the day's evidence left it alive, weakening, or dead, and what specifically moved it. The second is the **journal**: a few sentences on what you did today, why, and what (if anything) changed your view.

The post-close mark is the step people skip, and it is the step that most often saves them. The danger it defends against is the **quiet break** — a thesis that is invalidated not by a dramatic event but by a slow accumulation of small, contrary evidence that you would never notice without a daily forced check. You do not lose money on the theses that blow up loudly; those you notice. You lose money on the thesis that was quietly wrong for two weeks while you were not looking, because no single day's move was large enough to demand attention. The mark forces attention every single day, regardless of whether the day felt important.

The template for the mark is fixed, and the fixedness is the point. Every thesis gets the same four fields, so a break in any of them cannot hide.

![Daily thesis-health mark template with four stacked fields thesis would-change-my-mind today's evidence and verdict](/imgs/blogs/the-daily-and-weekly-process-a-repeatable-reading-routine-7.png)

The four fields are: the **thesis** itself in one line; the **would-change-my-mind** condition — the written invalidation level that tells you when you are wrong; **today's evidence** — what specifically happened to the thesis today; and the **verdict** — alive, weakening, or dead, plus one phrase of reasoning. Marking all four takes under a minute per thesis. With three open positions, the whole mark is three minutes. Those three minutes are the cheapest insurance you will ever buy, because they convert a quiet two-week break into a one-day flag.

The journal is the second compounding habit, and it is the one that turns the whole routine into something that improves over time rather than merely runs. A journal entry is not a diary of feelings; it is a record of decisions and their rationales, written close enough to the decision that you cannot rewrite history. We treat the journal as its own discipline in [The Decision Journal](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/the-decision-journal-the-habit-that-compounds); here it is the closing step of the daily block, and its minimum viable form is three sentences: *what I did, why, and what I am now watching for.*

The reason the journal must be written *the same day* is hindsight bias — the documented human tendency to remember, after an outcome is known, that you "always thought" the thing that happened would happen. A journal entry written a week later is not a record; it is a reconstruction contaminated by knowing how the trade turned out, and it teaches you nothing because it confirms whatever story the outcome suggests. An entry written the evening of the decision, before the outcome is known, captures your *actual reasoning* under your *actual uncertainty*, which is the only raw material from which calibration can be learned. The discipline of writing it close to the decision is what makes the later review honest. Two months on, reading an entry that said "I expect this to hold but I am only 60% confident," you can grade the 60% against what actually happened — something you cannot do with a memory that has quietly rounded itself up to 90% after the fact.

#### Worked example: the cost of skipping the post-close mark on a \$15,000 position

Quantify the skipped mark on a single, concrete position. You are short a sector ETF, a \$15,000 position, on the thesis that margins are compressing. Your written would-change-my-mind condition was: *if the next two earnings reports show stable margins, the thesis is dead.*

The first report comes out on a busy Thursday. You are tired, the market was choppy, and you skip the post-close mark that evening. The report quietly showed stable margins — your invalidation condition was half-met — but because you did not mark it, you do not register it. You skip the mark again the following week. The second report, also stable, fully invalidates the thesis. Your written rule said the thesis was dead, but you never read your own rule because you stopped marking. You hold the losing short for three more weeks out of inattention, and it grinds against you another 9%. On a \$15,000 position that is **about \$1,350 of avoidable loss**, every dollar of it attributable not to a bad thesis — the thesis was reasonable when you put it on — but to skipping a one-minute mark twice. The mark would have flagged the first stable report the evening it printed, and your own pre-written rule would have closed the position weeks earlier and dollars cheaper. The lesson is that the post-close mark's value is precisely the losses it prevents on the days you least feel like doing it.

## The weekly routine

The daily routine keeps you from getting blindsided and from overtrading. But it operates on a short horizon, and it cannot, by itself, tell you whether your *overall stance* is still right. That is the job of the weekly routine: a weekend deep-dive that resets the big picture, scores your own performance, and refills the pipeline of ideas for the week ahead. Where the daily routine is fast and tactical, the weekly routine is slow and strategic. The pipeline below shows its six stages, run in order over an hour or two on a weekend.

![Weekend deep-dive pipeline from regime state through thesis review scorecard idea pipeline and week-ahead calendar to written plan](/imgs/blogs/the-daily-and-weekly-process-a-repeatable-reading-routine-2.png)

### Stage 1 — the regime state

You begin by stepping back from individual positions and asking the largest question: **what market are we in?** Is this a risk-on or risk-off environment? Is the dominant theme inflation, growth, liquidity, or policy? Is volatility low and trending, or high and chaotic? You are not forecasting here; you are *describing the current state* so that every thesis you hold can be checked for consistency with it. A short thesis that depends on tightening financial conditions is in trouble if the regime has quietly shifted to easing, and you will only notice the inconsistency if you state the regime explicitly each week. The output is one paragraph: *the regime as I see it right now, and the one thing that would tell me it has changed.*

### Stage 2 — the thesis review

Next you walk every open thesis, the way the daily mark does — but at a deeper level and against a longer baseline. The daily mark asks "did today break this?" The weekly review asks "is this thesis still better than the alternatives, and is it still consistent with the regime I just described?" This is where you catch the thesis that survived every daily mark on its own terms but no longer fits the bigger picture. The output is a verdict per thesis: *keep, trim, add, or close*, with a written reason. We treat the live tracking of a thesis as its own discipline in [Monitoring a Live Thesis](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/monitoring-a-live-thesis-building-your-watch-dashboard); the weekly review is where that dashboard gets its full read.

### Stage 3 — the scorecard

Then you grade yourself, honestly and in writing. How many of the week's decisions were good *decisions* (correct given what you knew at the time), regardless of whether they made money? What was your biggest miss, and was it a process failure or bad luck? Did you follow your own rules, or did you break the surprise-versus-prior rule and overtrade? The scorecard is not about P&L; a good decision can lose money and a bad one can win. It is about whether the *process* ran, because the process is the only thing you control and the only thing that compounds. The output is a short honest grade and the single most important lesson of the week.

The crucial discipline of the scorecard is separating **decision quality from outcome quality**. In a domain with as much randomness as markets, a single week's P&L tells you almost nothing about whether you played well; a perfectly reasoned trade can lose to noise and a reckless one can win on luck. If you grade yourself on P&L, you will learn exactly the wrong lessons — you will reinforce the lucky reckless trades and punish the unlucky good ones, training yourself toward worse process over time. Grading on decision quality inverts this: you reward the trade that was correct given what you knew, even when it lost, and you flag the trade that was lucky, even when it won. Over a quarter, decision-quality grading is what bends your process toward skill, while outcome grading bends it toward superstition. The scorecard's one job is to keep the feedback signal clean, which means deliberately ignoring the noisiest variable in the room — this week's money.

### Stage 4 — the idea pipeline

With the existing book reviewed, you refill the pipeline. You generate three to five candidate ideas for the week ahead — not trades, *candidates* — each with a written thesis and, crucially, a written **trigger**: the specific condition under which it would become a trade. An idea without a trigger is just an opinion; an idea with a trigger is a pre-loaded decision that fires only when reality meets your condition. This stage is what lets the daily routine be reactive-in-a-good-way: by the time a trigger fires intraday, the thinking was already done on the calm weekend. The output is a short watchlist with a thesis and a trigger for each name.

### Stage 5 — the week-ahead calendar scan

Now you map the week's scheduled events and pre-write your reaction to each. This is the single most blindside-preventing step in the entire system. You go through every scheduled release, decision, and event, rank them by *surprise potential* rather than by how much coverage they will get, and for the one or two that matter you pre-write the if-then reaction. The grid below is the output: every event gets a slot, a consensus, and a written reaction, so that on the morning of the event your daily routine simply reads a plan you already made.

![Week-ahead calendar scan grid with days of the week consensus values and pre-written reaction rules](/imgs/blogs/the-daily-and-weekly-process-a-repeatable-reading-routine-6.png)

Notice what the grid does. Most days are marked quiet — no catalyst, hold and do nothing — and that is correct; most days *are* quiet. The week's one real event (here, a Friday CPI print) is flagged in red with its consensus and a fully pre-written reaction: above 3.4%, add short duration; below 2.8%, cover the hedge; inside the band, do nothing because it is priced. An FOMC decision mid-week is flagged amber as a known risk to trim into. The weighting principle at the bottom is the discipline: rank by surprise potential, not coverage, because one print can dominate the week while a dozen loud headlines change nothing.

### Stage 6 — the written week-ahead plan

Finally, you compress all of the above into a single page set before Monday. The regime in a paragraph, the book in a line per position, the watchlist with triggers, the calendar with reactions, and the one lesson from last week's scorecard. This page is the artifact the whole weekly routine exists to produce, and it is what the daily routine reads from all week. When Monday's pre-market sweep asks "what is scheduled today, and what is my plan?", the answer is already written on this page.

#### Worked example: a \$3,000 loss avoided by the calendar scan

Put a number on the calendar scan. You hold a \$30,000 long position in a single stock. During Saturday's week-ahead scan, you check the calendar and notice — easy to miss without the scan — that the company reports earnings on Wednesday after the close. Your daily routine never would have surfaced this on Wednesday morning, because by then it would be too late to act calmly; the scan catches it three days early, while you are unhurried.

You now have a pre-written decision to make. Holding a \$30,000 single-name position through an earnings report is a coin-flip on a binary event, not a position consistent with your thesis (which was about the slow margin story, not the quarterly print). You decide, on the calm weekend, to trim the position by half into Wednesday's close, taking your exposure to \$15,000 across the event. Wednesday night the company misses and gaps down 20% the next morning. On the full \$30,000 position that would have been a \$6,000 loss. On the trimmed \$15,000 position it is a \$3,000 loss — **\$3,000 saved by a five-minute calendar scan three days earlier.** The scan did not predict the miss; it could not. What it did was flag a scheduled binary event in time to size the position calmly, converting a blindside into a managed risk. The lesson is that the calendar scan's payoff is not in forecasting events but in never being surprised that one is coming.

## The monthly and quarterly cadence

The daily routine operates on hours, the weekly on a week. Two slower loops complete the system, and skipping them is how a routine that is technically running can still drift off course over months.

The **monthly review** zooms out to the level of the whole book and the whole process. Once a month you ask: across the last four weekly scorecards, what pattern repeats? Is there a recurring process failure — always overtrading on FOMC days, always holding losers too long, always cutting winners early? Monthly is the right horizon to catch these, because a single week is too noisy to reveal a pattern and a quarter is too slow to fix one. You also rebalance attention here: which sources earned their place this month, and which should be cut from the information diet? We treat the diet itself as its own discipline in [Building Your Information Diet](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/building-your-information-diet-signal-versus-noise); the monthly review is when you prune it. The output is one identified pattern and one concrete change to the process for next month.

The **quarterly review** is the largest loop, and it questions the routine itself rather than the trades. Is the regime view from three months ago still right, or did the whole environment shift while the weekly cadence was tracking individual theses? Is the routine still well-matched to the market you are now trading, or has it calcified into a ritual that no longer surfaces what matters? This is also where you check your *overall calibration*: across a quarter of marks and journal entries, were your written priors systematically too confident, too cautious, biased in some direction? A quarter is enough data to see calibration that a week cannot. The output is a verdict on the process: *keep as is, adjust these specific steps, or rebuild this part.*

The four loops nest. The daily catches the surprise; the weekly resets the stance; the monthly catches the recurring mistake; the quarterly catches the drift in the routine itself. Each longer loop audits the loop below it, which is why a single broken loop does not silently sink the whole process — the loop above it is built to catch the failure.

#### Worked example: the compounding value of the four-loop cadence on a \$100,000 account

Tie the loops together with a dollar estimate on a \$100,000 account over a year. We have already costed two of the failures the routine prevents: roughly \$17,000 a year in overtrading tax (the intraday surprise rule), and the kind of \$1,350 quiet-break loss the daily mark catches, of which a realistic year might hold three or four — call it \$5,000. Add the weekly calendar scan saving the occasional managed-event loss like the \$3,000 example, perhaps \$6,000 across a year of several such events. Add the monthly review catching one recurring process failure before it does real damage — conservatively another \$4,000.

That is roughly \$32,000 of avoided losses and friction a year on a \$100,000 account — **about 32% of capital** — and not one dollar of it came from a better forecast or a smarter trade. Every dollar came from *not doing* something the routine prevented: not overtrading, not holding a quietly broken thesis, not being blindsided, not repeating a known mistake. This is the central, counterintuitive economics of process: its return shows up as losses that never happened, which is exactly why it is so easy to neglect — you never see the disasters you avoided. The lesson is that on a typical retail account, the process is worth more than the alpha, because the process is what stops the leaks that would otherwise drain the alpha away.

## Templating it so it survives a bad week

A routine that depends on your willpower will fail on exactly the week you most need it: the bad week, when you are down money, stressed, and least inclined to do anything methodical. The whole point of templating is to build a routine that runs *without* willpower, so that the worst week is the week it protects you most. Four design principles make a routine bad-week-proof.

**Make it written and physical.** The checklist exists on paper taped to your monitor or in a single pinned document, not in your memory. On a calm day this seems redundant; on a panic day it is the only thing standing between you and improvisation. The template should be runnable mechanically by a version of you who is too rattled to think — that is its design spec.

**Make each step produce a forced output.** A step that produces nothing is a step you can skip without noticing. A step that produces a written line — a verdict, a level, a mark — cannot be silently skipped, because the missing line is visible. Forced outputs are how a routine audits its own completion.

**Make it short enough to run on the worst day.** A two-hour daily routine will be abandoned within a month; a 45-minute one survives because it is cheaper than the guilt of skipping it. The constraint is real: design the routine to the time budget of your worst, busiest, most stressed day, not your idealized calm Sunday. A routine you actually run at 70% is infinitely better than a perfect one you abandon.

**Separate the calm decision from the hot execution.** The deepest principle in the whole system. Every decision that can be made calmly should be made calmly and written down — the levels, the triggers, the reaction rules, the invalidation conditions — so that the hot, live moment is reduced to *executing a pre-made decision* rather than *making a new one under pressure*. The pre-market plan, the weekly triggers, and the calendar reactions all do this: they move the thinking to the calm hours and leave the live hours for mechanical execution. This is why the template survives a bad week — on a bad week you are not deciding, you are executing decisions your calmer self already made.

## Common misconceptions

A few beliefs reliably keep traders improvising when they should be running a routine. Each is worth correcting directly.

**"More screen time means a better view."** This is the doomscroller's premise, and it is backwards. Beyond a small daily budget of decision-relevant reading, additional screen time degrades your view rather than improving it: it raises your confidence without raising your accuracy, it floods you with salient noise that crowds out interpretation, and it manufactures the urge to trade. The disciplined trader's 45 minutes beats the doomscroller's four hours not despite being shorter but *because* it is shorter — the extra hours were buying churn. The right metric is decision-relevant information per minute, and a routine maximizes it precisely by stopping early.

**"I'll remember to check that."** You will not, and the belief that you will is exactly why blindsides happen. Human working memory under stress is unreliable in a specific, predictable way: it drops routine checks first, and it drops them hardest on the days they matter most. The fix is not to try harder to remember; it is to externalize the memory into a written checklist that does not depend on your recall. Every "I'll remember to check X" is a future blindside with a date on it.

**"Routine kills intuition."** The opposite is true: routine *frees* intuition by handling the mechanical work so your judgment can focus on the genuinely hard, genuinely novel parts. A routine does not tell you what your view should be; it guarantees you have read the right inputs in the right order before you form one, and it pre-handles the repetitive decisions so your scarce judgment is spent on the few that actually require it. The improviser's intuition is not free; it is consumed by remembering to check things and fighting recency bias. The routine returns that bandwidth to where intuition is actually valuable.

**"I react to news."** No — you react to *surprises relative to your written priors*, and the difference is the whole game. Reacting to news means trading every dramatic headline, most of which is already priced and therefore contains no edge. Reacting to surprises versus priors means trading only when reality diverges from what you had already written down you expected, which is rare and is where the edge lives. If you do not have written priors, you cannot tell a surprise from a headline, which is why the pre-market plan is load-bearing for the entire intraday rule.

**"A routine is for beginners; pros wing it."** Exactly inverted. The professionals run the most disciplined routines precisely because they have learned, expensively, that improvisation fails under pressure. The image of the gunslinger trader trading on feel is a myth that survives because the survivors are visible and the much larger population who blew up winging it are not. Routine is not training wheels you discard; it is the load-bearing structure that lets a professional sustain a process across decades and bad weeks without burning out. We treat the psychology of sticking to a process under pressure in [Trading Psychology and the Execution Gap](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/trading-psychology-and-the-execution-gap).

## How it plays out in real markets

The abstract case for routine becomes concrete in the moments where it either saves an account or where its absence wrecks one. Three patterns, drawn from the kinds of episodes every active trader has lived through.

**The blindside avoided by a five-minute calendar scan.** Take the run-up to any major scheduled event — a CPI print during the 2022 inflation scare, an FOMC decision, a marquee earnings report. In 2022, CPI prints became the single most important scheduled events in markets; a hot print could move the entire equity and rates complex by multiple percent in minutes. The trader running a weekly calendar scan flagged each CPI date the prior weekend, knew the consensus, sized positions calmly into the print, and had a pre-written reaction rule for a surprise in either direction. The improviser, with no scan, was repeatedly caught at full size into a binary macro event he had not flagged, taking the full violence of the move with no plan. The scan did not let the disciplined trader predict the prints — nobody could — but it converted every print from a blindside into a managed, pre-sized risk. Over a year of monthly CPI releases, that difference compounds into the gap between a controlled book and a series of avoidable disasters.

**The quiet break caught by a post-close mark.** The 2020 COVID crash is remembered for the violent February-March collapse, but the more instructive process lesson is in the slow theses that broke quietly *before* the crash, in the calm of January and early February. A trader long a travel or hospitality thesis, marking it each evening against a written would-change-my-mind condition, would have registered the steady drip of contrary evidence — early supply-chain notes, the first demand warnings — as it accumulated, days before the market repriced violently. No single day's evidence was dramatic enough to demand attention; the value of the daily mark was that it forced attention regardless, so the accumulation became visible. The trader who skipped the mark saw nothing until the thesis broke loudly and expensively. The mark does not give you foresight; it gives you the discipline to notice a thesis dying slowly, which is the only kind of death you can act on in time.

**Doomscrolling churn versus a disciplined week.** The clearest contrast is the ordinary, eventful week with no single defining crisis — the kind that makes up most of a trading year. The doomscroller spends the week reacting: a Monday headline, a Tuesday rumor, a Wednesday move he chases, a Thursday reversal he gets caught in, a Friday he cannot reconstruct. He has traded a dozen times, paid friction on all of it, and ends the week with a confused book and a worse-calibrated view than he started with, because the week's noise has overwritten his priors. The disciplined trader read the same week through a fixed routine, traded twice — only where a surprise diverged from a written prior — marked every thesis each evening, and ends the week with three coherent positions and a written record of exactly why she holds each one. By Friday the difference is not luck; it is the entirely predictable output of process versus improvisation, repeated five days running. The episodes that matter are rarely the famous crashes; they are the ordinary weeks where one trader compounds a process and the other compounds churn.

## The playbook

This is the routine to run tomorrow. Print it, tape it to the monitor, and run it mechanically until it is a habit. It is deliberately short enough to survive your worst, busiest day.

**The daily routine — pre-market (45 minutes):**

```
1. Calendar     -> what is scheduled today? time + consensus. One line.
2. Overnight    -> what moved more than usual? does it touch what I hold?
3. Key levels   -> my lines, written: add at X, trim at Y, stop at Z.
4. Positioning  -> where is the crowd leaning? where is the pain trade?
5. Thesis health-> each open thesis: alive / weakening / dead, and why.
6. Day plan     -> if-then rules, written before the open. Pre-commit reactions.
```

**The daily routine — intraday (the one rule):**

```
Act ONLY when reality diverges from a written prior.
- Number matches consensus?     confirmation, not a trade. Do nothing.
- Headline restates known fact?  priced. Do nothing.
- Reality breaks a written prior? the pre-written if-then rule fires. Act.
The empty trade log is the rule working.
```

**The daily routine — post-close (10 minutes):**

```
For EACH open thesis, mark four fields:
  THESIS:               one line.
  WOULD CHANGE MY MIND:  the written invalidation level.
  TODAY'S EVIDENCE:      what specifically happened to it today.
  VERDICT:               alive / weakening / dead, + one phrase of reasoning.
Then journal: what I did, why, what I am now watching for. Three sentences.
```

**The weekly routine — weekend deep-dive (60 to 90 minutes):**

```
1. Regime state   -> what market are we in? one paragraph + what would change it.
2. Thesis review  -> each position: keep / trim / add / close, with a reason.
3. Scorecard      -> grade the DECISIONS, not the P&L. Biggest lesson of the week.
4. Idea pipeline  -> 3-5 candidates, each with a thesis AND a written trigger.
5. Calendar scan  -> every event, ranked by SURPRISE potential. Pre-write reactions.
6. Week-ahead plan-> compress all of the above to ONE page, set before Monday.
```

**The slower loops:**

```
Monthly  -> across 4 scorecards, what pattern repeats? One process change.
Quarterly-> is the regime view still right? is the routine still well-matched?
            check calibration: were my priors systematically over/under-confident?
```

**The four design rules that make it survive a bad week:**

```
- Written and physical: runnable by a rattled version of you. No reliance on memory.
- Forced output per step: a missing line is visible; a skipped step is not.
- Short enough for your WORST day, not your ideal Sunday.
- Decide calm, execute hot: move every decision you can to the calm hours,
  so the live moment only executes a pre-made decision.
```

The discipline is not in any single step; it is in running all of them on the boring days, because the boring days build the baseline that makes the dangerous days legible, and because the routine's entire value is the disasters it quietly prevents — the ones you never see because they never happened. A good view is not produced by inspiration on a good morning. It is produced by a process that runs the same way every morning, including the bad ones. Routine beats IQ because IQ is inconsistent under stress and a routine, by construction, is not.
