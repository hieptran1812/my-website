> **TL;DR** — Oil is the one commodity that is *both* a direct ingredient of the inflation number *and* an input cost to almost everything else, so a big oil move does not stay in the energy aisle — it runs through headline inflation, into central-bank policy, into bond yields, and out the other side as a re-pricing of every stock. The single skill this post teaches is to tell a *demand-pull* oil rise (good news, growth is strong) from a *supply-shock* oil rise (a tax on growth plus an inflation impulse), because the same price move sends the opposite signal for stocks and bonds.
>
> - **A supply-shock oil spike is the worst regime for a 60/40 portfolio**, because it hits stocks (slower growth *and* a lower valuation multiple) and bonds (higher yields) at the same time. In **2022**, WTI ripped to ~\$124, CPI hit a 40-year high of **9.06%**, the Fed lifted rates **4.5 points** in nine months, US stocks fell **18.1%**, bonds fell **13.0%**, and a 60/40 lost **16.0%** — its worst year since the 1930s.
> - **Same oil move, opposite meaning.** When strong demand pulls oil up, stocks usually rise *with* it (positive correlation, benign). When a war or an OPEC cut shoves oil up, stocks usually fall (negative correlation, a growth tax). Reading *which* driver is at work is the whole game.
> - **Energy equities earn their keep in exactly the regime that hurts everything else.** In 2022, the Bloomberg Commodity index returned **+16.1%** and the energy sector led the S&P — the only large pockets that *won* the year a diversified portfolio bled.
> - The one number to remember: a **\$50** oil spike removes tens of billions of dollars from US household spending *and* pushes headline CPI up roughly **1 to 1.5 points** — a tax and an inflation impulse in the same move.

In the autumn of 1973, a group of Arab oil-exporting nations announced an embargo on shipments to the United States and several of its allies. Within a few months the price of a barrel of oil roughly *quadrupled* — from around \$3 to about \$12. That sounds quaint by today's prices, but the proportion is the point: the cost of the single most important input to a modern economy went up four-fold, almost overnight, for reasons that had nothing to do with how the American economy was doing. Gas lines stretched around the block. And then something the textbooks of the day said should not happen, happened: prices kept rising *while* the economy shrank. Unemployment and inflation went up together. Economists had to coin a word for it — *stagflation* — and central bankers spent the next decade trying to wrestle it back down.

Almost fifty years later, in the spring of 2022, the pattern repeated with eerie fidelity. Russia invaded Ukraine, the West moved to cut off one of the world's largest energy exporters, and WTI crude oil spiked to about **\$124** a barrel. US inflation, already climbing, peaked that June at **9.06%** year over year — a number Americans had not seen since 1981. The Federal Reserve responded with the fastest series of interest-rate hikes in four decades. And a plain-vanilla portfolio of 60% stocks and 40% bonds — the default retirement allocation for tens of millions of people — had its worst year since 1937. Stocks fell 18%. Bonds, which are supposed to *protect* you when stocks fall, fell 13% at the same time.

Those two episodes, half a century apart, are the same story, and it is the story of this post: **oil is not just another commodity, it is the commodity that connects energy markets to the entire macro-asset complex.** The diagram below is the mental model we will build the whole post around — a chain that starts with an oil price and ends with a re-priced stock market, with central-bank policy and bond yields in between.

This is the deep-dive on the *energy-macro linkage* in the *Cross-Asset Playbook* series. By the end, you will be able to look at an oil move on the news and answer the only question an allocator cares about: *is this helping my portfolio or taxing it, and what should I do about it?*

## Foundations: why oil is special in the inflation number

Let us start from absolute zero, because the whole chain hinges on one fact that is easy to state and easy to underestimate: **oil shows up in the inflation number twice.** Once directly, as a thing you buy. And again indirectly, as a cost baked into the price of almost everything else you buy. No other single commodity does both at that scale. To see why that matters, we first need to be precise about what "inflation" actually measures.

### What inflation is, from scratch

*Inflation* is the rate at which the general level of prices rises over time. If a basket of goods that cost \$100 last year costs \$104 this year, inflation over that year was 4%. The most-watched measure in the United States is the **Consumer Price Index**, or CPI — a number the Bureau of Labor Statistics computes every month by pricing a fixed "basket" of goods and services that a typical household buys: groceries, rent, gasoline, airfares, haircuts, doctor visits, and so on. Each item gets a *weight* roughly proportional to how much of the average budget it eats. The headline CPI number you hear quoted ("inflation came in at 3.2% year over year") is the percentage change in the total cost of that basket compared with twelve months earlier.

A *year-over-year* figure compares this month with the same month a year ago. That framing matters for oil: a big oil spike does not just lift this month's number, it stays *in* the year-over-year comparison for a full twelve months, and then, when it rolls off, it can pull the number *down* even if prices are not actually falling. We will come back to that — it is why inflation "base effects" confuse people every cycle.

### Headline versus core, and why the distinction exists

Open any discussion of inflation and you will quickly hit two terms:

- **Headline CPI** is the whole basket, including food and energy. It is what households actually experience, because households actually buy food and gasoline.
- **Core CPI** strips out food and energy. It is what central bankers watch most closely, because food and energy prices are *volatile* — they swing around on weather, harvests, wars, and OPEC decisions that have nothing to do with the underlying trend of the economy. Stripping them out gives a cleaner read on the *persistent* inflation that monetary policy can actually influence.

Here is the subtlety that makes oil so important. Energy is *excluded* from core CPI as a direct component — but it cannot be excluded from the things core measures, because energy is an *input* to producing and delivering nearly everything. The diesel that moves a truckload of groceries, the natural gas that runs a fertilizer plant, the jet fuel in an airline ticket, the electricity in a factory, the petrochemical feedstock in a plastic bottle — energy is upstream of all of it. So a sustained oil shock first hits headline directly (through the gasoline you pump), and then, with a lag of months, seeps into core (through every price that has energy in its cost structure). That second channel is called **pass-through**, and it is why a central banker cannot simply wave away an oil spike as "just energy, we look at core."

### How much of CPI is energy, directly

Energy — gasoline, electricity, natural gas, heating oil — is roughly **7% to 8%** of the US CPI basket directly, with gasoline alone around 3% to 4% in a normal year. That sounds small. But the *volatility* of energy is enormous relative to the rest of the basket, so its contribution to the *change* in CPI is far larger than its weight. Rent barely moves month to month; gasoline can move 30% in a quarter. A category that is 4% of the basket but swings 40% contributes as much to the monthly inflation print as a category that is 40% of the basket and swings 4%. That is the arithmetic that lets a single commodity move the national inflation number.

#### Worked example: how a gasoline spike moves the CPI number

Let us make the direct channel concrete with round numbers. Suppose gasoline is **4%** of the CPI basket and the rest of the basket (96%) is rising at a steady **3%** a year. Now oil rises sharply and gasoline prices jump **40%** over the year.

The contribution to headline CPI from the non-energy 96% is `0.96 × 3% = 2.88%`. The contribution from the 4% gasoline slice is `0.04 × 40% = 1.6%`. Add them: `2.88% + 1.6% = 4.48%`. So a basket that *would* have shown 3% inflation now shows about **4.5%** — a **1.5-point** jump in headline inflation, produced almost entirely by one category that is only 4% of the basket.

That is exactly the magnitude in our TL;DR: a large oil spike lifts headline CPI by roughly 1 to 1.5 points through the *direct* channel alone, before any pass-through into the rest of the basket. The intuition: oil moves the inflation number out of all proportion to its budget share, because it is the most volatile thing in the basket.

### Oil is an input to almost everything: transport, food, and plastics

The direct gasoline channel is only half of why oil is special. The other half is that oil is a *physical input* to producing and moving nearly every good in the economy. Walk through the three biggest channels:

- **Transport.** Almost everything you buy was moved by something burning a refined oil product. Trucks burn diesel; ships burn bunker fuel and diesel; planes burn jet fuel. When oil rises, the *freight* cost of every physical good rises with it. A higher oil price is, in effect, a surcharge stapled onto the delivery of every item in the economy — from the cement on a construction site to the package on your doorstep.
- **Food.** This is the channel people underestimate. Modern agriculture is astonishingly energy-intensive. Nitrogen fertilizer is made largely from natural gas; tractors and harvesters burn diesel; irrigation pumps run on electricity; crops are dried, processed, refrigerated, and trucked using energy at every step. When energy spikes, food production costs spike with it — which is why food and energy inflation so often move together, and why central banks lump them into the same "volatile" bucket they strip out of core.
- **Plastics and chemicals.** Crude oil is the *feedstock*, the raw material, for petrochemicals — the building blocks of plastics, synthetic fabrics, paints, solvents, packaging, and countless industrial inputs. A water bottle, a phone case, a car bumper, a polyester shirt: each one started, chemically, as a fraction of a barrel of oil. When crude rises, the cost of making all of them rises.

Put these together and you see why an economist would never treat oil as "just 4% of the basket." Oil is upstream of the *other 96%*. The direct gasoline component is the part you can see; the transport, food, and plastics channels are the part that seeps in afterward and shows up, months later, in the prices of goods that have nothing to do with energy on the label.

### The reader's everyday version of pass-through

If the arithmetic feels abstract, here is the kitchen-table version. When the price at the pump jumps, you feel it immediately — that is the direct hit. But a few weeks later your grocery bill creeps up too, because the trucks that delivered the food burned more expensive diesel, and the farm that grew it paid more for fuel and fertilizer. Then your online orders cost a little more, because shipping went up. None of those price tags say "energy" on them, but energy is hiding inside each one. *That* is pass-through: the oil price laundering itself through the cost structure of the whole economy until it shows up everywhere, including in the "core" inflation that excludes energy directly.

This is also why the *timing* of an oil shock's effect on inflation is staggered, not instant. The pump price moves the same day oil moves. But the freight surcharge takes weeks to flow into shelf prices, the fertilizer cost takes a growing season to reach the food aisle, and the petrochemical cost takes months to work through the manufacturing chain. So a single oil spike does not produce a single inflation bump — it produces a *first* bump (the direct pump hit) followed by a slow *second* wave (pass-through into everything else) that can keep core inflation elevated long after oil itself has stabilized. That lag is exactly what makes supply-shock inflation so sticky, and so hard for a central bank to wait out.

## The transmission chain: from a barrel to a stock multiple

Now we can walk the chain in the cover figure link by link. Each link is a cause-and-effect step that economists and central bankers actually trace, and each one is where an allocator can lose or make money.

### Link 1: oil up → headline CPI up

We just did this one. A supply-driven oil spike feeds straight into the gasoline component of CPI and lifts the headline number within the same month, with the magnitude we computed: roughly a point to a point-and-a-half for a large move. Over the following months, pass-through pushes it into core too.

The data makes the link visible. The chart below puts WTI crude oil and US headline CPI on the same timeline through 2021 and 2022. They climb together, and they peak within a quarter of each other.

![Dual axis line chart of WTI crude oil and CPI year over year both surging through 2021 and 2022](/imgs/blogs/oil-inflation-and-equities-the-energy-linkage-2.png)

From mid-2021, WTI marched from the \$60s toward triple digits, spiking to about **\$124** in March 2022 on the war. CPI tracked it up the staircase — **4.99%** in May 2021, **6.81%** by December, **8.54%** in March 2022, and a 40-year high of **9.06%** that June. Oil was not the *only* cause of that inflation (pandemic supply chains and a flood of stimulus mattered enormously), but it was the most visible accelerant, and the timing of the energy surge and the CPI peak is no coincidence.

### Link 2: headline CPI up → inflation expectations up

Here is a link that sounds soft but is, to a central banker, the most dangerous one. *Inflation expectations* are what households, businesses, and bond investors believe inflation will be in the future. They matter because they are partly self-fulfilling: if workers expect 6% inflation, they ask for 6% raises; if firms expect their costs to rise 6%, they pre-emptively raise prices 6%. Expectations can turn a one-off price shock into an ongoing *wage-price spiral*, which is exactly what happened in the 1970s and exactly what central banks are now terrified of.

And of all the prices in the basket, the gas pump is the one households see most often and remember most vividly. You buy gasoline weekly and watch a giant illuminated sign tell you the price. So a gasoline spike does outsized damage to *expectations* — it is the price that most shapes the public's gut sense of "is inflation getting worse?" Central bankers know this. A spike in oil that lifts expectations is far more threatening to them than the same-sized spike in, say, used-car prices, which nobody watches on a billboard.

### Link 3: inflation expectations up → the central bank hikes

A central bank like the Federal Reserve has a mandate to keep inflation low and stable — in the US, an explicit target of **2%** over the long run. When a supply-shock oil spike threatens to unanchor expectations and drag inflation persistently above target, the bank's main tool is to *raise its policy interest rate* — the rate at which banks lend to each other overnight, which sets the floor for the cost of money across the whole economy. We cover the machinery of this in [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates); here we only need the consequence.

Raising rates is the central bank deliberately slowing the economy: making borrowing more expensive, cooling demand, and — they hope — taking the air out of inflation before expectations run away. The cruel part is that the *cause* of the inflation (an oil shock) is something rate hikes cannot fix. The Fed cannot drill a well or end a war. It can only crush *demand* hard enough that the inflation comes down anyway. That is why a supply shock puts a central bank in an impossible spot: the inflation is supply-driven, but the only lever they have works on demand.

The Fed's 2022 response is one of the most aggressive on record. The chart below shows the target rate marching up.

![Step chart of the Fed funds target range upper bound rising from 0.5 percent to 5.5 percent through 2022 and 2023](/imgs/blogs/oil-inflation-and-equities-the-energy-linkage-4.png)

In March 2022, with oil spiking and CPI at 8.5%, the Fed funds target upper bound was just **0.50%**. By June it was 1.75%, by September 3.25%, by December 4.50%, and it peaked at **5.50%** in July 2023. That is **5 full points** of tightening, **4.5 of them inside nine months** — the steepest hiking campaign since the early 1980s. The trigger was an inflation print that oil had helped push to a four-decade high.

### Link 4: the central bank hikes → bond yields up

When the central bank raises the overnight rate, the *yield* on longer-term government bonds rises too — partly because short rates anchor the front of the curve, and partly because bond investors, seeing higher inflation and a hawkish Fed, demand more yield to hold a fixed-income asset whose payments inflation is eroding. A *yield* is the annual return a bond pays relative to its price; a *bond* is a loan you make to a government or company in exchange for fixed interest payments. We unpack the mechanics fully in [government bonds, the risk-free anchor](/blog/trading/cross-asset/government-bonds-the-risk-free-anchor-and-duration); the key fact here is the direction.

In 2022, the yield on the 10-year US Treasury — the single most important interest rate in the world, because it anchors the pricing of nearly every other asset — rose from about **1.6%** in January to **3.01%** in June and **4.05%** by October. It more than doubled in ten months. And bond prices move *opposite* to yields: when the yield on a bond rises, the price of the bond you already own *falls*. That is why bondholders, who thought they owned the safe asset, lost 13% in 2022. The oil-fed inflation that forced the Fed to hike is what drove their yields up and their prices down.

#### Worked example: why rising yields knock a bond's price down

This is the step most beginners miss, so let us nail it. Suppose you own a 10-year bond that pays a fixed **\$30** a year (a 3% coupon on a \$1,000 face value). The market interest rate for new bonds is also 3%, so your bond is worth its \$1,000 face value — nobody will pay more or less, because they could buy a new bond yielding the same 3%.

Now an oil-fed inflation surge forces the Fed to hike, and the market rate for new 10-year bonds jumps to **5%**. A fresh \$1,000 bond now pays \$50 a year. Yours still pays only \$30. Why would anyone pay \$1,000 for your \$30-a-year bond when \$1,000 buys a \$50-a-year bond instead? They would not. The price of your bond has to *fall* until its \$30 payment represents a competitive yield. Roughly, the price drops until `\$30 / price ≈ 5%`, i.e. price ≈ \$600 — and for a 10-year bond the fuller calculation (discounting all the future payments at 5% instead of 3%) lands the price near **\$850**, a loss of about **15%**.

So a 2-point rise in yields can cost a long-term bondholder ~15% of their capital — no default, no missed payment, just the math of fixed payments re-priced against higher rates. The intuition: a bond is a promise of fixed dollars, and when the going rate of money rises, those fixed dollars are worth less today.

### Link 5: bond yields up → equity multiples compress

This is the final link, and it is where the chain reaches the stock market. A stock is a claim on a company's future profits, stretching out for decades. To decide what that stream of future profits is worth *today*, investors *discount* it — they reduce the value of far-off dollars, because a dollar in ten years is worth less than a dollar now. The rate they discount at is built on the risk-free bond yield. So when the 10-year yield rises, the discount rate rises, and the present value of those distant future profits *falls*. The stock is worth less even if the company's profits have not changed at all.

The market shorthand for this is the *valuation multiple*, usually the **price-to-earnings (P/E) ratio** — the price of a stock divided by its annual earnings per share. A P/E of 20 means investors pay \$20 for each \$1 of current annual earnings, betting on future growth. When yields rise, that multiple *compresses* — investors will pay 16 or 17 times earnings instead of 20, because the future earnings they are paying up for are now discounted more harshly. *Multiple compression* is the mechanical channel through which higher bond yields drag down stock prices.

#### Worked example: how a higher discount rate compresses a stock's multiple

Take a simplified growth stock that investors value as a stream of future earnings. A crude but instructive rule: the fair P/E multiple is roughly inversely related to the discount rate. Suppose at a **3%** bond yield plus a 4% equity risk premium, investors discount future earnings at **7%**, and that supports a P/E of about **20** for a company growing earnings steadily.

Now the oil-fed inflation drives the 10-year yield to **5%**. With the same 4% risk premium, the discount rate rises to **9%**. The fair multiple compresses. A rough proportional approximation: `20 × (7% / 9%) ≈ 15.6`. So the P/E falls from 20 to about **16** — a **~22% drop** in the stock's price — *with no change at all in the company's earnings*. The entire move came from the discount rate, which came from the bond yield, which came from the Fed, which came from the oil-fed inflation print.

The intuition: a long-duration growth stock is a very long bond in disguise, and like a long bond it loses the most value when rates rise. This is why technology and growth stocks — whose value is mostly far-off future profits — got hit hardest in 2022. The longer-dated the profits, the more a higher discount rate hurts.

### And the margin hit, layered on top

There is a second, smaller channel worth naming. Higher oil is not only a discount-rate problem for stocks; for many companies it is a *cost* problem. Airlines burn jet fuel; shippers burn diesel; chemical makers buy oil-derived feedstock; manufacturers pay higher energy bills. When oil spikes, their input costs rise, and unless they can pass it all on to customers, their *profit margins* shrink. So a supply-shock oil spike can hit equities twice: through the multiple (the discount-rate channel) and through earnings (the margin channel). Only one sector escapes both — the energy sector itself, whose earnings *rise* when oil rises. Hold that thought; it is the seed of the entire playbook.

#### Worked example: how an oil spike eats an airline's profit

Make the margin channel concrete, because it explains why some stocks fall *far* more than the market average. Fuel is typically one of the single largest costs for an airline — often around **25% to 30%** of its operating expenses. Suppose an airline has \$10 billion of annual revenue, \$9.4 billion of costs, and so a thin **\$600 million** of operating profit (a 6% margin — airlines are famously low-margin). Of those \$9.4 billion of costs, say **\$2.5 billion** is jet fuel.

Now a supply-shock oil spike raises jet fuel prices by **40%**. The fuel bill rises by `0.40 × \$2.5 billion = \$1.0 billion`. If the airline cannot raise fares fast enough to pass it on — and in the short run it usually cannot — that \$1.0 billion comes straight out of profit. The \$600 million operating profit becomes a **\$400 million loss**. The stock does not fall 20% like the broad market; it can fall by half, because the oil spike did not just compress its multiple, it *erased its earnings*.

The intuition: for a fuel-heavy, low-margin business, an oil spike is not a headwind, it is an existential cost shock — which is why airlines, trucking, and shipping stocks are among the worst places to be in a supply-shock regime, and why they sit firmly on the "losers" side of the next section's ledger.

### How big does an oil move have to be to matter?

Not every wiggle in the oil price runs the whole chain. Oil bounces around constantly; most of those moves never reach the Fed or the stock market. So when does an oil move become a *macro* event? Two rough thresholds help.

First, **size.** A move of a few dollars is noise. A move that *doubles* the price, or adds \$40–\$50 a barrel in a few months, is the kind of shock that adds a full point or more to headline inflation and forces a policy response. The 1973 quadrupling, the 2008 run to \$147, and the 2022 spike to \$124 were all large enough, in percentage terms, to be felt economy-wide. A drift from \$70 to \$80 is not.

Second, **speed and persistence.** A spike that reverses in a month barely registers — it rolls off the year-over-year inflation comparison before it can unanchor expectations. A spike that *holds* for many months is the dangerous one, because it has time to pass through into core inflation (the slow second wave we described) and into wage demands. Central bankers often say they can "look through" a transitory oil spike; what they cannot look through is a *sustained* one. The skill, again, is judging not just the size of the move but whether it is the kind that will *last* — which usually comes back to whether the supply disruption behind it is temporary (a brief refinery outage) or structural (a war, a multi-year OPEC stance, a sanctions regime).

This is also why a country's **energy intensity** — how much oil it burns per dollar of output — changes how powerful the chain is over time. A modern, services-heavy, more electrified economy burns far less oil per unit of GDP than the manufacturing-heavy economy of 1973. So the *same* percentage oil spike transmits less forcefully today than it did fifty years ago: the 1973 quadrupling caused a deeper economic wound than an equivalent move would now. The chain still runs — 2022 proved that — but each link is a little weaker than it once was, which is worth remembering before assuming every oil headline is a 1970s rerun.

## Demand-pull versus supply-shock: same price, opposite signal

We now arrive at the single most important idea in the post — the one that separates an allocator who panics at every oil headline from one who reads it correctly. **Not all oil rises are the same.** The exact same move — say, WTI from \$70 to \$100 — can be wonderful news for stocks or terrible news for stocks, depending entirely on *why* oil is rising. The 2×2 below is the map.

![Two by two matrix of demand driven versus supply driven oil moves showing opposite implications for stocks](/imgs/blogs/oil-inflation-and-equities-the-energy-linkage-3.png)

### Demand-pull: oil rises because the world is booming

A **demand-pull** oil rise happens when the *global economy is strong*. Factories are humming, people are flying and driving and buying, China is building, and the world is simply burning more oil than it was. Demand outpaces supply, and the price rises. Crucially, the *cause* of the higher oil price — a booming economy — is also wonderful for corporate profits. So in a demand-pull regime, **oil and stocks rise together**. The correlation is *positive*. Energy is going up, but so are industrials, banks, and consumer stocks, because the same strong-growth tide is lifting all of them.

The poster child is the 2003–2007 global expansion: oil climbed from the \$30s toward \$100 as China industrialized and the world grew, and the S&P 500 rose right alongside it. Higher oil was a *symptom* of strength, not a cause of weakness. An allocator in that regime should not fear the rising oil price; it is confirming that the cycle is healthy.

### Supply-shock: oil rises because supply got cut

A **supply-shock** oil rise is the opposite animal. Here the global economy is *not* booming — instead, something has constrained the *supply* of oil. A war takes a major exporter's barrels off the market. OPEC announces a production cut. A pipeline blows up; a region is sanctioned. Supply falls, the price spikes, but demand did not change for any *good* reason. Now the higher oil price is a pure **tax on growth**: households and businesses must spend more on energy and have less left for everything else, which *slows* the economy. And because it is simultaneously an *inflation* impulse, it forces the central bank to *tighten into the slowdown*. Worst of both worlds.

In a supply-shock regime, **oil rises while stocks fall.** The correlation flips *negative*. Energy stocks go up (their product just got more valuable), but the broad market goes down (growth is being taxed and the Fed is hiking). The poster children are 1973–74, the 1990 Gulf War spike, and 2022. Same direction of oil move as the demand-pull case, opposite consequence for your portfolio.

#### Worked example: a supply-shock oil spike as a household tax

Let us quantify the "tax on growth" so the phrase is not just rhetoric. Suppose oil rises from **\$70** to **\$120** a barrel — a **+71%** move, the kind of supply shock 2022 delivered. Gasoline at the pump rises in rough proportion; say it climbs about **\$1.50** a gallon.

Take a single US household that drives **12,000 miles a year** in a car that gets **25 miles per gallon**. That household burns `12,000 / 25 = 480` gallons of gasoline a year. At \$1.50 more per gallon, its fuel bill rises by `480 × \$1.50 = \$720` a year — and it gets *nothing extra* for that \$720. Same commute, same car, same miles. It is pure dead-weight, a direct tax on the household budget.

Now scale it. There are roughly **130 million** US households. If the spike hits even a large fraction of them at that order of magnitude, you are removing *tens of billions of dollars* of discretionary spending from the economy — money that would otherwise have bought dinners out, clothes, gadgets, vacations. That spending is exactly what drives corporate revenues. So the supply-shock oil spike *directly* shrinks the demand that stocks depend on, *while* — as we computed earlier — pushing headline CPI up 1 to 1.5 points and forcing the Fed to hike. The intuition: a supply-shock oil rise is a tax bill the whole economy pays at once, and it lands on stocks (demand and multiple) and bonds (yields) simultaneously.

### Telling them apart in real time

So how do you know which regime you are in *while it is happening*, not in hindsight? A few practical tells:

- **What else is moving?** In a demand-pull rise, *other cyclical assets confirm the boom* — copper is up, stocks are up, credit spreads are tight, the economic data is strong. In a supply shock, oil is up but stocks are *down*, copper is mixed, and the move traces to a specific *event* (a war, an OPEC headline, a sanction).
- **Is there a named supply event?** Demand-pull rises are gradual and broad. Supply shocks have a date and a headline: "OPEC+ cuts 2 million barrels," "Russia invades Ukraine," "Strait of Hormuz tensions." If you can point to the barrel that was removed, it is a supply shock.
- **What is the stock-bond correlation doing?** This is the deepest tell, and it connects to the [60/40 engine](/blog/trading/cross-asset/stock-bond-correlation-the-60-40-engine). In a supply-shock/inflation regime, stocks and bonds fall *together* — the correlation goes positive, and bonds stop hedging stocks. That regime shift is itself the signature of a supply-driven, inflationary world.

## Who wins and who loses

If you accept that a supply-shock oil spike is a growth tax plus an inflation impulse, the winners and losers fall out almost mechanically. Knowing the roster is what turns the analysis into a portfolio.

### The winners

- **Energy equities and oil producers.** This is the cleanest winner. When oil rises, an oil producer's revenue per barrel rises while many of its costs are fixed, so its profits soar. The energy *sector* of the stock market — producers, refiners, oilfield services, pipelines — is the one corner of equities whose earnings move *with* oil, not against it. That is exactly why it goes up in the regime that drags the rest of the market down.
- **Commodities broadly.** Owning the commodity itself — through [a commodity index](/blog/trading/cross-asset/energy-oil-gas-the-inflation-engine), futures, or producer equities — is the most direct way to be long the oil price. In 2022 the Bloomberg Commodity index returned **+16.1%**, the only major asset class in the green.
- **Some value and "real-asset" sectors.** Companies with hard assets and pricing power — certain materials, infrastructure, parts of financials that benefit from higher rates — tend to weather an inflation/rates shock better than long-duration growth.

### The losers

- **Consumer and transport stocks.** Airlines (jet fuel), trucking and shipping (diesel), and consumer-discretionary companies (households have less to spend after the energy tax) all suffer. Their costs go up and their customers' budgets go down at the same time.
- **Long-duration growth stocks.** As the discount-rate worked example showed, the stocks whose value is mostly far-off future profits — high-multiple technology and growth names — fall the most when yields rise. They are the longest bonds in the equity market, and a supply-shock rate spike hits them hardest.
- **Bondholders.** The whole point of the chain: oil-fed inflation forces the Fed to hike, yields rise, and the price of existing bonds falls. The 60/40 investor who held bonds *for safety* gets hit on that sleeve too.

The 2022 scoreboard makes the entire roster vivid in one picture.

![Horizontal bar chart of 2022 total returns showing commodities up 16 percent and stocks bonds and 60 40 all down](/imgs/blogs/oil-inflation-and-equities-the-energy-linkage-5.png)

Read it from the bottom up: US REITs **−24.9%** (long-duration *and* rate-sensitive), US stocks **−18.1%**, the 60/40 portfolio **−16.0%**, US bonds **−13.0%**, high yield **−11.2%**, gold roughly flat at **−0.3%** — and then, alone in the green, commodities at **+16.1%**. Every traditional building block of a diversified portfolio lost money in the same year, and the only large winner was the very thing — commodity/energy exposure — that most diversified portfolios do not own. That is not a coincidence; it is the supply-shock regime doing exactly what the chain says it does.

## Common misconceptions

A handful of beliefs about oil and markets are widespread, intuitive, and wrong. Correcting them is where the analysis earns its keep.

**"Higher oil is always bad for stocks."** No — it depends entirely on *why* oil is rising. A demand-pull rise (strong growth) lifts stocks *with* oil; the correlation is positive. Only a supply-shock rise (a growth tax) sends stocks down. In the 2003–07 boom, oil tripled and the S&P rose the whole way. The sign of the oil-stock relationship is not fixed; it flips with the regime, and reading that flip is the whole skill.

**"Energy is only 4% of CPI, so an oil spike can't really move inflation."** The weight is small but the volatility is enormous, and inflation is about *changes*. As the worked example showed, a category that is 4% of the basket but swings 40% adds 1.5 points to headline inflation on its own — as much as a 40%-weight category swinging 4%. And that ignores pass-through, which seeps the oil cost into the *other* 96% over the following months. Weight is not impact when the item is the most volatile thing in the basket.

**"Bonds will protect me when an oil shock hits stocks."** Usually the most dangerous belief of all. Bonds hedge stocks in a *growth* shock (a recession, where the Fed *cuts* and bond prices *rise*). But a supply-shock oil spike is an *inflation* shock, where the Fed *hikes* and bond prices *fall* — at the same time stocks fall. That is precisely why 2022 was so brutal for the 60/40: the two halves dropped together. The hedge fails in exactly the regime an oil shock creates. See [the 60/40 engine](/blog/trading/cross-asset/stock-bond-correlation-the-60-40-engine) for why the stock-bond correlation flips with the inflation regime.

**"The Fed can fix an oil-driven inflation."** It can only fix it the hard way. The Fed cannot increase the oil supply — it cannot end a war or reverse an OPEC cut. Its only tool, raising rates, works by crushing *demand*. So when inflation is supply-driven, the Fed's cure is to slow the whole economy until even the supply-shocked prices come down. That is why supply-shock inflation so often comes with a recession risk attached: the only available medicine is itself a growth tax.

**"Gold is the obvious inflation hedge, so it will save me in an oil shock."** Gold is a real-asset store of value, but in the *early* part of a supply-shock regime — when the Fed is hiking and *real* yields are rising — gold often struggles, because higher real yields raise the opportunity cost of holding a metal that pays no income. In 2022, with the fastest hikes in decades, gold returned **−0.3%** — flat, not a savior. The cleaner hedge for a supply-shock *oil* spike is the oil exposure itself. We unpack gold's nuanced behavior in [gold: money, insurance, or just a rock](/blog/trading/cross-asset/gold-money-insurance-or-just-a-rock).

## How it shows up in real markets

Three historical episodes show the chain firing — and one shows it firing in *reverse*, which is just as instructive.

### 1973–74: the embargo and the birth of stagflation

In October 1973, the Arab members of OPEC declared an oil embargo against nations seen as supporting Israel in the Yom Kippur War. Oil roughly quadrupled, from about **\$3** to about **\$12** a barrel, over the following months. This was a textbook supply shock — barrels removed from the market for geopolitical reasons, with the economy not booming. The result was *stagflation*: US inflation surged into double digits while the economy fell into recession and unemployment rose. The S&P 500 fell roughly 15% in 1973 and another 26% in 1974 — a brutal two-year bear market. Bonds gave little shelter, because the inflation was the problem. Real assets and gold, by contrast, soared. It was the regime that taught a generation of investors what a supply-shock oil spike does to a stock-and-bond portfolio: it hurts both, and only real assets hedge it. Every modern allocator's case for owning commodities traces back to this episode.

### 2008: the spike, the crash, and the demand destruction

2008 is the subtle one, because oil did *both* things in a single year. In the first half, WTI ripped to an all-time high of about **\$147** in July 2008 — a late-cycle, partly demand-pull, partly speculative blow-off. Then the global financial crisis hit, demand collapsed, and oil *crashed* to about **\$44** by December — a fall of more than two-thirds in five months. This is the *other* face of the oil-stock relationship: a demand-*destruction* move. As the economy imploded, oil fell *with* stocks (the S&P lost 37% that year), because the same demand collapse hit both. The lesson: when oil falls *because growth is collapsing*, it is not good news for stocks — falling oil and falling stocks are the same recession showing up in two markets. (Contrast that with oil falling because *supply* surged, which *is* good for stocks — see 2014–15 below.)

### 2022: the war, the 9% print, and the worst 60/40 year since 1937

The cleanest modern example of the full chain. Russia invaded Ukraine in February 2022, the West moved to cut off Russian energy, and WTI spiked to about **\$124** in March. Headline CPI, already elevated from pandemic supply chains and stimulus, peaked at **9.06%** in June — the highest since 1981. The Fed responded with the fastest hiking campaign in four decades, lifting the funds rate from 0.50% to 4.50% by year-end and 5.50% by mid-2023. The 10-year Treasury yield more than doubled, from ~1.6% to over 4%. And the cross-asset damage was historic: US stocks **−18.1%**, US bonds **−13.0%**, the 60/40 **−16.0%** — its worst calendar year since 1937 — while commodities returned **+16.1%** and the energy sector led the S&P. Every link in the cover figure lit up in sequence, and the only assets that *won* were the ones at the front of the chain.

### 2014–15: oil falls because supply *surged* — the benign mirror image

For contrast, consider the opposite shock. From mid-2014, the US shale boom and an OPEC decision *not* to cut production flooded the market with supply, and WTI fell from about **\$107** to the **\$40s** and eventually the **\$20s** by early 2016. This was a *supply-driven fall* — more barrels, not less demand. For consumers it was a tax *cut*: cheaper gasoline left more in household budgets. Headline inflation fell, which gave the Fed room to stay easy. US stocks were roughly flat to modestly positive through 2014–15 (the S&P gained 13.7% in 2014, then 1.4% in 2015), absorbing the energy-sector pain but benefiting from the consumer windfall elsewhere. The mirror image of 2022: a supply-driven oil *fall* is a stimulus, just as a supply-driven oil *rise* is a tax. The driver, not the direction, sets the sign.

### The fifty-year picture in one chart

Stack those episodes on one timeline and the pattern is unmistakable — oil is the asset that lurches in both directions, and each lurch reorganizes the rest of the market.

![Line chart of WTI crude oil from 1973 to 2024 annotating the 1973 embargo 2008 peak 2020 negative print and 2022 war spike](/imgs/blogs/oil-inflation-and-equities-the-energy-linkage-6.png)

From the ~\$3-to-\$12 embargo quadrupling of 1973–74, to the **\$147** peak and subsequent crash to **\$44** in 2008, to the surreal **−\$37.63** negative print in April 2020 when a pandemic glut left no place to store barrels, to the **\$124** war spike of 2022 and the drift back to about **\$72** by end-2024 — no other asset whipsaws like this. Each major move is a story about *why*, and the why is what tells you whether stocks rose or fell alongside it.

## When to own it: the energy-macro playbook

Everything above lands here. The payoff of distinguishing demand-pull from supply-shock oil is not academic — it is a set of concrete portfolio decisions. The matrix below is the playbook; the prose underneath walks each row.

![Matrix mapping demand pull supply shock and falling oil regimes to what to own and what to trim](/imgs/blogs/oil-inflation-and-equities-the-energy-linkage-7.png)

*(This is educational, not individual financial advice — it explains how the linkages work and how allocators reason about them, not what any specific person should buy.)*

### Step 1: read the driver before you react

The first and most important move is to *not* react to the oil headline reflexively. Ask the diagnostic question: **is oil rising because demand is strong, or because supply got cut?** Look at what else is moving (are stocks and copper confirming a boom, or is the move tied to a named supply event?) and at the stock-bond correlation (negative and stable = growth-driven world; positive and rising = inflation/supply-driven world). Get the driver right and the rest of the playbook follows; get it wrong and you will sell your energy hedge in exactly the regime it was built for.

### Step 2: in a demand-pull rise, stay broad and cyclical

If oil is climbing because the global economy is booming, this is the *benign* regime. Oil and stocks are rising together, the correlation is positive, and a 60/40 is doing fine. There is nothing urgent to hedge. You can lean into cyclicals and broad equities and ride the expansion; the rising oil price is confirming the cycle's strength, not threatening it. The energy sleeve is a participant in the rally, not a hedge against it.

### Step 3: in a supply shock, own energy/value/short-duration and trim long-duration

This is the regime the whole post was written for. If oil is spiking because supply got cut — a war, an OPEC cut, a sanction — you are facing a growth tax plus an inflation impulse plus a hiking central bank. The playbook:

- **Own more energy and commodity exposure.** Energy equities and commodities are the assets whose earnings *rise* with oil; they are the supply-shock hedge. In 2022 they were the only large green on the board.
- **Tilt toward value and short-duration.** Value stocks (lower multiples, nearer-term earnings) and short-duration bonds are far less hurt by rising discount rates than long-duration growth. Short-duration fixed income also lets you *re-invest* at the new higher yields quickly.
- **Trim long-duration growth and long bonds.** The longest-dated assets — high-multiple tech, long Treasuries, rate-sensitive REITs — take the worst of the discount-rate and yield channels. This is the regime where they fall the most and the 60/40 gets hit on *both* sleeves.

### Step 4: in a supply-driven oil *fall*, do the reverse

If oil is falling because supply surged (more barrels, as in 2014–15), it is a stimulus and a disinflationary tailwind. The Fed gets room to ease *later*, multiples can re-expand, and the assets that suffered in the supply-shock regime — long-duration growth, long bonds — become the place to be. The energy sleeve, conversely, lags in this regime; it is the cost of owning the hedge. Be careful, though, to distinguish a *supply-driven* oil fall (good, a tax cut) from a *demand-destruction* oil fall (bad, a recession showing up in the oil market, as in late 2008). Same direction, opposite meaning — the symmetry of the demand-versus-supply lesson runs in both directions.

### How much to hold, and what it costs you

Sizing the energy sleeve is a tradeoff between insurance value and drag. A small allocation — commonly discussed in the **5% to 10%** range for a diversified portfolio, via a broad commodity index, energy-equity exposure, or a mix — is large enough to matter in a supply-shock year and small enough not to dominate the portfolio in the long stretches when it lags. The exact number is a personal choice that depends on the rest of the portfolio and the risk an investor is trying to hedge; the point is that this is a *hedge-sized* position, not a core holding.

And it does cost you. As the [energy post](/blog/trading/cross-asset/energy-oil-gas-the-inflation-engine) explains, you cannot buy and hold a barrel — you hold oil exposure through futures, and rolling those futures can *bleed* roughly 10% a year when the curve is in *contango* (an upward-sloping curve where each roll sells low and buys high). Energy *equities* avoid the roll problem but bring their own equity risk. Either way, in the long demand-driven booms that make up most of market history, the energy sleeve typically underperforms a plain stock-bond portfolio. That underperformance is the *premium* you pay for the insurance — the same way you pay for fire insurance every year and are "happy to lose" the premium because the house did not burn. The energy sleeve is fire insurance against the supply-shock fire, and you should expect to pay for it in calm years.

#### Worked example: what the energy hedge does to a 60/40 in a supply-shock year

Put a number on the insurance. Take the brutal 2022 figures. A plain **60/40** portfolio returned **−16.0%**. Now suppose an investor had carved out a **10%** sleeve for commodities, funded by trimming the stock-and-bond core to 54/36, and that sleeve tracked the Bloomberg Commodity index at **+16.1%**.

The blended return is `0.90 × (−16.0%) + 0.10 × (+16.1%)`. That is `−14.4% + 1.6% = −12.8%`. The 10% energy sleeve turned a **−16.0%** year into a **−12.8%** year — it cushioned the loss by **3.2 percentage points** in the single worst regime for a 60/40 in nearly a century. On a \$1,000,000 portfolio, that is \$32,000 of drawdown avoided in one year.

The intuition: the energy sleeve does not make a supply-shock year *good* — nothing does for a long-only portfolio — but it makes the worst year meaningfully less bad, precisely because its single best year coincided with the core's single worst. That negative co-movement, in the regime that matters, is the entire point of holding it.

### Why the energy sleeve earns its keep

Step back and notice the deep point. A diversified portfolio is built to weather most shocks — but its classic engine, the stock-bond pairing, fails in precisely *one* regime: the supply-shock/inflation regime, where stocks and bonds fall together. That is the regime a supply-driven oil spike *creates*. And it is the one regime where a dedicated energy/commodity sleeve pays off, because energy's earnings rise when everything else's fall. So the energy allocation is not there to beat the market in good times — it usually lags in the long demand-driven booms that dominate market history. It is there as insurance against the *one* environment that breaks the rest of the portfolio. You hold it for the regime it hedges, not for its average return. That is the allocator's case for owning energy, and it is the whole reason the energy-macro linkage is worth understanding.

### What would invalidate the case

Be honest about when this reasoning breaks. The energy-as-supply-shock-hedge case weakens if: the world's energy intensity keeps falling (electrification and efficiency mean each dollar of GDP burns less oil, so oil shocks transmit less powerfully than in 1973); if a credible build-out of spare capacity makes large supply shocks rarer; or if the *inflation* regime gives way to a durable *growth-shock* regime, in which bonds resume their hedging role and the supply-shock playbook is the wrong fight. The sign that you are wrong is simple and the same one we keep returning to: watch the **stock-bond correlation**. As long as it is positive and inflation is the market's dominant fear, the supply-shock playbook is live. When it turns durably negative again, the world has changed, and so should the allocation.
