> **TL;DR** — The "overheat" or late-cycle phase is when growth is still strong but inflation is accelerating and the Fed is hiking. This is the one phase where **real assets** — commodities, energy, materials — and **value** stocks and **short duration** win, while long Treasuries and expensive growth stocks get crushed by rising real yields. It is also the single most dangerous phase for the classic 60/40 portfolio.
>
> - The overheat quadrant is **growth up AND inflation up**. Strong demand strains capacity, prices accelerate, the Fed tightens, and the yield curve flattens then inverts. In this regime, what protects you is *inflation exposure* and *short duration*, not long bonds.
> - In the textbook overheat of 2021 into 2022, **commodities returned +27.1% then +16.1%**, the S&P energy sector returned roughly **+54% then +65%**, and **gold held flat** — while the S&P 500 fell **−18.1%**, bonds fell **−13.0%**, and the 60/40 fell **−16.0%**, its worst year in decades.
> - The engine of the damage was the **10-year real yield**, which surged from **−1.04% to +1.74%** in 2022. Rising real yields are the long-duration killer: they re-price long bonds and expensive growth stocks downward at the same time.
> - The one number to remember: in overheat, the **stock-bond correlation turns positive**, so bonds stop hedging stocks. Your diversifier becomes commodities, energy, gold and cash — not the 40 in your 60/40.

In January 2022, a careful investor looked at the strongest economy in a generation and felt good about their portfolio. Unemployment was near a fifty-year low. Consumers were flush with savings. Corporate profits were at records. Stocks had just finished 2021 up almost 29%. Everything that a healthy economy is supposed to look like, it looked like. And then, over the next ten months, that same investor watched a 60/40 portfolio — 60% stocks, 40% bonds, the most "sensible" allocation in finance — lose about 16% of its value. Not because the economy collapsed. The economy was *too strong*. It overheated.

This is the phase of the business cycle that catches the most people off guard, because it does not feel like danger while you are in it. A recession announces itself with layoffs and fear. An overheat announces itself with good news: strong jobs, rising wages, busy restaurants, full order books. The trap is that all that strength shows up as something the market hates — *accelerating inflation* — which forces the central bank to slam on the brakes by raising interest rates. And rising rates are poison for exactly the assets a "balanced" portfolio is stuffed with: long-term bonds and high-priced growth stocks. The very thing that makes the economy feel safe is the thing that re-prices your portfolio down.

The diagram below is the mental model for this whole post: the four-quadrant map of the business cycle, with growth on one axis and inflation on the other. We are going to live in the top-right box — **growth up and inflation up**, the "overheat" — and learn to recognize it early, understand exactly *why* real assets lead there, and build the rotation that turns the most dangerous phase for a passive portfolio into one of the most profitable phases for an alert one.

By the end, you will be able to look at the inflation prints, the Fed's tone, and the shape of the yield curve and say, with real confidence, "we are late cycle — I want more commodities and energy, more value, shorter duration, some gold, and some cash, and I do not want to lean on long bonds to save me." That single read would have changed everything about how 2022 felt.

## Foundations: what "late cycle" and "overheat" actually mean

Before we can trade the overheat, we have to define it from zero. The phrase "late cycle" gets thrown around as if everyone agrees what it means; they do not. So let us build the idea carefully, one piece at a time, assuming you have never seen a business cycle chart in your life.

### What a "business cycle" is, in plain language

An economy does not grow in a smooth straight line. It grows in **cycles**: a period of expansion (everything speeding up — more jobs, more spending, more production), followed by a slowdown, sometimes a contraction (a *recession*, where the economy actually shrinks), then a recovery, and around again. The *business cycle* is the name for this repeating wave. One full lap — trough to trough — has historically lasted anywhere from a few years to a decade.

The reason this matters for an investor is simple and powerful: **different assets win in different parts of the cycle.** The thing that makes you money in an early recovery is not the thing that makes you money late in an expansion, and the thing that protects you in a recession is something else again. If you own the same portfolio in every phase, you are guaranteed to be holding the wrong thing some of the time. Learning the cycle is learning *when to own what* — which is the entire point of this series.

### The two dials that define every phase: growth and inflation

There are countless economic statistics, but for the purpose of choosing assets, almost everything collapses onto two dials.

The first dial is **growth** — is economic activity speeding up or slowing down? We usually read this off measures like GDP (gross domestic product, the total value of everything the economy produces), the unemployment rate, manufacturing surveys, and corporate earnings. Growth *up* means the economy is accelerating; growth *down* means it is decelerating.

The second dial is **inflation** — are prices, on average, rising faster or slower than before? We read this off the Consumer Price Index, or *CPI*, which tracks the cost of a basket of typical goods and services. When we say "inflation is 5%," we mean that basket costs 5% more than it did a year ago. Inflation *up* means prices are accelerating; inflation *down* means they are decelerating (note: decelerating inflation is *disinflation*; actually *falling* prices is *deflation* — different thing).

Put these two dials together and you get four boxes, which is the matrix in the figure above. This framing is sometimes called the **Investment Clock** (popularized by Merrill Lynch), and it is the single most useful map in cross-asset investing:

| Quadrant | Growth | Inflation | Where the economy is | What tends to lead |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Recovery | Up | Down | Early cycle, coming out of recession | Stocks |
| Overheat | **Up** | **Up** | **Late cycle, this post** | **Commodities, energy, value, short duration** |
| Stagflation | Down | Up | Late cycle rolling over | Cash, gold |
| Reflation | Down | Down | Recession / early recovery setup | Bonds |

We cover the early-cycle box in [mid-cycle expansion, the steady grind](/blog/trading/cross-asset/mid-cycle-expansion-the-steady-grind) and the downturn box in [the recession playbook, defense and duration](/blog/trading/cross-asset/recession-playbook-defense-and-duration). This post lives entirely in the top-right box: **Overheat. Growth up, inflation up.**

### So what *is* the overheat, mechanically?

Picture the economy late in a long expansion — except do not just picture it, walk through the mechanics, because the mechanics are what tell you which assets to own.

After years of growth, two things have happened at once. First, demand is strong: people have jobs, wages, and confidence, so they are spending. Second, the economy is running near its *capacity* — factories are near full utilization, and the labor market is *tight*, meaning there are more job openings than people to fill them, so employers have to bid up wages to hire. When demand is high and there is little spare capacity to meet it, the only thing that gives is **price**. Sellers can raise prices and customers still buy; workers can demand raises and still get hired. That is the textbook recipe for **accelerating inflation**: too much money chasing not enough supply.

Now the central bank — in the United States, the Federal Reserve, or "the Fed" — has a mandate to keep inflation low and stable (around 2% a year). When inflation accelerates well past that, the Fed's job is to *cool the economy down*. Its main tool is the **interest rate** it controls (the federal funds rate). By raising that rate, the Fed makes borrowing more expensive — mortgages, car loans, business loans all cost more — which slows spending and investment, which eventually slows inflation. The point for us: **in the overheat, the Fed is hiking rates, and it keeps hiking until something breaks.**

So the overheat is a specific, recognizable cluster of conditions:

- **Growth above trend** — the economy is running hot, not cooling.
- **Inflation accelerating** — CPI is rising and surprising to the upside.
- **Unemployment low** — the labor market is tight, wages are climbing.
- **Capacity strained** — factories and supply chains are running near their limits.
- **The Fed hiking** — and signaling more hikes to come (turning *hawkish*).
- **The yield curve flattening, then inverting** — we will explain this signal in detail below.
- **Credit spreads still tight but vulnerable** — borrowers can still borrow cheaply, but that calm is fragile.

That last cluster is the *setup*. The payoff of recognizing it is knowing which assets thrive when the Fed is hiking into a hot economy — and which ones get destroyed.

#### Worked example: how a tight labor market becomes inflation

To make the overheat mechanism concrete, follow the money through a tight labor market. Suppose a restaurant pays a cook \$15/hour, but the labor market is so tight that the cook gets a competing offer at \$18/hour. To keep the cook, the owner raises pay to \$18 — a 20% wage increase.

- That \$3/hour is a real cost. To cover it without losing profit, the owner raises menu prices by, say, 8%. A \$25 meal becomes \$27.
- The cook, now earning more, can afford the \$27 meal — and so can everyone else getting raises across the hot economy. Demand does not fall; prices simply rose.
- Multiply this across millions of businesses all bidding for scarce workers, and you get the macro result: **wages up, prices up, inflation accelerating** — with no slack to absorb it.

This is the *wage-price* dynamic at the heart of the overheat: when the economy runs out of spare workers and spare capacity, extra demand turns into higher *prices* rather than higher *output*. The intuition: an overheating economy converts strength into inflation, and inflation is precisely what forces the Fed to hike and re-prices your portfolio.

### The crucial term: duration

One word will appear again and again in this post, so let us nail it now. **Duration** is a measure of how sensitive an asset's price is to a change in interest rates. The longer an asset's cash flows stretch into the future, the higher its duration, and the more its price falls when rates rise.

A bond that pays you back in 2 years has *short* duration. A bond that pays you back in 30 years has *long* duration. If interest rates rise by 1 percentage point, the 2-year bond's price barely moves (maybe −2%); the 30-year bond's price can fall 15% or more. Same rate move, wildly different damage, because of duration.

And here is the move that unlocks the whole post: **duration is not just a bond concept.** A growth stock — a company whose profits are expected to be tiny today but enormous a decade from now — is a *long-duration asset too*, because most of its value sits far in the future. When rates rise, the present value of those far-off profits shrinks, just like a long bond's. So when the Fed hikes in the overheat, two seemingly unrelated things fall together: long bonds and expensive growth stocks. They share a hidden trait — long duration — and rising rates punish duration. Hold that thought; it is the engine of everything that follows.

## What leads in the overheat, and exactly why

Now we get to the payoff: in the overheat, a very specific set of assets wins, and each one wins for a reason you can articulate. Let us go through the winners one at a time, then the losers, and ground every claim in the real numbers from 2021–2022 — the cleanest overheat in modern memory.

![Grouped bars of 2021 and 2022 total returns for commodities gold stocks bonds and 60/40](/imgs/blogs/late-cycle-overheat-when-real-assets-win-2.png)

The chart above is the whole thesis in one picture. Read the 2022 bars (the bright ones): commodities are the lone large green bar; gold is essentially flat; stocks, bonds, and the 60/40 are all deep red. That pattern — real assets up, everything financial down — *is* the overheat signature.

### Winner 1: commodities and energy

A **commodity** is a raw physical good — oil, natural gas, copper, wheat, gold. The price of a commodity is set by physical supply and demand for the actual stuff. Why do commodities lead in the overheat? Two reinforcing reasons.

First, **strong growth means strong demand for raw materials.** A booming economy builds more, ships more, drives more, manufactures more — all of which consumes oil, metals, and energy. Demand for the physical inputs rises right when the cycle is hottest.

Second, and more importantly, **commodities *are* inflation.** When we measure inflation, a large chunk of what we are measuring is the price of energy and food, which are commodities. So when inflation accelerates, commodity prices are not just *correlated* with inflation — they are *part of its definition*. Owning commodities is the most direct way to own inflation itself. This is why we say commodities are a *real asset*: their value is tied to the price of real-world goods, so it rises with the price level rather than getting eroded by it.

Energy is the standout. We dig into the full mechanism in [energy, oil and gas, the inflation engine](/blog/trading/cross-asset/energy-oil-gas-the-inflation-engine), but the short version is that an energy supply shock — an OPEC cut, a war, a pipeline outage — pushes up both inflation *and* energy company profits at the same time, which is the perfect late-cycle cocktail.

#### Worked example: commodities as the overheat winner

Suppose at the start of 2021 you put \$10,000 into a broad commodity index fund (tracking the Bloomberg Commodity Index, BCOM) and another \$10,000 into the S&P 500. Watch the two sleeves diverge across the overheat.

- **Commodities, 2021:** the index returned **+27.1%**. Your \$10,000 grew to \$10,000 × 1.271 = **\$12,710**.
- **Commodities, 2022:** the index returned another **+16.1%**. Your \$12,710 grew to \$12,710 × 1.161 ≈ **\$14,756**. Over the two years, the commodity sleeve gained roughly \$4,756, a two-year return of about +47.6%.
- **Stocks, same two years:** the S&P 500 returned +28.7% in 2021, lifting your \$10,000 to \$12,870 — then fell **−18.1%** in 2022, dropping it to \$12,870 × 0.819 ≈ **\$10,540**. Over the two years, the stock sleeve gained only about \$540.

The commodity sleeve made roughly **nine times** what the stock sleeve made across the overheat, and it did it by going *up* in the exact year stocks fell. The intuition: commodities are inflation in physical form, so they shine in the one regime — accelerating inflation — that wrecks paper assets.

### Winner 2: value and cyclical-value equities

Not all stocks suffer equally in the overheat. The key split is **value versus growth.**

A **growth stock** is a company priced mostly for its *future* — think a young software or chip company whose profits are small today but expected to explode years from now. Investors pay a high price relative to current earnings (a high *price-to-earnings ratio*) because they are buying the distant future. That makes a growth stock a **long-duration** asset, for exactly the reason we built above.

A **value stock** is the opposite: a company priced mostly for its *present* — a bank, an oil major, an industrial manufacturer, a utility. Its profits are here *now*, its price-to-earnings ratio is low, and much of your return comes from dividends paid today. That makes it a **short-duration** asset: most of its value is near-term cash, not far-off promises.

In the overheat, when the Fed hikes and the discount rate on all future cash flows rises, the long-duration growth stocks get re-priced down hard, while the short-duration value stocks — many of which are *cyclical*, meaning their profits actually *benefit* from the hot economy (banks earn more on higher rates, energy firms earn more on higher oil) — hold up far better. This is the "value over growth" rotation, and it is one of the most reliable tells that you have entered the overheat.

#### Worked example: why value crushes growth when real yields rise

Take two \$100 stocks. Stock G ("growth") is priced on \$2 of earnings expected mostly far in the future — say its value is dominated by a stream of profits a decade out, so we discount at a long horizon. Stock V ("value") is priced on \$8 of earnings happening *now*, discounted over a short horizon. Now the relevant discount rate jumps by 2 percentage points because the Fed is hiking.

- For **growth stock G**, whose value sits a decade out, a 2-point rise in the discount rate compounds over ten years. Roughly, \$1 due in 10 years discounted at 5% is worth about 61 cents; at 7% it is worth about 51 cents — a **drop of about 17%** in present value, just from the rate move. Pile that onto a high starting valuation and the price can fall 30% or more.
- For **value stock V**, whose value arrives in roughly 2 years, the same 2-point rise barely bites: \$1 due in 2 years at 5% is worth about 91 cents; at 7% it is worth about 87 cents — a **drop of about 4%**.

This is exactly the pattern 2022 delivered: as real yields surged, growth and tech fell roughly **−29% to −33%** while value fell only about **−5% to −7%**. The intuition: rising rates punish whoever is most dependent on the distant future, and growth stocks are bets on the distant future.

![Diverging bars showing value equities down about 6 percent versus growth down about 31 percent in 2022](/imgs/blogs/late-cycle-overheat-when-real-assets-win-4.png)

The chart makes the gap visceral: in a single year, the choice between owning value and owning growth was worth about **25 percentage points**. The overheat does not just decide *whether* you own stocks — it decides *which* stocks.

### Winner 3: gold, as real-rate expectations peak

**Gold** is a strange asset: it pays no interest, no dividend, produces nothing, and just sits in a vault. Its entire appeal is that it cannot be printed and it holds value when paper money is being debased by inflation. Here is the overheat angle.

Gold's biggest enemy is the **real yield** — the interest rate *after* subtracting expected inflation. Because gold pays nothing, when safe bonds pay a high *real* return, gold looks unattractive by comparison (why hold a rock that pays 0% real when a Treasury pays 2% real?). So gold tends to *fall* when real yields are *rising*. Early in the overheat, as the Fed hikes and real yields climb, gold can struggle — which is exactly what it did in 2022, slipping a touch.

But notice what "slipping a touch" meant in 2022: gold returned **−0.3%** — essentially *flat* — in a year when stocks fell 18% and bonds fell 13%. Holding flat while everything around it burned is, in portfolio terms, a *win*. And gold's real moment comes near the *end* of the overheat, when the market starts to believe real-rate expectations have **peaked** — that the Fed is almost done hiking and may soon have to cut. At that turn, the headwind reverses and gold rallies. (Gold then went on to surge above \$3,000/oz in 2025 as that turn played out.) So gold's role in the overheat is to be the *ballast that does not bleed* — the one financial asset that does not fall apart while the Fed is tightening.

### Winner 4: short-duration and floating-rate debt

If long bonds are the villains of the overheat (more on that next), their close cousins — *short-duration* and *floating-rate* debt — are quiet heroes.

A **short-duration bond** matures soon (say in 1–3 years), so a rise in rates barely dents its price, as we saw in the duration section. A **floating-rate** instrument is even better: instead of paying a fixed coupon, its interest payment *resets upward* as rates rise. So when the Fed hikes, a floating-rate holder's income actually *increases*, and the price stays near par. The very shortest-duration asset of all — cash, parked in Treasury bills — finally pays a real yield in the overheat, which is why it stops being dead weight and starts being a genuine holding.

In the overheat, this is the sweet spot of the bond market: you stay invested in debt, you collect rising interest, and you avoid the price destruction that hits long bonds. You are trading away some yield in exchange for not getting run over by duration. In a hiking cycle, that is a trade worth making.

### The losers: long Treasuries and long-duration growth

We have named the winners; now name the losers explicitly, because avoiding them matters as much as owning the winners.

The **long Treasury** — a 10-year or 30-year government bond — is the purest long-duration asset there is. Its price is the present value of a fixed stream of coupons stretching decades out, discounted at the prevailing interest rate. When the Fed hikes and rates rise across the curve, that present value collapses. In 2022, the broad US bond index (the "Agg") fell **−13.0%**, and *long* Treasuries fell far more than that. The "safe" asset had one of its worst years in a century.

The **long-duration growth stock** is the equity twin, as we built above. Both losers share the same disease: their value lives in the far future, and rising rates tax the far future hardest. **In the overheat, "long duration" — whether it wears a bond costume or a stock costume — is the thing to avoid.**

## How real assets behave: volatility, drawdowns, and correlations

Knowing that commodities and energy *win* in the overheat is only half of owning them well. The other half is understanding how they *behave* — how violently they move, how deep their drawdowns run, and how they sit alongside the rest of your portfolio. Real assets are not gentle. They are the highest-octane part of the cross-asset map, and owning them sensibly means respecting that.

### Commodities are volatile, in both directions

A **drawdown** is the percentage drop from an asset's peak to its subsequent trough — the worst loss you would have suffered if you bought at the top. Stocks are volatile; commodities are *more* so. Oil is the clearest case: it spiked to \$147/barrel in mid-2008, then crashed to \$44 by year-end — a roughly 70% drawdown in months. It even printed *negative* for the first time in history in April 2020 (a settlement of −\$37.63), when storage filled up and holders had to pay to offload barrels. So while commodities are the overheat's leading winners, they are not a "set and forget" holding. Their up-years are huge (BCOM +27.1% in 2021), but their down-years are brutal (BCOM −24.7% in 2015). You own them *because* of the regime, and you trim them *when the regime turns* — they are a tactical tilt, not a permanent core.

This is the crucial honesty point: a real-asset tilt is a bet *on the regime*, and if you are wrong about the regime, commodities can hurt you fast. If inflation rolls over and growth fades before you expected, the same volatility that powered your gains works against you. The overheat playbook is not "always own commodities" — it is "own commodities *while the overheat conditions hold*, and have an exit plan for when they break."

#### Worked example: sizing a volatile real-asset sleeve

Suppose you decide to add a 15% commodity sleeve to a \$100,000 portfolio — \$15,000 — because you have confirmed the overheat. How much portfolio-level risk does that add, and what is the realistic range of outcomes?

- In a *good* overheat year like 2021 (+27.1%), the sleeve contributes 0.15 × 0.271 = **+4.1%** to the whole portfolio. On its own, \$15,000 grows by 0.271 × \$15,000 ≈ **+\$4,065**.
- In a *bad* year like 2015 (−24.7%), the same sleeve subtracts 0.15 × 0.247 = **−3.7%** from the portfolio. The \$15,000 falls by 0.247 × \$15,000 ≈ **−\$3,705**.

So a 15% sleeve swings the whole portfolio by roughly ±4% depending on the commodity year — a large but survivable contribution. Size it any bigger and a single bad commodity year dominates your results; size it much smaller and it cannot move the needle when the overheat pays. The intuition: real assets are powerful *and* dangerous, so you size them to be meaningful but not portfolio-defining — typically a 10–20% sleeve, never the whole book.

### The correlation that matters: real assets versus the rest

The deepest reason to own commodities in the overheat is not their *return* — it is their *correlation*. Recall that **correlation** measures whether two assets move together (positive) or apart (negative). Over the long run, commodities have a *low* correlation with stocks and bonds, and crucially, in inflation-driven regimes their correlation with bonds turns *negative*: when rising inflation crushes bonds, it lifts commodities. That makes commodities the natural *replacement* hedge in exactly the regime where bonds fail.

The table below lays out how the key cross-asset relationships shift between the two great regimes — and why the overheat demands a different diversifier.

| Relationship | Growth-fear regime (recession) | Inflation regime (overheat) |
|---|---|---|
| Stocks vs bonds | **Negative** — bonds hedge stocks, 60/40 works | **Positive** — bonds fall *with* stocks, 60/40 breaks |
| Stocks vs commodities | Low/positive — both can fall in a slump | Mixed — commodities can rise as stocks fall |
| Bonds vs commodities | Positive-ish — both can rally on Fed cuts | **Negative** — inflation lifts commodities, sinks bonds |
| What hedges your stocks | Long Treasuries (the classic airbag) | **Commodities, energy, gold, cash** |
| Your master variable | Growth / the recession risk | **Real yields / inflation** |

Read the right-hand column top to bottom and you have the overheat thesis: the relationships you relied on in the last regime have *inverted*, so the asset that protects you has changed. In a recession, your hedge is duration (long bonds). In the overheat, your hedge is real assets. Same word — "hedge" — opposite instrument. We unpack the full regime-dependence of these correlations in [the stock-bond correlation, the 60/40 engine](/blog/trading/cross-asset/stock-bond-correlation-the-60-40-engine), but the table is the operational summary: **let the regime pick your diversifier.**

### What it costs to hold the overheat tilt

Nothing in markets is free, and the overheat tilt has real costs you should name before you put it on.

The first cost is **tracking error against the crowd.** When you overweight energy and underweight tech, you will look very different from the index — wonderful when energy is up 65% and tech is down 30%, painful in the years that pattern reverses. You have to be willing to *not* own the market, which is psychologically hard.

The second cost is **the roll, for commodity futures.** Most investors own commodities through futures-based funds, and the mechanics of rolling expiring contracts can cost you a few percent a year when the futures curve is unfavorable (in *contango*) — a drag we detail in [energy, oil and gas, the inflation engine](/blog/trading/cross-asset/energy-oil-gas-the-inflation-engine). The favorable case (*backwardation*) pays you, but you cannot assume it.

The third cost is **timing risk.** Rotate too early and you give up upside in the still-running mid-cycle; rotate too late and you eat the start of the squeeze. The five tells are designed to keep your timing honest, but no signal is perfect, and the overheat tilt is a *probabilistic* edge, not a guarantee. This is education about mechanisms and history, not individualized advice — you are managing odds, not certainties.

## The single variable behind it all: real yields

If you remember one mechanism from this post, make it this one. The reason long bonds *and* growth stocks both got crushed in 2022 was not two separate stories — it was one variable moving: the **real yield**.

We define it fully in [real yields, the variable that prices everything](/blog/trading/cross-asset/real-yields-the-variable-that-prices-everything), but the one-line version: the **real yield** is the interest rate *after* subtracting expected inflation. If a 10-year Treasury yields 4% and the market expects 2% inflation, the real yield is about 2%. It is the *true*, inflation-adjusted reward for tying up your money — and it is the discount rate the whole market uses to price every long-dated asset.

We can read the real yield directly off a special bond called a **TIPS** (Treasury Inflation-Protected Security), whose payments adjust with inflation. The yield on a 10-year TIPS *is* the 10-year real yield. And in 2022, that number did something violent.

![Line chart of 10 year TIPS real yield rising from negative 1.04 percent to positive 1.74 percent in 2022](/imgs/blogs/late-cycle-overheat-when-real-assets-win-5.png)

The chart tells the whole story of the 2022 squeeze in one line. The 10-year real yield began at **−1.04%** in December 2021 — *deeply negative*, meaning bondholders were locking in a guaranteed loss after inflation, a condition that only made sense because the Fed was holding rates at zero. As the Fed hiked through 2022, the real yield climbed relentlessly, crossing zero and reaching **+1.74%** by October 2022. That is a swing of about **278 basis points** (a *basis point* is one hundredth of a percent, so 278 bps is 2.78 percentage points) in under a year.

Why does this one move explain so much? Because the real yield is the discount rate for *all* long-duration assets at once. When it leaps from −1% to +1.74%, you are raising the discount rate on a 30-year Treasury, on a 10-year corporate bond, on a growth stock priced for profits in 2032, and on gold — *simultaneously*. Every long-duration asset gets re-priced downward by the same lever. That is why bonds and growth stocks fell *together* in 2022, why gold struggled, and why the only equities that held up were the short-duration value names. One variable, one regime, one coordinated repricing.

#### Worked example: a 278-bp real-yield move on a 30-year bond

Let us put a number on how brutal that real-yield surge was for long duration. A 30-year zero-coupon Treasury — a bond that pays one lump sum 30 years out and nothing in between — has a duration of essentially 30 years. The rule of thumb: a bond's percentage price change is approximately *minus duration times the change in yield*.

- The yield (here, the real yield) rose by **+2.78 percentage points** (from −1.04% to +1.74%).
- Price change ≈ −duration × change in yield = −30 × 0.0278 = **−0.834**, or about **−83%**.
- On a \$10,000 position, that is a paper loss of roughly **\$8,340**, leaving about \$1,660.

A loss of that magnitude on the "safest" asset class on Earth is what the overheat can do to long duration. Real 30-year bonds are not zero-coupon, so the actual hit was smaller — long Treasuries fell on the order of 30% in 2022, not 83% — but the direction and the cause are exactly this. The intuition: when the real yield surges, the longer your duration, the bigger the crater, and the 30-year bond has the most duration of all.

## The 60/40 danger: when your hedge stops hedging

Here is where the overheat becomes genuinely dangerous, because it breaks the one assumption a "balanced" portfolio is built on.

The classic **60/40 portfolio** — 60% stocks, 40% bonds — works on a quiet promise: when stocks fall, bonds rise, so the bond sleeve cushions the stock losses. That promise depends entirely on the **stock-bond correlation** being *negative* (stocks and bonds moving in opposite directions). We dedicate a whole post to this — [the stock-bond correlation, the 60/40 engine](/blog/trading/cross-asset/stock-bond-correlation-the-60-40-engine) — but the overheat is precisely the regime where the promise breaks.

Why? Because in a *recession*, the thing driving markets is **growth fear**: stocks fall on weak earnings, and the Fed *cuts* rates to help, so bonds *rise*. Opposite directions. The hedge works. But in the *overheat*, the thing driving markets is **inflation and rising rates**: stocks fall because higher rates re-price them down, and bonds *also* fall because higher rates re-price them down. *Same* direction. The correlation flips from negative to **positive**, and the bond sleeve, instead of being an airbag, becomes a second collision.

This is not a tail risk — it is the defining feature of the overheat. When the dominant market force shifts from *growth* to *inflation*, the sign of the stock-bond correlation flips with it. In the pre-2000 inflationary era the correlation was positive; from 2000 to 2021 (a low-inflation, growth-fear era) it went negative, which is why the 60/40 felt invincible; and in 2022 it snapped *positive* again, to roughly +0.5. The 60/40 had no idea what hit it because it had not seen this regime in a generation.

#### Worked example: the 60/40 in the 2022 squeeze versus a late-cycle tilt

This is the comparison that makes the whole post pay off. Take two \$100,000 portfolios entering 2022.

**Portfolio A — the classic 60/40:** \$60,000 in stocks, \$40,000 in bonds.

- Stocks fell **−18.1%**: 0.181 × \$60,000 = a loss of **−\$10,860**.
- Bonds fell **−13.0%**: 0.130 × \$40,000 = a loss of **−\$5,200**.
- Total: −\$10,860 − \$5,200 = **−\$16,060**, a loss of about **−16%**. The bonds did not cushion anything — they *added* to the loss.

**Portfolio B — the late-cycle-aware tilt:** rotate into the overheat winners. Say \$30,000 energy/commodities, \$25,000 value equities, \$20,000 short-duration bonds, \$15,000 gold, \$10,000 cash.

- Energy/commodities **+30%**: 0.30 × \$30,000 = **+\$9,000**.
- Value equities **−6%**: −0.06 × \$25,000 = **−\$1,500**.
- Short-duration bonds **−2%**: −0.02 × \$20,000 = **−\$400**.
- Gold **−0.3%**: −0.003 × \$15,000 = **−\$45**.
- Cash **+1.5%**: 0.015 × \$10,000 = **+\$150**.
- Total: \$9,000 − \$1,500 − \$400 − \$45 + \$150 = **+\$7,205**, a gain of about **+7.2%**.

The two portfolios held the same dollars, in the same year, in the same market. The 60/40 lost about \$16,060; the late-cycle tilt *gained* about \$7,205 — a swing of roughly **\$23,000**, or about **23 percentage points**, purely from rotating into the overheat leaders before the squeeze. That single decision — recognizing the regime and tilting toward real assets and short duration — was the most valuable allocation call of the decade. The intuition: in the overheat, *what you diversify into* matters far more than *how much* you diversify, because the usual diversifier (bonds) is failing exactly when you need it.

## Reading the late-cycle turn: the five tells

Knowing real assets win in the overheat is useless if you cannot recognize the overheat *while it is happening*. So here are the five concrete signals, in roughly the order they tend to appear. None is sufficient alone; together they are a high-confidence read.

### Tell 1: inflation surprising to the upside

The first crack is inflation data coming in *hotter than expected*, month after month. Not just high inflation — *accelerating* and *surprising* inflation. Markets price in what they expect; what moves them is the *surprise*. In 2021, CPI ran at 4.99% year-over-year in May; by June 2022 it had hit **9.06%**, a 40-year high. The relentless upside surprises were the market's first warning that the regime had changed.

### Tell 2: the Fed turning hawkish

*Hawkish* means leaning toward higher rates to fight inflation; *dovish* means leaning toward lower rates to support growth. The turn from dovish to hawkish — when the Fed stops calling inflation "transitory" and starts hiking aggressively — is the regime confirmation. In 2022, the Fed took its policy rate (the upper bound of the federal funds target) from **0.25% to 4.50%** in a single calendar year, the fastest hiking cycle in four decades. When the Fed is moving that fast, you are unambiguously in the overheat.

### Tell 3: the curve flattening, then inverting

This is the bond market's own late-cycle clock, and it deserves its own picture.

![Yield curve diagram showing early cycle upward sloping curve flattening then inverting](/imgs/blogs/late-cycle-overheat-when-real-assets-win-6.png)

A **yield curve** plots the interest rate (yield) on government bonds against how long until they mature — 3 months, 2 years, 10 years, 30 years. Normally it slopes *upward*: longer bonds pay more, because you are tying up your money longer and bearing more uncertainty. That upward slope is the *early-cycle* shape.

As the overheat builds and the Fed hikes, something specific happens. The Fed controls the *short* end of the curve directly, so its hikes push *short* rates up fast. But the *long* end is set by the market's expectations of future growth and inflation — and as investors start to fear that all this tightening will eventually cause a recession, they *buy* long bonds (pushing long yields down) even as short yields rise. The curve **flattens** (the gap between long and short shrinks), and then it **inverts** — short rates rise *above* long rates, so the curve slopes *downward*.

An inverted curve is the bond market's collective statement that "the Fed is tightening so hard it will probably cause a recession." The most-watched version is the **10-year minus 2-year spread**: when it goes negative, the curve is inverted. In July 2022 that spread inverted to its deepest level since 1981. An inversion has preceded every US recession of the past half-century, usually by 6 to 18 months. So inversion is both the confirmation that you are late cycle *and* the early warning that the *next* phase — recession — is coming.

### Tell 4: commodity leadership

Watch the *relative performance* of asset classes. When commodities and energy start *leading* — outperforming stocks and bonds week after week — the market is voting that inflation, not growth fear, is the dominant force. Commodity leadership is the rotation showing up in prices before most people have named the regime.

![Horizontal bars showing energy sector up 54 percent and 65 percent versus the index down 18 percent](/imgs/blogs/late-cycle-overheat-when-real-assets-win-3.png)

The energy sector is the clearest tell of all, as the chart shows. While the S&P 500 fell **−18.1%** in 2022, the S&P energy sector returned roughly **+65%** — after already returning about **+54%** in 2021. When one sector is up 65% in a year the index is down 18%, the market is screaming "inflation regime." Energy leadership of that magnitude is about as unambiguous a late-cycle signal as exists.

### Tell 5: value outperforming growth

The final equity-level tell is the *value-over-growth* rotation we built earlier. When boring, cheap, dividend-paying value stocks (banks, energy, industrials) start beating exciting, expensive growth stocks (software, chips, anything priced for 2032), the discount-rate engine is at work: rising real yields are taxing the long-duration growth names and sparing the short-duration value names. A sustained value-over-growth move is the stock market's internal confirmation of the overheat.

## Common misconceptions

The overheat trips up even experienced investors because it inverts the rules that work in every other phase. Here are the beliefs that cost people money, each corrected with a number.

**"Bonds are always the safe diversifier."** This is the most expensive myth in finance. Bonds diversify stocks *only when the stock-bond correlation is negative* — that is, in growth-driven selloffs where the Fed is cutting. In the overheat, the correlation flips positive and bonds fall *with* stocks. In 2022 the broad bond index fell **−13.0%** alongside the S&P's −18.1%. "Safe" is a property of the regime, not of the asset.

**"A strong economy is good for my portfolio."** Strong growth is good for *earnings*, but in the overheat it is the *cause* of the problem: strength produces inflation, inflation produces Fed hikes, and hikes re-price your assets down. The 2022 economy was *strong* — unemployment near a 50-year low — and that is exactly *why* the 60/40 lost 16%. A too-strong economy is a portfolio risk, not a comfort.

**"Gold is a great inflation hedge, so it should soar when inflation spikes."** Gold's master variable is the *real* yield, not headline inflation. When inflation rises but the Fed raises *real* yields even faster (as in early 2022), gold can be flat or fall — it returned **−0.3%** in 2022 despite 9% inflation. Gold's big move comes later, when real-yield expectations *peak* and the Fed is seen to be near done. It is a *real-rate* hedge with an inflation flavor, not a simple inflation thermometer.

**"Growth stocks are always the best long-term holding."** Over a full cycle, maybe — but growth stocks are *long-duration*, and the overheat is the one regime built to destroy long duration. In 2022 growth and tech fell roughly **−29% to −33%** while value fell only about **−6%**. "Best long-term" does not mean "safe in every regime," and the overheat is the regime where the long-term winners take the worst short-term beating.

**"An inverted yield curve means sell stocks right now."** Inversion is a *late-cycle confirmation* and a *recession early-warning*, not a same-day sell signal. Curves often invert 6 to 18 months *before* the recession, and stocks (especially energy and value) can keep rising during that lag — the S&P energy sector returned +65% in 2022 with the curve inverted the whole second half. Inversion tells you to *prepare* and *rotate*, not to dump everything that afternoon.

**"Tight credit spreads mean the overheat is safe."** A *credit spread* is the extra yield a risky corporate bond pays over a safe Treasury — it widens when investors fear defaults and narrows when they are complacent. Late in the overheat, spreads are often still *tight*, which feels reassuring. But tight spreads in a late cycle are *fragile*, not *safe*: they are priced for a soft landing the Fed's own hiking is working to prevent. When the squeeze bites and growth rolls over, spreads can widen violently — in 2022, high-yield bonds fell **−11.2%** even though the economy had not yet entered recession. Tight spreads are the calm before the credit storm, not proof of safety.

## How it shows up in real markets

The overheat is not a textbook theory — it has recurred throughout history with the same fingerprints. Here are the three clearest episodes, each a real-world test of everything above.

### 2021–early 2022: the textbook overheat and the 2022 break

This is the cleanest overheat in modern memory, which is why we have leaned on it throughout. After the COVID shock, an extraordinary flood of fiscal stimulus (government checks) and monetary stimulus (the Fed holding rates at zero and buying bonds) collided with snarled supply chains. Demand roared back; supply could not keep up; prices accelerated. CPI climbed from about 5% in mid-2021 to **9.06%** by June 2022, a 40-year high. The Fed, after insisting inflation was "transitory" for most of 2021, capitulated and hiked from **0.25% to 4.50%** across 2022. The 10-year real yield surged from **−1.04% to +1.74%**.

The asset-class results were the overheat playbook in pure form. Commodities returned **+27.1%** in 2021 and **+16.1%** in 2022. The S&P energy sector returned roughly **+54%** then **+65%**. Value outperformed growth by some 25 points. Gold held flat at **−0.3%**. And the losers obeyed the script: the S&P 500 fell **−18.1%**, the bond Agg fell **−13.0%**, and the 60/40 fell **−16.0%**, its worst calendar year since the 1930s. Then, right on cue, the curve inverted in July 2022 — and a growth slowdown began to take shape in 2023, the overheat handing off to the next phase. The lesson: the overheat rewarded *exactly* the assets this framework names, and punished *exactly* the ones it warns about.

### 2006–2007: the late-cycle squeeze before the GFC

The mid-2000s expansion ran hot. Growth was solid, unemployment was low, commodity demand from a booming global economy (especially China) was voracious, and inflation was creeping up. The Fed hiked its policy rate from 1% in 2004 all the way to 5.25% by mid-2006, and the yield curve inverted in 2006 — the classic late-cycle sequence. Commodities and energy led: oil climbed toward record highs (it would hit \$147/barrel in mid-2008), and energy and materials stocks were among the strongest sectors. Long bonds and rate-sensitive assets lagged. The overheat tells were all flashing. The episode is also a warning about *what comes next*: the inversion in 2006 preceded the 2008 financial crisis, a reminder that the overheat is the *last good* phase before the downturn — which is why the playbook below includes raising cash for the recession that often follows.

### 1979–1980: the great inflation and the Volcker squeeze

The most extreme overheat-into-stagflation episode of the modern era. Through the 1970s, repeated oil shocks and loose policy let inflation spiral, reaching nearly 14% by 1980. Real assets were the only refuge: across the broader 1970s, **gold rose from about \$35/oz to over \$800/oz**, and oil prices multiplied severalfold. Stocks and bonds, by contrast, delivered terrible *real* (inflation-adjusted) returns for over a decade — the "lost" decade for paper assets. The episode ended when Fed Chair Paul Volcker pushed the policy rate to nearly 20% to finally break inflation, inverting the curve dramatically and triggering a deep recession. The lesson 1979–1980 burned into a generation: when inflation is the dominant force, real assets are not a tactical tilt — they are *survival*, and paper assets can be dead money for years.

### A note on the handoff to recession

Across all three episodes, the same final beat recurs: the overheat does not last forever. The Fed hikes until something breaks, the curve inverts, and the economy tips into a downturn (2008, the early-1980s recession, the 2023 slowdown). That is why a complete late-cycle plan is not just "buy commodities" — it is "buy commodities *and start preparing for the next phase*," in which the leadership flips again toward bonds and cash. We hand off to that phase in [the recession playbook, defense and duration](/blog/trading/cross-asset/recession-playbook-defense-and-duration).

#### Worked example: the energy sleeve earning its keep in the squeeze

Bring the historical lessons together into one portfolio number. Suppose you held a \$100,000 portfolio through 2022 and, reading the overheat, you carved out a \$20,000 energy sleeve while keeping \$50,000 in broad stocks and \$30,000 in bonds.

- **The \$50,000 stock sleeve** fell with the S&P's −18.1%: −0.181 × \$50,000 = **−\$9,050**.
- **The \$30,000 bond sleeve** fell with the Agg's −13.0%: −0.130 × \$30,000 = **−\$3,900**.
- **The \$20,000 energy sleeve** rose roughly +65% with the S&P energy sector: +0.65 × \$20,000 = **+\$13,000**.
- Net: −\$9,050 − \$3,900 + \$13,000 = **+\$50** — essentially break-even, in a year the 60/40 lost 16%.

A single 20% energy sleeve was large enough to *offset the entire loss* from stocks and bonds combined. That is what "the energy sleeve earns its keep" means in dollars: in the overheat, one inflation-beneficiary holding can rescue a whole portfolio. The intuition: because energy goes *up* for the same reason stocks and bonds go *down* (inflation), even a modest energy slice is a disproportionately powerful overheat hedge.

## The allocation playbook: when to own real assets and short duration

Here is the payoff — the concrete rotation. When you have read the five tells and concluded you are in the overheat, the moves are mechanical. The matrix below is the whole playbook on one page.

![Two column matrix of late cycle rotation showing what to add and what to trim](/imgs/blogs/late-cycle-overheat-when-real-assets-win-7.png)

### What to add (the overheat winners)

- **Commodities and energy (overweight).** This is the leadership. A meaningful sleeve — often 10–20% across broad commodities and energy equities — is the core of the overheat tilt. It went up double-digits in 2022 while everything financial fell. Energy equities give you the inflation exposure *plus* a dividend.
- **Materials and cyclical-value equities.** Tilt the equity book away from expensive growth and toward cheap, near-term-cash businesses: banks, energy, industrials, materials. They are short-duration and many *benefit* from the hot economy directly.
- **Short-duration and floating-rate debt.** Stay in the bond market but at the *short* end, where rising rates do little damage and floating-rate coupons actually rise as the Fed hikes.
- **Gold and inflation hedges (a ballast sleeve).** Gold's job here is to *not fall apart* while the Fed tightens, and to be positioned for the rally that comes when real-rate expectations peak. A 5–15% sleeve is common.
- **Cash (a growing buffer).** As the curve inverts and the recession warning flashes, *raise some cash*. In the overheat, cash finally pays a real yield (T-bills yielded 1.5%+ and rising through 2022), and it is the dry powder you will deploy into the cheap assets the *next* phase creates.

### What to trim (the overheat losers)

- **Long Treasuries and long-duration bonds.** The purest victims of rising real yields. Cut duration hard; the Agg fell 13% in 2022 and long Treasuries fell far more.
- **Expensive, long-duration growth and tech.** The equity twin of the long bond. As real yields surge, the present value of distant profits collapses — growth/tech fell roughly 30% in 2022.
- **Do not lean on the classic 60/40.** Its hidden assumption — bonds hedge stocks — is false in this regime. If you hold a 60/40, the overheat is when you most need to *add* the inflation hedges the 60/40 lacks.

### Rough sizing and what invalidates the case

A common late-cycle tilt for an investor who has confirmed the overheat: shift perhaps 15–25% of the portfolio out of long bonds and expensive growth, and into the basket of commodities/energy, value, short-duration debt, gold, and cash. The exact numbers depend on your situation — and this is education, not individualized advice — but the *direction* is the point: more real assets, more value, shorter duration, more cash.

**What invalidates the overheat case?** Watch for the regime to *change*, because it always does:

- **Inflation rolls over convincingly** (CPI decelerating, surprising to the *downside*) and the Fed pivots from hawkish to dovish. That is the signal the overheat is ending — real assets lose their tailwind, and leadership rotates back toward bonds and growth.
- **The economy tips into recession** (the inversion's promise comes due). Now *growth fear* replaces inflation as the dominant force, the stock-bond correlation flips back to negative, and the playbook inverts: long bonds become the hedge again, commodities and value give way to defensives. This is the handoff covered in [the recession playbook, defense and duration](/blog/trading/cross-asset/recession-playbook-defense-and-duration).

The overheat is the most counterintuitive phase of the cycle: it arrives dressed as good news, it breaks the portfolio most people are told is safe, and it rewards the assets — commodities, energy, value, gold, cash — that a "balanced" investor typically owns least. But it is also the most *legible* phase, because its signals are loud: hot inflation, a hawkish Fed, an inverting curve, energy and value leading. If you can read those five tells and rotate toward real assets and short duration *before* the squeeze, you turn the year that broke the 60/40 into the year your portfolio quietly pulled ahead. That single regime read — from a −16% loss to a +7% gain in 2022 — is the most valuable thing in cross-asset investing.
