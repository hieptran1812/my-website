> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The 1970s were the one decade in modern history when **high-and-rising inflation with weak growth** — *stagflation* — crushed both stocks and bonds in real (after-inflation) terms while **real assets** soared. Gold, oil and commodities were the winners; the classic 60/40 portfolio was exactly the wrong thing to own.
>
> - **The setup:** in August 1971 the dollar came off gold (the end of *Bretton Woods*), policy ran loose, and then OPEC quadrupled the oil price in the 1973-74 embargo. The result was inflation that ran from ~3% to over 14%, while growth stalled.
> - **The scoreboard:** across the 1973-74 stagflation window, **gold rose ~+210%**, **oil ~+300%**, while the **S&P 500 fell ~-37%** and long Treasuries returned just **~+4% nominal** against **~+22% cumulative inflation**. Over the full period the S&P was roughly *flat in nominal terms but down ~65% in real terms*, and gold ran about **24x** from ~\$35 to ~\$850 an ounce.
> - **The lesson:** "stocks are an inflation hedge" and "bonds are always safe" both failed badly here. When inflation is the enemy, *paper assets get repriced down and eroded* while *real assets, which have intrinsic value, win*.
> - The one number to remember: the same dollar bought about **65% less** at the end of the 1970s equity round-trip than at the start — even though the stock index looked unchanged.

In the autumn of 1974, an American who had spent two decades doing everything "right" with their money looked at their statement and felt something close to disbelief. They had owned a sensible, diversified portfolio — blue-chip stocks and government bonds, the bedrock of every prudent saver. Over the prior twenty-one months, the S&P 500 had fallen roughly 48% from its peak. The bonds that were supposed to cushion the fall were drifting lower too, because interest rates were climbing. And the cruelest part was invisible on the statement: the dollars themselves were losing value fast. Prices at the grocery store and the gas pump were up double digits year over year. The saver had lost money on the stocks, lost money on the bonds, and lost purchasing power on every dollar that survived. There was, it seemed, nowhere to hide.

Except there was. While paper wealth was bleeding, a quiet set of assets was doing the opposite. Gold, which an American couldn't even legally own for most of the prior forty years, had multiplied several times over. Oil had quadrupled in a matter of months. Farmland, commodities, and energy producers were among the few things actually gaining real value. The decade had a brutal, clarifying logic to it: when inflation is high and rising and growth is weak, the things that represent a *claim on paper* — a share of future profits, a promise of fixed future dollars — get punished, and the things that *are* something real get rewarded. This is the regime we call **stagflation**, and the 1970s are its defining case study.

The figure below is the scoreboard for the whole decade, and it is the thesis of this post in one picture: the real assets — oil, gold, broad commodities — went up, a lot, while the paper assets — stocks and bonds — went down in real terms. Everything that follows is the story of *why* that happened, told chronologically with the real dates and numbers, and then the playbook for what to do if that world ever comes back.

![Horizontal bar chart of 1970s returns showing oil, gold and commodities up sharply and stocks and bonds down in real terms](/imgs/blogs/case-study-1970s-stagflation-commodities-win-1.png)

By the end, you will be able to look at a world of accelerating inflation and stalling growth and say, with real historical grounding, "this is stagflation — I want commodities, energy, gold and short-duration real assets, and I do not want to lean on a portfolio of long bonds and expensive stocks." That single read is the most expensive lesson of the entire decade, and the 1970s taught it the hard way.

## Foundations: the setup — why the great inflation happened

Before we can trade the lesson, we have to understand the machine. Stagflation did not appear out of nowhere; it was the product of a specific chain of decisions and shocks that, in hindsight, fit together with grim inevitability. Let us build the setup from zero, assuming you have never read a word about Bretton Woods, OPEC, or wage-price spirals.

### First, two words you need: real, nominal, and stagflation itself

Three terms run through this entire post, so let us pin them down immediately.

A **nominal** number is the number printed on the screen or the statement — the raw dollar figure, before adjusting for anything. If your stock portfolio goes from \$100 to \$105, your *nominal* return is +5%.

A **real** number adjusts for inflation — it tells you what your money can actually *buy*. If your portfolio rose 5% (nominal) but the prices of everything you buy rose 8% over the same year, then in *real* terms you got poorer: your money buys less than it did. Real return is roughly the nominal return minus the inflation rate. The single most important idea in this post is that **the 1970s were a decade of positive nominal returns and deeply negative real returns for paper assets.** The numbers on the statement weren't always falling. The *purchasing power* behind them was collapsing. (We go deep on this distinction in [real vs nominal: inflation, real yields, and the master signal](/blog/trading/macro-trading/real-vs-nominal-inflation-real-yields-master-signal).)

**Inflation** is the rate at which the general price level rises. We usually read it off the *Consumer Price Index*, or CPI — the cost of a typical basket of goods and services. "Inflation is 12%" means that basket costs 12% more than it did a year ago. A normal-inflation world runs around 2%; the 1970s ran several times that.

**Stagflation** is the unhappy combination that gives the decade its name: *stagnation* (weak or negative growth, high unemployment) happening *at the same time* as high inflation. For decades, economists thought this combination was nearly impossible — the going theory was that you got inflation *or* recession, and you could trade one off against the other. The 1970s proved you could get both at once, and that discovery rewrote macroeconomics.

### Why the experts were blindsided: the Phillips curve

It is worth understanding *why* stagflation was such a shock, because the reason is also the reason investors were caught holding the wrong portfolio. Through the 1950s and 1960s, the dominant framework was the **Phillips curve** — a relationship, named for economist A.W. Phillips, that said inflation and unemployment trade off against each other. When unemployment was low (a hot economy), inflation tended to be high; when unemployment was high (a cold economy), inflation tended to be low. The policy implication was seductive: a government could "buy" lower unemployment by accepting a little more inflation, dialing the economy up or down like a thermostat.

The 1970s shattered this. Unemployment and inflation rose *together* — the thermostat broke. By 1975, the US had roughly 8-9% unemployment *and* high single-digit-to-double-digit inflation, a combination the Phillips curve said shouldn't exist. The reason, worked out afterward, is that the curve only holds when inflation *expectations* are stable. Once people start *expecting* high inflation, they build it into wages and prices regardless of how weak the economy is — so you get inflation and unemployment at once. For an investor, the lesson is brutal and direct: the people running the economy were navigating with a map that did not include the territory they were in. If the experts didn't see stagflation coming, a saver leaning on the conventional "stocks and bonds are all you need" wisdom had no chance — unless they understood, from first principles, which assets actually protect purchasing power when inflation is the master force.

### The building block: what Bretton Woods was, and why ending it mattered

To understand why inflation got loose, you have to understand the monetary system that had contained it. After World War II, the major economies agreed to a framework called **Bretton Woods** (named for the New Hampshire town where it was negotiated in 1944). The deal was simple in spirit: the US dollar was pegged to gold at \$35 an ounce, and every other major currency was pegged to the dollar. Foreign governments could, in principle, hand the US \$35 and demand an ounce of gold in return.

This is a *hard-money* system. It put a leash on how much money the US could create, because every dollar was, at least theoretically, convertible into a fixed amount of gold. If the US printed too many dollars, foreigners would show up wanting gold, and the US would run out. The discipline of the gold link is what kept inflation low and stable through the 1950s and most of the 1960s.

By the late 1960s, that discipline was breaking. The US was spending heavily — on the Vietnam War and on domestic "Great Society" programs — and running deficits. More dollars were sloshing around the world than there was gold at \$35 to back them. Foreign governments, especially France, started doing the arithmetic and demanding gold for their dollars. The US gold reserves were draining.

On **August 15, 1971**, President Richard Nixon went on television and closed the gold window — the US would no longer convert dollars into gold for anyone. This is the **"Nixon shock,"** and it is the hinge of the whole decade. With one announcement, the dollar became a pure *fiat* currency — money backed by nothing but the government's word and the public's faith. The leash was off. There was now no hard limit on how many dollars could be created, and the world's reserve currency was free to lose value against real things — most visibly, against gold and oil.

### The second ingredient: loose policy and the wage-price spiral

A money system without a gold anchor is not automatically inflationary — it depends on how the people running it behave. In the 1970s, they ran it hot. Interest rates were held too low for too long relative to inflation, partly out of a genuine (and mistaken) belief that a little inflation was a fair price for low unemployment, and partly because fighting inflation hard means causing a recession, which no politician wants to own.

Once inflation got going, it fed on itself through what is called a **wage-price spiral**. Here is the loop: prices rise, so workers demand higher wages to keep up; higher wages raise companies' costs, so companies raise prices to protect their margins; higher prices push workers to demand still-higher wages; and around it goes. Each turn of the wheel bakes inflation deeper into the economy and into people's *expectations*. And expectations are the dangerous part: once everyone *assumes* prices will rise 10% next year, they act in ways — demanding raises, raising prices preemptively, buying now rather than later — that *make* prices rise 10%. Inflation becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy.

There is one more piece of the policy story worth naming, because it is the mechanism that turns "loose policy" from a vague phrase into a concrete cause. Inflation is, at its root, **too much money chasing too few goods.** When the central bank keeps interest rates low and the banking system creates credit freely, the *quantity of money* in the economy grows faster than the quantity of goods and services it can buy. More dollars bidding for the same stuff means each dollar buys less — which is inflation by definition. Through the 1970s, broad money supply grew rapidly, and with the gold anchor gone there was nothing mechanical to stop it. This is the connection between the Nixon shock and the inflation: ending convertibility didn't *cause* inflation by itself, but it removed the brake, and a policy regime that wanted low unemployment more than it feared inflation then pressed the accelerator. The currency was now backed only by trust, and a decade of running it hot is exactly how that trust gets spent.

### The detonator: the 1973 oil embargo

The slow-burning inflation of the early 1970s got a violent accelerant in October 1973. In response to Western support for Israel in the Yom Kippur War, the Arab members of OPEC (the Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries, the cartel of major oil producers) declared an **embargo**, cutting oil supply to the United States and its allies. Within months, the price of oil roughly **quadrupled** — from around \$3 a barrel to around \$12.

Oil is not just another commodity. It is the input to almost everything: transportation, manufacturing, plastics, fertilizer, heating, electricity. When the price of oil quadruples, the cost of running the *entire economy* jumps. This is what economists call a **supply shock** — a sudden, externally imposed increase in the cost of producing goods. And a supply shock is uniquely nasty, because it pushes inflation *up* (everything costs more) and growth *down* (the economy can't afford to do as much) at the same time. It is, in other words, a machine for manufacturing stagflation.

The diagram below is the mental model for the whole machine: the monetary setup (Nixon ending the gold link), the policy choice (running money hot, feeding the wage-price spiral), and the supply shock (OPEC) all feed into *high-and-rising inflation* and *weak growth*, which combine into stagflation — and stagflation is the regime that lifts real assets and crushes paper ones.

![Flow diagram of the stagflation engine from Bretton Woods end, loose policy and oil shock into inflation, weak growth, stagflation, real assets up and paper assets down](/imgs/blogs/case-study-1970s-stagflation-commodities-win-2.png)

So the setup, in one sentence: a money system cut loose from gold, run hot by policymakers, then hit by a quadrupling of the world's most important input. Each of those three by itself would have caused inflation. Together, they caused the worst peacetime inflation in modern US history — and a decade-long lesson in which assets survive it.

## The chronology: how the decade actually unfolded

A case study earns its name by telling the story in order, with dates. Stagflation was not a single event; it was a decade-long arc with a beginning (1971), a first violent peak (1973-74), a deceptive lull (mid-decade), and a second, higher peak (1979-80). Let us walk it.

### 1971: the dollar floats, and gold is freed

The decade's defining act, as we saw, was the August 1971 closing of the gold window. To grasp what this did to asset prices, you have to appreciate where gold started: it had been *fixed* at \$35 an ounce for decades. Americans had even been *barred* from owning gold bullion since 1933. So for a whole generation, gold was a sleepy, government-controlled price, not a market.

When the dollar came off gold, the price was no longer pinned. Gold could finally rise to reflect how much the dollar was actually worth — which, after years of over-issuance, was a lot less than \$35-an-ounce implied. The chart below tells the story of the freed gold price across the decade: from ~\$35 in 1971, to ~\$183 by 1974, a dip to ~\$125 in 1976, and then a parabolic run to about \$850 by January 1980. That is roughly a **24x** move over the decade. Gold did nothing more complicated than measure the dollar's loss of value against something the government couldn't print — and it was the single best-performing major asset of the entire era.

![Line chart of gold price per ounce from 1971 to 1980 rising from thirty-five dollars to about eight hundred fifty dollars](/imgs/blogs/case-study-1970s-stagflation-commodities-win-3.png)

It is worth sitting with the size of that move. We cover gold's full mechanism in [gold: money, insurance, or just a rock?](/blog/trading/cross-asset/gold-money-insurance-or-just-a-rock), but the 1970s are its single greatest piece of evidence. Gold pays no dividend, earns no interest, and does nothing useful in a factory. In a normal world, that makes it a mediocre asset. But in a world where the currency itself is being debased, gold's one property — *you cannot print more of it* — becomes the most valuable property an asset can have. The 1970s were that world.

#### Worked example: what \$1,000 in gold did versus \$1,000 in cash

Suppose at the start of 1971, with gold near \$35 an ounce, you put \$1,000 into gold. That buys you about \$1,000 ÷ \$35 ≈ **28.6 ounces**.

Now hold it to January 1980, when gold is near \$850 an ounce. Your 28.6 ounces are worth 28.6 × \$850 ≈ **\$24,300**. Your \$1,000 became roughly **\$24,300** — about a 24x return, matching the price move.

Meanwhile, your neighbor kept their \$1,000 in cash under the mattress. Over that same period, cumulative inflation ran roughly +112%, meaning prices more than doubled. Their \$1,000 of cash still says "\$1,000," but it now buys what about **\$470** bought in 1971 — they lost more than half their purchasing power by doing "nothing." The lesson in one line: in a debasement decade, sitting in cash is not safety, it is a slow guaranteed loss, while the asset you cannot print quietly multiplied.

### 1973-74: the first oil shock and the great bear market

The slow inflation of the early 1970s turned into a crisis with the October 1973 OPEC embargo. As oil quadrupled from ~\$3 to ~\$12 a barrel, CPI inflation accelerated from the low single digits to **~12.3% by 1974**. And the stock market did what stock markets do when inflation and rates surge and growth craters: it collapsed.

From its early-1973 peak to its late-1974 trough, the S&P 500 fell roughly **48%**. This was, at the time, the worst bear market since the Great Depression. The classic explanation people reach for — "stocks fell because the economy was bad" — is only half right and misses the deeper mechanism, which we will unpack in the misconceptions section. The short version: rising inflation forces up the *interest rate* used to value all future cash flows, and a higher discount rate mechanically lowers the present value of every stock and every long bond at once.

We trace the oil mechanism in detail in [energy, oil and gas: the inflation engine](/blog/trading/cross-asset/energy-oil-gas-the-inflation-engine), but the chart below shows the two shocks that bracketed the decade. The first, in 1973-74, took oil from ~\$3 to ~\$12 — a quadrupling. The second, in 1979-80, took it from ~\$15 to ~\$39. Oil was the clearest "real asset wins" story of the decade: it didn't just hold value, it *led* the inflation it helped cause.

![Line chart of crude oil price per barrel from 1972 to 1980 showing two supply-shock spikes](/imgs/blogs/case-study-1970s-stagflation-commodities-win-4.png)

#### Worked example: the 1973-74 bear market on a stock-heavy portfolio

Say you started 1973 with \$10,000 entirely in US stocks — the "stocks are for the long run, just ride it out" approach. Across 1973 and 1974, the S&P 500 fell about 37% on a total-return basis (it fell roughly 14.7% in 1973, then about 26.5% in 1974; combine those and you are down about 37%).

So your \$10,000 became \$10,000 × (1 − 0.37) = **\$6,300** in nominal terms. Already painful. But now apply the ~22% cumulative inflation over those two years: the \$6,300 you have left buys what about \$6,300 ÷ 1.22 ≈ **\$5,160** bought at the start of 1973. In real terms — in terms of what your money can actually purchase — you lost nearly **half** of your wealth in two years. The lesson: a 37% nominal loss is bad, but when inflation is eating ~22% on top of it, the *real* damage to your purchasing power is far worse than the statement suggests.

### Mid-decade: the deceptive lull (1975-77)

After the 1974 trough, the world exhaled. The embargo ended, oil prices stabilized, the economy began to recover from the 1973-75 recession, and inflation cooled — CPI fell back to **~4.9% by 1976**. Stocks rallied hard off the bottom. Gold actually *fell*, from its 1974 high near \$183 down to about **\$125 by 1976**, as the panic eased and the obvious inflation trade looked played out.

This mid-decade lull is one of the most important and underappreciated parts of the case study, because it is exactly the kind of period that lulls investors back into the wrong portfolio. Inflation looked beaten. The "own real assets" thesis looked like yesterday's trade. The temptation to declare the inflation problem solved and pile back into stocks and bonds was enormous — and, as the back half of the decade would prove, completely wrong. The inflationary forces hadn't been fixed; they had merely paused. The wage-price spiral was still embedded, expectations were still elevated, and the monetary discipline of the gold standard was still gone. The fire was banked, not out.

### 1979-80: the second oil shock, the inflation peak, and the gold blow-off

The lull ended in 1979. The Iranian Revolution disrupted oil supply again, and the **second oil shock** sent crude from ~\$15 toward ~\$39 a barrel. Inflation, which had never been truly tamed, came roaring back: CPI hit **~13.3% in 1979** and peaked around **~14.8% in 1980** — the highest sustained inflation in modern US peacetime history.

This second wave broke the back of the "it's transitory" belief. With inflation in the teens and the dollar visibly losing value, money fled into the one asset that had protected purchasing power all decade: gold. The result was a *blow-off top* — a near-vertical, panic-buying surge. Gold rocketed from a few hundred dollars to a peak near **\$850 an ounce in January 1980**. Silver went along for the ride. Commodities of every kind surged. It was the climactic, almost manic, expression of the decade's entire thesis: trust nothing made of paper, own things that are real.

The chart below shows the full inflation wave that drove all of this — the two peaks (around the 1974 and 1980 oil shocks) separated by the deceptive mid-decade dip. This single line is the master variable of the decade. Almost everything that happened to asset prices was downstream of where this line was and which way it was pointing.

![Line chart of US CPI inflation year over year from 1970 to 1982 showing a wave peaking near fifteen percent](/imgs/blogs/case-study-1970s-stagflation-commodities-win-5.png)

It is worth pausing on what this felt like in the real economy, because asset prices were only the financial shadow of a genuinely disorienting decade. The 1973 embargo brought gas lines that snaked around blocks, "odd-even" rationing where you could only fill up on certain days based on your license plate, and gas stations simply running dry. The 1979 shock brought a second round of the same. Inflation in the teens meant the price of a loaf of bread or a tank of gas was visibly different month to month — the kind of environment where people rushed to spend paychecks before they lost value, which only fed the spiral further. Mortgage rates climbed toward the high teens, making homes unaffordable on a financed basis even as the homes themselves (a real asset) held value. This is the texture behind the numbers: a society where the unit of account — the dollar — was visibly failing at its one job of holding value, and where every economic actor was scrambling to convert paper into something real before the paper shrank further. The flight into gold, oil, land and commodities was not an abstract portfolio decision; it was a rational response to watching money melt.

The gold blow-off of January 1980 also marked the *end* of the era, though no one knew it at the time. Within months, a new Federal Reserve chairman named Paul Volcker would do the one thing politicians had refused to do for a decade — raise interest rates high enough to actually break inflation, recession be damned — and the entire trade would reverse. That reversal is its own case study, which we cover in [Volcker 1980 and the great bond bull](/blog/trading/cross-asset/case-study-volcker-1980-and-the-bond-bull). But it is crucial to understand that the bond bull market and the disinflation of the 1980s were *not* a refutation of the 1970s lesson. They were the result of finally administering the cure. While inflation was high and rising — the entire 1970s — real assets won. The moment that condition reversed, so did the trade.

## Why it happened: inflation as the master variable

We have told the story. Now let us extract the mechanism, because a case study without a "why" is just a list of dates. The deep question is this: *why* do high and rising inflation crush paper assets while lifting real ones? The answer comes down to two ideas — how paper assets are valued, and what gives real assets their floor.

### Paper assets are priced by discounting future cash flows — and inflation poisons the discount rate

A stock is a claim on a company's *future* profits. A bond is a claim on a stream of *future* fixed dollar payments. To value either, the market does one thing: it adds up all those future cash flows and "discounts" them back to today's value, using an interest rate. The higher the interest rate used, the *less* those future dollars are worth today. This is the single most important valuation idea in finance, and we develop it fully in [interest rates: the price of money, the master variable](/blog/trading/macro-trading/interest-rates-the-price-of-money-master-variable).

Here is why inflation is poison for this. When inflation rises, two things happen to the discount rate, both bad for paper assets:

1. **Interest rates rise.** Lenders won't lend at 4% when inflation is 12% — they'd lose purchasing power. So they demand higher rates to compensate. As market interest rates climb, the discount rate climbs, and the present value of every future cash flow falls. A long bond promising \$1,000 in twenty years is worth far less today when the discount rate is 12% than when it's 4%.

2. **The future cash flows themselves are worth less in real terms.** A bond's payments are *fixed in nominal dollars*. If it pays \$50 a year, that \$50 is the same number whether inflation is 2% or 12% — but at 12% inflation, that \$50 buys far less each year. The bondholder is locked into receiving dollars that are melting.

Stocks are a more subtle case, and we will treat the "stocks are an inflation hedge" myth carefully below. For now, the key point: in the 1970s, rising inflation drove rising interest rates, which drove down the present value of *both* stocks and bonds at the same time. The thing that is supposed to make a portfolio "diversified" — that stocks and bonds usually move differently — broke, because a single force (rising inflation and rates) was driving both of them down together. This is the *positive stock-bond correlation* regime, and it is exactly why the 60/40 fails in stagflation. We unpack that relationship in [the stock-bond correlation and the 60/40 engine](/blog/trading/cross-asset/stock-bond-correlation-the-60-40-engine).

### Real assets have intrinsic value — a floor that inflation can't erode

Now contrast that with a barrel of oil, an ounce of gold, a bushel of wheat, an acre of farmland. These assets are not promises of future dollars. They *are* something. Their value comes from what they can do or what they are made of, not from a stream of payments denominated in a currency that's being debased.

When inflation rises, the *nominal* price of a real asset rises too — almost by definition, because inflation *is* the general rise in the prices of real things. Oil costs more, gold costs more, food costs more; that's what inflation means. So real assets don't get repriced down by a rising discount rate the way paper claims do — instead, their prices rise *with* the inflation, and often faster, because when investors get scared of inflation they pile in, pushing real-asset prices above the rate of inflation itself.

There is one more layer. The assets that did best in the 1970s — gold and oil especially — had a *supply* constraint that paper assets lack. You cannot print more gold; you cannot conjure more oil. When the currency is being created without limit but the real asset is fixed or constrained in supply, the price of the real asset *in that currency* has only one way to go. This is the deepest version of the lesson: in stagflation, scarcity beats a printing press.

#### Worked example: the same future dollar, valued at 4% versus 12%

Let us make the discount-rate mechanism concrete with one number. Take a simple bond that will pay you exactly \$1,000 ten years from now and nothing in between (a *zero-coupon* bond).

When interest rates are 4%, the value today is \$1,000 ÷ (1.04)^10 ≈ \$1,000 ÷ 1.48 ≈ **\$676**.

Now inflation surges and interest rates rise to 12%. The value today becomes \$1,000 ÷ (1.12)^10 ≈ \$1,000 ÷ 3.11 ≈ **\$322**.

The exact same promise — \$1,000 in ten years — is now worth less than *half* what it was, purely because the discount rate rose. And we haven't even adjusted that \$322 for inflation yet; in real purchasing-power terms the holder did even worse. This is the entire reason long bonds were destroyed in the 1970s: the math of discounting future fixed dollars is merciless when rates climb, and the longer the bond, the more brutal the hit.

### Duration is the hidden variable: why "long" assets suffered most

There is a deeper pattern hiding inside the discount-rate story, and it explains *which* paper assets bled most. The key concept is **duration** — a measure of how sensitive an asset's price is to a change in interest rates, driven mainly by how far in the future its cash flows sit. A bond that pays you back next year has *low* duration: a jump in rates barely dents its price, because you get your money so soon. A bond that pays you back in thirty years has *high* duration: the same jump in rates slashes its price, because all that value is sitting decades away being discounted ever harder.

This is why, in the 1970s, the damage was not uniform. *Long* Treasuries were devastated while *short* T-bills barely flinched — same issuer, same "safety," wildly different outcomes, purely because of duration. And the insight generalizes beyond bonds. A high-multiple growth stock — one priced for big profits far in the future — behaves like a *long-duration* asset, because its value, too, sits years out and gets crushed by a rising discount rate. A cheap, cash-generating value stock or an energy producer with profits *today* behaves like a *short-duration* asset, more resistant to rising rates. So the single rule that explains most of the 1970s cross-asset scoreboard is this: **rising inflation and rates punish long-duration assets — long bonds and expensive growth stocks — and spare or reward short-duration and real assets.** When you hear "the stagflation playbook is short duration," this is what it means: own the things whose value is near, not far. We develop the duration mechanism formally in [the stock-bond correlation and the 60/40 engine](/blog/trading/cross-asset/stock-bond-correlation-the-60-40-engine).

#### Worked example: the real yield, and why bonds bled even when prices held

There is a concept that ties the whole bond story together: the **real yield**, which is roughly the bond's interest rate minus the inflation rate. It tells you whether a bondholder is actually getting ahead of inflation or falling behind.

Suppose in 1975 you hold a long Treasury bond yielding 8%. That 8% sounds healthy. But inflation that year is running near 9%. Your *real* yield is roughly 8% − 9% = **−1%**. You are *losing* purchasing power every year just by holding the bond, even though the coupon arrives reliably and the price hasn't moved. The bond is doing exactly what it promised — paying you 8% in dollars — and you are still getting poorer, because the dollars themselves are shrinking faster than the coupon.

Now run it forward to 1980, with the long yield near 11-12% and inflation near 14-15%. The real yield is *still* negative — around −3% to −4%. For most of the decade, bondholders earned a negative real yield: a guaranteed, slow loss of purchasing power, on the asset everyone called "safe." This is the quiet, second way bonds failed — not just the visible capital losses when prices fell, but the invisible erosion of a negative real yield year after year. The takeaway: in a high-inflation regime, the headline yield on a bond can look fine while the *real* return is a steady drain, which is why short-duration cash that reprices upward beat being locked into a long bond's melting coupon.

## Common misconceptions

Several of the most popular pieces of investing "wisdom" are exactly the beliefs the 1970s demolished. Each one cost real money, and each is corrected by a number from the decade.

### "Stocks are an inflation hedge"

This is the most dangerous myth in the whole study, because it contains a grain of truth that makes it sticky. Over *very* long horizons and at *moderate* inflation, stocks do tend to outpace inflation — companies can raise prices, so their nominal earnings grow with the price level. That logic is real. But in the 1970s, with inflation high and *rising fast*, it failed completely.

The numbers are stark. From its 1968 level around 108, the S&P 500 was at roughly 110 in 1982 — essentially **flat in nominal terms across fourteen years**. The Dow first touched 1,000 in 1972 and did not durably exceed it until 1982 — a *decade* of going nowhere. And "flat nominal" with ~112% cumulative inflation over the period means stocks lost roughly **65% in real terms**. The "hedge" lost two-thirds of your purchasing power.

The figure below makes the trap visible. On the left is what the price screen showed — a nominal index wandering sideways, looking merely boring. On the right is what your money actually did — a near-two-thirds destruction of real value, hidden behind an unchanged number.

![Two-column comparison of nominal flat stock prices on the left versus deeply negative real returns after inflation on the right](/imgs/blogs/case-study-1970s-stagflation-commodities-win-6.png)

Why did the hedge fail? Because of the discount-rate mechanism above. When inflation rises *fast*, the rate used to value stocks rises faster than companies can grow their earnings, so the present value of those earnings falls. Plus, high inflation is *disorderly* — it scrambles business planning, squeezes margins, and raises uncertainty, which investors hate and pay less for. Stocks are an inflation hedge only when inflation is mild and stable. When inflation is the *defining problem*, stocks are a victim, not a hedge.

### "Bonds are always safe"

Bonds are called "fixed income" and "safe" so reflexively that people forget *what* they're safe from. A government bond protects you from *default* (the government will pay you the dollars it promised) and tends to do well in *recessions* and *deflation*. It does **not** protect you from inflation — in fact, inflation is its kryptonite, because the bond's payments are fixed in dollars that are losing value, and rising rates crush its price.

In the 1970s this played out exactly as the math demands. Long Treasury yields rose through the decade toward **11-12% by 1980**. And when yields rise, existing bond prices fall — the longer the bond, the harder the fall. Across the 1973-74 window, long Treasuries managed only about **+4% nominal**, against ~22% cumulative inflation — a sharp *real* loss. Over the full decade, holding long bonds was one of the worst things you could do. "Safe" depends entirely on *what regime you are in*. In a deflationary recession, bonds are a fortress. In stagflation, they are a trap.

### "If stocks and bonds both fall, just hold cash"

Cash *did* better than stocks or bonds in the 1970s — short-term Treasury bills, which reprice upward as rates rise, roughly kept pace with inflation, so you didn't lose much in real terms. That makes cash a legitimate *defensive* holding in stagflation, far better than long bonds. But "roughly kept pace" is the ceiling. Cash didn't *win*; it survived. The assets that actually *gained* real wealth — gold, oil, commodities — required you to leave the safety of cash and own something real. Cash is the place to hide; real assets are the place to win.

### "Commodities and gold are too volatile to own"

The flip side of the inflation-hedge myth is the belief that real assets are too wild to hold — that gold and commodities swing too violently to be a serious part of a portfolio. It is true that they *are* volatile: gold fell from ~\$183 in 1974 to ~\$125 in 1976 (a ~32% drop) right in the middle of its great decade, and commodities lurch around in ways that can be stomach-churning in isolation. But this misses the entire point of *correlation*. The reason to own a small sleeve of real assets is not that they're smooth — it's that they tend to *zig when paper assets zag* in exactly the regime where you need it most. A volatile asset that rises when everything else falls is not a risk to a portfolio; it is insurance. The mid-decade gold dip looks scary in isolation, but an investor holding gold *as a hedge* alongside stocks and bonds still came out vastly ahead over the decade, because the years gold soared were the same years the rest of the portfolio was bleeding. Judging a hedge by its standalone volatility is like judging a fire extinguisher by how much it weighs — you're measuring the wrong thing.

### "The 1970s can't happen again — we understand inflation now"

This is the comforting story, and it is half-true. Central banks did learn the lesson: the Volcker Fed proved that determined rate hikes can break inflation, and the credibility built since has kept inflation expectations anchored for decades. But "we understand it" is not the same as "it can't recur." The ingredients of the 1970s — large deficits, loose money, and a supply shock to a critical input (energy, food, or supply chains) — are not extinct. The 2021-2022 inflation spike, where commodities were the only major asset class with a positive year while stocks fell ~18% and bonds fell ~13%, was a sharp, compressed echo of the same dynamics. The right posture is not "it's impossible" but "I know what it looks like and I know what to own if it comes."

## How it played out across assets: the cross-asset scoreboard

Let us now lay the whole decade out as a cross-asset scoreboard — the play-by-play, asset by asset, with the numbers. This is the heart of the case study: not just *that* real assets won, but *by how much*, against *what* the paper assets did.

### Gold: the asset of the decade

Gold was the standout. From the freed price of ~\$35 in 1971 to the ~\$850 blow-off peak in January 1980, gold ran roughly **24x**. Across the concentrated 1973-74 stagflation window alone, gold compounded about **+210%** (roughly +73% in 1973 and +66% in 1974). Gold did exactly one job — measure the dollar's debasement — and it did it better than any other asset. No dividend, no yield, no utility, and yet the single best place to have been.

### Oil and energy: the inflation engine

Oil was the second great winner, and uniquely it both *caused* and *captured* the inflation. Across the 1973 embargo, crude roughly quadrupled (~\$3 → ~\$12); across the 1979-80 second shock, it more than doubled again (~\$15 → ~\$39). Energy producers — the companies sitting on oil and gas reserves — were among the few equities that thrived, because the value of what they owned was soaring. This is the energy-equity linkage in its purest form: when the input to the economy is the thing going up, owning the input (or the companies that own it) is how you stay ahead.

### Broad commodities: the real-asset complex

It wasn't just gold and oil. The broad commodity complex — industrial metals, agricultural goods, energy — all rose as inflation accelerated. Commodities are, almost by definition, the assets whose prices *are* inflation, so a decade of rising inflation was a decade of rising commodities. In the 1973-74 window, the commodity surge ran on the order of **+300%** for oil and very strong gains across the complex. This is why a commodity sleeve is the textbook stagflation diversifier.

### Stocks: flat nominal, devastating real

US equities were the great disappointment. Nominally roughly flat from 1968 to 1982; in real terms down about **65%**. The 1973-74 bear took the S&P down ~48% peak-to-trough. An investor who "stayed the course" in stocks spent fourteen years going nowhere on the screen and losing two-thirds of their purchasing power underneath it.

Within equities, the dispersion is instructive — it is the *same* short-versus-long-duration logic from the discount-rate section, playing out inside the stock market. At the start of the 1970s, the most beloved stocks were the **"Nifty Fifty"** — a group of blue-chip growth darlings (think the leading consumer and technology names of the era) trading at extreme valuations, sometimes 40, 50, even 80 times earnings. These were the *long-duration* equities: priced for profits far in the future, exquisitely sensitive to a rising discount rate. When inflation and rates surged, the Nifty Fifty were among the worst casualties, many falling 50-80% from peak. Meanwhile, **energy producers and hard-asset companies** — sitting on oil, gas, metals, and timber whose value was soaring — were among the few equities that thrived. The index masked this: the cap-weighted S&P went sideways while its expensive components collapsed and its real-asset components rose. The lesson for an investor is that "own stocks" was too blunt; *which* stocks mattered enormously, and the rule was the same as everywhere else in the decade — short duration and real assets won, long duration and paper promises lost.

### Bonds: destroyed by rising yields

Long Treasuries were repriced down relentlessly as yields climbed toward 11-12%. They eked out small nominal gains in some windows (~+4% across 1973-74) but lost badly in real terms throughout. The "safe" asset was, for this regime, one of the most dangerous places to be.

### Cash: barely kept up

Short T-bills, repricing higher with rates, roughly tracked inflation — a real return near zero. Not a win, but a survival. Cash was the right place to *hide* if you didn't want to own real assets; it was not a place to *grow* wealth.

#### Worked example: a \$10,000 60/40 portfolio versus a real-asset tilt, 1973-74

Let us put the whole scoreboard into one head-to-head, using the 1973-74 stagflation window. Take \$10,000 at the start of 1973.

**Portfolio A — the classic 60/40 (60% stocks, 40% bonds):**
- Stocks: \$6,000 × (1 − 0.37) → a loss of about **−\$2,220**, leaving \$3,780.
- Bonds: \$4,000 × (1 + 0.04) → a gain of about **+\$160**, leaving \$4,160.
- Total: \$3,780 + \$4,160 = **\$7,940**, a nominal loss of about −\$2,060.
- Now apply ~22% cumulative inflation: \$7,940 ÷ 1.22 ≈ **\$6,510** of 1973 purchasing power — a *real* loss of roughly **−\$3,490**, or about a third of your wealth gone.

**Portfolio B — same \$10,000 but with a real-asset sleeve (say \$5,000 stocks, \$3,000 gold, \$2,000 commodities):**
- Stocks: \$5,000 × (1 − 0.37) → −\$1,850, leaving \$3,150.
- Gold: \$3,000 × (1 + 2.10) → a gain of **+\$6,300**, leaving \$9,300.
- Commodities: \$2,000, up sharply across the embargo — even a conservative +100% adds **+\$2,000**, leaving \$4,000.
- Total: \$3,150 + \$9,300 + \$4,000 = **\$16,450** nominal.
- Even after ~22% inflation: \$16,450 ÷ 1.22 ≈ **\$13,480** of 1973 purchasing power — a *real gain* of roughly **+\$3,480**.

The two portfolios started identical. Two years later, the 60/40 had lost about a third of its real value while the real-asset tilt had *gained* about a third — a swing of roughly \$7,000 on a \$10,000 stake. The takeaway in one line: the gold and commodity sleeve didn't just cushion the equity loss, it *swamped* it, because in stagflation the real assets do the heavy lifting while the paper assets bleed.

## The lessons: the stagflation playbook

A case study is only worth the time if it changes what you would *do*. So here is the playbook the 1970s wrote — what to own, what to avoid, and what tells you you're wrong, if a high-and-rising-inflation, weak-growth regime ever returns.

### Step 1: recognize the regime before you trade it

Stagflation has a signature, and the decade taught us to read it. You are likely in (or heading into) stagflation when: **inflation is high and still accelerating** (CPI rising, not just elevated); **growth is weak or rolling over** (slowing activity, rising unemployment); and there's often a **supply shock** in the mix (a spike in energy, food, or a critical input). The master variable to watch is the *direction of inflation*. The 1970s reward came not from being in stagflation but from owning the right assets *while inflation was rising* — and the trade reversed the moment inflation was decisively broken. This is the late-cycle / overheat-into-contraction part of the business cycle; we map it in [late cycle and overheat: when real assets win](/blog/trading/cross-asset/late-cycle-overheat-when-real-assets-win).

The signs are not subtle once you know to look for them, but they are easy to *dismiss*, because each one individually has an innocent explanation. Rising inflation gets called "transitory." Weak growth gets called "a soft patch." A supply shock gets called "a one-off." The mistake the 1970s punished was treating each signal in isolation and waiting for confirmation that never came in a comfortable form. The discipline the decade teaches is to take the *combination* seriously early — to notice when inflation is broadening (not just energy, but services and wages), when it's becoming *embedded in expectations* (people demanding raises, businesses raising prices preemptively), and when the central bank is *behind the curve* (rates well below inflation, real yields deeply negative). When those three line up, you are in the regime, and the cost of waiting for certainty is exactly the real losses the scoreboard above documents.

We saw a compressed, modern version of this in 2021-2022. Inflation surged from supply-chain disruption and an energy spike (the war in Ukraine sent oil and gas soaring), growth slowed, and for a while the Fed was visibly behind the curve. The cross-asset result rhymed with the 1970s: in 2022, the S&P 500 fell about −18%, US bonds fell about −13% (their worst year in modern history), the 60/40 fell about −16% — and **commodities were the only major asset class with a positive year**, up about +16%. It was a sharp, single-year echo of the decade-long lesson: when inflation is the driver, real assets are the diversifier and the 60/40 is exposed. That recent rhyme is the best evidence that the 1970s playbook is not a museum piece.

### Step 2: own the winners — real assets and short duration

The decade's winners were consistent: **commodities** (the broad complex, because their prices *are* inflation), **energy** (oil, gas, and the producers — the input the whole economy needs), **gold** (the purest debasement hedge, the thing you can't print), and **short-duration / floating-rate instruments** (cash and short bills that reprice upward with rates instead of being locked into melting dollars). Within equities, the tilt is toward *real-asset producers* and *value* — companies with pricing power and hard assets — and away from expensive growth.

### Step 3: underweight the losers — long bonds and expensive paper

The decade's losers were equally consistent: **long-duration government bonds** (destroyed by rising yields and eroded by inflation — the longer the bond, the worse the hit) and **expensive, long-duration equities** (high-multiple growth stocks, whose value sits far in the future and gets crushed by a rising discount rate). The single most important takeaway is structural: **the 60/40 is the wrong portfolio for stagflation.** Its diversification depends on stocks and bonds moving differently, but in stagflation a single force — rising inflation and rates — drives both down together. The diversifier you need is not the "40" in bonds; it is commodities, energy, gold and cash.

### Step 4: know what would tell you you're wrong

Every thesis needs an exit, and the 1970s gave us a clean one. The real-asset trade works *while inflation is high and rising*. It stops working — violently — when a credible central bank raises real interest rates high enough to actually break inflation. That is precisely what happened when Volcker pushed rates into the high teens in 1980-81: gold collapsed from its \$850 peak, commodities rolled over, and a forty-year bull market in bonds began. So the invalidation signal is *decisive disinflation engineered by a determined central bank*. When real yields turn sharply positive and inflation is clearly broken, the stagflation playbook is over and you rotate back toward bonds and growth. (That reversal is the subject of [Volcker 1980 and the great bond bull](/blog/trading/cross-asset/case-study-volcker-1980-and-the-bond-bull).)

### How big a real-asset sleeve, in practice

The 1970s show *what* to own, but they raise the obvious follow-up: how much? This is where humility matters, because the answer depends on how confident you are in the regime read and how much pain you can tolerate if you're early or wrong. A few principles fall out of the case study rather than out of any single magic number. First, a *little goes a long way*, because real assets are so explosive in the right regime — recall the worked example where a \$3,000 gold sleeve and a \$2,000 commodity sleeve, just half the portfolio, turned a real loss into a real gain. You do not need to bet the whole portfolio on stagflation to be protected from it; a sleeve on the order of 10-25% in commodities, energy and gold is enough to change the *character* of a portfolio's stagflation outcome. Second, the sleeve is *insurance*, so accept that it will look like a drag in the long stretches when inflation is falling — that's the premium you pay for protection in the regime that breaks the 60/40. Third, *short duration is cheaper insurance than it looks*: simply holding more cash and short bills, and less in long bonds, costs you very little in a benign world and protects you enormously in a rising-rate one. The art is owning enough of the insurance to matter without owning so much that you're making a concentrated macro bet — because, again, stagflation is a *possible* regime to hedge, not a certain one to wager everything on.

### A note on sizing and humility

None of this is a recommendation to bet a portfolio on stagflation returning — it is educational history, not advice, and stagflation is a *regime* you tilt toward when the signs are present, not a permanent posture. For most of modern history, the opposite regime has prevailed: disinflation, falling rates, and a 60/40 that worked beautifully. The point of studying the 1970s is not to predict its return but to *recognize it if it comes* and to understand, in your bones, that the "safe" balanced portfolio has a specific enemy. Real assets are the insurance against that enemy. You hold a modest amount of that insurance precisely *because* you can't be sure when — or whether — you'll need it.

The deepest lesson of the decade is a humbling one for anyone who thinks any single portfolio is "safe" forever. The 60/40 is not safe; it is *adapted* — beautifully suited to a falling-inflation world and dangerously exposed to a rising-inflation one. The 1970s were the rising-inflation world, and in it, the prudent, diversified, blue-chip portfolio lost a third of its real value while a sleeve of gold and commodities you could have bought at the corner coin shop multiplied. That is the whole case study in one sentence: **when inflation is the enemy, own things that are real.**
