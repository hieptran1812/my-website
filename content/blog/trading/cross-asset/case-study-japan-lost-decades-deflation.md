In December 1989, a Japanese investor had every reason to feel like a genius. The stock market had risen for years; the Nikkei 225 — the headline index of Japan's biggest companies — was about to print an all-time high near **38,915**. Property was worth a fortune; the land under central Tokyo was so valuable that, by one famous back-of-the-envelope calculation, the grounds of the Imperial Palace alone were worth more than the entire state of California. Japan was the future. Its companies were buying trophy assets around the world. Banks lent freely against ever-rising collateral. To own Japanese stocks and Japanese real estate in 1989 felt like the safest, smartest thing in the world.

It was the single worst moment in modern financial history to feel that way. Over the next nineteen years the Nikkei would fall roughly **82%**, to about **7,055** at its March 2009 low. It would not reclaim that 1989 high until **February 2024** — about **34 years** later, longer than many working careers. A person who bought the index at the top and simply held it was underwater, in plain nominal terms, for over three decades. "Stocks always recover" is true in the very long run; it is *cold comfort* if "the long run" outlasts your career, your mortgage, and a good part of your retirement.

The figure below is the whole story in one line. It is the thesis of this post: equities, the asset everyone trusts to recover, can take a generation to do so — and the rest of the post is about what *did* work while they didn't.

![Line chart of the Nikkei 225 from its 1989 peak down to the 2009 low and back to a new high in 2024](/imgs/blogs/case-study-japan-lost-decades-deflation-1.png)

Here is the twist that makes this case study so valuable. While Japanese equities were dead money, a quiet set of "boring" assets did beautifully. Japanese **government bonds** (JGBs) went on one of the great multi-decade bull markets in financial history, as their yields fell from about 8% toward zero. **Cash** — the asset everyone loves to mock as "trash" — earned a *positive real return*, because gently falling prices meant the same yen bought a little more each year. The lesson is the mirror image of the 1970s, where high inflation crushed bonds and cash and lifted commodities. In Japan, *deflation* did the opposite: it rewarded the holders of money and fixed income and punished the owners of growth and hard assets. By the end of this post, you will be able to look at a world of bursting credit bubbles and falling prices and say, with real historical grounding, "this is deflation — I want long government bonds, cash, and quality, and I do not want to lean on equities and real estate to save me." That single read is the most expensive lesson Japan taught, and it taught it over more than three decades.

This is educational, not financial advice. We are studying a historical regime and the mechanisms behind it, not recommending what anyone should buy today.

## Foundations: the setup — how the great deflation happened

Before we can trade the lesson, we have to understand the machine. Deflation did not fall from the sky; it was the predictable result of a specific sequence — an enormous credit-fueled bubble, a sharp tightening to pop it, and then a slow, grinding process of paying down debt that quietly pushed prices lower for two decades. Let us build the setup from zero, assuming you have never read a word about Japan's bubble, the Bank of Japan, or balance-sheet recessions.

### First, the words you need: deflation, real, nominal, and duration

Four terms run through this entire post, so let us pin them down immediately.

A **nominal** number is the raw figure on the statement — the dollar (or yen) amount, before adjusting for anything. If your savings account goes from \$10,000 to \$10,100, your *nominal* return is +1%.

A **real** number adjusts for the change in prices — it tells you what your money can actually *buy*. Real return is roughly the nominal return minus the inflation rate. The central fact of this post is that in Japan, inflation was often *negative* — prices were *falling*. When prices fall, the subtraction flips: a 1% nominal return in a year when prices fell 1% is a **+2% real return**. This is the engine of the whole story. (We go deep on this distinction in [real vs nominal: inflation, real yields, and the master signal](/blog/trading/macro-trading/real-vs-nominal-inflation-real-yields-master-signal).)

**Inflation** is the rate at which the general price level rises, usually read off the *Consumer Price Index* (CPI) — the cost of a typical basket of goods. **Deflation** is simply *negative inflation*: the basket gets *cheaper* over time. A normal economy targets about +2% inflation. Japan, for much of two decades, ran somewhere between roughly **−1% and +0.5%** — mild but persistent deflation. That word "mild" matters: this was not a violent 1930s-style price collapse. It was a slow, grinding drift, just enough to flip the math of saving and investing on its head.

**Duration** is the one bond term you need. It measures how sensitive a bond's price is to a change in interest rates. A bond with a duration of 8 means that if its yield falls by 1 percentage point, its price rises by roughly 8%. The key fact: bond *prices move opposite to yields*. When yields fall, existing bonds — which pay the old, higher coupons — become more valuable, so their prices rise. Japan's decades-long fall in yields was therefore a decades-long *rise* in bond prices. We lean on this throughout, and it is developed fully in [government bonds: the risk-free anchor and duration](/blog/trading/cross-asset/government-bonds-the-risk-free-anchor-and-duration).

### The 1980s bubble: how valuations got absurd

To understand the crash, you have to appreciate how far prices had run. Through the 1980s, Japan had a credit boom of historic proportions. The economy was genuinely strong — Japanese manufacturing was the envy of the world — but on top of that real strength sat a tower of speculation. Banks, flush with deposits and eager to lend, extended enormous loans against stock and property collateral. As asset prices rose, the collateral was worth more, which justified more lending, which pushed asset prices higher still. It was a self-reinforcing loop, and like all such loops it felt like permanent prosperity right up until it didn't.

The numbers from the top are genuinely hard to believe. The Nikkei 225 rose from around 6,000 in the early 1980s to its peak near **38,915** at the end of 1989 — more than a sixfold increase. Valuations detached from any sane relationship to earnings: many Japanese stocks traded at price-to-earnings ratios (the price of a share divided by the company's per-share profit) of 50, 60, even higher, where a more normal number is the mid-teens. Property was wilder still. Tokyo land prices reached levels where the implied value of all the land in Japan — a country smaller than California — exceeded the value of all the land in the United States several times over. The line everyone repeated was that the grounds of the Imperial Palace were worth more than all the real estate in California. Whether the exact comparison held to the dollar, the *spirit* of it was correct: prices had reached a point that only made sense if they kept rising forever.

The fuel for all of this was credit, and the credit came from a banking system with very particular incentives. Japanese banks held large equity stakes in the very companies they lent to — a web of cross-shareholdings that meant rising stock prices made the banks themselves look better capitalized, which let them lend even more. Land was the universal collateral; as long as land prices rose, a loan against land was "safe," and the bank could book the rising collateral as ever-growing security. This is the part of a bubble that ordinary investors rarely see but that determines how violent the bust will be: the leverage was not just in individual speculators' accounts, it was wired into the core of the financial system. When the collateral finally fell, it did not just hurt the speculators — it hollowed out the banks, and a wounded banking system that cannot lend is how a stock-market crash metastasizes into a twenty-year economic stagnation.

#### Worked example: what a sixfold run does to valuation

Suppose a Japanese company earns a steady \$2 per share, year after year. In a normal market, investors might pay about 15 times earnings for that — a share price of \$30. That implies an *earnings yield* (the inverse of the P/E ratio: earnings divided by price) of \$2 / \$30 = **6.7%**, a reasonable return on the price you pay.

Now run the bubble. The same \$2-per-share company sees its stock bid up to a P/E of 60, so the price is \$2 × 60 = \$120. The earnings yield collapses to \$2 / \$120 = **1.7%**. You are paying \$120 for a \$2 stream of profit. For that to be a good deal, you need either the profits to grow explosively or the next buyer to pay even more. When neither happens, the only way the price comes back to earth is to *fall* — and from \$120 back toward \$30 is a drop of **−75%**. That is the arithmetic of every bubble: the higher the multiple goes, the further the price has to fall just to return to normal. Japan's index P/E was bubble-wide, so the whole market had this much air underneath it.

The one-sentence intuition: a sky-high valuation is not a cushion, it is a *trapdoor* — the more you pay for a dollar of earnings, the further the price has to fall to get back to sane.

### The detonator: the Bank of Japan tightens, 1989-1990

Every bubble needs a pin. Japan's was the central bank. By the late 1980s, policymakers at the **Bank of Japan** (the BOJ — Japan's central bank, the equivalent of the US Federal Reserve) were alarmed by the asset mania. Stock and land prices looked dangerously detached from reality, and the BOJ decided to let the air out by raising interest rates. Between 1989 and 1990 it pushed its policy rate up sharply, from 2.5% to 6%.

Higher interest rates are poison to a bubble built on borrowed money. They raise the cost of carrying leveraged positions, they make the safe alternative (just holding cash or bonds) more attractive, and they slow the credit creation that was feeding the loop. The speculative tower had been built on the assumption of endlessly cheap money and endlessly rising collateral; the BOJ removed the first pillar, and the second followed. At the very end of December 1989 the Nikkei made its high, and as 1990 opened it began to fall — first in a way that looked like a normal correction, then in a way that did not stop.

The crucial point for an allocator is that this was not a one-off crash that bounced. The burst of the bubble set off a *chain reaction* that played out over decades, because the problem was not just that prices were too high — it was that the entire economy was loaded with debt that had been taken on against those now-falling prices. That is what turns a crash into a lost decade, and it is the topic of the next section.

### Why a bubble burst becomes a decade of deflation

Here is the mechanism that ties everything together, and it is the single most important idea in this post. When a debt-fueled bubble bursts, asset prices fall but the *debt does not*. A company that borrowed \$100 to buy land worth \$120 still owes \$100 even after the land falls to \$60. Its assets have collapsed but its liabilities are fixed. In the language used by the economist Richard Koo, who studied Japan closely, the borrower is now technically insolvent on a mark-to-market basis — and its only rational response is to stop borrowing, stop spending on expansion, and devote every spare yen to *paying down debt* to repair its balance sheet. Koo called the result a **balance-sheet recession**.

Now run that across an entire economy at once. If every company and household is simultaneously trying to pay down debt and rebuild savings, then nobody is borrowing and nobody is spending. Demand collapses — not because people are poor, but because they are all rationally hoarding to repair their finances. With too few buyers chasing the available goods, prices stop rising and begin to *fall*. That is deflation. And deflation makes the trap worse: when prices and wages fall, the *real* burden of a fixed debt grows heavier (you owe the same yen but each yen is harder to earn), which makes borrowers pay down debt even more aggressively, which crushes demand further. The loop feeds on itself, slowly, for years.

The diagram below is the mental model for the whole machine: a bubble built on debt bursts, borrowers are left underwater, everyone deleverages at once, demand and prices fall, and the result is a deflation that rewards the holders of cash and bonds while it punishes the owners of equities and real estate.

![Flow diagram of the deflation engine from bubble burst through deleveraging and falling demand into deflation, with cash and bonds winning and equities and real estate losing](/imgs/blogs/case-study-japan-lost-decades-deflation-2.png)

So the setup, in one sentence: an enormous credit bubble, popped by a central bank, left a whole economy underwater on its debts, and the collective scramble to pay those debts down drained demand for two decades and turned mild deflation into a self-sustaining regime. Everything that follows is what that regime did to each asset class.

## The chronology: how the lost decades actually unfolded

A case study earns its name by telling the story in order, with dates. Japan's deflation was not a single event; it was a multi-decade arc with a peak (1989), a long grind down (the 1990s), a deflationary trough and policy experiments (1999-2001), a false dawn or two, a second crushing low (2009), and a very long, very slow road back to the old high (2024). Let us walk it.

### 1989: the peak

The decade's defining moment came on **December 29, 1989**, the last trading day of the year, when the Nikkei 225 closed near its all-time high of about **38,915**. Sentiment was euphoric. Brokerage houses published forecasts of the index reaching 60,000 or higher within a few years. The consensus was not that the market was a bubble; the consensus was that Japan had figured out a superior model of capitalism and that its asset prices reflected genuine, permanent superiority. The handful of voices warning about valuations were dismissed as not understanding the new era. This is what a top feels like from the inside: not fear, but confidence.

### The 1990s: the slow grind down

The fall did not arrive as a single dramatic crash, the way 1929 or 2008 did. It came as a long, grinding decline punctuated by hopeful rallies that failed. Through 1990 the Nikkei dropped sharply, ending the year down roughly 40%. By 1992 it was around 17,000 — already less than half its peak. Then came years of the cruelest pattern in markets: the index would rally 20% or 30%, every commentator would declare the bottom was in, and then it would roll over and make new lows. Land prices fell too, year after year, with no spectacular crash — just a relentless drift lower that wiped out the collateral underpinning the banking system.

Behind the price action, the balance-sheet recession was doing its slow work. Banks were saddled with bad loans they were slow to recognize; "zombie" companies — firms kept alive by lenders unwilling to admit losses — clogged the economy; and the broad price level tipped over into outright deflation in the late 1990s. The CPI figure below tells that part of the story: from the late 1990s onward, Japan's headline inflation rate spent year after year sitting at or below zero.

![Bar chart of Japan annual CPI inflation showing many years at or below zero from the late 1990s through the 2010s](/imgs/blogs/case-study-japan-lost-decades-deflation-4.png)

Notice the shape of that chart. There is no dramatic crash in prices — no year where the basket fell 10%. Instead there is a long string of years bunched around zero, dipping a little below, poking a little above, almost never reaching the +2% that a healthy economy targets (the one apparent exception, 2014, was an artifact of a consumption-tax hike, not real demand). This is what *persistent mild deflation* looks like, and it is precisely its mildness that makes it so dangerous: it is gentle enough that policymakers keep believing recovery is around the corner, yet relentless enough that it reshapes every investment return for two decades.

### 1999 and 2001: the BOJ pioneers ZIRP and QE

By the late 1990s the Bank of Japan had cut its policy interest rate essentially to zero and could cut no further — you cannot, in the normal course of things, charge a negative interest rate to ordinary savers. The economy was still weak and prices were still falling. So in 1999 the BOJ did something no major central bank had done before: it formally committed to a **zero interest rate policy**, which the world came to call **ZIRP**, promising to hold rates at zero for as long as deflationary pressure persisted.

When even that was not enough, in 2001 the BOJ went a step further and pioneered **quantitative easing** — usually shortened to **QE** — the practice of a central bank creating new money to buy bonds and other assets in bulk, in order to push down longer-term interest rates and pump cash into the banking system once the short-term rate is already at zero. (We explain the mechanics fully in [quantitative easing explained: printing money](/blog/trading/finance/quantitative-easing-explained-printing-money).)

The historic point is the timing. Japan invented both ZIRP and QE years before the rest of the world had any need for them. When the 2008 financial crisis hit the United States and Europe, the Federal Reserve and the European Central Bank reached for exactly these tools — zero rates, then asset purchases — and they were copying a playbook the BOJ had written nearly a decade earlier. Japan was the laboratory of deflation, and its central bank ran the experiments first. For an investor, the lesson embedded here is that *deflation forces interest rates to the floor and keeps them there*, which is the entire reason bonds did so well — a point we will quantify shortly.

It is worth dwelling on *why* a central bank gets stuck at zero, because it explains the durability of the bond bull market. In a normal cycle, a central bank cuts rates in a recession and raises them in a recovery, so yields oscillate and a bond bull market is a temporary, cyclical thing. In a balance-sheet recession, the private sector is determined to pay down debt regardless of how cheap money is — cutting the rate to zero does not make a deeply indebted firm want to borrow more. So the usual transmission breaks: the central bank can offer free money and find no takers. That is why ZIRP, once it arrived, *stayed*. It was not a one-year emergency setting that would be reversed at the first sign of recovery; it was a multi-decade fixture, because the underlying deleveraging took multi-decades to complete. For a bondholder, a rate that is pinned to the floor and stays there is the dream scenario: you keep collecting the coupon and you never suffer the capital losses that come when a central bank hikes. The very feature that made Japan's economy so frustrating — that nothing the BOJ did seemed to restart growth — was the feature that made its bonds so reliably profitable.

### 2003 and 2009: the bottoms

The Nikkei found a major low around **7,800 in 2003**, in the wash-out that followed the global dot-com bust — already a fall of about 80% from the peak, fourteen years on. It then rallied for a few years on the back of a global expansion, reaching around 18,000 by 2007, and for a moment it looked like the lost decade might finally be ending. Then the 2008 global financial crisis arrived, and the Nikkei was dragged down with every other market to a fresh low of about **7,055 in March 2009** — the deepest point of the entire saga, roughly **82% below** the 1989 peak, nineteen years later. A generation after the top, an index investor had nothing to show but losses.

### 2024: the reclaim, after ~34 years

The road back was long and is its own story — involving a much later, far more aggressive round of stimulus (the "Abenomics" program of the 2010s), a weaker yen, corporate reform, and a global bull market. But the single fact an allocator must absorb is the endpoint. The Nikkei 225 did not surpass its December 1989 high until **February 2024** — about **34 years** after the top — before pressing on toward 40,000. A buy-and-hold investor who bought the index at the 1989 peak and never sold spent roughly **34 years underwater in nominal terms**. Even counting reinvested dividends, which softened the blow, it was a generation of dead money. "Stocks always recover" is technically true here. It is also the most expensive truth in the book, because for most investors a 34-year wait is indistinguishable from a permanent loss.

## The cross-asset scoreboard in deflation

Now we get to the part that makes this a *cross-asset* case study rather than just a sad story about the stock market. The whole point of studying Japan is that while one asset (equities) was a disaster, the regime quietly rewarded a completely different set of holdings. The scoreboard in deflation looks almost nothing like the scoreboard in the inflationary 1970s. Let us go asset by asset.

### Government bonds (JGBs): the trade of the decades

The clear winner of Japan's lost decades was the most boring asset on the board: long-dated Japanese government bonds. To see why, you have to combine two sources of return.

The first is the **coupon** — the fixed interest payment a bond makes. In 1990, a 10-year JGB yielded about **8%**. An investor who bought it locked in an 8% annual payment in yen — and in a country where prices were about to *fall*, that 8% nominal coupon was an even larger return in real, purchasing-power terms.

The second source is **capital gains from falling yields**. Remember duration: when yields fall, the prices of existing bonds rise. Over the following decades, the 10-year JGB yield fell from about 8% to roughly 1.5% in the 2000s and then all the way to about **0% by 2016**, briefly turning *negative*. Every leg down in yields was a capital gain for whoever already held the higher-yielding bonds. The chart below is that great trade in one line.

![Line chart of Japan's 10-year government bond yield falling from about 8 percent in 1990 toward zero by 2016](/imgs/blogs/case-study-japan-lost-decades-deflation-3.png)

Read that chart as a bond investor, not a saver. Every point on that downward slope is money in the pocket of someone who already owned the bonds. A 30-year JGB bought in 1990 paid a fat coupon for decades *and* gained in price as yields collapsed. While Nikkei investors were losing 82% of their money, JGB investors were compounding steadily, year after year, on the single asset everyone assumed was too dull to bother with. This is the mechanism behind why government bonds are the anchor of a deflation portfolio, and it is developed in full in [government bonds: the risk-free anchor and duration](/blog/trading/cross-asset/government-bonds-the-risk-free-anchor-and-duration).

#### Worked example: the JGB total return versus the Nikkei

Let us put \$10,000 to work at the end of 1989 two ways and follow it.

**Path A — the Nikkei.** You buy the index at the 1989 peak. By the March 2009 low it is down about 82%, so your \$10,000 is worth roughly \$10,000 × (1 − 0.82) = **\$1,800**. It does not return to \$10,000 in nominal terms until 2024 — about 34 years of zero nominal price return. Dividends help, but the *price* of the asset gave you nothing for a generation.

**Path B — the long JGB.** You buy a long-dated government bond yielding about 8%. Forget capital gains for a moment and just take the coupon: 8% a year, reinvested, roughly doubles your money every nine years (the rule of 72: 72 ÷ 8 ≈ 9 years to double). Over, say, 18 years of high-then-falling coupons your money would have multiplied several times over from the coupon stream alone — and on top of that, as the yield fell from 8% toward 0%, the *price* of the bonds you held kept rising, adding capital gains to the coupons. The boring asset compounded; the exciting asset went to zero and back.

The one-sentence intuition: in deflation the prize goes to whoever locked in a fixed stream of money early and let falling yields lift its price — the exact opposite of who wins in inflation.

#### Worked example: how much a falling yield lifts a bond's price

It is worth seeing the *capital-gain* half of the JGB trade with real numbers, because most people underestimate it. Take a 10-year government bond with a duration of about 8 — meaning its price moves roughly 8% for every 1 percentage point move in yield.

In the early 1990s, the JGB yield fell from about 8% to about 5% over a few years — a drop of 3 percentage points. The capital gain on a bond you already held is roughly duration × yield change = 8 × 3% = **+24%** in price, *on top of* the 8% coupons you were collecting along the way. Then the yield ground from 5% down to about 1.5% by the 2000s — another 3.5-point drop, for roughly another 8 × 3.5% = **+28%** in price. And later it fell from 1.5% toward 0%, adding still more.

Stack those legs together with the coupons and you have an asset that delivered high-single-digit-percent returns year after year, with low volatility, while equities lost 82%. The one-sentence intuition: a falling yield is not just "lower future income" — for whoever already owns the bond, each step down is a *capital gain*, and Japan delivered three decades of them.

### Cash: positive real returns when prices fall

Here is the result that most surprises people raised on the slogan "cash is trash." In Japan, cash was *good*. The reason is the real-versus-nominal flip we set up in Foundations. A Japanese saver who simply held yen in the bank earned almost no nominal interest — call it 0%. But if prices were falling by, say, 1% a year, then those static yen bought 1% *more* goods each year. The saver's **real return was about +1%** for doing nothing and taking no risk.

Compare that to the equity holder, who took enormous risk and lost 82% of their money. Cash beat stocks for two decades not because cash did anything clever, but because gentle deflation handed it a free positive real return while stocks bled. This is the single most counterintuitive lesson of the regime, and it is why a whole post in this series is devoted to it: [cash and money markets: the underrated asset](/blog/trading/cross-asset/cash-money-markets-the-underrated-asset).

#### Worked example: cash beats stocks in deflation

Picture two Japanese savers, each starting with \$10,000 at the end of 1989, and run them out twenty years to the 2009 low.

**Saver One holds cash.** Nominal interest is roughly 0%, so the balance stays about \$10,000 in yen. But prices fell gently over those years — say cumulative deflation of about 5% across the period. The *purchasing power* of that \$10,000 is now equivalent to about \$10,000 / (1 − 0.05) ≈ **\$10,530** in 1989 goods. Saver One is slightly *richer* in real terms, having taken no risk at all.

**Saver Two holds the Nikkei.** Their \$10,000 is worth about \$1,800 in nominal terms at the 2009 low — and since prices fell, the real value is a touch higher than \$1,800, but only a touch. Call it roughly **\$1,900** in purchasing power. Saver Two took maximum risk and ended with less than a fifth of what they started with.

The gap is staggering: the do-nothing saver ended up with more than *five times* the real wealth of the brave equity investor. The one-sentence intuition: in deflation, sitting in cash is not laziness, it is a positive-real-return strategy that quietly beats the people taking all the risk.

### Equities and real estate: dead money for a generation

We have covered the Nikkei in detail; the short version is that equities were the *losers* of the regime, the mirror of the role real assets played in the 1970s. There is a deeper reason equities suffer so badly in deflation, beyond the simple fact that the bubble had to deflate. A stock is a claim on a company's future profits, and deflation attacks profits from two directions at once. First, a company in a deflationary economy struggles to *raise prices* — if anything it must cut them to win customers in a world of weak demand — so its revenue stagnates or shrinks. Second, many of its costs are sticky: wages and debt service do not fall as easily as selling prices, so margins get squeezed from both ends. Stagnant revenue and compressed margins mean stagnant or falling profits, and a stock is only worth what its future profits are worth. On top of that, deflation erases the case for paying a premium "growth multiple," because there is no growth to pay for. The double hit — falling profits *and* falling willingness to pay up for them — is why equities can stay dead for decades, not just dip and recover.

Real estate was, if anything, worse, because it was the most leveraged asset and the one most directly tied to the collapsing collateral. Tokyo commercial land prices fell for roughly two decades straight, year after year, with none of the dramatic-then-bounce pattern that property usually shows. Property is a leveraged bet on rising prices and on the availability of cheap credit; deflation removes both legs at once. A landlord who borrowed heavily to buy a building in 1990 watched the building's value fall for twenty years while the mortgage stayed fixed — the balance-sheet recession in microcosm. The leverage that magnifies gains on the way up magnifies losses on the way down: a property bought with 80% borrowed money needs only a 20% price fall to wipe out the owner's entire equity, and Tokyo land fell far more than 20%. Real estate's behavior across the cycle, and its sensitivity to exactly this rates-and-leverage dynamic, is the subject of [real estate and REITs: income, leverage, and rates](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-map-of-asset-classes-what-you-can-own).

### The yen: a safe haven

One more asset deserves a mention: the currency itself. Counterintuitively, the Japanese yen tended to *strengthen* during global episodes of fear. The mechanism is the unwinding of the "carry trade." For years, global investors borrowed cheaply in low-yielding yen to buy higher-yielding assets elsewhere — a strategy that works until markets panic. When a global risk-off episode hit, those investors had to sell their foreign assets and buy back yen to repay their yen loans, which pushed the yen *up* exactly when everything else was falling. So the yen behaved as a defensive, safe-haven currency — another way the deflation regime rewarded the conservative and the cash-like. The cross-asset role of currencies and the dollar is developed across this series; for the macro backdrop see [how money rotates risk-on to risk-off](/blog/trading/macro-trading/risk-on-risk-off-how-money-rotates).

## Why deflation flips everything

We now have the scoreboard. Let us step back and state the principle that explains all of it at once, because it is the takeaway that travels to any future deflation, not just Japan's.

Falling prices do two opposing things. On one side, they **raise the real value of money and of fixed claims on money.** A yen in your pocket buys more next year; a fixed 8% coupon is worth more in purchasing power when prices are dropping; a fixed promise to repay \$1,000 in ten years becomes a heavier, more valuable obligation. So *cash and bonds — the holders of money and the receivers of fixed money — win.* On the other side, falling prices **crush nominal growth, pricing power, and the value of real things.** A company can't raise prices, so its revenues stagnate or shrink; its profits get squeezed; the case for paying a high multiple for "future growth" evaporates. And a physical asset like property, whose whole appeal was that its price would rise, becomes a depreciating liability when prices fall instead. So *equities, real estate, and commodities — the bets on growth and rising prices — lose.*

This is the exact opposite of the 1970s, and seeing the two side by side is the clearest way to lock in the lesson. In the [1970s stagflation case study](/blog/trading/cross-asset/case-study-1970s-stagflation-commodities-win), high-and-rising inflation crushed bonds and cash (a fixed coupon is worthless when prices are racing ahead) and lifted real assets (things with intrinsic value held their worth). In Japan's deflation, the entire ranking inverts. The figure below puts the two regimes next to each other, asset by asset.

![Matrix comparing how each asset class performs in 1970s stagflation versus Japan deflation with the winners and losers flipped](/imgs/blogs/case-study-japan-lost-decades-deflation-5.png)

Read that matrix as a single claim: *the asset ranking is not fixed; it is a function of the inflation regime.* Government bonds are the worst asset in stagflation and the best in deflation. Commodities are the best in stagflation and a loser in deflation. Cash roughly keeps pace in inflation and quietly wins in deflation. There is no permanently "safe" or permanently "good" asset — there is only the right asset *for the regime you are in*. This regime-dependence is the organizing idea of the whole series, and it is treated head-on in [correlation by regime: growth and inflation](/blog/trading/cross-asset/correlation-by-regime-growth-and-inflation).

#### Worked example: the same coupon in inflation versus deflation

Take one fixed instrument — a bond paying a flat \$80 a year on a \$1,000 face value (an 8% coupon, Japan-in-1990 style) — and run it through both worlds to feel the flip.

**In deflation (prices fall 1% a year).** The \$80 coupon is fixed in nominal terms, but each year those dollars buy more. After ten years of 1% annual deflation, prices are about 10% lower, so the \$80 coupon has the purchasing power of roughly \$80 / (1 − 0.10) ≈ **\$89** in original-year goods. The real value of your income stream is *rising*. And because the prevailing yield is falling toward zero, the *price* of your 8% bond soars above its \$1,000 face value, because no new bond offers anything close to 8%. You win twice.

**In inflation (prices rise 8% a year, 1970s style).** The same \$80 coupon now buys less every year. After ten years of 8% annual inflation, prices have more than doubled, so your \$80 coupon has the purchasing power of only about \$80 / (1 + 0.08)^10 ≈ **\$37** in original-year goods — less than half. And because new bonds now yield far more than 8%, the *price* of your old bond *falls*. You lose twice.

The one-sentence intuition: a fixed coupon is a brilliant asset when prices fall and a terrible one when prices rise — which is the whole reason bonds were Japan's hero and the 1970s' villain.

## The home-bias warning: why a Japanese investor needed the world

There is one more lesson buried in this case study, and it may be the most practical of all. Everything above describes what happened *inside Japan*. But a Japanese investor in 1989 was not forced to own only Japanese assets. The ones who suffered the full 34-year purgatory were, overwhelmingly, the ones with **home bias** — the natural tendency to hold mostly the stocks and property of your own country, because they feel familiar and safe.

Consider what the *world* did over those same decades. While the Nikkei went nowhere from 1989 to 2024, the US S&P 500 rose many times over; European and emerging markets had multiple powerful bull markets; and global government bonds, including JGBs themselves, rallied as yields fell worldwide. A Japanese saver who had spread their money across Japan, the United States, Europe, and emerging markets would have caught all of those gains. Their domestic slice would still have been dead money, but it would have been one slice of a portfolio that grew handsomely elsewhere. Global diversification was not a marginal improvement here; it was the difference between a lost generation and a perfectly good investing lifetime.

![Two-column comparison of a domestic-only Japanese portfolio that stayed underwater versus a globally diversified portfolio that grew](/imgs/blogs/case-study-japan-lost-decades-deflation-6.png)

The figure above is the entire warning in one picture: the left column is the home-bias trap (100% Japanese stocks bought near the top, dead money for 34 years), and the right column is the escape (the same money spread globally, riding the US and world bull markets plus the worldwide bond rally). The lesson generalizes far beyond Japan. *Any* single market can have a lost decade — the US had one in the 1970s and again in the 2000s, and Japan had one that lasted three. The defense is not to predict which market will stall; it is to own enough of all of them that no single market's lost decade can sink your retirement.

#### Worked example: home bias versus global diversification

Two Japanese investors each put \$10,000 to work at the end of 1989 and hold for the long run.

**Investor A — home bias.** 100% in the Nikkei. By the 2009 low the \$10,000 is worth about \$1,800. Even by 2024, when the index finally reclaims its high, the *price* return is roughly zero — call the position worth about \$10,000 in nominal terms after 34 years, before dividends. A working lifetime of zero nominal price growth.

**Investor B — globally diversified.** Suppose they put just \$2,500 (a quarter) in the Nikkei and spread the other \$7,500 across global stocks and bonds that compounded at, say, a modest 6% a year over those decades. The Japanese slice does what it did — call it roughly flat, worth about \$2,500 in 2024. But the \$7,500 global slice, compounding at 6% for ~34 years, grows by a factor of (1.06)^34 ≈ 7.25, to about \$7,500 × 7.25 ≈ **\$54,000**. The whole portfolio is worth roughly \$2,500 + \$54,000 ≈ **\$56,500** — more than five times Investor A's outcome, from the *same* starting money and the *same* unlucky home market.

The one-sentence intuition: you cannot always avoid owning a market that has a lost decade, but global diversification ensures it is only a fraction of your wealth rather than all of it.

## Common misconceptions

A regime this counterintuitive breeds a lot of comfortable myths. Here are the ones the lost decades demolish, each corrected with a number.

**"Equities always recover within a decade."** This is the big one, and Japan is its definitive refutation. The Nikkei took about **34 years** — not one decade, but more than three — to reclaim its 1989 high in nominal terms. For most of that span an index buy-and-hold investor was sitting on losses. "Stocks recover in the long run" is true, but "the long run" can be longer than a career, a mortgage, or a retirement horizon. The honest version is: *equities have very high expected returns over a lifetime, but the path can include a drawdown that lasts decades* — which is exactly why you diversify across assets and across countries.

**"Cash is always trash."** In the inflationary world most investors grew up in, cash slowly loses purchasing power, so the slogan holds. But it is regime-dependent, not a law. In Japan's deflation, cash earned a *positive real return* of roughly +1% a year for taking zero risk, and over two decades it handily beat the Nikkei. When prices are falling, holding money is not a drag — it is a quietly winning position. (This is the case [the cash post](/blog/trading/cross-asset/cash-money-markets-the-underrated-asset) makes in full.)

**"Bonds are boring and barely beat inflation."** Long JGBs were one of the great trades of the era. As the 10-year yield fell from about **8% to 0%**, holders collected fat coupons *and* large capital gains for decades. The "boring" asset was the decade's winner. Bonds are only a low-return asset when you buy them at low yields; bought at 8% in a world heading to 0%, they are spectacular.

**"Deflation is just low inflation — no big deal."** Deflation is a *distinct regime* with its own physics, not a milder version of the inflation you know. It raises the real burden of debt, it rewards hoarding over spending, it traps central banks at the zero bound, and it inverts the entire asset-return ranking. Treating Japan's −1%-to-0% world as "basically the same as a 2% target, just lower" would have led you to own exactly the wrong things for 30 years.

**"It can't happen here / it was a uniquely Japanese cultural problem."** Japan's bubble and bust had local features, but the mechanism — a debt-fueled asset bubble, a central-bank tightening, a balance-sheet recession, and deflation — is general. The United States and Europe slid toward the same zero-rate, QE world after 2008, reaching for the very tools the BOJ had invented. Deflation is a regime any debt-heavy economy can fall into after a big enough credit bust. The right response is not to assume immunity; it is to know the playbook.

## How it shows up in real markets

Japan is the headline case, but the deflation pattern and the assets it favors show up in several concrete episodes worth knowing.

**The Nikkei's December 1989 top.** The single cleanest example of a valuation top. With the index near 38,915 and the market-wide P/E ratio bloated into the 50-60 range, the math left no room for anything but a fall. The lesson an allocator takes is mechanical: an extreme valuation is a measure of how far prices have to drop just to get back to normal, and Japan's was the most extreme in the modern record. When you see a whole market priced for permanent superiority, the trapdoor is wide open.

**The 1999 ZIRP and 2001 QE announcements.** These were the moments the BOJ admitted it had run out of conventional ammunition. For a bond investor they were a green light: a central bank publicly committing to zero rates and bond buying is telling you yields are pinned to the floor and likely heading lower. The investors who understood that the rate cut to zero would *stay* at zero — that this was a regime, not a cyclical dip — held their long JGBs and were rewarded with another decade-plus of falling yields and rising bond prices.

**The March 2009 low at ~7,055.** The deepest point of the saga, made worse because the global financial crisis dragged Japan down right when it looked like it might be healing. The lesson is about correlation: in a true global risk-off event, even a market that is "cheap" after a 20-year bear market goes *down*, because [in a crisis correlations go to one](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-map-of-asset-classes-what-you-can-own) and almost everything risky falls together. The assets that held up in that moment were, again, government bonds and the safe-haven yen.

**The 2008 Western echo.** When the US housing bubble burst, the Federal Reserve cut rates to zero and launched QE — pulling the exact levers the BOJ had pioneered. The US avoided a 34-year lost decade (its banks were forced to recognize losses faster, and policy was more aggressive sooner), but the *direction* was identical, and for several years the world's biggest economies lived in the low-rate, low-inflation, bond-friendly world Japan had inhabited since the 1990s. Investors who had studied Japan and owned duration were positioned correctly for the post-2008 bond rally.

**The yen in every risk-off episode since.** From the 2008 crisis to the 2011 turmoil to assorted "flash" panics, the yen has repeatedly strengthened when global markets fell, as carry trades unwound. It is the cleanest live demonstration that the deflation regime made Japan's currency a defensive asset — a place global money runs *to* when it is scared, not away from.

**The "zombie company" problem.** A subtler but instructive episode is what happened to Japan's banks and the firms they kept alive. Rather than recognize losses on bad loans and force insolvent borrowers into bankruptcy, many Japanese banks "evergreened" the debt — rolling it over and lending just enough to keep hopeless firms breathing. The result was a generation of "zombie" companies that consumed capital and labor without producing growth, which is one reason the recovery took so long. The lesson for an allocator is about how *policy choices interact with the regime*: a deflationary bust that is met with denial and forbearance lasts far longer than one met with rapid loss-recognition and recapitalization. The United States after 2008, for all its faults, forced its banks to take losses and raise capital quickly, which is a large part of why its lost decade was measured in years rather than decades.

**The European near-deflation of the 2010s.** After its own debt crisis, the euro area flirted with deflation in the mid-2010s, and the European Central Bank — having watched both Japan and the post-2008 US — pushed its policy rate *negative* and ran large-scale QE. German government bond yields, like JGBs before them, fell below zero. An investor who had internalized the Japan playbook and owned European duration into that move was, once again, paid handsomely for holding the "boring" asset as yields collapsed. Deflation, or even the serious threat of it, kept rewarding the same holdings across three different continents.

## When to own it: the deflation playbook

This is the payoff. Strip away the history and here is what the lost decades teach an allocator to actually *do* when the signs point to a deflation or debt-deleveraging regime. The matrix below is the playbook in one frame; the text walks each row.

![Matrix of the deflation playbook showing long government bonds and cash and quality as overweights and equities and real estate as underweights](/imgs/blogs/case-study-japan-lost-decades-deflation-7.png)

**Recognize the regime first.** Deflation is not "low inflation." The tells are specific: a major asset bubble has burst; private debt is high and households and firms are paying it down rather than borrowing; credit growth has stalled; the central bank has cut to zero and is reaching for QE; and CPI is flirting with or below zero. When you see that cluster — a balance-sheet recession with prices drifting down — you are in a different world than a normal cycle, and the normal "buy the dip in stocks" reflex is the wrong one. (For the live version of reading these signals, see [correlation by regime: growth and inflation](/blog/trading/cross-asset/correlation-by-regime-growth-and-inflation).)

**Overweight long government bonds.** This is the single highest-conviction move. Falling yields driven to the zero bound mean long-duration government bonds collect their coupons *and* gain in price for years. The lower the starting yield, the smaller this prize — bought at 8% it was spectacular; bought at 0% there is little left to harvest — so the trade is most powerful early, when yields still have room to fall. The mechanism is in [government bonds: the risk-free anchor and duration](/blog/trading/cross-asset/government-bonds-the-risk-free-anchor-and-duration).

**Hold meaningful cash.** In deflation cash is not a drag, it is a positive-real-return asset that beats most risk-taking. It also gives you optionality: dry powder to buy genuinely cheap assets once the deleveraging finally exhausts itself. The full argument is in [cash and money markets: the underrated asset](/blog/trading/cross-asset/cash-money-markets-the-underrated-asset).

**Favor quality over leverage in whatever equity you own.** You do not have to own zero stocks, but the equities that survive a deflationary squeeze are the ones with strong balance sheets, low debt, and pricing power or genuine global growth — not the leveraged, domestically-trapped, expensive names. Japan's most leveraged property and zombie firms were the worst place to be; the few globally competitive exporters fared far better.

**Underweight real estate and commodities.** Leveraged property is a double loser in deflation — the asset price falls while the fixed debt stays — and commodities have no inflation tailwind to lift them. These are the assets to trim, the mirror of their starring role in stagflation.

**Diversify globally — this is non-negotiable.** The deepest lesson of the lost decades is that no single market is safe from a 30-year stall. Spreading across countries and regions does not require predicting which market will stall; it simply ensures that any one market's lost decade is a slice of your portfolio rather than the whole thing. A globally diversified Japanese investor would have had a perfectly good 34 years; a home-biased one had a catastrophe.

The whole playbook reduces to a single sentence: in a deflation or debt-deleveraging regime, *own the holders of money* — long government bonds, cash, and the strongest balance sheets — *underweight the bets on rising prices* — leveraged property, commodities, expensive growth — *and diversify across countries so no single market's lost decade is your lost decade.* That is the mirror image of the stagflation playbook, and together the two regimes bracket the full range of what inflation can do to a portfolio. Japan paid for this lesson with 34 years; you can learn it in an afternoon.
