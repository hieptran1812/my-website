> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The mid-cycle expansion is the long, calm middle of the business cycle: growth is steady and above trend, inflation sits near target, the Fed is neutral-to-gradual, and profit margins are healthy. It is the phase you spend most of your investing life in — and the one most investors damage their returns trying to outsmart.
>
> - Mid cycle is **by far the longest phase**. The 2009-2020 US expansion ran **128 months** — the longest in recorded history — and the years inside it were a quiet, repetitive grind of single-digit-to-thirty-percent equity gains.
> - **Nothing dominates.** Unlike early cycle (cyclicals lead) or late cycle (commodities lead), the mid-cycle edge is *broad* exposure plus **carry** — collecting the equity drift and the credit spread — not a clever rotation.
> - The returns are steady and the drawdowns are shallow, which breeds **complacency**. The real risk in mid cycle is not a crash; it is you, reaching for leverage and yield because everything feels easy, or selling a trend that has years left to run.
> - The one number to remember: a plain **60/40 portfolio left untouched through the 2013-2019 grind turned \$100,000 into roughly \$196,000** — and an investor who tried to time it gave up about **\$29,000** in compounding for protection that was never needed.

In late 2015, the financial press was a wall of worry. China had devalued its currency in August and the S&P 500 had a sharp, scary drop. Oil was collapsing toward \$26 a barrel. Pundits warned that the post-2009 bull market was "long in the tooth," that a recession was overdue, that the easy money had been made. An investor reading the headlines could be forgiven for moving to cash and waiting for the storm.

The storm never came. The S&P 500 limped to a +1.4% total return for 2015 — its worst year of the decade — and then promptly returned +12.0% in 2016, +21.8% in 2017, and +31.5% in 2019. The expansion that everyone kept calling old in 2015 ran for another four and a half years. The 2009-2020 cycle became the longest on record: 128 months from trough to peak. The investor who sold in the autumn of 2015 spent years watching a trend they had abandoned grind relentlessly higher.

This is the defining feature, and the defining trap, of the mid-cycle expansion. It is the longest, calmest, and most underrated phase of the entire business cycle — and it is psychologically the hardest to hold, precisely because nothing dramatic happens. This post is about that phase: what it is, why it lasts so long, what actually earns money while it runs, and the single most valuable skill it demands of you, which is the discipline to stay invested and not trade away the easiest returns the market ever hands out. The diagram below is the mental model we will keep returning to: in any cycle, the turning points are brief, and the steady middle is long.

![Mid-cycle is the long calm middle of the expansion with brief early and late phases](/imgs/blogs/mid-cycle-expansion-the-steady-grind-1.png)

If you have read the other posts in this series, you already know the cast: stocks, bonds, credit, commodities, cash. This post is about the *regime* in which you hold them — and it pairs directly with the two phases on either side of it, [early-cycle recovery](/blog/trading/cross-asset/early-cycle-recovery-what-leads-the-rebound) and [late-cycle overheat](/blog/trading/cross-asset/late-cycle-overheat-when-real-assets-win). Let us build the whole thing from zero.

## Foundations: what "mid cycle" actually means

The *business cycle* is the rhythm of an economy expanding and contracting over time. It is not a calendar; it has no fixed length. But it does have a recognizable shape, and economists usually carve it into four phases: **early-cycle recovery** (the economy climbing out of a recession), **mid-cycle expansion** (steady growth, the subject of this post), **late-cycle overheat** (growth still positive but inflation and rates rising, the economy running hot), and **recession** (the contraction). The four phases form a loop, and the loop repeats — but the phases are wildly different in length. We covered the full loop in [the business cycle and the investment clock](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-business-cycle-and-the-investment-clock); here we zoom all the way in on the middle.

So what is mid cycle, concretely? It is the stretch after the early-recovery snapback has run its course but before the economy overheats. Five things are true at once, and you should learn them as a checklist:

1. **Growth is above trend, but steady.** *Trend growth* is the economy's long-run sustainable speed — for the US, roughly 2% real GDP growth a year. In mid cycle the economy grows a bit faster than that, but it is not accelerating violently the way it does in the first year of a recovery. It is a cruise, not a launch.
2. **Inflation is near target.** Central banks aim for inflation around 2% a year. *Inflation* is the rate at which the general price level rises — at 2%, a \$100 basket of goods costs \$102 a year later. In mid cycle, inflation sits near that target. It is not falling toward deflation (the early-cycle fear) and it is not spiraling upward (the late-cycle fear). It is boring, which is exactly what a central bank wants.
3. **The Fed is neutral to gradually tightening.** The *Federal Reserve* (the Fed) is the US central bank; it sets the short-term interest rate that ripples through every other asset. In mid cycle the Fed is not slamming on the brakes and it is not flooring the accelerator. It is at a *neutral* rate — one that neither stimulates nor restrains — or it is raising rates slowly and telegraphing every move months in advance. If you want the deep mechanics, see [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates).
4. **Credit is healthy.** *Credit* means borrowing — companies issuing bonds, households taking loans. In mid cycle, lending flows freely, default rates are low, and the extra yield investors demand to hold corporate bonds over government bonds (the *credit spread*) is narrow and stable. Money is available and being repaid.
5. **Recession risk is low.** Put the first four together and the odds of an imminent contraction are small. The yield curve is positive, profit margins are healthy, and there is no obvious imbalance about to unwind.

When all five hold, you are in the mid-cycle expansion. And here is the crucial fact that the rest of this post hangs on: **this configuration is stable, so it tends to persist for years.**

### Why it lasts: the absence of a reason to end

Early cycle is short because the snapback from a recession is, by nature, a violent one-time adjustment — pent-up demand released, inventories rebuilt, the most beaten-down assets bouncing the hardest. Late cycle is short because overheating is unsustainable almost by definition: once inflation forces the central bank to tighten aggressively, the clock to a recession starts ticking. Both of those phases contain the seed of their own ending.

Mid cycle does not. A steady economy growing modestly above trend, with contained inflation and a patient central bank, has no internal deadline. There is no imbalance screaming to be corrected. So it can run — and run, and run. The US expansion from June 2009 to February 2020 lasted **128 months**, more than ten and a half years. The 1991-2001 expansion ran 120 months. The 1961-1969 expansion ran 106 months. These are not flukes; they are what happens when the economy finds a sustainable speed and nothing forces it off the road.

For you as an investor, that single fact reorganizes everything. **You spend most of your investing life in mid cycle.** The dramatic phases — the crash, the snapback, the overheat — grab the headlines and the textbooks, but they are a small fraction of the calendar. The patient middle is where the bulk of your compounding actually happens. A strategy that is brilliant in the 18 months of a recession but mishandles the 8 years of a steady expansion is a losing strategy, because it optimizes for the rare and fumbles the common.

It helps to see the four phases side by side, because mid cycle is defined as much by what it is *not* as by what it is. Each phase has a distinct economic signature and a distinct market personality:

| Phase | Growth | Inflation | The Fed | Volatility | Typical length | Market personality |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Early-cycle recovery | turning up, fast | low / falling | very easy | falling from high | ~1-2 years | violent rebound; cyclicals and the beaten-down lead |
| **Mid-cycle expansion** | **above trend, steady** | **near target** | **neutral / gradual** | **low** | **often 5-11 years** | **steady grind; nothing dominates; carry pays** |
| Late-cycle overheat | still positive, slowing | rising / above target | tightening hard | rising | ~1-2 years | real assets win; rotations get violent again |
| Recession | negative | falling | easing fast | high / spiking | ~6-18 months | defensives and long bonds win; everything risky falls |

Read down the "typical length" column and the whole argument of this post is sitting right there. The two turning points and the recession are each a year or two; the steady middle is often five to eleven. You will live through far more mid-cycle months than all the other phases combined. The rest of the table is the diagnostic: when growth is steady, inflation is near target, the Fed is gradual, and volatility is low, you are in the row in bold — and the right personality to adopt is patience.

#### Worked example: how much of a decade is "the boring part"?

Walk through the 2009-2020 cycle by phase to feel the proportions. The expansion ran 128 months. The early-cycle snapback — the violent recovery off the March 2009 low — was roughly the first 18 to 24 months. The late-cycle phase, when the Fed was raising rates toward a peak and the curve was flattening toward inversion, was roughly the last 18 to 24 months (call it 2018 into early 2020). That leaves the middle:

$$
128 \text{ months total} - 24 \text{ (early)} - 24 \text{ (late)} \approx 80 \text{ months of mid cycle}
$$

So **roughly 80 of the 128 months — about 62% of the entire expansion — were the steady mid-cycle grind.** And that is just one cycle. Stack several cycles together across a 40-year investing life and the arithmetic is even more lopsided, because recessions are short (typically 6 to 18 months) and the expansions between them are long. The intuition: if you only learn how to invest in the exciting 20% of the time, you have mismanaged the 80% where your money actually grows.

## The character of mid-cycle returns: steady, shallow, and quietly large

Now that we know *what* mid cycle is and *why* it lasts, let us look at *how it pays*. The returns of a mid-cycle expansion have a distinct fingerprint, and once you learn to recognize it you will never confuse it with the other phases.

The fingerprint is: **steady positive years, shallow drawdowns, and low volatility.** No single year is a moonshot. No single year is a disaster. The index just grinds higher, year after year, with pullbacks that scare you in the moment and look like nothing on a chart five years later. Investors call this the "Goldilocks" feel — not too hot, not too cold — and the danger hidden inside it is complacency.

Look at the actual numbers from the 2009-2020 grind. The S&P 500's calendar-year total returns marched through the long middle of that expansion like this:

![S&P 500 calendar-year total returns through the mid-cycle grind, all positive](/imgs/blogs/mid-cycle-expansion-the-steady-grind-2.png)

Read that chart slowly, because it *is* the regime. 2013: +32.4%. 2014: +13.7%. 2015: +1.4%. 2016: +12.0%. 2017: +21.8%. 2019: +31.5%. Every year green. None of them a single explosive event you could point to — just steady participation in an economy that kept growing. The average across those years was over +18% a year, but no single year *felt* like +18%; each one was a slog of small daily moves that added up.

The contrast with the cycle's ends is stark. In 2009, the first year of the recovery, the most beaten-down assets returned anywhere from +50% to +140% as they bounced off the bottom — a violent early-cycle launch you had to be positioned for in advance. In 2022, a late-cycle inflation-and-rates shock, the classic 60/40 portfolio fell −16%, its worst year since 1937, as stocks *and* bonds dropped together. Those are the dramatic bookends. The mid-cycle years in between clustered quietly in the +10% to +30% range, with pullbacks that rarely exceeded single digits.

### Low volatility is the tell — and the trap

*Volatility* is the size of a market's day-to-day swings; high volatility means big jumps in both directions, low volatility means calm. The standard market gauge is the **VIX**, an index of the volatility that options traders expect in the S&P 500 over the next 30 days. A VIX around 12 means "the market expects calm"; a VIX above 30 means "the market expects turmoil." We unpack the VIX itself in [volatility as an asset](/blog/trading/cross-asset/volatility-as-an-asset-owning-fear); here it is a regime thermometer.

Mid cycle runs cold. 2017 was the most placid year in modern market history: the VIX averaged around **11**, and the S&P 500 never had a peak-to-trough drawdown larger than about **3%** the entire year. Not once in twelve months did the index fall even 5% from a high. 2019 was nearly as calm. Compare those calm years to the spikes that bracket a cycle, and the regime jumps off the page:

![VIX year-end levels showing calm mid-cycle years versus stress spikes](/imgs/blogs/mid-cycle-expansion-the-steady-grind-3.png)

The 2017 VIX close of 11.0 and the 2019 close of 13.8 are the signature of mid cycle. When you see the VIX living in the low teens for months, with shallow dips that get bought back within weeks, you are very likely in the steady middle.

Here is the trap, and it is entirely psychological. When volatility is low and every dip recovers, the market *teaches you* that risk does not exist. The lesson feels free: buy the dip, it always comes back; add leverage, it always pays; reach for the higher-yielding, riskier bond, it never defaults. The calm itself is what lures investors into taking too much risk right at the moment the cycle is quietly maturing toward its end. The 2017 calm was followed by the February 2018 "volmageddon," when a popular bet *against* volatility blew up in a single day. The lesson of mid cycle is not "risk is gone." It is "risk is cheap and quiet right now, which is exactly why you should not over-reach for it."

#### Worked example: a shallow drawdown versus a real one

Put numbers on "shallow." Suppose you hold \$100,000 in the S&P 500 during a typical mid-cycle pullback — say the kind that happened repeatedly from 2013 to 2019. The index drops 5% over a few weeks on some scary headline. Your account falls from \$100,000 to:

$$
\$100{,}000 \times (1 - 0.05) = \$95{,}000
$$

You are down \$5,000 on paper. It feels bad. The news explains why it is the start of something worse. Then, over the next month or two, the index recovers and your \$95,000 climbs back to \$100,000 and beyond. The drawdown left no permanent mark.

Now compare that to a *late-cycle or recessionary* drawdown. In the 2022 rate shock, a 60/40 portfolio fell −16%. Your \$100,000 became:

$$
\$100{,}000 \times (1 - 0.16) = \$84{,}000
$$

You are down \$16,000, and unlike the mid-cycle dip it did not snap back in a month — it took well over a year to recover. The intuition: a mid-cycle pullback is a flesh wound that heals in weeks, while a cycle-turn drawdown is the one that actually costs you time, so panicking and selling during the shallow mid-cycle dips is how investors turn a flesh wound into a self-inflicted real loss.

### Why the diversification "free lunch" works best in mid cycle

There is a second, subtler reason mid-cycle returns feel so smooth, and it has to do with how the assets move *relative to each other*. *Correlation* measures whether two assets move together: a correlation of +1 means they rise and fall in perfect lockstep, 0 means they are unrelated, and −1 means one zigs whenever the other zags. The magic of a balanced portfolio is that when stocks and bonds have a *low or negative* correlation, their swings partly cancel out, so the combined portfolio is calmer than either piece on its own. That is the "free lunch" of diversification we cover in depth in [correlation and the diversification free lunch](/blog/trading/cross-asset/correlation-and-the-diversification-free-lunch).

Mid cycle is the regime where this works best. When growth is steady and inflation is contained, the dominant driver of markets is *growth expectations* — and in a growth-driven world, stocks and bonds tend to move *oppositely*: bad growth news hurts stocks but helps bonds (because the Fed is expected to ease), so bonds rally exactly when stocks fall, cushioning the blow. Across the 2000-2021 era, the stock-bond correlation was reliably *negative*, which is why the 60/40 portfolio rode so smoothly through the 2013-2019 grind. The danger sign is when inflation takes over as the driver — as it did in 2022 — because then stocks and bonds fall *together* and the cushion vanishes. As long as you are genuinely in mid cycle, with inflation contained, the cushion is intact and your portfolio's calm is real, not luck.

#### Worked example: why steady compounding beats lumpy compounding

A subtle reason the steady mid-cycle grind is so valuable is that *smooth* returns compound better than *lumpy* ones, even when the simple average is the same. Compare two portfolios over two years, each with an *average* annual return of +10%.

**Portfolio A — steady (mid-cycle style).** It returns +10% each year. Starting with \$100,000:

$$
\$100{,}000 \times 1.10 \times 1.10 = \$121{,}000
$$

**Portfolio B — lumpy (cycle-ends style).** It returns +40% one year and −20% the next. The simple average is still +10%, but:

$$
\$100{,}000 \times 1.40 \times 0.80 = \$112{,}000
$$

Portfolio A ends with \$121,000; Portfolio B with only \$112,000 — a \$9,000 gap, despite identical average returns. The drawdown in Portfolio B did real, permanent damage: a −20% loss requires a +25% gain just to break even, and that asymmetry is what eats the lumpy portfolio. The intuition: the shallow, steady drawdowns of a mid-cycle grind are not just more comfortable to hold — they actually compound your money faster than the same average return delivered in violent lurches, which is a quiet, mathematical reason to value the calm.

## What works in mid cycle: everything a little, nothing a lot

If early cycle is about owning the cyclicals that lead the rebound, and late cycle is about owning the real assets that win when inflation runs, what do you own in the long, steady middle? The honest answer is the one investors find hardest to accept: **nothing in particular dominates, so you own a broad basket and collect.**

This is the deepest difference between mid cycle and the cycle ends. At the turning points, there is a clear hero. Early cycle: the most beaten-down cyclical sectors and small-caps explode off the bottom. Late cycle: energy, metals, and broad commodities surge as inflation bites. Those are concentrated bets with a clear thesis. Mid cycle has no such hero. Growth is steady, so no sector is suddenly re-rating; inflation is contained, so commodities are not surging; rates are stable, so there is no violent rotation between "expensive growth" and "cheap value." The big, exciting rotations of the cycle ends quiet down. What is left is a calm, broad drift higher — and your job is to be exposed to it and to harvest the income it throws off.

![Mid-cycle what-works matrix across equities, factors, credit, and commodities](/imgs/blogs/mid-cycle-expansion-the-steady-grind-4.png)

Four things earn their keep in the steady middle, and the matrix above lays them out. Let us take each in turn.

### Broad equities: the index grinds higher

The core of a mid-cycle portfolio is simply **broad equity exposure** — the whole index, not a clever sector bet. The mechanism is straightforward: in a steady expansion, corporate earnings grow roughly in line with the economy, and as earnings grow, the index grinds higher. There is no need to pick the winning sector because the *whole market* is being lifted by the same rising tide of steady growth. We covered the engine of equity returns in [equities: owning a slice of growth](/blog/trading/cross-asset/equities-stocks-owning-a-slice-of-growth); in mid cycle that engine simply runs at a steady, unspectacular cruise.

This is why "stay invested in the index" beats "trade in and out" so decisively in this phase. The returns are not delivered in a few dramatic days you can wait for; they accumulate in a slow drip of small up-days that you only capture if you are continuously present. Step out for even a few of the best weeks and you give up a chunk of the whole grind.

There is a well-documented version of this in the data. Over long stretches, a large share of the total return comes from a small number of the best days, and those best days cluster *right next to* the worst days — in the choppy moments when a nervous investor is most likely to have sold. Studies of the US market repeatedly find that missing just the ten best days over a couple of decades cuts your total return roughly in half. In a mid-cycle grind, where the gains are spread thin across hundreds of small up-days, the cost of being out is at its most insidious precisely because no single day looks important enough to wait for. The index does not ring a bell on the days you needed to be present; it just quietly drifts up while you watch from cash.

A second mechanism reinforces this. As the economy grows steadily, companies are not just earning more — many are *returning* more to shareholders through dividends and buybacks, and they are reinvesting retained earnings into growth that becomes next year's higher earnings. Each of those channels feeds the index's slow climb. None of it is dramatic in any given quarter. All of it compounds if you hold. The broad index in mid cycle is a machine that converts steady economic growth into a steady rise in your account, and the only input it asks of you is that you keep your money in the machine.

### Quality and momentum: the factors that suit a calm trend

Within equities, two *factors* tend to do well in mid cycle. A *factor* is a measurable characteristic of stocks that has historically been associated with a return premium — a way of tilting your equity exposure rather than picking individual names.

- **Quality** means companies that are profitable, stable, and not heavily indebted — steady earners. In a calm expansion, the market rewards reliable compounding, and quality firms keep delivering it without drama.
- **Momentum** means buying what has recently been rising. Momentum works best when trends are *persistent* — when the thing that went up last quarter keeps going up. Mid cycle, with its long steady drift and absence of violent rotations, is the ideal habitat for momentum, because the trend just keeps trending.

The opposite factor, *deep value* — buying the cheapest, most beaten-down stocks — tends to shine at the *turning points* (early cycle especially), when the trash bounces hardest. In the steady middle, betting on the laggards to suddenly outperform is fighting the calm; tilting toward quality and momentum is leaning with it.

This factor logic is the same cycle story told one level down. Early cycle rewards *value* and high-beta junk because the snapback lifts the most damaged names the most. Late cycle rewards *low-volatility* and defensive characteristics as investors brace for the turn. Mid cycle, the long calm trend, rewards *quality* and *momentum* because both are bets on continuity — on good companies staying good and on trends staying trends — and continuity is exactly what a steady expansion supplies. You are not trying to be a hero stock-picker; you are tilting your broad exposure toward the characteristics that a calm, persistent uptrend pays for.

### Credit carry: collect the spread

This is the quiet workhorse of mid cycle, and it deserves real attention because beginners almost always overlook it. *Carry* is the income you earn just for holding an asset, before any price change — the dividend on a stock, the coupon on a bond, the interest on cash. *Credit carry* specifically means the extra yield you collect by holding corporate bonds instead of government bonds.

Here is why it works in mid cycle. A corporate bond pays more than a government bond because the company might default — that extra yield is the *credit spread*, your compensation for default risk. But in a healthy mid-cycle expansion, defaults are *rare*. Margins are healthy, money is available, companies can refinance. So you collect the spread, and the defaults that the spread was compensating you for mostly do not happen. You are being paid for a risk that, in this regime, largely fails to show up. We cover this dynamic in depth in [corporate credit and spreads](/blog/trading/cross-asset/corporate-credit-investment-grade-high-yield-spreads); the mid-cycle takeaway is simply that **the spread is yours to collect, and the steady economy is what makes it safe to collect it.**

Carry is worth dwelling on because it is the mid-cycle return source that beginners systematically miss. The mind reaches for *price gains* — buy low, sell high — as the only way to make money. But a large share of long-run investment return is not price appreciation at all; it is the steady drip of income you collect simply for holding: dividends from stocks, coupons from bonds, the spread from credit, interest from cash. In the violent phases of the cycle, price moves swamp this income and you barely notice it. In the calm middle, with price moves small, carry becomes the dominant, reliable contributor — and it is the one return source that does not require you to be right about anything. You do not have to forecast the next move; you just have to hold the asset and let it pay you. That is why the mid-cycle instruction "harvest carry" is not a minor footnote to "stay invested" — in this regime, the carry *is* a large part of the return.

#### Worked example: harvesting the credit spread

You have \$50,000 to put in bonds, and you face a choice. A 5-year US Treasury yields 3.0% a year. A 5-year investment-grade corporate bond from a solid company yields 4.5% a year. The difference — 1.5 percentage points, or **150 basis points** (a *basis point* is one hundredth of a percent, so 150 bps = 1.50%) — is the credit spread.

Hold the Treasury for a year and you collect:

$$
\$50{,}000 \times 3.0\% = \$1{,}500
$$

Hold the corporate bond instead and you collect:

$$
\$50{,}000 \times 4.5\% = \$2{,}250
$$

The extra \$750 a year is your carry — your pay for taking on the company's default risk. In a healthy mid-cycle expansion, that company is very unlikely to default; investment-grade default rates in good years run well under 0.5%. So across a steady expansion you pocket the extra \$750 a year, again and again, for a risk that mostly does not materialize. The intuition: in mid cycle the credit spread is closer to free money than it ever is at the cycle ends, because the steady economy keeps the defaults the spread is paid for from actually happening.

### Moderate commodities: a small late-cycle hedge

The last slice is a *small* allocation to commodities — oil, copper, a broad commodity basket. In mid cycle, steady demand lifts commodities mildly, so they contribute a little. But their bigger role is as **insurance for the phase to come.** Mid cycle eventually becomes late cycle, and the late-cycle overheat is exactly when commodities surge as inflation bites — the [late-cycle, real-assets-win regime](/blog/trading/cross-asset/late-cycle-overheat-when-real-assets-win). Holding a modest commodity sleeve through mid cycle means you already own the asset that wins when the regime turns, instead of scrambling to buy it after it has run. It is a small position — 5% to 10% — sized as a hedge, not a main bet.

The unifying point across all four is in the matrix's title: **everything a little, nothing a lot.** No single position is supposed to carry the portfolio. The mid-cycle edge is the sum of broad equity drift plus credit carry plus a quality/momentum tilt plus a small commodity hedge — a diversified harvest, not a heroic call. This is exactly the regime where the [diversification "free lunch"](/blog/trading/cross-asset/correlation-and-the-diversification-free-lunch) pays best, because the assets are all quietly working without any one of them blowing up.

## How to tell you are mid, not late

Because mid eventually becomes late, the single most useful diagnostic skill in this phase is telling the two apart. Get it wrong in one direction and you de-risk years early, sitting in cash while the grind runs on without you. Get it wrong in the other direction and you stay fully exposed into an overheating economy that is about to roll over. So you watch four signals, and as long as all four say "mid," you stay the course.

![Decision graph of four signals that distinguish mid cycle from late cycle](/imgs/blogs/mid-cycle-expansion-the-steady-grind-7.png)

1. **Inflation still contained.** As long as inflation hovers near the central bank's target — roughly 2% to 3%, and not accelerating — you are mid. The first reliable sign of late cycle is inflation breaking meaningfully above target and trending up, which forces the central bank's hand. (For the deep version of why inflation reorganizes every asset, see [real-vs-nominal and the real-yield signal](/blog/trading/macro-trading/real-vs-nominal-inflation-real-yields-master-signal).)
2. **The yield curve still positive.** The *yield curve* plots the yield on government bonds against their maturity. Normally long-term yields exceed short-term yields — the curve slopes up, it is "positive." When the central bank tightens aggressively, short rates rise above long rates and the curve *inverts*. An inverted curve has preceded nearly every US recession. A positive, upward-sloping curve is a mid-cycle hallmark; the flattening toward inversion is the late-cycle warning.
3. **The Fed not yet aggressively tightening.** Slow, telegraphed, quarter-point hikes are a mid-cycle Fed managing a steady economy. *Aggressive* tightening — large hikes, hawkish surprises, a stated intent to slow the economy down — is the late-cycle Fed fighting inflation, and it is the most reliable signal that the regime is turning.
4. **No broad margin squeeze.** *Profit margins* are the share of revenue a company keeps as profit. In mid cycle they are healthy and stable. When wages, input costs, and interest expense all rise faster than companies can pass them on, margins get squeezed across the whole market — a classic late-cycle development that erodes the earnings the index depends on.

Notice how these four signals relate. The Fed's behavior is *downstream* of inflation; the curve's shape is *downstream* of the Fed; margins are *downstream* of the whole hot environment. They tend to flip in sequence — inflation first, then the Fed, then the curve, then margins — which is why watching all four gives you a staged warning rather than a single cliff. The decision graph above is the cheat sheet: four yeses and you are still in the grind; the first clear no is the cue to start trimming risk and adding hedges, the subject of [the late-cycle playbook](/blog/trading/cross-asset/late-cycle-overheat-when-real-assets-win).

### A calibration point: the 2009-2020 Fed

To anchor "gradual," look at what the Fed actually did across the long mid-cycle expansion. It held its policy rate at essentially zero (a range of 0% to 0.25%) from 2009 all the way through 2015 — six years of emergency support while the economy healed. Then it began to lift, and it lifted *slowly*: one quarter-point hike in December 2015, one in 2016, three in 2017, four in 2018, reaching about 2.50% by late 2018. That is roughly 2.5 percentage points of tightening spread over three years, every move telegraphed months in advance.

That pace is the very definition of "neutral-to-gradual." It did not disrupt the expansion; it accompanied it. Contrast that with 2022, when the Fed raised rates by more than 4 percentage points in a *single year* to fight an inflation spike — that is aggressive tightening, the late-cycle signal, and it broke the 60/40. The speed and surprise of the Fed, far more than the level of rates, is what tells you which regime you are in. The mechanics of all this live in [interest rates: the price of money](/blog/trading/macro-trading/interest-rates-the-price-of-money-master-variable).

## Common misconceptions

Mid cycle is the phase investors *think* they understand and consistently mishandle. Five beliefs do the most damage.

**"A long expansion is 'overdue' for a recession."** This is the gambler's fallacy applied to the economy. Expansions do not die of old age; they die of an imbalance — overheating, an aggressive central bank, a credit bust. The 2009-2020 expansion was called "overdue" every year from about 2014 onward, and it ran until a once-in-a-century pandemic ended it externally in 2020. Age alone tells you nothing. The signals in the previous section — inflation, the curve, the Fed, margins — tell you something. Watch those, not the calendar.

**"Low volatility means low risk."** It is closer to the opposite. Calm markets are when risk is *cheapest and most tempting*, which is precisely when investors load up on leverage and reach for yield, building the fragility that the next shock exposes. The 2017 record-calm was followed directly by the February 2018 volatility blow-up. Low volatility is a description of the recent past, not a forecast of the future, and treating it as a green light to take more risk is how the calm sets the trap.

**"If nothing is dominating, there is nothing to do."** Beginners hear "no single asset leads in mid cycle" and conclude the phase is uninteresting. The opposite is true: the *doing* in mid cycle is the quiet, unglamorous work of harvesting carry, rebalancing, and staying invested. That work compounds into the bulk of a lifetime's returns. The absence of a hero asset is not the absence of a job; it is the job changing from "make a big call" to "execute the boring discipline relentlessly."

**"I should take profits after a big year."** After a +30% year, the urge to "lock in gains" is overwhelming. But selling a trend in a steady mid-cycle expansion has historically been a costly mistake, because the next year is usually green too. The investor who sold after the +32.4% of 2013 missed +13.7%, +1.4%, +12.0%, +21.8% and +31.5% over the years that followed. Profits feel safe in cash; in mid cycle they compound far better left invested.

**"Bonds are dead weight when stocks are grinding higher."** During a calm expansion, bonds feel boring — they return low single digits while stocks deliver double digits, so investors are tempted to dump them. But bonds in mid cycle are doing two jobs: they pay you a steady coupon (carry), and they are your cushion for when the cycle turns. In the 2014-2019 grind the US Aggregate bond index returned +6.0%, +0.5%, +2.6%, +3.5%, 0.0%, and +8.7% — low but mostly positive, and the diversification let the whole 60/40 portfolio ride smoothly. Selling your cushion because the sun is out is how you end up unprotected when it rains. The full mechanics are in [the 60/40 engine](/blog/trading/cross-asset/stock-bond-correlation-the-60-40-engine).

**"I can always get out before the cycle turns."** The most seductive belief of all is that you will spot the top and step aside just in time. In practice, the turn from mid to late is gradual and ambiguous, the scare-that-wasn't (2015, 1994) looks identical to the scare-that-was right up until months later, and the cost of acting on every false alarm — as the timing worked example shows — is enormous. The disciplined answer is not to predict the turn but to *watch the four signals* and scale risk down in stages as they flip, accepting that you will give back a little near the top in exchange for never selling years early into a grind that keeps running. Precision market-timing is a fantasy; staged, signal-driven de-risking is a discipline.

## How it shows up in real markets

Five episodes show the mid-cycle fingerprint in the data — the steady grind, the low volatility, the cost of trying to be clever.

### The long grind: 2013-2019

The cleanest example of a mid-cycle expansion in modern history is the middle of the 2009-2020 cycle. By 2013 the early-recovery snapback was over and the economy settled into a steady cruise: GDP growing modestly above trend, inflation stuck stubbornly *below* the Fed's 2% target for much of the period, unemployment falling slowly, the Fed at zero and then hiking with glacial caution. The S&P 500 responded exactly as the regime predicts: steady positive years, none explosive, with shallow pullbacks that always recovered. +32.4% in 2013, then +13.7%, +1.4%, +12.0%, +21.8%, and +31.5% in 2019. Six years, every one green, no recession in sight. An investor who simply stayed in a diversified portfolio and rebalanced was richly rewarded for doing almost nothing.

### The 2017 calm: a year without fear

2017 deserves its own mention because it was an outlier even within the grind. The S&P 500 rose every single month on a total-return basis — the first time that had ever happened in a calendar year. The VIX averaged around 11 and closed the year at 11.0. The largest peak-to-trough drawdown all year was roughly 3%. It was, by the standard measures, the calmest year in market history. And it is the single best illustration of the mid-cycle trap: the calm was so total that investors concluded volatility was permanently dead and piled into bets *against* it — bets that detonated in a few hours in early February 2018 when volatility finally twitched. The lesson of 2017 is not "markets are safe"; it is "the safest-feeling moment is when the most dangerous bets get built."

### The 1990s expansion: a decade of grind

The longest pre-2009 expansion ran from March 1991 to March 2001 — 120 months, a full decade. Its middle years, roughly 1992 through 1997, are a classic mid-cycle stretch: growth steady and above trend, inflation drifting down toward 2-3%, the Fed broadly accommodative with one disciplined tightening campaign in 1994, and equities grinding higher year after year. The S&P 500 strung together gains of roughly +7.6% (1992), +10.1% (1993), +1.3% (1994), +37.6% (1995), +23.0% (1996), and +33.4% (1997) on a total-return basis — the same fingerprint as 2013-2019: a flat scare year (1994, when the Fed surprised markets with aggressive hikes) embedded in an otherwise relentless grind. The investor who got rattled by the 1994 bond-market rout and stepped aside missed three of the best years in market history immediately after. The lesson rhymes exactly with 2015: the mid-cycle scare that *feels* like the end is usually a pause in a trend with years left.

### 2004-2006: a textbook mid-cycle expansion

The expansion of the mid-2000s is another clean case. After the 2001 recession and the early-cycle recovery of 2003, the economy settled into a steady mid-cycle expansion through 2004-2006. The Fed raised rates in a long, telegraphed, quarter-point-at-a-time campaign — 17 consecutive hikes from 1% in 2004 to 5.25% by 2006 — the very picture of "gradual, not disruptive." Equities ground higher, credit spreads stayed tight, and volatility stayed low. It was, in its character, almost indistinguishable from 2013-2019: steady growth, contained inflation, a patient-then-gradual Fed. (It ended, of course, in the 2008 crisis — but the *mid-cycle* portion behaved exactly as the regime says it should, and the signals that it was turning late showed up first.)

### 2015: the scare that wasn't

Within the long grind, 2015 is the instructive false alarm. China devalued the yuan in August, the S&P had a sharp August drop, oil was collapsing, and the chorus of "the cycle is ending" reached a roar. The S&P 500's total return for the whole year was +1.4% — flat, scary, its worst of the decade. An investor who read the year as the beginning of the end and moved to cash then watched the index return +12.0%, +21.8%, then +31.5% over the next years. 2015 is the perfect mid-cycle teaching case: a genuinely uncomfortable, headline-driven scare that, against every signal flashing "still mid" — contained inflation, positive curve, gradual Fed, healthy margins — turned out to be a shallow dip in an expansion with years left to run.

### 2022: the contrast that defines the phase

Finally, the counter-example that throws mid cycle into relief. 2022 was *not* mid cycle — it was a late-cycle, inflation-and-rates regime — and watching what it did to a portfolio shows you exactly what mid cycle protects you from. Inflation spiked to 9%, the Fed raised rates by more than 4 percentage points in a year, the yield curve inverted, and stocks and bonds fell *together*: the S&P 500 −18.1%, the US Aggregate bond index −13.0%, and the 60/40 portfolio −16%, its worst year since 1937. None of the four mid-cycle signals held — inflation was up, the curve inverted, the Fed was aggressive, margins were squeezed. The whole point of telling mid from late is to be positioned for the 2013-2019 grind *and* prepared for the 2022 shock, and the four-signal checklist is what gets you from one to the other in time.

## The mid-cycle playbook: stay invested, harvest, rebalance, don't fight the trend

Everything above converges on a single, almost anticlimactic conclusion: in mid cycle, the winning move is to stay near a neutral strategic allocation with a slight risk-on tilt, harvest the carry, rebalance mechanically, and resist the overwhelming urge to over-trade. The hard part is not knowing this. The hard part is *doing* it for years while your instincts scream for action. So let us make it concrete.

**Stay near a neutral strategic allocation with a slight risk-on tilt.** Your *strategic allocation* is your long-run, baseline mix of assets — say 60% stocks, 40% bonds, the classic balanced portfolio. In mid cycle you hold close to that baseline, tilted *slightly* toward risk: maybe 65/35 instead of 60/40, a touch more equity, a touch more credit, because the regime rewards being invested. You do not make a big concentrated bet, because no single asset is the hero. You hold the broad, diversified core and let the steady economy do the work.

**Harvest carry.** Actively collect the income the regime offers: the equity dividends, the credit spread on corporate bonds, the yield on cash. This is the quiet engine of mid-cycle returns. The credit spread in particular is close to free money in a healthy expansion, as the worked example earlier showed — you are paid for defaults that mostly do not happen.

**Rebalance.** *Rebalancing* means periodically selling what has grown too large in your portfolio and buying what has shrunk, to return to your target mix. In a steady grind, equities keep outgrowing bonds, so your 65/35 drifts toward 70/30, then 75/25 — quietly becoming riskier than you intended right as the cycle matures. Rebalancing back to target is an automatic discipline that trims risk after it has run and adds to laggards, all without requiring you to make a market call. It is the closest thing to a free, mechanical edge in this phase.

#### Worked example: the rebalancing trim in action

You hold a \$100,000 portfolio at your target of 65% stocks (\$65,000) and 35% bonds (\$35,000). One good mid-cycle year passes: stocks return +25% and bonds return +3%. Now your holdings are:

$$
\text{stocks: } \$65{,}000 \times 1.25 = \$81{,}250 \qquad \text{bonds: } \$35{,}000 \times 1.03 = \$36{,}050
$$

Your portfolio is now worth \$117,300, but the mix has drifted: stocks are \$81,250 / \$117,300 ≈ **69%**, not your target 65%. You are now carrying more equity risk than you signed up for, right as the expansion ages. Rebalancing means selling about \$5,000 of stock and buying \$5,000 of bonds to get back to 65/35:

$$
0.65 \times \$117{,}300 = \$76{,}245 \text{ target stocks} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \text{sell } \approx \$5{,}000 \text{ of stock}
$$

You just sold high (trimmed stocks after a +25% year) and bought the laggard, mechanically, without forecasting anything. The intuition: rebalancing forces you to take a little risk off the table after it has run and add it back to what is cheap — a disciplined, emotion-free edge that is especially valuable in a grind where your instinct is to let the winners run unchecked.

Here is a rough sketch of how the pieces fit together as sizing — not a recommendation, just a picture of the "slight risk-on tilt, harvest carry, hold a hedge" stance made concrete:

| Sleeve | Rough mid-cycle weight | Its job | What it pays you |
|---|---|---|---|
| Broad equities (quality / momentum tilt) | ~55-65% | the core growth engine | the index drift + dividends |
| Investment-grade + some high-yield credit | ~20-30% | harvest the spread | the credit carry |
| Government bonds | ~10-20% | the cushion for the turn | coupon + diversification |
| Commodities / real assets | ~5-10% | the late-cycle hedge | mild gains + inflation insurance |

The exact numbers matter less than the shape: a big diversified core, a deliberate carry harvest, and a small but non-zero hedge that you never let go to zero because mid eventually becomes late.

**Resist over-trading.** This is the whole psychological battle. Mid cycle is *boring*, and bored investors trade. They take profits after good years, they panic-sell shallow dips, they chase whatever sector is briefly hot, they reach for leverage because the calm makes it feel safe. Every one of those impulses, executed across a multi-year grind, bleeds returns. The discipline of mid cycle is to do *less*, not more.

It helps to name *why* the boredom is so dangerous. A multi-year grind gives you dozens of opportunities to second-guess a plan that is working, and every trade carries a cost — the bid-ask spread you cross, the taxes a realized gain triggers, and above all the risk of being out for the small up-days that deliver the grind's returns. Activity *feels* like diligence. In this phase it is usually the opposite: each clever-seeming adjustment is a small leak, and across years the leaks add up to the gap between staying invested and timing it. The investor who checks the portfolio quarterly, rebalances on a schedule, and otherwise leaves it alone will, in a mid-cycle expansion, almost always beat the one who is constantly tinkering — not because tinkering is unsophisticated, but because in *this regime* the reward goes to presence, not cleverness.

**Keep a small hedge, because mid becomes late.** Hold that modest commodity sleeve and your full bond allocation even when they feel like dead weight, because they are your insurance for the regime change you know is eventually coming. The four-signal checklist tells you *when* to start scaling that hedge up. Until it flips, you stay invested — but you never let the hedge go to zero.

The chart above is the entire playbook in one line. A plain 60/40 portfolio, left alone and rebalanced once a year through the 2013-2019 mid-cycle grind, turned \$100,000 into roughly \$196,000 — about a +96% total return over seven years — with the one real wobble being a shallow late-2018 dip that fully recovered within months. No heroics. No clever rotation. Just staying invested and harvesting through the steady middle.

#### Worked example: the cost of trying to time the grind

Now put a price on the over-trading impulse. You start with \$100,000 in a 60/40 portfolio in early 2013. Two strategies:

**Strategy A — stay invested.** You leave the portfolio alone for all seven years (2013-2019), rebalancing annually. Using the curated calendar-year returns, the portfolio compounds to roughly **\$195,800.**

**Strategy B — try to time it.** You are a careful, sensible investor who gets nervous twice: you take profits and sit in cash after the strong year, then get scared again during a mid-cycle wobble. Concretely, you spend two of the seven years in cash earning about 1%, and stay invested the other five. Running the same returns, you finish with roughly **\$166,800.**

The gap:

$$
\$195{,}800 - \$166{,}800 \approx \$29{,}000
$$

You gave up about **\$29,000** — nearly a third of your *starting* capital — for the comfort of sitting out two of the calm years. And what did that \$29,000 buy you? Protection against a dip that never came: the deepest drawdown the 60/40 actually suffered in that span was only about −9%, intra-year, in late 2018, and it recovered within months.

![Bar chart comparing staying invested versus timing the mid-cycle grind](/imgs/blogs/mid-cycle-expansion-the-steady-grind-6.png)

The chart makes the asymmetry visceral: the cost of being *out* (about \$29,000 of forgone compounding) dwarfed the worst dip of being *in* (a shallow, fully-recovered −9%). The intuition that the whole post builds to: in a mid-cycle expansion, the cost of being out of the market almost always dwarfs the cost of riding through a shallow dip, so the highest-value thing a disciplined investor does is *nothing dramatic at all*.

### What invalidates the mid-cycle case

A playbook is only honest if it tells you when it stops applying. The mid-cycle stance — stay invested, slight risk-on tilt, harvest carry — is invalidated the moment the four signals turn. When inflation breaks decisively above target and keeps climbing, when the yield curve flattens toward inversion, when the Fed shifts from gradual to aggressive, and when margins start getting squeezed across the market, you are no longer in the steady grind. That is the cue to scale down the risk-on tilt, raise the quality of what you hold, build up the commodity and real-asset hedge, and shorten your bond duration — the transition into [the late-cycle playbook](/blog/trading/cross-asset/late-cycle-overheat-when-real-assets-win). Mid cycle is not a place you stay forever; it is the long, profitable middle you ride patiently until the signals tell you the regime is turning.

The reason this matters so much is the lopsided arithmetic we opened with. You spend most of your investing life in this phase. A strategy that captures the boring middle — that stays invested, harvests carry, and rebalances without flinching — will compound into far more than one that is clever at the turning points but anxious through the grind. The single most valuable skill the mid-cycle expansion demands is also the least glamorous: the patience to hold a trend that can run for years, and to not trade away the easiest returns the market ever offers.

## When this matters to you, and where to go next

If you are a long-term investor, mid cycle is where your money actually grows — so the practical takeaway is to recognize the regime and let it work. When you see steady-but-unspectacular growth, inflation near target, a patient central bank, a positive yield curve, and low volatility, you are very likely in the steady middle, and the right instinct is to stay invested, collect the carry, rebalance on a schedule, and stop checking your account every day. The market is paying you to be patient.

The danger to guard against is your own boredom. The mid-cycle grind tempts even disciplined investors into over-trading, profit-taking, and reaching for leverage and yield, and those impulses are exactly what erode the returns the regime is handing you. Treat the calm as the time to do the unglamorous maintenance work well, not the time to get creative.
