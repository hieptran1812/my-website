> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — From the March 2009 bottom to the February 2020 peak, the United States ran the longest economic expansion in its history, and a specific recipe — **zero interest rates, quantitative easing, and persistently low inflation** — lifted *nearly every asset at once*. It was the "everything bull," and it made the boring 60/40 portfolio look invincible. The crucial lesson is that those returns were a product of the **regime**, not a law of nature.
>
> - The S&P 500 rose from about **666** to about **3,386** — roughly **+400%** with no bear market for eleven years — while the Fed's balance sheet swelled from **\$0.9 trillion to \$4.5 trillion** and rates sat at **0%** for seven years.
> - It was not just stocks. Over 2014-2019, US stocks returned **+98%**, REITs **+92%**, high-yield credit **+38%**, gold **+26%**, and even safe bonds **+23%**. Almost the only loser was commodities, **−32%**, on the 2014-15 oil crash.
> - The whole thing rested on **low inflation** (CPI mostly 1-2%, *below* the Fed's 2% target). That single condition let rates stay low, let multiples expand, and kept the stock-bond correlation negative — the engine of the dream 60/40.
> - The one fact to remember: when inflation returned in 2021-22, the same forces ran in reverse, and the 60/40 lost **−16%** in a single year. The everything-bull and the everything-bust were two faces of the same regime.

In March of 2009, the S&P 500 — the index of America's 500 biggest public companies, the single most-watched number in finance — touched 666. It had fallen by more than half in eighteen months. Banks were failing, unemployment was climbing toward 10%, and respectable people were saying capitalism itself might be broken. If you had told an investor standing at that bottom that they were about to live through the calmest, most generous decade for owning assets in living memory — that almost *everything* they could buy would go up, and keep going up, for eleven straight years — they would have laughed at you.

And yet that is exactly what happened. From that 666 low, the S&P climbed to about 3,386 by February 2020, a gain of roughly **400%**, without a single bear market (a fall of 20% or more) along the way. But the stranger fact is not that stocks rose. It is that *bonds rose too*. And credit. And real estate. And gold. The textbook says different assets are supposed to take turns — when one zigs, another zags, and that is what gives you diversification. For most of the 2010s, they didn't take turns. They rose together, all of them, on a single rising tide. Investors called it the **everything bull**.

The diagram below is the mental model for the whole post. At the top is the source — the Federal Reserve, the US central bank, pinning interest rates at zero and printing money to buy bonds. That cheap money is a tide, and underneath it sits the condition that made the tide safe to ride: inflation stayed low. On that tide, every boat rose at once. Our job is to understand *why* the tide lifted everything, why it could only do so while inflation stayed tame, and why the moment inflation came back the tide reversed and the everything-bull became the everything-bust of 2022.

![Liquidity tide from QE and zero rates lifting stocks bonds credit real estate and growth all at once](/imgs/blogs/case-study-2010s-qe-everything-bull-1.png)

This is a case study, so we will move in order: first the setup (the post-crisis world the Fed built), then the chronology (the long grind up and the wobbles that were bought every time), then the cross-asset scoreboard (who won and by how much), then the mechanism (why it all rose together), and finally the trap — the seed of 2022 that was planted at the very start. By the end you will not just know what the 2010s did; you will know the *conditions* that produced it, so you can recognize when you are in such a regime and, more importantly, when you have left it.

## Foundations: the setup — the world the Fed built after 2008

Before any chronology, we have to build the stage from zero, because every move in this decade is a consequence of three policy choices the Fed made in the rubble of the 2008 crisis. Get these three ideas straight and the rest of the post is just watching them play out.

### What "the Fed" and "interest rates" actually are

The **Federal Reserve** — "the Fed" — is the central bank of the United States. Its most powerful lever is the **policy interest rate**, the rate at which banks lend money to each other overnight. That single rate sets the *price of money* for the whole economy: when the Fed's rate is low, borrowing is cheap and savers earn almost nothing on cash; when it is high, borrowing is expensive and cash pays well. Almost every other interest rate — mortgages, corporate loans, the yield on government bonds — is built up from that base. (We cover the mechanics in detail in [the liquidity cycle and asset prices](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-liquidity-cycle-and-asset-prices).)

Here is the chain that makes the policy rate matter for *every* asset, not just loans. The value of any asset is the value today of the money it will pay you in the future. To convert a future dollar into a today-dollar, you shrink it by an interest rate — the **discount rate**. A higher discount rate shrinks future money more, so prices fall; a lower discount rate shrinks it less, so prices rise. When the Fed drives its rate to zero, it drives the discount rate for nearly everything toward zero, and the present value of every future dollar — every coupon, every dividend, every rent check, every far-off tech profit — goes *up*. Hold that. It is the single most important idea in the whole decade.

### ZIRP — the zero interest rate policy

In December 2008, with the economy in free fall, the Fed cut its policy rate to a range of **0% to 0.25%** — effectively zero — and *left it there for seven years*, until December 2015. This is **ZIRP**, the **zero interest rate policy**. (A *basis point*, by the way, is one hundredth of a percent: 0.01%. You will see rates quoted in basis points, or "bps", constantly.)

Seven years of zero is the part people forget. It was not an emergency measure that lasted a few months; it was the *background condition* of the entire first half of the decade. When the safe rate you can earn on cash is zero, every other asset has to be re-priced relative to that zero. A stock that yields 2% in dividends suddenly looks generous next to cash that yields nothing. A bond that pays 3% looks like a feast. The whole investment universe got re-anchored to a zero baseline, and that re-anchoring is most of the bull market.

### QE — quantitative easing, the balance-sheet expansion

Cutting the policy rate to zero exhausted the Fed's normal tool. So it reached for a new one: **quantitative easing**, or **QE**. The idea is simple even if the name is intimidating. The Fed creates new money (electronically — no printing press required) and uses it to *buy bonds* in the open market, mostly government Treasury bonds and mortgage bonds. (We go deep on the plumbing in [quantitative easing explained](/blog/trading/finance/quantitative-easing-explained-printing-money).)

Buying bonds does two things. First, it pushes bond prices up and therefore bond *yields* down — that is how the Fed got long-term interest rates lower even after its policy rate hit zero. Second, it floods the financial system with cash: the sellers of those bonds now hold money instead, and that money goes looking for a return somewhere. The Fed did this in three waves — **QE1** (2008-2010), **QE2** (2010-2011), and the open-ended **QE3** (2012-2014) — and the cumulative effect was to balloon its **balance sheet** (the total value of assets it owns) from about **\$0.9 trillion** before the crisis to about **\$4.5 trillion** by 2015.

The chart below is that balloon. Each step up is a QE program; the plateau at \$4.5 trillion is the new normal the Fed held into 2019. The number to carry is the multiple: the Fed roughly *quintupled* its balance sheet, manufacturing trillions of dollars of liquidity that had to land somewhere.

### How QE money actually reaches asset prices

It is worth pausing on *exactly how* the Fed buying bonds lifts the price of, say, a tech stock the Fed never touched — because the mechanism is not magic, and understanding it is the difference between believing in the everything-bull and merely betting on it. There are two channels, and they reinforce each other.

The first is the **portfolio-balance channel**. When the Fed buys safe Treasury bonds, it takes them out of private hands and hands the sellers cash. Those sellers — pension funds, insurers, banks — did not want to hold cash earning zero; they were holding Treasuries for a reason. So they go and buy the *next-safest, next-highest-yielding* thing: high-grade corporate bonds. That demand pushes corporate-bond prices up and their yields down, which pushes the *previous* holders of corporate bonds to reach further out — into high-yield bonds, then into dividend stocks, then into growth stocks. The Fed pressed on one end of the risk spectrum (the safest bonds), and the pressure rippled all the way out to the riskiest assets. Nobody had to buy stocks *from* the Fed; the Fed just squeezed everyone one rung up the risk ladder.

The second is the **signaling channel**. QE is also a promise. By committing to buy bonds and hold rates at zero, the Fed told markets, in effect, "easy money is here and we are not taking it away soon." That promise lowered the *expected future* path of interest rates, and since asset prices depend on expected future rates, not just today's, the promise itself moved prices. This is why a mere *hint* of slowing QE — the 2013 taper tantrum — could move markets violently without a single rate actually changing: the market was repricing the *promise*, not the present.

#### Worked example: the ripple from a Fed bond purchase

Picture the chain with round numbers. Suppose the Fed buys **\$1 billion** of Treasury bonds from a pension fund.

- The pension fund now holds **\$1 billion in cash** earning ~0% instead of Treasuries earning ~2%. It will not sit on that; it buys \$1 billion of high-grade corporate bonds yielding, say, 3%.
- The sellers of *those* corporate bonds now hold **\$1 billion in cash**, and they reach further — buying \$1 billion of high-yield bonds or dividend stocks yielding 5%.
- At each step, the buying pushes that asset's price up and its yield down, and shoves the seller one rung riskier. A single \$1 billion Fed purchase becomes a wave of demand that crests, eventually, on stocks.

Multiply that by the **\$3.6 trillion** the Fed added across QE1-QE3, and you have a standing bid under every risk asset for a decade. The takeaway in one line: QE does not put money *into* the stock market directly — it makes every safe asset so unrewarding that money is herded, rung by rung, toward stocks.

![Fed balance sheet stepping up from 0.9 to 4.5 trillion dollars through QE1 QE2 and QE3](/imgs/blogs/case-study-2010s-qe-everything-bull-3.png)

### The low-inflation miracle that made it all safe

Here is the part that bewildered nearly everyone at the time, and the part that turns out to be the hinge of the entire story. The Fed quintupled the money supply, held rates at zero for seven years, and bought trillions in bonds — and **inflation did not show up**. Through the 2010s, the Consumer Price Index (CPI, the standard measure of how fast the cost of living rises) mostly ran between **1% and 2% a year**, repeatedly *undershooting* the Fed's own 2% target.

This was the opposite of what the textbooks predicted. Generations of economists had warned that printing money causes inflation. For a decade, it didn't — for reasons we are still debating (globalization, technology, an aging population, weak wage growth, the money piling up in financial assets rather than the real economy). But the *effect* matters more than the cause for our purposes: **because inflation stayed low, the Fed never had to slam on the brakes.** It could keep rates at zero, keep buying bonds, and keep the tide rising, year after year, with no reason to stop. Low inflation was the permission slip for the everything-bull. Remember that, because the day the permission slip was revoked — 2021 — the bull ended.

### It was a global tide, not just an American one

One more piece of the setup, because it explains the *sheer scale* of the liquidity. The Fed was not acting alone. After their own crises, the European Central Bank and the Bank of Japan ran their own QE programs — and went further, pushing their policy rates *below zero* into outright **negative interest rates**, an idea that had been considered impossible. At the peak, something like \$17 trillion of government bonds around the world traded at *negative yields*, meaning investors were paying for the privilege of lending to governments. When a German or Japanese bond yields less than nothing, the global pool of money that needs a positive return has to go somewhere, and a great deal of it flowed into US assets, which at least paid *something*. The everything-bull was floated not only by the Fed's \$3.6 trillion but by a synchronized, worldwide wall of central-bank money. That is why this regime is best understood as a *global liquidity tide* rather than a purely American event — and why, when the major central banks turned together in 2022, the reversal was global too.

### TINA — there is no alternative

Put those pieces together and you get a phrase that defined the decade: **TINA**, "There Is No Alternative." If cash pays zero and safe bonds pay only ~2%, where is an investor supposed to put their money? Not cash; it earns nothing and inflation, even at 1.5%, eats it. Not bonds; 2% barely keeps up. The only place left with real expected return was stocks (and credit, and real estate). So money flowed into risk assets not always because investors loved them, but because *the alternatives had been deliberately made unattractive by policy*. TINA is the demand side of the everything-bull: a wall of money with nowhere else to go.

#### Worked example: what zero rates do to a stock's "fair" price

Let us make the discount-rate idea concrete with the simplest possible stock. Take a company expected to pay you **\$5 per share in earnings every year, forever**. A simple way to value that stream is to divide the annual payout by the discount rate: price = payout ÷ rate.

- In a **normal-rate** world, suppose the discount rate for this stock is **8%**. Its fair price is \$5 ÷ 0.08 = **\$62.50**.
- Now drop the discount rate to **5%**, the kind of level a zero-rate world produces. The same \$5 stream is now worth \$5 ÷ 0.05 = **\$100.00**.
- Drop it again to **4%**. The price becomes \$5 ÷ 0.04 = **\$125.00**.

Nothing about the company changed — same \$5 of earnings the whole time. The price rose 60%, from \$62.50 to \$100, purely because the discount rate fell from 8% to 5%. That is the entire mechanism of the bull market in one line: when the Fed pushes the discount rate down, the *present value* of every future dollar goes up, and prices rise even with flat earnings. The takeaway in one line: lower rates make tomorrow's money worth more today, so every asset that pays you tomorrow gets repriced upward.

## The chronology: the long grind up

With the stage set, here is the decade as it actually unfolded — not a straight line, but a long climb interrupted by scares that were, every single time, bought back. The chart below is the spine of this section: the S&P 500 from its 666 bottom to its 3,386 peak, with the three notable wobbles marked. Keep it in view as we walk through the years.

![S&P 500 line rising from 666 in 2009 to 3386 in 2020 with three wobbles that were bought](/imgs/blogs/case-study-2010s-qe-everything-bull-2.png)

### 2009: the bottom that no one trusted

The S&P bottomed at 666 on March 9, 2009. What is easy to forget is how *disbelieved* the recovery was. For years after, every rally was called a "sucker's rally" or a "dead-cat bounce." The wall of worry was enormous — and that very skepticism is part of why the bull ran so long. A market that everyone already loves has no buyers left to convert; a market that everyone fears has a long line of skeptics who get pulled in one at a time as it keeps rising. The 2010s climbed that wall of worry for eleven years.

### 2013: the taper tantrum

The first real test came in mid-2013. The Fed's chair, Ben Bernanke, merely *suggested* that the Fed might eventually slow ("taper") its QE bond-buying. He did not raise rates; he did not even stop buying; he just hinted at buying *less* in the future. The bond market threw a fit — the 10-year Treasury yield jumped from about 1.6% to 3% in months — and stocks wobbled. This was the **taper tantrum**, and it taught the market a lesson it would over-learn: *the Fed's liquidity is the thing that matters, and any threat to it is dangerous.* The tantrum passed, QE continued, and stocks finished 2013 up sharply. But the episode revealed how dependent the whole edifice had become on the flow of central-bank money.

### 2015-2016: the first liftoff and a growth scare

In December 2015, after seven years at zero, the Fed finally raised rates — by a tiny 0.25%. Almost immediately, markets got nervous: China's growth was slowing, oil was collapsing (more on that shortly), and the S&P fell about 13% from its mid-2015 high into early 2016, touching roughly 1,932. It was the closest the decade came to a bear market without quite getting there. And then it was bought. The Fed went slow on further hikes, the oil crash stabilized, and stocks resumed climbing. The pattern was now established: a scare, a dip, a dovish Fed, and a recovery to new highs. **Buy the dip** stopped being a phrase and became a strategy that simply *worked*, over and over, which made more people do it, which made it work better still.

### 2017: the year of no volatility

2017 was the strangest year of the bull. The S&P went up almost every month and *never* fell more than about 3% from a high — the calmest year in the index's modern history. Volatility (the size of the market's daily swings, often measured by the VIX index) collapsed to record lows. Investors got so comfortable that betting *against* volatility — wagering that markets would stay calm — became a crowded, profitable trade. It felt like a new normal: low rates, low inflation, low volatility, steadily rising prices. The complacency was a warning in hindsight, but in the moment it was simply pleasant. The S&P returned **+21.8%** for the year.

### 2018: the wobble that hiking caused

2018 was the year the regime's dependence on easy money showed its teeth. The Fed, now under Jerome Powell, hiked rates four times (toward 2.5%) and began *shrinking* its balance sheet — the opposite of QE, called **quantitative tightening (QT)**. The market did not like it. After peaking near 2,914 in September, the S&P fell hard into December, bottoming around 2,507 on Christmas Eve — a drop of about 20%, right at the edge of a bear market. Stocks finished 2018 down **−4.4%**, the only negative year of the bull's core run. And then, in early 2019, Powell blinked: the Fed stopped hiking, then started *cutting* again, and ended QT. The market roared back, returning **+31.5%** in 2019. The lesson was hammered home one final time: this bull lived and died on the Fed's posture toward liquidity.

### What carried the index higher: the engines under the hood

Two structural forces beyond the Fed deserve naming, because they amplified the bull and concentrated its gains. The first is **share buybacks**. Flush with cheap credit — companies could borrow at historically low rates — corporations borrowed money and used it to buy back their own shares, shrinking the share count and mechanically lifting earnings *per share* even when total earnings grew slowly. Low rates did not just lift the discount rate side of the equation; they financed a steady, price-insensitive buyer of stocks in the form of corporations buying themselves.

The second is the rise of the mega-cap technology companies — the cluster that came to be called FAANG (and later the "Magnificent Seven"). These were long-duration growth stories *par excellence*: companies whose profits were expected to compound for decades, whose value therefore sat far in the future, and whose prices were therefore the most sensitive of all to the falling discount rate. As the cap-weighted index gives the biggest companies the biggest weight, the index automatically tilted more and more toward exactly the stocks that benefited most from the regime. The everything-bull was, increasingly, a *concentrated* bull — a smaller and smaller group of giant growth names doing more and more of the lifting. That concentration was a feature in the 2010s and a vulnerability later, but in the moment it simply meant the index kept setting records.

### February 2020: the peak

The S&P reached its pre-pandemic peak of about **3,386** on February 19, 2020. From the 666 bottom, that is a gain of roughly **(3,386 − 666) ÷ 666 ≈ 408%** in price alone, more than 11 years without a bear market — the longest such run in the index's history. Days later, COVID-19 would crash it 34% in a month, but that crash is a different story (a pandemic shock, not the slow unwinding of the regime). The everything-bull, as a regime, peaked here.

#### Worked example: turning \$100,000 into half a million

Suppose you had done the unthinkable and put **\$100,000** into an S&P 500 index fund at the March 2009 bottom, when the index was 666, and simply held it to the February 2020 peak at 3,386.

- The index multiplied by 3,386 ÷ 666 = **5.08×** in price.
- Your \$100,000 became 5.08 × \$100,000 = about **\$508,000** on price alone.
- Dividends, reinvested over eleven years, would push the total meaningfully higher — well past \$600,000 on a total-return basis.

So a single, do-nothing bet on the broad US market roughly *quintupled* across the decade. The takeaway in one line: the everything-bull rewarded the simplest possible behavior — buy the whole market and refuse to sell — more richly than almost any clever strategy, precisely because the rising tide lifted the index itself.

## The cross-asset scoreboard: nearly everything rose

Now the heart of the case study, and the thing that makes the 2010s genuinely unusual. In most decades, the winners and losers across asset classes are sharply different — stocks soar while bonds languish, or vice versa. In the 2010s, almost *everything* won at the same time. The bar chart below shows the cumulative total return of each major asset class over 2014-2019 (the window where we have clean total-return data for all of them). Look at how many bars are tall and green.

![Bar chart of cumulative 2014 to 2019 returns showing stocks REITs credit gold and bonds all positive and commodities negative](/imgs/blogs/case-study-2010s-qe-everything-bull-5.png)

### Stocks led, but everything followed

Over 2014-2019, US stocks compounded to about **+98%** — nearly doubling in six years. That is the headline. But look at the rest of the board:

- **US REITs** (real estate investment trusts — companies that own income-producing property, a clean proxy for commercial real estate) returned about **+92%**, almost matching stocks. Low rates make the rents that property throws off more valuable (the discount-rate effect again) *and* make the mortgages that property uses cheaper. Both forces pushed real estate up.
- **High-yield credit** (bonds issued by riskier companies, paying higher interest to compensate) returned about **+38%**. In a TINA world, investors *reached for yield* — they accepted more credit risk to escape the zero on cash — and that demand pushed credit prices up and **credit spreads** (the extra yield risky bonds pay over safe Treasuries) ground tighter and tighter. The compression was so persistent that lending to a shaky company came to feel almost as safe as lending to the government, which is exactly the kind of mispricing a long easy-money regime breeds: when safe yields are zero, the market stops charging properly for risk.
- **Gold** returned about **+26%** over the window — though, importantly, it was *range-bound* for much of the decade (roughly \$1,100-\$1,300 from 2013 to 2018) and contributed little to the everything-bull until later. Gold's quiet decade is a clue: it tends to shine on inflation and fear, and the 2010s had little of either.
- **US bonds** (the Bloomberg US Aggregate, the standard broad investment-grade bond index) returned about **+23%** — and here is the remarkable part. Bonds are supposed to be the boring ballast that *doesn't* boom. But because rates kept falling — the 10-year Treasury yield drifted from about **3.8% in 2009** toward **1.5%** by 2016 and again by 2019 — bond prices kept rising. Falling rates are a tailwind for bond prices, so the Agg quietly compounded positive returns in most years: **+6.0%** in 2014, **+3.5%** in 2017, **+8.7%** in 2019.

### The quiet miracle in bonds

The bond story deserves its own paragraph, because it is the most counterintuitive piece of the everything-bull and the one that made the 60/40 sing. A bond pays a fixed coupon, so the only way its *price* rises is if interest rates fall — and rates fell, relentlessly, for the whole decade. The 10-year Treasury yield drifted from about 3.8% in 2009 to roughly 1.5% by 2016 and again by 2019, and every leg down in yield was a leg up in bond prices. That is why the "safe, boring" Aggregate bond index delivered genuinely good returns — +6.0% in 2014, +8.7% in 2019 — instead of the low single digits bonds usually grind out. Investors got *both* the coupon income *and* a capital gain from falling rates, year after year.

This is the tailwind that nobody appreciated until it became a headwind. A falling-rate decade is a paradise for bondholders: the income comes in, and the price goes up on top of it. But the same mechanism that gave bondholders a gift in the 2010s set the trap, because rates that have fallen to 1.5% have very little room left to fall and enormous room to rise. The lower yields went, the more *duration risk* was packed into every bond — and the bigger the eventual loss when rates finally turned. The bond bull was real, and it was borrowed from the future.

#### Worked example: the bond tailwind, then the bill comes due

Take a 10-year Treasury bond with a duration of about 8, and watch it across the regime change.

- In the **falling-rate 2010s**, suppose the 10-year yield falls from 2.5% to 1.5% over a stretch — a 1 percentage point (100 bps) drop. The bond's price rises by roughly duration × yield change = 8 × 1% = **+8%** in capital gain, *on top of* its ~2% coupon. A safe government bond returning ~10% in a year felt like a free lunch.
- In **2022**, the regime reverses and the same kind of yield *rises* by 2 percentage points (from ~1.5% toward ~3.9%). The price falls by roughly 8 × 2% = **−16%** in capital loss, swamping the tiny coupon. The Aggregate index, full of these bonds, returned **−13.0%** — its worst year ever.

The very duration that paid bondholders so handsomely on the way down hammered them on the way up. The takeaway in one line: a bond's interest-rate sensitivity is symmetric — the tailwind of falling rates and the headwind of rising rates are the same force pointed in opposite directions.

### The losers were the tell

Almost nothing fell — but the exceptions are revealing. **Commodities** (raw materials: oil, metals, grains, tracked by the Bloomberg Commodity Index) lost about **−32%** over 2014-2019, dragged down by the great oil crash: West Texas crude fell from over \$100 a barrel in mid-2014 to under \$30 by early 2016, returning **−17.0%** (2014) and **−24.7%** (2015) for the commodity index. Why did commodities fall while everything financial rose? Because commodities are not priced by discount rates — they are priced by *supply and demand for physical stuff*. Low rates don't make oil worth more; they just make the future cash flows of financial assets worth more. And with inflation absent, there was no inflationary bid for real assets. **Commodities falling was the everything-bull's signature**: the same tame inflation that let the bull run kept real assets in the cold.

#### Worked example: the boring 60/40, the easiest decade ever

The **60/40 portfolio** — 60% stocks, 40% bonds — is the default "balanced" portfolio sold to ordinary investors. Let us see how it did in the 2010s, and why it felt invincible. Take the calendar-year total returns of stocks and bonds and blend them 60/40 for 2014 through 2019:

- 2014: 0.6 × 13.7% + 0.4 × 6.0% = **+10.6%**
- 2015: 0.6 × 1.4% + 0.4 × 0.5% = **+1.0%**
- 2016: 0.6 × 12.0% + 0.4 × 2.6% = **+8.2%**
- 2017: 0.6 × 21.8% + 0.4 × 3.5% = **+14.5%**
- 2018: 0.6 × (−4.4%) + 0.4 × 0.0% = **−2.6%**
- 2019: 0.6 × 31.5% + 0.4 × 8.7% = **+22.4%**

Compound those six years together and **\$100,000 grows to about \$160,000** over 2014-2019 alone — and stretching the same blend across the full 2010-2019 decade lands a \$100,000 stake in the **\$240,000-\$260,000** range, with no year worse than a shallow −2.6%. A balanced portfolio that more than doubled with barely a scratch is *not normal*. The takeaway in one line: the 2010s 60/40 looked like a free lunch because both of its ingredients — stocks *and* bonds — were rising at the same time, which almost never happens for a full decade.

## Why it happened: liquidity, low rates, and the negative correlation

We have seen *what* happened. Now the mechanism — why one set of policies lifted such different assets together. The figure below lays out the causal chain: three ingredients on the left (abundant liquidity, a low discount rate, low inflation), feeding three channels in the middle (multiples expand, spreads compress, the stock-bond correlation goes negative), producing two outcomes on the right (the everything-bull, and the dream 60/40).

![Flow diagram from liquidity low rates and low inflation through multiple expansion spread compression and negative correlation to the everything bull and the dream 60/40](/imgs/blogs/case-study-2010s-qe-everything-bull-4.png)

### Channel 1: multiples expanded

A stock's price can be split into two parts: the company's *earnings* and the *multiple* the market pays per dollar of those earnings (the price-to-earnings, or P/E, ratio). Earnings grew respectably in the 2010s, but a large share of the stock-market gain came not from earnings but from the *multiple expanding* — investors willing to pay more and more per dollar of profit. Why pay up? Because the discount rate was low (our worked example showed a stock's fair price rising 60% on a falling rate with flat earnings), and because TINA left nowhere else to go. Multiple expansion is the discount-rate effect wearing a stock-market costume.

### Channel 2: spreads compressed

For credit (corporate bonds), the relevant number is the **spread** — the extra yield a risky bond pays over a safe government bond, compensation for the risk the borrower defaults. When cash pays zero and safe bonds pay 2%, investors desperate for income *reach for yield*: they buy riskier bonds, accepting thinner compensation. That buying pushes risky-bond prices up and spreads *down*. Through the 2010s, spreads ground tighter and tighter, which is just another way of saying credit boomed. Same liquidity, same low rates, different costume.

### Channel 3: the stock-bond correlation stayed negative

This is the subtle one, and it is what made the 60/40 a dream. The **correlation** between stocks and bonds — whether they move together or apart — was *negative* throughout the 2010s, meaning bonds tended to rise on the days stocks fell. Why? Because the dominant market fear was *slow growth*, not inflation. When a growth scare hit (2015-16, late 2018), investors feared recession, the Fed signaled easier policy, and bonds rallied *as* stocks fell — bonds did their hedging job. As long as inflation stayed quiet, growth was the only thing to be afraid of, so bonds reliably zigged when stocks zagged. (We unpack exactly why the sign flips in [the stock-bond correlation](/blog/trading/cross-asset/stock-bond-correlation-the-60-40-engine).) That negative correlation is the entire reason the 60/40 had such a smooth ride: in the rare years stocks stumbled, bonds caught the portfolio.

### The keystone: why low inflation held the whole arch together

Notice that all three channels trace back, ultimately, to one condition: **low inflation**. Multiples could expand only because the Fed could keep the discount rate low, which it could do only because inflation gave it no reason to hike. Spreads could compress only because the reach-for-yield could continue, which it could only while the Fed kept the zero in place. And the stock-bond correlation could stay negative only because *growth*, not inflation, was the dominant fear — the instant inflation becomes the fear, bonds and stocks start falling together. Low inflation is not one ingredient among three; it is the keystone that lets the other two bear weight. Pull it, and the arch collapses, which is the entire mechanism of 2022 stated in advance.

It is worth being precise about *why* inflation is the master switch for the stock-bond correlation, because this is the single most valuable idea to carry out of the decade. When the scary news is a growth slowdown, the Fed's response is to *ease* — to cut rates — which is good for bonds (their prices rise) at the same moment that the slowdown is bad for stocks. Opposite directions: bonds hedge. But when the scary news is *inflation*, the Fed's response is to *tighten* — to hike rates — which is bad for bonds (their prices fall) at the same moment that higher rates and a slowing economy are bad for stocks. Same direction: bonds do not hedge, they pile on. The 2010s lived entirely in the first world. The whole decade's diversification "free lunch" was a bet that the market's dominant fear would stay growth, not prices — and for eleven years, it did.

### Why duration and growth were the magic words

Two words capture *what specifically* the regime rewarded, and they are worth defining because they will reverse exactly in 2022.

**Duration** is a measure of how sensitive an asset's price is to interest rates — technically, roughly how many years out its cash flows sit. A 30-year bond has high duration; a money-market fund has almost none. But duration is not just a bond word. A high-growth tech company whose profits are mostly far in the future is a *long-duration equity* — its value, like a long bond's, is dominated by distant cash flows that a low discount rate makes hugely valuable. In a falling-rate decade, **the longer your duration, the more you won**, because every cut to the discount rate juiced the far-off cash flows the most. Long bonds beat short bonds; growth stocks beat value stocks; the most speculative, profits-far-in-the-future companies beat the steady cash-cows. (More in [real yields, the variable that prices everything](/blog/trading/cross-asset/real-yields-the-variable-that-prices-everything).)

You can see the duration premium most clearly in the **growth-versus-value** divergence. The chart below indexes \$100 invested at the end of 2009 in a growth-stock basket versus a value-stock basket. Growth — the long-duration, profits-tomorrow companies — pulled dramatically ahead, turning \$100 into roughly **\$370** while value managed only about **\$250**. The decade did not just reward owning stocks; it rewarded owning the *longest-duration* stocks.

![Line chart of 100 dollars invested in growth versus value from 2010 to 2019 with growth reaching 370 and value 250](/imgs/blogs/case-study-2010s-qe-everything-bull-6.png)

#### Worked example: why a long bond crushed a short bond when rates fell

Duration is abstract until you price it. Compare two bonds when the 10-year yield falls by **1 percentage point** (100 bps), say from 2.5% to 1.5%.

- A **2-year bond** has a duration of roughly **2**. Its price change is approximately −duration × yield change = −2 × (−1%) = **+2%**. A modest gain.
- A **30-year bond** has a duration of roughly **20**. Its price change is approximately −20 × (−1%) = **+20%**. A huge gain.

From the *same* one-point fall in rates, the long bond made ten times as much as the short bond. Now translate that to stocks: a long-duration growth stock behaves like the 30-year bond — its price rockets when the discount rate falls — while a short-duration value stock behaves like the 2-year. That is why a falling-rate decade was a growth-stock decade. The takeaway in one line: when rates are falling, length is leverage — the further out your cash flows, the more a rate cut is worth to you.

## The seeds of 2022: a regime, not a law of nature

Everything above describes a paradise for investors. The trap was that the paradise had a single load-bearing assumption, and it was hiding in plain sight from the first page: **low inflation**. Every channel we just walked through depended on it. Low inflation let the Fed keep rates at zero (multiples expand). Low inflation let investors reach for yield without fear (spreads compress). Low inflation kept *growth* the dominant fear rather than *prices* (the stock-bond correlation stays negative, the 60/40 works). Pull out that one block and the whole structure inverts.

That is precisely what 2021-2022 did. After the pandemic, a collision of supply-chain breakdowns, enormous fiscal stimulus, and pent-up demand sent inflation roaring back — CPI hit **9%** in mid-2022, a 40-year high. The Fed was forced to do everything the everything-bull required it *not* to do: it hiked rates from 0% toward 5% in a single year and switched from QE to QT. And then every channel ran in reverse.

The matrix below is the case study's punchline: the same five things that won in the 2010s regime, and what each did when the regime broke in 2022. Read it column by column. The left column is green — the everything-bull. The right column is red — the everything-bust. And notice the bottom row flips color: commodities, the lone loser of the 2010s, became the *only winner* of 2022.

![Comparison matrix of stocks bonds 60/40 credit and commodities in the 2010s regime versus the 2022 inflation shock](/imgs/blogs/case-study-2010s-qe-everything-bull-7.png)

- **Stocks** fell **−18.1%** as the discount rate spiked and multiples *compressed* — the engine ran backward. And the longest-duration stocks, the growth/tech darlings that had won the most, fell the hardest.
- **Bonds**, supposedly the safe ballast, fell **−13.0%** — their worst year on record — because rising rates crush bond prices, and the duration that had been a tailwind became a wreck.
- The **60/40**, the dream portfolio, lost **−16.0%** — its worst year since 1937 — because for the first time in the careers of most investors, *stocks and bonds fell together*. The negative correlation that powered the dream flipped positive the instant inflation, not growth, became the market's dominant fear.
- **Commodities** *rose* **+16.1%**, the one asset that thrives when inflation is the problem rather than the solution.

The deepest lesson of the 2010s is therefore not "stocks go up" or "buy the dip works." It is that **a regime is a set of conditions, and the returns it produces are conditional on those conditions holding.** The 2010s returns were real, but they were the output of low inflation plus easy liquidity. Mistake the output for a permanent law, and you will hold exactly the wrong portfolio when the conditions change — which is precisely what happened to a generation of investors in 2022.

#### Worked example: the same 60/40, two regimes

Take a single \$100,000 60/40 portfolio and run it through both regimes to feel the difference.

- In the **2010s regime**, a representative good year blends stocks +20% and bonds +5%: 0.6 × 20% + 0.4 × 5% = **+14%**. Your \$100,000 becomes **\$114,000**. Bonds and stocks both helping.
- In the **2022 regime**, stocks −18.1% and bonds −13.0%: 0.6 × (−18.1%) + 0.4 × (−13.0%) = **−16.1%**. Your \$100,000 becomes about **\$83,900**. Both sleeves bleeding at once, no hedge anywhere in the portfolio.

The *same portfolio*, the same 60/40 weights, went from a reliable +14% machine to a −16% disaster — and the only thing that changed was the inflation regime. The takeaway in one line: the 60/40 is not a strategy that "works" or "doesn't"; it is a bet on the stock-bond correlation staying negative, which is a bet on inflation staying low.

## Common misconceptions

The 2010s left behind a set of beliefs that felt like timeless wisdom but were really just descriptions of one specific regime. Here are the ones that cost people the most when the regime turned.

**"The 60/40 always works."** No — the 60/40 worked spectacularly in the 2010s because the stock-bond correlation was negative, which was true because inflation was low. When inflation returned in 2022, the correlation flipped positive, both sleeves fell together, and the 60/40 lost −16% in a year, its worst since 1937. The 60/40 is a *regime-dependent* portfolio, not a permanent free lunch. It is a bet that bonds will hedge stocks — a bet that pays off in growth-scare regimes and fails in inflation regimes.

**"Passive indexing always wins."** Passive index funds (buy the whole market, hold forever, pay almost nothing in fees) genuinely crushed most active managers in the 2010s — but partly because they were *supercharged by the QE bull*. When a rising tide lifts the whole index, simply owning the index is hard to beat, and the cap-weighted index concentrates into the biggest winners (the mega-cap tech names) automatically. Indexing's edge in fees and discipline is real and durable; its *2010s magnitude* of outperformance was inflated by a once-in-a-generation everything-bull. Expecting the next decade's index returns to match the last one's is a regime error.

**"Buy the dip is a law of markets."** Buy-the-dip worked every time in the 2010s — 2013, 2015-16, 2018 — because there was always a dovish Fed standing by to ease, and inflation never forced its hand. The strategy was really "buy the dip *because the Fed has your back*." When inflation took away the Fed's freedom to ease in 2022, the Fed could not rescue the dip; it was the *cause* of the dip. Buy-the-dip is not a law; it is a bet on the central bank's reaction function.

**"Money printing must cause inflation."** The single most confident prediction of the early 2010s — that quintupling the Fed's balance sheet would unleash runaway inflation — was wrong for a decade. The newly created money largely flowed into *financial assets* (stocks, bonds, real estate) rather than into wages and goods prices, inflating asset prices instead of consumer prices. That distinction — asset-price inflation versus consumer-price inflation — is exactly what let the everything-bull run without triggering the Fed.

**"Cash is trash."** In a zero-rate world, holding cash felt foolish — it earned nothing while everything else soared, and TINA shamed you into risk. But that was a property of *ZIRP*, not of cash. The moment rates rose to roughly 5% in 2022-2023, cash quietly became one of the best risk-adjusted holdings in the market, paying a real, safe yield while stocks and bonds fell together. An investor who had internalized "cash is trash" as a permanent truth held none of it precisely when it was the only thing working. "Cash is trash" was true only for the regime that produced it, and false the moment the regime turned — the recurring shape of every misconception on this list.

## How it shows up in real markets

A few concrete episodes from the decade make the regime mechanics tangible.

**The 2013 taper tantrum (May-September 2013).** Bernanke's hint that QE might slow sent the 10-year Treasury yield from about 1.6% to 3% in four months. Nothing in the real economy changed — this was a pure *liquidity* repricing. The episode proved that by 2013 the market's price level was wired directly to the *expected flow* of central-bank money, not just to earnings. It was a small-scale rehearsal of 2022: a threat to liquidity, and everything wobbled at once.

**The 2014-15 oil crash.** West Texas crude fell from over \$100/barrel in June 2014 to under \$30 by early 2016 — a supply glut (US shale) meeting soft demand. Commodities returned −17.0% (2014) and −24.7% (2015), even as US stocks kept rising. This divergence is the everything-bull's defining signature: financial assets, lifted by discount rates, rose, while real assets, with no inflationary bid, collapsed. The same low inflation that powered the bull was what kept commodities down.

**The 2015-16 China growth scare.** Slowing Chinese growth and the oil crash combined to knock the S&P down about 13% from its 2015 high into early 2016. It was the closest the decade came to a bear market — and it was bought. The Fed slowed its planned hikes, the scares faded, and stocks made new highs. The episode cemented "buy the dip" as a strategy and showed that a *growth* scare, in a low-inflation world, was always survivable because the Fed could ease.

**The 2018 hiking tantrum.** The cleanest natural experiment of the decade. The Fed hiked four times and ran QT, and the S&P fell about 20% into Christmas Eve 2018 — the regime's dependence on easy money laid bare. When Powell reversed course in early 2019 (no more hikes, then cuts, QT ended), the market roared back +31.5%. The 2018-19 round trip is the everything-bull in miniature: tighten liquidity and it falls; restore liquidity and it soars.

**The 2017 short-volatility boom and the February 2018 "Volmageddon."** The calm of 2017 made betting against volatility wildly profitable — until February 5, 2018, when volatility spiked, several short-volatility products lost most of their value overnight, and one collapsed entirely. It was a tiny preview of the danger in mistaking a calm regime for a permanent one: the strategies that work best in the late innings of an easy-money regime are often the ones that blow up first when it ends.

**The 2019 dovish pivot and the quiet QE restart.** After the 2018 hiking tantrum, 2019 showed how completely the market had come to depend on the Fed's posture. The Fed cut rates three times and, in the autumn, after a spasm in the overnight funding ("repo") market, restarted bond purchases — officially insisting these were technical operations and "not QE," even as the balance sheet expanded again. The market did not care what it was called; it cared that liquidity was flowing, and it rallied +31.5% on the year. The episode is the cleanest possible demonstration that the everything-bull ran on liquidity: take it away and stocks fall (2018), restore it and they soar (2019), whatever the official label.

**The international and emerging-market underperformance.** The everything-bull was, importantly, a *US* phenomenon above all. International developed-market stocks (the EAFE index) compounded only about +25% over 2014-2019, and emerging markets about +29% — respectable, but a fraction of the S&P's +98%. The US won because it had the deepest pool of long-duration growth companies (the tech giants), the most aggressive corporate buybacks, and a central bank whose easy money flowed first and most powerfully into US assets. The everything-bull lifted nearly every asset, but it lifted American growth stocks most of all — another way the regime concentrated its rewards in exactly the longest-duration corner of the market.

## When to own it: the playbook

This case study is not just history; it is a template for recognizing the *kind* of regime you are in and holding the right portfolio for it. Here is the lesson distilled into a decision.

**In a low-inflation, easy-liquidity regime** — rates falling or pinned low, central banks expanding their balance sheets, inflation tame and below target — the playbook is to **own duration, own growth, and own risk broadly.** Concretely, in such a regime you want the longest-duration assets (long bonds, growth/tech equities), you want to be invested rather than in cash (TINA is real when cash pays zero), and you want the 60/40 or something like it, because the negative stock-bond correlation makes bonds a genuine hedge. The everything-bull rewards being long, being long-duration, and refusing to overthink it. The 2010s proved that the simplest version of this — buy the index, hold, rebalance — beat almost everything fancier.

**But the regime is conditional, and the single thing that defines it is inflation.** This is the part the 2010s investor must internalize. The everything-bull is not a permanent state of the world; it is what happens *while inflation stays low*. The signal that the regime is turning is inflation rising and the central bank being *forced* to tighten into it (rather than choosing to ease). When that happens — as in 2021-22 — the playbook inverts: duration becomes a liability (long bonds and growth stocks fall hardest), the 60/40 stops protecting you (stocks and bonds fall together), cash stops being trash (it pays a real yield again), and the assets to own become the ones that thrive on inflation — commodities, real assets, and shorter-duration value stocks. (We map the full regime rotation in [real yields, the variable that prices everything](/blog/trading/cross-asset/real-yields-the-variable-that-prices-everything) and [mid-cycle expansion, the steady grind](/blog/trading/cross-asset/mid-cycle-expansion-the-steady-grind).)

So the practical sizing and rules a 2010s-literate allocator carries forward:

- **Identify the regime before you set the portfolio.** Two numbers tell you almost everything: the trend in inflation and the direction of the central bank. Falling/low inflation with an easing or neutral central bank is everything-bull territory — own duration and risk. Rising inflation forcing the bank to tighten is the danger zone — cut duration, raise cash, add real assets.
- **Do not size the everything-bull portfolio as if its returns are guaranteed.** The 2010s 60/40 returned double digits with shallow drawdowns; do not bake those numbers into your retirement plan as if they are normal. They were the output of a specific regime that has already reversed once.
- **Treat the stock-bond correlation as your single most important diversification gauge.** When it is negative, bonds hedge and the 60/40 is genuine diversification. When it turns positive, you need a *different* hedge — commodities, gold, cash, or inflation-linked bonds — because your bonds will fall with your stocks.
- **Size duration to the regime, not to the last decade's reward.** In the easy-money 2010s, stretching for duration — long bonds, growth stocks — was the highest-paying decision an investor could make. The same stretch in 2022 was the most punishing. Duration is leverage on the discount rate: it multiplies your gains when rates fall and your losses when they rise. Carry as much of it as the regime justifies and no more, and be ready to cut it fast when inflation forces the central bank's hand.
- **What invalidates the everything-bull thesis:** a sustained rise in inflation above the central bank's target that forces it to tighten *despite* weak growth. That is the exact configuration — inflation up, central bank tightening, the Fed unable to rescue the dip — that ended the 2010s and produced 2022. When you see it, the everything you were happily long becomes the everything that falls together, and the diversification you thought you owned evaporates exactly when you reach for it.

*This is educational, not investment advice.* The deeper point of the 2010s is humility about regimes: the most generous decade in living memory for owning assets was generous only *because* of conditions that were never guaranteed to last, and the investors hurt worst in 2022 were the ones who had come to believe the everything-bull was simply how markets work. Knowing it was a regime — and knowing which one you are in now — is the whole game.
