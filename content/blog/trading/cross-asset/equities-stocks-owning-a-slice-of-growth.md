> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A share is a residual claim on a company's profits — you get paid last, but you get the upside. Its price is just two numbers multiplied together: **earnings per share × the P/E multiple**, and the multiple is mostly driven by interest rates and risk appetite.
>
> - Over a lifetime, stocks have earned about **+6.5% per year after inflation** — versus **+1.7%** for bonds and **+0.4%** for cash. That gap is the whole reason to own them: compounding turns it into an order-of-magnitude difference over 50 years.
> - The price is paid for in volatility. Equities revisit **−30% to −55% peak-to-trough drawdowns** every cycle. The 2007–09 crash was −56.8%; 2020 was −33.9% in 33 days.
> - Most of a *good* year is often **multiple expansion, not earnings**. Falling real yields lift the multiple; rising real yields compress it. This is why "rates" matter so much to your stock portfolio.
> - The one number to remember: **the equity risk premium ≈ 6 percentage points a year over cash** — your pay for sitting through the drawdowns.

In March 2000, the S&P 500 traded at a price-to-earnings multiple of around 30. The companies underneath it were real, profitable, and growing. But buyers were paying \$30 for every \$1 of annual earnings — a bet that those earnings would keep compounding fast enough to justify the price. Over the next two and a half years, earnings barely fell. The *multiple* fell — from 30 down toward 15 — and the index dropped roughly 49%. Almost none of that loss was companies making less money. It was investors deciding the same dollar of earnings was worth half as much.

Eight years later, in the autumn of 2008, the opposite engine seized up. This time earnings *did* collapse as the financial system froze, and the index fell 56.8% from its 2007 peak. Two different crashes, two different causes — but the same two-part machine running in both: a stock's price is **what it earns** multiplied by **what people will pay for those earnings**. Understand those two levers and you understand most of what stocks do.

This post is about equities as an asset class — not how to pick a winning stock, but what owning *the whole market* gives you and costs you, and where it fits alongside bonds, gold, and cash. The diagram above is the mental model we will keep returning to: the index level is earnings per share times the multiple, and a 22% year can come entirely from the multiple, with the companies earning not a penny more.

![Index level equals earnings per share times P/E multiple decomposition](/imgs/blogs/equities-stocks-owning-a-slice-of-growth-1.png)

Let us build the whole thing from zero — what a share *is*, what moves the price, how it behaves over a lifetime, and the payoff at the end: when equities deserve a bigger or smaller slice of what you own.

## Foundations: a share is a residual claim on a company's profits

Start with the simplest possible company. You and a friend each put in \$50,000 to open a coffee shop. You now own a *business* worth \$100,000, split into two equal shares. You don't own a coffee machine or a lease — you own a **claim on the profits** the business throws off, forever, after every other obligation is paid. That claim is a *share of stock*. Multiply this picture by ten thousand and add a stock exchange where shares change hands, and you have a public company. Nothing else is different in principle.

The crucial word is **residual**. A company pays a strict pecking order. First it pays its suppliers and staff. Then it pays interest to anyone it borrowed from — its *bondholders* and banks (lenders who are owed a fixed amount). Then it pays its taxes. Whatever is left at the very bottom — the *residual* — belongs to the shareholders. A *residual claim* means you are paid last and you are paid out of what remains, not a fixed amount promised to you in advance.

That ordering cuts both ways, and it is the heart of why stocks behave the way they do:

- **You bear the first loss.** If profits shrink, the bondholders still get their fixed interest and your residual is what gets squeezed. If the company goes bankrupt, lenders are repaid from the assets before shareholders see anything — and shareholders very often see nothing.
- **You keep the unlimited upside.** The bondholder who lent the coffee shop \$50,000 gets their interest and their \$50,000 back — no more, no matter how successful the shop becomes. But if the shop turns into a chain worth \$10 million, that entire windfall, minus the fixed debts, flows to the residual claimants: the shareholders.

This is the fundamental trade of equity. *You take the risk that there is nothing left, in exchange for the right to everything that is left.* A bond is a promise; a stock is a leftover. That asymmetry — capped downside for the lender, uncapped upside for the owner — is why, over long stretches, owners are paid more than lenders. We will put a number on "more" shortly.

#### Worked example: the bankruptcy waterfall

To feel the residual claim in your bones, walk a company through failure. Imagine a business with \$100 million of assets that goes bankrupt. It owes \$60 million to lenders (bonds and banks) and had issued shares to its owners. When the assets are sold and the proceeds are distributed, the law enforces a strict *waterfall* — each tier is paid in full before the next sees a cent:

1. **Secured and senior lenders first.** They are owed \$60 million. The \$100 million of assets, sold in distress, fetch only — say — \$55 million. The lenders take all \$55 million and are still \$5 million short.
2. **Shareholders last.** There is nothing left. The residual is zero. The shareholders, who own the leftover, receive **\$0.** Their shares go to zero.

Now run the *good* version. The same company thrives; its assets and franchise are worth \$300 million. Lenders are still owed exactly \$60 million — they get their \$60 million and not a dollar more, because a loan is a fixed promise. Everything above that — $\$300\text{m} - \$60\text{m} = \$240$ million — belongs to the shareholders. The lender's outcome was \$60 million in both the thriving and the merely-fine case; the shareholder's outcome ranged from \$0 to \$240 million. The intuition: the bondholder owns a *capped, fixed* claim and the shareholder owns the *uncapped residual* — which is exactly why the shareholder must be paid a higher average return to accept the zero-floor risk.

### Where the money actually reaches you: earnings, dividends, buybacks

A company that earns a profit can do three things with it, and as a shareholder, all three are yours:

1. **Pay it out as a dividend.** A *dividend* is a cash payment, usually quarterly, sent directly to shareholders — your literal slice of the profits, in your pocket. If a company earns \$5 per share and pays \$2 of it out, you receive \$2 per share in cash.
2. **Buy back its own shares.** In a *buyback* (or *share repurchase*), the company uses profits to purchase its own shares on the market and cancel them. There are now fewer shares, so each remaining share owns a bigger slice of the same company. It is a dividend in disguise — instead of cash in your hand, your ownership percentage quietly grows.
3. **Reinvest it in the business.** *Retained earnings* are profits plowed back into new stores, factories, research — the engine of future growth. You don't see this money now; you see it later, as larger future earnings.

A share's value is, in the end, the present value of all the cash the business will eventually hand its owners — whether through dividends now or reinvestment that becomes bigger dividends later. You don't need to track which bucket the money sits in; over a long horizon it all flows to the residual claimant, which is you.

### The one equation that matters: price = earnings × multiple

Here is the formula the entire post hangs on. For a single share:

$$
P = \text{EPS} \times \frac{P}{E}
$$

where $P$ is the **price** of the share, $\text{EPS}$ is **earnings per share** (the company's annual profit divided by the number of shares), and $P/E$ is the **price-to-earnings multiple** — how many dollars investors will pay for each \$1 of annual earnings. The same identity holds for a whole index like the S&P 500: the index level is the index's aggregate earnings per share times the market's P/E.

Read it slowly, because it is doing two jobs at once. It is an *identity* — it is always exactly true, because P/E is literally defined as price divided by earnings, so multiplying it back by earnings returns the price. But it is also a *decomposition*: it splits every move in the price into two distinct, separately-driven sources. Earnings can rise. The multiple can rise. Both can rise. They can move in opposite directions. When you learn to ask "of this 20% move, how much was earnings and how much was the multiple?", you have learned to read the market.

#### Worked example: pricing one share two ways

You are valuing a single share of a company. It earns \$10 per share this year ($\text{EPS} = \$10$), and the market is currently paying 18 times earnings ($P/E = 18$).

$$
P = \$10 \times 18 = \$180
$$

The share is worth \$180. Now suppose nothing about the company changes — it still earns \$10 — but interest rates fall and investors grow more optimistic, so they are now willing to pay 22 times earnings:

$$
P = \$10 \times 22 = \$220
$$

The share is now \$220. You made \$40, a +22% gain, and the company **earned not one cent more**. The entire move came from the multiple. The intuition: a stock's price is partly the company and partly the mood of the people pricing it, and the mood can move the price a lot all by itself.

Now scale that exact example to the whole index, using the curated numbers from the cover figure. If the S&P 500's aggregate earnings are \$240 per "share" of the index and the multiple is 18, the index sits at $\$240 \times 18 = 4{,}320$. Let the multiple expand to 22 on the *same* \$240 of earnings and the index is $\$240 \times 22 = 5{,}280$ — a +22% year. This is not a thought experiment; it is roughly what a strong "everything re-rates higher" year looks like. Most of the gain is the multiple.

### Total return: price plus the dividends along the way

One more foundational definition, because the headlines get it wrong constantly. When you hear "the S&P 500 returned X% last year," that X is almost always the *total return* — the change in price **plus** the dividends you received and reinvested.

$$
\text{Total return} = \underbrace{\frac{P_{\text{end}} - P_{\text{start}}}{P_{\text{start}}}}_{\text{price return}} + \underbrace{\frac{\text{dividends}}{P_{\text{start}}}}_{\text{dividend yield}}
$$

The first term is the *price return* — what the index level did. The second is the *dividend yield* — the cash paid out along the way, as a percentage of what you paid. Over the very long run, reinvested dividends are not a rounding error: they have historically supplied a large chunk of the total. Every return number in this post that says "total return" already includes them; the drawdown numbers, which measure how far the *price* fell peak-to-trough, do not. Keep the two straight and you will never be confused by why "the market is down 50% but my long-run return is still positive."

## What drives equity prices: earnings, the multiple, and rates

We now have the machine: price = earnings × multiple. Everything that moves a stock has to move it *through one of those two levers*. Let us take them one at a time, because they behave completely differently and respond to different forces.

### Lever 1: earnings growth — the slow, real engine

Over the long run, the first lever does the heavy lifting. Companies retain part of their profits, reinvest them, and grow. Aggregate corporate earnings tend to grow over decades roughly in line with the economy plus inflation — call it a mid-single-digit pace in nominal terms in a healthy economy. This is the part of your return that comes from the businesses actually getting bigger and more profitable. It is *real* in the sense that it reflects more cars sold, more software subscribed, more chips shipped.

Earnings growth is relatively slow and steady from year to year — and then it isn't. In recessions, earnings can fall sharply: corporate profits roughly halved in the 2008–09 downturn. That is the danger of the residual claim showing up in the numerator of our equation. But across a full cycle, the earnings line trends up and to the right, and that upward drift is the deep source of the equity return.

Where does the growth actually come from? Recall the three uses of profit: dividends out, buybacks, and reinvestment. The reinvested portion — the *retained earnings* — is the seed of next year's bigger earnings. If a company keeps half its profit and reinvests it at a healthy return on that capital, those retained dollars become new earnings, which are partly retained again, and the earnings base compounds. This is why a company that pays *no* dividend at all can still be a phenomenal investment: it is reinvesting your slice of the profit on your behalf, and the value shows up as a rising share price rather than a check in the mail.

#### Worked example: a no-dividend compounder vs a high payer

Two companies each earn \$10 per share today. Company A pays it all out as dividends and grows earnings 2% a year. Company B pays nothing, reinvests everything at a 12% return on that capital, and so grows earnings about 12% a year. Hold each for 15 years and compare the *earnings per share* the business throws off:

- **Company A:** $\$10 \times (1.02)^{15} = \$13.46$ of earnings, plus 15 years of \$10-ish dividends collected along the way (~\$150 in cash, undiscounted).
- **Company B:** $\$10 \times (1.12)^{15} = \$54.74$ of earnings — the business now earns over five times as much per share, and at the same multiple the share price would be worth roughly five times more.

Company B never sent you a dollar, yet it almost certainly built more wealth, because it compounded your profit internally at 12% instead of handing it back to earn 2%. The intuition: a dividend is not free money — it is profit the company chose *not* to reinvest, and whether that is good for you depends entirely on what return the company could have earned on it.

### Lever 2: the multiple — the fast, sentiment-and-rates engine

The second lever, the P/E multiple, is where the drama lives. It can swing from 10 to 30 and back within a decade without earnings doing anything unusual. The multiple is the market's collective answer to one question: *how much is a dollar of future earnings worth to me today?* Two forces set that answer.

**Force one: interest rates — specifically real yields.** This is the single most important driver to internalize, so we will go slowly. A stock is a claim on earnings stretching out for decades into the future. To value a stream of future dollars, you have to *discount* them — a dollar arriving in 10 years is worth less than a dollar today, because you could have earned a safe return in the meantime. The rate you discount at is tied to the *real yield* — the interest rate on safe government bonds after subtracting expected inflation, which is the genuinely safe return your future dollars are competing against. (We unpack this in detail in [real vs nominal yields, the master signal](/blog/trading/macro-trading/real-vs-nominal-inflation-real-yields-master-signal).)

When the real yield falls, you discount future earnings less harshly, so those far-off dollars are worth *more* today, so you will pay a *higher* multiple for them. When the real yield rises, the opposite: distant earnings are discounted more brutally, worth less today, so the multiple *compresses*. This is why "the Fed is cutting rates" tends to be rocket fuel for the multiple, and "yields are surging" tends to crush it — even when nothing about company earnings has changed.

![Falling real yields lift the P/E multiple through the discount-rate chain](/imgs/blogs/equities-stocks-owning-a-slice-of-growth-6.png)

The diagram traces the chain end to end. Real yields fall from 2.0% to 1.0% → future earnings are discounted at a lower rate → their present value rises (especially the far-off, years-5-to-30 earnings of fast growers) → the P/E expands from 18 to 22 → the index re-rates 22% higher on identical earnings. Run it backward — rates rise — and the same chain *compresses* the multiple. A "growth" stock, whose value sits mostly in distant earnings, is the most sensitive of all to this: it is the longest-*duration* asset in your equity book, where *duration* means how far in the future its cash flows lie. (The same long-duration logic governs long government bonds — see [government bonds, the risk-free anchor and duration](/blog/trading/cross-asset/government-bonds-the-risk-free-anchor-and-duration).)

**Force two: risk appetite.** Even at a fixed interest rate, the multiple breathes with how much *fear* is in the system. When investors are confident — credit is easy, the economy is humming, recent returns have been good — they demand a smaller premium for bearing equity risk, which means a higher multiple. When they are frightened, they demand a larger premium, which means a lower multiple. This is the "risk-on / risk-off" rotation that governs which assets are bid up and which are dumped on any given day; we map it in full in [risk-on, risk-off: how money rotates](/blog/trading/macro-trading/risk-on-risk-off-how-money-rotates). For equities, risk-on lifts the multiple and risk-off slams it.

### What is a "fair" multiple? The earnings yield vs the bond yield

A useful way to sanity-check whether the multiple is rich or cheap is to flip it upside down. The reciprocal of the P/E is the **earnings yield** — earnings divided by price, expressed as a percentage. A P/E of 20 is an earnings yield of $1/20 = 5\%$; a P/E of 10 is an earnings yield of 10%. The earnings yield is, roughly, the return the business's profits represent on the price you pay — and it lets you compare a stock directly against a bond, because a bond's yield is also "what I earn per dollar paid."

The comparison makes the rates-drive-the-multiple story concrete. If safe government bonds yield 2%, an earnings yield of 5% (P/E of 20) looks generous — you are paid three extra points to own the risky residual claim, so investors happily bid the multiple up. But if bond yields rise to 5%, that same 5% earnings yield offers *no* extra reward over the safe bond, so investors demand a higher earnings yield to compensate — which means a *lower* P/E. The multiple compresses until the earnings yield is once again attractive relative to bonds. This is the same discount-rate mechanism from a different angle: when the safe alternative pays more, the price you will pay for risky earnings falls.

#### Worked example: what a higher bond yield does to the "fair" multiple

Suppose investors want a 3-point equity risk premium over the safe bond yield — that is, they want the earnings yield to sit 3 points above bonds.

- **Bonds at 2%:** required earnings yield $= 2\% + 3\% = 5\%$, so the fair P/E $= 1 / 0.05 = 20$.
- **Bonds at 5%:** required earnings yield $= 5\% + 3\% = 8\%$, so the fair P/E $= 1 / 0.08 = 12.5$.

Holding earnings fixed, the fair multiple fell from 20 to 12.5 — a 38% lower index level — purely because the safe alternative got more attractive. The intuition: a stock's multiple is anchored to the yield on safe bonds, so a big move in rates can revalue the entire equity market even with profits unchanged. This is why an equity investor must watch the bond market.

#### Worked example: splitting a 25% year into its two sources

The S&P 500 returned +25.0% in 2024 (total return). Suppose — illustratively — that corporate earnings grew about 8% that year. Where did the other ~17 points come from? The multiple.

Let's reconstruct it with round numbers. Start the year at an index of 4,800, with earnings of \$240 and a multiple of 20: $\$240 \times 20 = 4{,}800$. Over the year, earnings grow 8% to \$259, and the multiple expands from 20 to 22.5:

$$
\text{End} = \$259 \times 22.5 = 5{,}828
$$

That is a price return of $5{,}828 / 4{,}800 - 1 = +21.4\%$, before dividends. Of that, the earnings did $1.08\times$ and the multiple did $22.5/20 = 1.125\times$. Multiply them: $1.08 \times 1.125 = 1.215$, i.e. +21.5%. **Roughly eight points came from companies earning more; roughly thirteen came from investors paying more per dollar of earnings.** The lesson: in a strong year, the multiple usually does more of the work than the businesses do — which is exactly why strong years are fragile, because the multiple can hand the gains back as fast as it gave them.

## How equities behave: high return, deep drawdowns, long compounding

We have the drivers. Now the behavior — what it actually *feels like* to own this asset across a lifetime. Three facts define equity behavior: the long-run return is high, the path to it is violent, and the magic is in the compounding.

### The long-run return: ~+6.5% per year, after inflation

Here is the number that justifies the entire asset class. Across 124 years (1900–2023), measured carefully across the whole US market and adjusted for inflation, **US equities returned about +6.5% per year in real terms.** The same study (the Dimson-Marsh-Staunton dataset, published in the UBS / Credit Suisse Global Investment Returns Yearbook) puts **US bonds at +1.7% real** and **US Treasury bills — essentially cash — at +0.4% real.** World equities, blending every country, came in at +5.1% real.

"Real" matters enormously here. A +6.5% *real* return means +6.5% of genuine purchasing power gained every year, on average, *after* inflation has eaten its share. That is the rate at which the slice of the world's productive output that you own actually grows.

The +6.5% does not sound dramatic next to +1.7% — until you compound it across a working life.

![Growth of one real dollar over fifty years in stocks bonds and cash](/imgs/blogs/equities-stocks-owning-a-slice-of-growth-2.png)

#### Worked example: the same dollar, three assets, fifty years

You invest \$1 of purchasing power and hold it for 50 years at each asset's long-run real rate. Compounding means multiplying by $(1 + r)$ once per year, so the end value is $\$1 \times (1+r)^{50}$.

- **Stocks at +6.5% real:** $(1.065)^{50} = 23.3$. Your \$1 of purchasing power becomes about **\$23.30.**
- **Bonds at +1.7% real:** $(1.017)^{50} = 2.32$. Your \$1 becomes about **\$2.32.**
- **Cash at +0.4% real:** $(1.004)^{50} = 1.22$. Your \$1 becomes about **\$1.22.**

Stop and stare at that. Over a working lifetime, real purchasing power held in stocks grew roughly *ten times* as much as in bonds and *nineteen times* as much as in cash. A gap of "only" 4.8 percentage points a year, run through the exponential, becomes an order of magnitude. The intuition: with compounding, small annual edges are not small — they are the whole game, and equities exist in a portfolio to capture the biggest sustainable edge there is.

### The path is violent: −30% to −55% drawdowns every cycle

That +6.5% is the destination. The road is a horror show, and you do not get the return unless you survive the road. A *drawdown* is the peak-to-trough decline — how far the price fell from its highest point before recovering. For equities, deep drawdowns are not rare accidents; they are a recurring feature, the price of admission.

![Major S&P 500 peak to trough drawdowns from 2000 to 2022](/imgs/blogs/equities-stocks-owning-a-slice-of-growth-4.png)

The four worst of the modern era, as price-index declines:

| Episode | Peak-to-trough decline | What broke |
|---|---|---|
| 2007–09 Global Financial Crisis | **−56.8%** | Earnings collapsed as the financial system froze |
| 2000–02 dot-com bust | **−49%** | The multiple deflated from ~30 toward ~15 |
| 2020 COVID crash | **−33.9%** (Feb 19 → Mar 23) | Multiple crash on a global-shutdown shock, in 33 days |
| 2022 rate shock | **−25.4%** | Multiple compressed as real yields surged |

Notice how cleanly these split by *which lever broke*. The 2000–02 bust and the 2022 selloff were almost pure **multiple compression** — earnings held up, but investors repriced what a dollar of earnings was worth. The 2008 crash was **earnings collapse** layered on top of a multiple crash. The COVID crash was a 33-day **risk-appetite** convulsion. The shaded band on the figure marks the recurring −30% to −55% zone: roughly once a decade, owning equities means watching a third to a half of the price evaporate, and not knowing in the moment whether it stops there.

This is not a bug to be engineered away — it is the *mechanism by which the premium is earned*. If stocks never crashed, they would be safe, and safe assets are not paid +6.5% real. You are paid the premium precisely *because* you must hold through the terror. The investor who sells at the bottom of a −50% drawdown converts a temporary paper loss into a permanent one and forfeits the recovery. The discipline to *not do that* is, in a real sense, the skill the entire +6.5% rewards.

### Volatility: the year-to-year ride

Even outside crashes, equity returns swing widely from year to year. The chart below is every S&P 500 calendar-year total return from 2014 to 2024 — the texture of "normal" equity volatility.

![S&P 500 calendar year total returns 2014 to 2024 green and red bars](/imgs/blogs/equities-stocks-owning-a-slice-of-growth-3.png)

Look at the spread. The best year shown is **+31.5%** (2019); the worst is **−18.1%** (2022). The average across these eleven years was roughly +14% per year — but you did not earn 14% every year. You earned +31.5%, then +18.4%, then +28.7%, then a gut-punch −18.1%, then +26.3%. The *average* is calm; the *experience* is a roller coaster. This dispersion — formally the *volatility*, or standard deviation of returns, which for US equities runs around 15–20% per year — is the statistical fingerprint of the asset. Bonds wobble a few percent; stocks routinely move 20+ points in a year in either direction.

A subtle but important point hides in those bars: the down years are fewer than the up years (two reds out of eleven here), but they are deep. Equities make money *most* of the time and lose money *badly* some of the time. The long-run +6.5% real is the net of many good years minus a few brutal ones.

There is a deeper reason volatility itself quietly costs you, beyond the discomfort — it is called *volatility drag*, and it falls straight out of the asymmetric recovery math. Because gains compound off a smaller base after a loss, a *volatile* path to a given average return ends up *below* a smooth one. Watch it with two extreme cases that have the same simple average:

#### Worked example: why a smoother path keeps more money

You start with \$100. Path A returns a steady +5% two years running. Path B returns +30% then −20% — same +5% *simple average* per year. Compound each:

- **Path A (smooth):** $\$100 \times 1.05 \times 1.05 = \$110.25$.
- **Path B (volatile):** $\$100 \times 1.30 \times 0.80 = \$104.00$.

Identical average return, yet the volatile path left you with \$6.25 less, because the −20% bit off the larger \$130 base. The wider the swings, the bigger this drag. The intuition: in compounding, *avoiding the deep down years is worth more than catching the big up years* — which is the real, mathematical reason an allocator pairs equities with assets that cushion the drawdowns rather than chasing maximum raw return.

### The equity risk premium: your pay for the ride

We can now name the thing precisely. The **equity risk premium (ERP)** is the *extra* return equities earn over safe assets, as compensation for all that volatility and drawdown risk. Using the long-run real numbers:

$$
\text{ERP over cash} = 6.5\% - 0.4\% \approx 6.1 \text{ percentage points per year}
$$

$$
\text{ERP over bonds} = 6.5\% - 1.7\% \approx 4.8 \text{ percentage points per year}
$$

Roughly **six points a year over cash, ~five over bonds** — that is the market's long-run rent for bearing equity risk. It is not a law of nature and it is not guaranteed in any given decade; it is the historical average reward for being the residual claimant who holds through the crashes. Every other asset in your portfolio is, in some sense, measured against this premium: bonds and cash give up most of it in exchange for not putting you through the −50% drawdowns.

#### Worked example: is the premium worth it for a 10-year horizon?

You have \$10,000 and a 10-year horizon. Two simple paths, using the long-run real rates:

- **All cash, +0.4% real:** $\$10{,}000 \times (1.004)^{10} = \$10{,}407$. After a decade, you preserved your purchasing power and gained \$407. You never lost a wink of sleep.
- **All stocks, +6.5% real:** $\$10{,}000 \times (1.065)^{10} = \$18{,}771$. You nearly doubled your real wealth — but somewhere in that decade you very likely sat through at least one 20–30% drawdown, watching \$15,000 of paper value briefly become \$10,500.

The difference — about \$8,360 of extra real purchasing power — *is* the equity risk premium, made concrete. The intuition: the premium is real money, but it is paid only to investors who can hold their position through the drawdown that earns it. Your true "risk tolerance" is whatever drawdown you can sit through without selling.

## Global equities: same engine, different risk dials

So far "stocks" has meant the US market. But equities are a global asset class, and the same machine — earnings × multiple — runs in every market. What differs is the *risk setting* on the dial. The standard three buckets:

- **US equities** (e.g. the S&P 500): the largest, deepest, most-owned market, heavy in technology and global-brand companies.
- **International developed** (MSCI EAFE — *Europe, Australasia, Far East*): the rich-world markets outside the US and Canada — think Europe and Japan. Same legal protections and mature economies, different sector mix (more banks, industrials, energy), and a currency exposure layered on top.
- **Emerging markets** (MSCI EM): faster-growing but less stable economies — China, India, Taiwan, Brazil, and others. Higher potential growth, but also higher political risk, currency risk, and governance risk.

![US versus international developed versus emerging market annual returns grouped bars](/imgs/blogs/equities-stocks-owning-a-slice-of-growth-5.png)

The grouped bars carry two lessons at once. First, **they move together** — in 2018 all three fell, in 2019 all three soared, in 2022 all three were hammered. There is a shared global "equity beta": when global risk appetite turns, it turns for everyone. *Beta* here just means sensitivity to the broad market's moves. Second, **the amplitude differs by bucket.** Emerging markets swing the widest: +37.3% in 2017, then −20.1% in 2022. The same engine, but EM has the volume turned up — more upside in good years, more pain in bad ones.

There is a third force hiding inside the international buckets: **currency.** When you, a dollar-based investor, buy European or Japanese shares, your return has two parts — what the foreign shares did *in their own currency*, and what that currency did against the dollar. If German stocks rise 10% in euros but the euro falls 5% against the dollar over the same period, your dollar return is roughly $1.10 \times 0.95 - 1 = +4.5\%$, not +10%. The strong-dollar years of the 2010s were a major reason US investors saw EAFE and EM lag the S&P 500: even when the foreign markets rose locally, a rising dollar clawed back a chunk of the gain when translated home. This currency layer is why international equity returns can diverge sharply from US returns *even when the underlying businesses do similarly well* — and it ties directly into the dollar's central role across all of markets.

The deeper reason to care about the regional split is **valuation, not just diversification.** Because the engine is identical (earnings × multiple), the difference in long-run regional returns comes mostly from *the price you pay going in*. For much of the 2010s and into the 2020s, US equities traded at meaningfully higher multiples than EAFE or EM — you paid more per dollar of earnings to own American companies. A higher starting multiple is a headwind to future returns (there is less room to expand and more to compress), which is the quiet case for not putting your entire equity sleeve in the single most expensive market. Cheap regions are not cheap for no reason — they carry more risk — but the residual-claim premium is, in the end, *paid out of the price you pay*.

#### Worked example: the diversification that didn't help, and the one that did

Suppose in 2017 you split \$30,000 evenly across the three buckets: \$10,000 each in US, EAFE, and EM. Returns that year were +21.8% (US), +25.0% (EAFE), +37.3% (EM).

$$
\$10{,}000(1.218) + \$10{,}000(1.250) + \$10{,}000(1.373) = \$12{,}180 + \$12{,}500 + \$13{,}730 = \$38{,}410
$$

A +28.0% year — EM did the heavy lifting. Now 2022, same \$10,000 each: −18.1%, −14.5%, −20.1%.

$$
\$8{,}190 + \$8{,}550 + \$7{,}990 = \$24{,}730 \quad (-17.6\%)
$$

Spreading across three regions barely softened the blow — because in a global risk-off year, all equity regions fall together. The intuition: diversifying *within* the equity sleeve smooths the edges and shifts your growth tilt, but it does **not** rescue you in a real equity bear market. For that you need a different asset class entirely — which is the whole point of the next section.

## How equities correlate with the rest of the portfolio

An asset is interesting for what it does *alongside* the others, not in isolation. *Correlation* measures whether two assets tend to move together (+1.0), oppositely (−1.0), or independently (0.0). For equities, the correlations are the difference between a portfolio that survives a crash and one that doesn't.

### Equities vs bonds: the hedge that changes sign

The most important cross-asset relationship in all of investing is between stocks and government bonds — because for two decades it was the foundation of every "balanced" portfolio, and then it broke. The classic logic: when stocks crash because growth is failing, central banks cut interest rates, which *raises bond prices* (bond prices move opposite to yields). So bonds rally exactly when stocks fall. Bonds are the *hedge* — the thing that goes up when your growth engine goes down.

The catch: that hedge only works in some regimes. The relationship is not fixed; it switches sign.

![Stock bond rolling correlation switches sign by regime from 1980 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/equities-stocks-owning-a-slice-of-growth-7.png)

The chart traces the rolling 24-month stock-bond correlation across eras. Three regimes:

- **Pre-2000: positive (+0.20 to +0.35).** When *inflation* is the dominant worry, bad inflation news hurts both stocks (it crushes the multiple) and bonds (it raises yields) at the same time. They fall together. No hedge.
- **2000–2021: negative (−0.25 to −0.40).** When *growth* is the dominant worry and inflation is dormant, a growth scare sinks stocks but sends investors fleeing into bonds, which rally. The classic hedge works beautifully. This is the era that made "60/40" famous.
- **2022 onward: positive again (+0.45 to +0.55).** Inflation came back, the Fed hiked aggressively, and stocks *and* bonds fell together — 2022 saw stocks −18.1% and bonds −13.0% in the same year, the worst year for a balanced portfolio in generations.

The lesson for an equity allocator is sharp: **bonds hedge your stocks when the market's fear is about growth, and fail to hedge them when the fear is about inflation.** Whether you can lean on bonds to cushion your equity drawdown depends on which regime you are in — which is why a serious allocator watches the inflation-vs-growth driver as closely as the level of stocks itself.

### Equities vs credit and REITs: the same growth bet in disguise

Some assets that *look* like diversifiers are really equities wearing a costume. Two big ones:

- **Credit (especially high-yield bonds):** lending to riskier companies. When the economy sours and equity holders fear for the residual, the same companies' ability to pay their debts is questioned — so high-yield bonds sell off right alongside stocks. The historical stock-to-high-yield correlation runs around **+0.75**. High yield is mostly an equity bet with a coupon attached.
- **REITs (real-estate investment trusts):** companies that own income-producing property. They are listed equities, sensitive to the same growth and rate forces — stock-to-REIT correlation also around **+0.75**. In the 2008 crash, REITs fell −37.7%, nearly identical to the S&P's −37.0% total return.

The trap: an investor who "diversifies" out of stocks and into high-yield bonds and REITs has often diversified into *more of the same risk*. In a real equity bear market, all three crater together. Genuine diversification has to come from somewhere with a *different* engine.

### Equities vs gold: roughly uncorrelated

Gold is the cleanest example of a different engine. Its long-run correlation with equities is around **zero** (~+0.05) — it dances to its own music (real yields, the dollar, fear) rather than to corporate earnings. That near-zero correlation is precisely what makes a sliver of it useful: it can rise, or at least hold, when stocks are falling, because nothing about a corporate-earnings recession mechanically hurts a bar of metal. We give gold its own full treatment in [gold: money, insurance, or just a rock](/blog/trading/cross-asset/gold-money-insurance-or-just-a-rock); for the equity holder, the one-line takeaway is that gold's value to a portfolio comes *entirely* from its low correlation, not from its return, which over the very long run has only modestly beaten cash.

## Common misconceptions

**"Stocks are basically a casino / gambling."** A casino is a *negative-sum* game: the house edge guarantees that, on average, players lose. Equities are *positive-sum*: you own a claim on real businesses that, in aggregate, produce more value over time — that is the source of the +6.5% real long-run return. Individual stock-picking over short horizons can resemble gambling, but owning the whole market for decades is the opposite: it is being the house, collecting the productivity of the global economy. The volatility feels like a casino; the expected value does not.

**"A higher dividend yield means a better, safer stock."** Not reliably. A dividend is just one of three ways earnings reach you (alongside buybacks and reinvestment), and a very high yield is often a *warning* — the price has fallen because the market doubts the business, mechanically inflating the yield. Total return, not dividend yield, is what compounds your wealth. A company that pays no dividend but reinvests at high returns can build far more value than a high-yielder in decline.

**"If the index is at an all-time high, it must be due for a crash."** All-time highs are the *normal state* of a rising market — an asset that compounds at +6.5% real spends most of its life at or near new highs. From 2014 to 2024 the S&P set hundreds of record closes on its way up. "It's at a high, so it must fall" confuses the *level* (which trends up forever) with the *valuation* (the multiple, which is what actually tells you whether it's expensive). A new high at a cheap multiple is very different from a new high at a rich one.

**"Stocks always come back, so they're safe if you just wait."** Mostly true for a broad, diversified index over long horizons — and dangerous if over-applied. The US market has always recovered, but the wait has sometimes been brutal: it took years to claw back from 2000 and 2008, and *individual* stocks and *single-country* markets have gone to zero or stagnated for decades (Japan's index took over 30 years to reclaim its 1989 peak). "Stocks come back" is a statement about a diversified index held long enough to outlast a drawdown — not a license to ignore concentration, leverage, or a horizon shorter than the next cycle.

**"A 50% drawdown is undone by a 50% gain."** The cruelest arithmetic in investing. If \$100 falls 50% to \$50, it takes a *+100%* gain — not +50% — to get back to \$100, because the gain is calculated off the smaller base. This is why deep drawdowns are so costly and why surviving them without forced selling matters so much: the recovery math is asymmetric and works against you.

**"I'll just sell before the crash and buy back at the bottom."** Almost no one does this reliably, including professionals, and the cost of being wrong is severe. The market's biggest *up* days cluster right next to its biggest *down* days — often in the same chaotic week of a crash — so an investor who steps out to dodge the decline tends to miss the violent rebound too. Historically, missing just a handful of the best days over a few decades cuts the long-run return dramatically, because those days do a disproportionate share of the compounding. "Time in the market beats timing the market" is a cliché precisely because the arithmetic of missed rebound days is so unforgiving. The tactical lean-in and trim tilts later in this post are *modest weight adjustments*, not all-in/all-out calls — the difference matters enormously.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The dot-com multiple deflation (2000–2002).** At the peak in March 2000, the S&P's multiple was around 30 — investors paying \$30 for each \$1 of earnings, betting on relentless future growth. Earnings over the next two years barely declined. But the *multiple* deflated toward 15 as the optimism drained out, and the index fell roughly 49%. This is the purest real-world demonstration of Lever 2: a crash caused almost entirely by the market repricing what a dollar of earnings was worth, with the companies' actual profits nearly unchanged. If you only watched earnings, you missed the entire bear market.

**The Global Financial Crisis (2007–2009).** This was Lever 1 and Lever 2 failing together. As the banking system froze, corporate earnings collapsed — roughly halving — *and* the multiple compressed as fear spiked. The S&P fell 56.8% peak-to-trough; the 2008 calendar-year total return was −37.0%. REITs (−37.7%) and high yield (−26.2%) fell right alongside, proving they were equity risk in disguise. The lesson allocators carried out of 2008: in a genuine systemic crash, every "risky" asset is correlated to roughly +1, and only truly different engines — government bonds (which rallied, long Treasuries +25.9%) and to a degree gold — actually diversified.

**The COVID crash and the policy rocket (2020).** From February 19 to March 23, 2020 — just 33 days — the S&P fell 33.9% as a global shutdown triggered pure, maximal risk-off. Then central banks slashed rates to zero and flooded the system with liquidity. With the discount rate crushed, the multiple exploded back upward, and the index *finished 2020 up 18.4% on a total-return basis* despite the deepest, fastest crash in history months earlier. It was the discount-rate chain (Figure 6) run forward at full throttle: rates to zero, multiple to the moon. A vivid lesson that *what drives the multiple* (rates and risk appetite) can reverse a crash faster than earnings could ever explain.

**The 2022 regime break.** The Fed hiked rates at the fastest pace in 40 years to fight inflation. Real yields surged from deeply negative to clearly positive. Per the discount-rate chain run *backward*, the multiple compressed hard, and the S&P fell 25.4% peak-to-trough (−18.1% on the year). Crucially, **bonds fell too** (−13.0%), because rising yields hurt them directly — the stock-bond correlation flipped positive, and the classic 60/40 hedge failed for the first time in a generation, posting roughly −16%, its worst year since the 1930s. The single most important regime lesson of the decade: when the dominant macro driver is *inflation/rates* rather than *growth*, bonds stop cushioning your equity drawdown.

**The single-year stress tests.** Strip out the multi-year crashes and look at the worst single calendar years: 2008 at −37.0% total return, 1973–74 at roughly −37% combined, 2022 at −18.1%. These are the "how bad can one year be" anchors. An equity-heavy portfolio has to be sized so that a −37% year is survivable — financially and, just as importantly, *emotionally*, since the investor who panic-sells in such a year locks in the loss and forfeits the rebound.

## When to own equities: the allocation playbook

Here is the payoff. Equities are the **growth engine** of a portfolio — the holding you own to actually compound real wealth over decades. Almost every long-horizon portfolio has equities as its largest single sleeve, for the simple reason established above: nothing else has historically compounded purchasing power like a +6.5% real return. The questions are not *whether* to own equities but *how much*, *when to lean in or trim*, and *what tells you you're wrong*.

![When equities deserve a bigger or smaller slice across regimes](/imgs/blogs/equities-stocks-owning-a-slice-of-growth-8.png)

### The core stance: equities are the default growth holding

Start from the recognition that the equity sleeve is doing one job: capturing the long-run risk premium. For a long-horizon investor, the base case is a *large* equity weight — often the majority of a growth-oriented portfolio — paired with enough bonds, cash, and uncorrelated assets to survive the drawdowns without forced selling. The bonds and gold are not there to compete with stocks on return; they are there so you can *hold* the stocks through a −50% year. The matrix above maps the tactical tilts around that core.

The reason the *pairing* matters as much as the equity weight itself comes straight from the correlation section. Stocks are paired with government bonds because, in growth-driven downturns, bonds rally as stocks fall — they refill the part of the portfolio you'd otherwise be tempted to sell at the bottom. They are paired with a sliver of gold because gold's near-zero correlation gives the portfolio a second engine that doesn't depend on corporate earnings at all. And they are *not* meaningfully diversified by high-yield bonds or REITs, which are equity risk in costume (recall the +0.75 correlations) — owning those *instead* of more stocks gives you the equity drawdown without the full equity premium. The art of allocation is choosing partners for equities that zig when equities zag, then sizing the whole thing so you never have to sell the growth engine at the moment it's cheapest.

### When to lean in (overweight)

Tilt your equity weight *up* when the two price levers are both pointing your way:

- **Early-cycle / recovery.** Growth is turning up from a low base, earnings are about to inflect higher (Lever 1 turning positive), and the worst of a downturn is behind you. Historically the strongest equity returns come early in an expansion.
- **Rates falling.** When central banks are cutting and real yields are dropping, the discount-rate chain (Figure 6) is running forward — the multiple has a tailwind. Falling rates plus recovering earnings is the best combination equities ever get.
- **Cheap multiples.** When the P/E is below its long-run average (say, sub-16x), you are buying earnings on sale; the multiple has more room to expand than compress. Cheap valuation is the single best long-horizon predictor of strong future equity returns.
- **Rising risk appetite from a fearful base.** Buying when others are still scared — when the risk premium is wide — is buying the premium cheaply.

### When to trim (underweight)

Tilt *down* when the levers point the other way:

- **Late-cycle / overheating.** Growth is slowing from a high base, earnings are near a peak and at risk of rolling over, and the easy gains of the expansion are spent.
- **Rising real yields.** When yields are surging — the discount-rate chain running backward — the multiple faces a headwind regardless of earnings. 2022 is the cautionary tale.
- **Rich multiples.** When the P/E is well above average (22x+), much of the future return has been pulled forward into today's price; the multiple has more room to compress than expand. Rich valuation doesn't *time* a crash, but it lowers the expected return and raises the downside.
- **Euphoric risk appetite.** When the premium is thin and everyone is bullish, you are being paid the least to bear the most risk.

### Position sizing and the role of time horizon

The single most important input to your equity weight is not the cycle — it is your **time horizon**, because horizon determines whether the drawdowns are survivable.

#### Worked example: sizing the equity sleeve to a horizon

You have \$100,000. The honest question is not "what's the upside?" but "what's the worst drawdown I can hold through without selling?" Model a −37% equity year (the 2008 anchor) on two different equity weights:

- **80% equities / 20% cash:** the equity sleeve is \$80,000. A −37% year takes it to \$50,400; total portfolio $\$50{,}400 + \$20{,}000 = \$70{,}400$, a **−29.6%** hit.
- **50% equities / 50% cash:** the equity sleeve is \$50,000 → \$31,500; total $\$31{,}500 + \$50{,}000 = \$81{,}500$, a **−18.5%** hit.

If you have a 30-year horizon and can stomach the −29.6% without flinching, the 80% weight captures far more of the +6.5% real premium over time. If you'll need the money in five years — or you know you'll panic-sell at −30% — the 50% weight is the *correct* choice even though it earns less, because the alternative is selling at the bottom and turning a paper loss into a permanent one. The intuition: **your equity weight should be the largest your horizon and your stomach can both hold through the next −37% year** — not a basis point more.

### What invalidates each case

A disciplined stance names what would change its mind. The lean-in case is invalidated when growth rolls over or rates start rising into the rally; the trim case is invalidated when rates fall back and valuations reset to cheap. And the *core* case for equities as the growth engine — the +6.5% real premium — is only invalidated over horizons long enough to outlast a full cycle. Over any given year, equities can do anything; over thirty, they have been the most reliable real-wealth compounder available. That asymmetry between the terrifying short run and the dependable long run *is* the asset class.

One last guardrail on the tactical tilts: keep them *modest*, and keep them *anchored to the two levers*, not to a feeling. A lean-in or trim is a move of, say, ten or fifteen percentage points of weight around your core — enough to matter over time, not so much that being early or wrong wrecks you. And the signals worth acting on are the ones tied to the machine we built: the *level and direction of real yields* (the multiple's master driver) and *where the multiple sits versus its own history* (cheap earnings on sale, or expensive earnings with the future already priced in). Everything else — a scary headline, a strong week, a confident pundit — is noise that the multiple has usually already absorbed. If you find yourself wanting to go all-in or all-out, that is the moment to reread the drawdown section: the entire +6.5% real premium is paid to the investor who *stays invested through the cycle* and merely leans, gently, with the levers. The growth engine only compounds for the owner who keeps it running.

Where equities sit in the broader menu — how they stack against bonds, cash, credit, commodities, gold, and real estate, and how a real multi-asset portfolio is assembled from all of them — is the subject of [the map of asset classes](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-map-of-asset-classes-what-you-can-own). Equities are the engine. The rest of the playbook is about everything you bolt on so you can keep the engine running through the crashes that earn its return.
