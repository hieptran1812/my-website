> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Cash is a real asset class with a real yield: hold it for liquidity and ballast, judge every other asset against it, and remember it quietly loses to inflation.
>
> - "Cash" means T-bills, money-market funds, repo, and bank deposits — all priced off the Fed's policy rate, which *is* the risk-free rate. Near-zero price risk, full reinvestment risk.
> - Cash is the **hurdle rate**: every risky asset has to out-earn it. When cash pays 5%, the bar for owning stocks is high; when cash pays 0%, the bar is on the floor.
> - In 2022, cash returned **+1.5%** while US stocks fell **−18.1%** and US bonds fell **−13.0%** — cash was the only positive of the three. That is the whole case for owning it.
> - But over 2014–2024, \$10,000 in cash grew to ~\$11,900 while in stocks it grew to ~\$34,000. The number to remember: over a century, US cash returned **+0.4% per year after inflation** — it barely beats rising prices.

In October 2022, almost nothing worked. US stocks were down 18% on the year. US bonds — the thing that's supposed to *protect* you when stocks fall — were down 13%, their worst year in modern history. A classic "balanced" 60/40 portfolio was off about 16%, its deepest annual loss since 1937. Gold was flat. Real estate was down a quarter. If you opened your brokerage statement that month, every line was red.

Except one. The most boring line on the page — the cash you hadn't gotten around to investing, sitting in a money-market fund — was *up*. Not by much: about 1.5% for the year. But green is green, and in 2022 it was the only green there was. The asset everyone treats as "not really an investment" was the single best-performing major asset class in a year when the textbook diversifier failed.

That is the paradox this post is about. Cash gets dismissed as the thing you hold when you can't think of anything better to do — a parking spot, a placeholder, "being on the sidelines." But cash is a genuine asset class with a genuine job. It has a yield (the risk-free rate). It is the benchmark every other asset is secretly measured against. It is the one thing that rises when everything else falls. And it is a strategic position — "dry powder," the option to buy when assets go on sale. The catch, the reason it can't be your whole portfolio, is that it quietly loses a race against inflation over the long run. The diagram above is the mental model: cash is the *hurdle* — the bar every other asset must clear before its extra risk has earned anything.

![Cash is the hurdle rate every asset must clear](/imgs/blogs/cash-money-markets-the-underrated-asset-1.png)

By the end of this post you'll be able to answer the allocator's questions: what cash actually *is* as an asset, what drives its yield, how much to hold, when to raise it, and when to spend it. We'll define every term as we go.

## Foundations: what "cash" means as an asset

Start with the word. In everyday speech "cash" means the bills in your wallet. In investing it means something broader and more useful: **cash and cash-equivalents** — claims that are safe, short-term, and so liquid you can turn them into spendable money almost instantly with almost no loss. The defining trait is *near-zero price risk*: a dollar of cash-equivalent is worth essentially a dollar tomorrow, next week, and next month. Stocks can fall 30% in a month; a T-bill cannot.

There are four main forms, and they're all priced off the same anchor. The figure below shows the family.

![What cash is: the policy rate flowing into four instruments](/imgs/blogs/cash-money-markets-the-underrated-asset-7.png)

**Treasury bills (T-bills).** A T-bill is a short-term IOU from the US government, maturing in a year or less (4-week, 13-week, 26-week, 52-week). You don't get coupons; instead you buy it at a *discount* to its face value and it matures at face value, and the gap is your interest. Buy a 1-year bill for \$9,520 and collect \$10,000 at maturity, and you've earned \$480 — about 5%. Because the issuer is the US Treasury, which can always pay dollars it prints, a T-bill is treated as having **no credit risk** — no chance the borrower fails to repay. That is what "risk-free" means in finance: not "you can't lose money to inflation," but "you will get your dollars back for sure."

**Money-market funds (MMFs).** Most people don't buy bills directly; they hold a money-market fund — a pool that owns a basket of bills, repo, and other ultra-short instruments and aims to keep its share price pinned at \$1.00. You can buy and sell daily, you earn the prevailing short-term yield minus a tiny management fee (often 0.1–0.2% a year), and it behaves like a higher-yielding savings account. As of late 2024, US money funds held more than \$6 trillion — a record — precisely because cash finally paid well. A *money market* is just the market for borrowing and lending money for short periods (overnight to a year); MMFs are how ordinary investors plug into it.

**Repo (repurchase agreements).** Repo is the plumbing. A *repo* is a very short loan — often overnight — where one party sells a bond (usually a Treasury) and agrees to buy it back the next day at a slightly higher price; the price gap is the interest. It's a collateralized loan dressed up as a sale, and it's how banks, funds, and dealers finance their holdings of bonds. You won't trade repo personally, but it sits inside your money-market fund and it's where the Fed's policy rate first bites. When you hear about "stress in funding markets," it's usually repo.

**Bank deposits.** Your checking and savings accounts are a claim *on a bank*, not on the government. The bank takes your dollars, lends them out, and promises you can withdraw on demand. In the US, deposits are insured by the FDIC up to \$250,000 per depositor per bank, which makes insured deposits about as safe as bills. The catch: banks set deposit rates themselves, and they're slow to raise them. In 2023, when bills paid 5%, the average US savings account paid well under 0.5%. Same "cash," very different yield — a distinction we'll come back to.

### The risk-free rate: where the yield comes from

Here is the single most important idea in this whole post. **Cash has a yield, and that yield is set by the central bank.**

The Federal Reserve sets a target for the *federal funds rate* — the rate banks charge each other for overnight loans. (A full mechanism is in [How the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates).) Every other short-term rate is anchored to it. Overnight repo trades a hair above or below it. T-bill yields track it closely, because a bill is just a slightly-longer risk-free loan to the government. Money-fund yields follow bills. So when the Fed moves its policy rate, the yield on *all* cash moves with it, fast.

This is why "the risk-free rate" and "the cash yield" and "the policy rate" are, for our purposes, three names for the same number. It's the rate you can earn for taking essentially no risk. It is the foundation of the entire pricing system for risky assets — the deep reason the Fed's decisions ripple through every market is covered in [Interest rates: the price of money, the master variable](/blog/trading/macro-trading/interest-rates-the-price-of-money-master-variable).

#### Worked example: how a T-bill actually pays you

Suppose the Fed's policy rate is 5%, so 1-year T-bills yield about 5% too. You have \$10,000.

You buy a 1-year T-bill. In practice the Treasury sells it to you at a discount: you pay about \$9,524 today, and at maturity in one year you receive the full face value of \$10,000.

- Your profit is \$10,000 − \$9,524 = **\$476**.
- As a percentage of what you put in: \$476 ÷ \$9,524 = **5.0%**.

There were no coupons, no surprises, and — critically — no price risk. You knew on day one exactly what you'd have on day 365. Compare that to putting the same \$10,000 in stocks: you might end the year with \$13,000 or with \$8,000, and you don't know which.

The intuition: a T-bill converts a known amount of money today into a slightly larger, *certain* amount of money in a year — the purest form of "being paid to wait."

### Where the yield really comes from: the auction and the curve

One more layer of "how" makes cash concrete instead of magic. The Treasury doesn't *post* a yield on a bill; it *auctions* the bill and the yield falls out of the price buyers bid. Every week the Treasury offers a batch of 4-, 13-, 26-, and 52-week bills, and big buyers — banks, money funds, foreign central banks — submit bids. The Treasury fills the lowest-cost bids first (the buyers willing to accept the smallest discount), and the price that clears the auction sets the yield for everyone. Because the largest buyers can always instead lend overnight at the Fed's rate, no one will pay a price that yields much *below* the policy rate, and competition stops the yield drifting much *above* it. The auction is the machinery that pins bill yields to the policy rate we keep invoking.

Short bills and slightly-longer bills don't all yield exactly the same, though, and the small differences are informative. The set of yields across maturities is the **yield curve**. In a normal economy, a 1-year bill yields a touch more than a 3-month bill — you demand a sliver of extra return for committing your money a bit longer. But in 2023–24 the front end of the curve was *inverted*: a 3-month bill yielded more than a 1-year bill, which yielded more than a 2-year note. That inversion is the market saying "we think the Fed will be *cutting* rates soon, so locking in today's high rate for longer is worth paying up for." For a cash holder, an inverted front end is a subtle signal: the high yield you're enjoying on overnight and 3-month cash may not last, so the reinvestment risk is elevated — a theme we develop below.

There's also a quoting wrinkle worth knowing so the numbers on a brokerage screen don't confuse you. Bills are often quoted two ways: the *discount rate* (the discount as a fraction of face value) and the *bond-equivalent yield* or *investment yield* (the return as a fraction of what you actually paid). They're close but not identical, and the investment yield — the one in our worked example, 5.0% on \$9,524 invested — is the apples-to-apples number to compare against a savings account or a bond. When you see a bill "trading at a 4.9% discount rate" but a "5.0% yield," that gap is just the two conventions, not a free lunch.

### Near-zero price risk, full reinvestment risk

Cash's safety comes with a specific, often-missed catch. To see it, contrast cash with a long-term bond.

A 10-year government bond has **price risk**: if interest rates rise, its market price falls (a lot — that's *duration*, the subject of [Government bonds: the risk-free anchor and duration](/blog/trading/cross-asset/government-bonds-the-risk-free-anchor-and-duration)). But it *locks in* its yield: buy a 10-year bond at 4% and you've contracted to receive roughly 4% a year for a decade, no matter what the Fed does next.

Cash is the mirror image. A T-bill has essentially **no price risk** — hold it the few weeks to maturity and you get exactly face value. But it has full **reinvestment risk**: when it matures, you have to roll into a new bill at whatever rate prevails *then*. If the Fed has cut from 5% to 2% in the meantime, your "5% cash" silently becomes "2% cash." You never lost a dollar of principal, but your income can evaporate.

| | Cash / T-bills | Long-term bonds |
|---|---|---|
| Price risk if rates move | Near zero | High (duration) |
| Income locked in? | No — re-set every few weeks | Yes — for the bond's life |
| Main risk you bear | **Reinvestment** (yield can vanish) | **Price** (mark-to-market loss) |
| Behaves well when... | Rates rising / unknown | Rates falling |

This trade-off is the deep reason cash and long bonds are *different* tools, not interchangeable "safe assets." Cash protects your principal and lets your income float. Bonds lock your income and float your principal. In 2022, when rates rose violently, the bond's price risk detonated (−13%) while cash's lack of price risk made it the winner (+1.5%). The very feature that makes cash "boring" — it doesn't react to rate moves — is exactly what made it safe that year.

## Cash is the hurdle rate: the bar every asset must clear

Now the big idea, the one that reframes how you look at *every* other asset.

Why would anyone buy a risky asset — a stock, a corporate bond, a building — that might lose money, when cash pays a guaranteed yield? Only one answer makes sense: because they expect the risky asset to pay them *more than cash*, enough extra to compensate for the risk of loss. That "enough extra" is the **risk premium**, and the rate it's measured against is the cash yield. Cash is the hurdle.

Stack it up, as in the cover figure. The expected return on any asset is:

$$\text{expected return} = \underbrace{\text{cash yield}}_{\text{risk-free rate}} + \underbrace{\text{risk premium}}_{\text{pay for the risk}}$$

- A **government bond** earns cash plus a *term premium* — extra yield for tying your money up longer and bearing price risk.
- A **corporate bond** earns cash plus the term premium plus a *credit spread* — extra yield because the company might default.
- A **stock** earns cash plus all of that plus an *equity risk premium* — the biggest premium, for the biggest uncertainty.

The crucial consequence: **when the cash yield rises, the bar rises for everything.** A stock that looked attractive offering an expected 8% when cash paid 0% looks much less attractive when cash pays 5% — because now you're only being paid 3 extra points to take on all that equity risk, instead of 8. The asset didn't change; the hurdle did.

This is the mechanism behind one of the most-quoted lines in markets: **"TINA"** — *There Is No Alternative* — which was true in the 2010s precisely because cash paid nothing, so investors were pushed into stocks and bonds to earn anything at all. When the Fed hiked in 2022–23 and cash suddenly paid 5%, the cash yield reasserted itself as a real alternative. The acronym flipped to **"TARA"** — *There Are Reasonable Alternatives*. The whole regime change came from one number: the hurdle rate.

#### Worked example: the equity risk premium against two different cash rates

The *equity risk premium* is simply the extra return you expect from stocks over cash. Let's compute the bar in two regimes.

Say the long-run expected return on stocks is about 7% a year (we'll defend that number later).

**Regime A — cash pays 0% (the 2010s):**
- Equity risk premium = expected stock return − cash = 7% − 0% = **7%**.
- You're being paid a fat 7 points to bear equity risk. Stocks look compelling. *There is no alternative.*

**Regime B — cash pays 5% (2023–24):**
- Equity risk premium = 7% − 5% = **2%**.
- Now you're paid only 2 points to take on all the volatility, drawdowns, and sleepless nights of owning stocks. A guaranteed 5% in cash suddenly looks like a serious competitor.

Same stock, same 7% expected return. The *premium for owning it* collapsed from 7% to 2% — entirely because the hurdle rose. The intuition: a high cash rate doesn't just reward cash holders; it raises the price of admission for every risky asset in the market.

The map of how these premia stack across the whole asset universe is the subject of the series opener, [The map of asset classes: what you can own](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-map-of-asset-classes-what-you-can-own). Cash sits at the bottom of that map — the floor everything else is built on.

## What drives the cash yield

If cash's whole appeal is its yield, you need to know what moves that yield. The answer is refreshingly direct compared to other assets: the cash yield is set by policy, and policy responds to the economy.

![The risk-free rate is a policy decision: Fed funds and cash yields](/imgs/blogs/cash-money-markets-the-underrated-asset-3.png)

The blue line is the Fed's policy rate; the green line is what cash actually earned (the 3-month T-bill's annual return). They move together because, as we said, bill yields are anchored to the policy rate. Read the story in the figure:

- **2020–early 2022: the floor.** The Fed slashed rates to 0.25% in March 2020 to fight the COVID shock and held them there. Cash earned essentially nothing: 0.5% in 2020, 0.0% in 2021. Holding cash in this era was almost free of yield — and, as we'll see, costly in real terms.
- **2022–2023: the fastest hiking cycle in 40 years.** To fight inflation, the Fed lifted its target from 0.25% to 5.50% in roughly 18 months — March 2022 (0.50%), June (1.75%), September (3.25%), December (4.50%), and on up to 5.50% by July 2023. Cash followed in lockstep: bill returns climbed from ~0% to ~5%.
- **Late 2024: the first cuts.** With inflation cooling, the Fed trimmed to 5.00% (September 2024) and 4.50% (December 2024). Cash yields started easing back down — the reinvestment risk from earlier in this post, made real.

The takeaway for an allocator: **the cash yield is the most policy-sensitive number in your portfolio, and the most predictable.** You don't have to forecast earnings or geopolitics to know roughly what cash will pay next month — you just have to read the Fed. That predictability is part of cash's value: it's the one return in your portfolio you can almost bank on.

Two second-order drivers worth knowing:

1. **The shape of the yield curve.** Usually longer bonds pay more than cash, because you demand a term premium for locking your money up. But sometimes the curve *inverts* — cash yields *more* than 10-year bonds, as it did through much of 2022–2024. An inverted curve is historically a recession warning, and it also means cash isn't even giving up income to stay short — a rare and powerful setup for holding cash.
2. **Inflation.** The Fed raises rates to fight inflation, so cash yields tend to be high *because* inflation is high. This is the cruel twist we turn to next: the very environment that makes cash's nominal yield juicy is often the environment eating its real value.

There's a third, slower force worth naming: **the level the Fed eventually settles on.** Rates don't just move with the business cycle; the whole *resting* level of rates drifts over decades with the economy's underlying growth, demographics, and savings. Economists call this anchor the *neutral rate* — the policy rate that neither stimulates nor restrains the economy. In the 1980s it was high; through the 2010s it sank toward zero, which is why cash paid nothing for a decade not as a fluke but as a structural condition. After 2022 many believe it has drifted back up. You don't need to forecast the neutral rate, but knowing it exists explains why "cash pays nothing" was the *default* for a whole generation of investors and why "cash pays 5%" felt so novel — it wasn't just a cyclical hike, it was a possible shift in the resting level itself. For an allocator, a higher neutral rate means cash is structurally more useful than it was in the 2010s, because its baseline yield is higher.

A note on the 5%-and-7% numbers we keep using. The "5% cash" is the actual late-2024 policy rate and bill yield. The "7% expected stock return" is a long-run *nominal* estimate — roughly the ~6.5% real equity return from the century-long record plus a few points of expected inflation, which is in the right neighborhood of what most institutional forecasters pencil in. We're not claiming the market will return exactly 7% next year (it could be +30% or −20%); we're using it as the long-run *expected* return to make the hurdle-rate math concrete. The point survives any reasonable number: when cash rises from 0% to 5%, the premium for owning stocks shrinks by 5 points, whatever the stock estimate is.

## How cash behaves: ballast, and the only green box

We've covered what cash is and what drives its yield. Now the part an allocator cares about most: how does it *behave* inside a portfolio, especially when things go wrong?

The defining feature is that cash's return is **bounded below at roughly zero and is essentially uncorrelated with everything else**. It can't crash. In a year when stocks fall 30%, cash still earns its small positive yield. That gives it a unique role: **ballast** — the part of the portfolio that holds steady and keeps the whole ship from capsizing.

The proof is the grouped-bar chart below. It plots three lines — cash, stocks, bonds — for every year from 2014 to 2024.

![Cash vs stocks vs bonds annual total returns 2014 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/cash-money-markets-the-underrated-asset-2.png)

Most years, cash is the short green bar at the bottom and the story is "stocks win, who cares about cash." 2019: stocks +31.5%, cash +2.2%. 2021: stocks +28.7%, cash +0.0%. In a bull market, cash is the dullard at the party.

Then look at 2022, the shaded column. Stocks: **−18.1%**. Bonds: **−13.0%**. Cash: **+1.5%**. The boring green bar is the only one above the zero line. This is what people mean by **"cash is the only asset that's up when everything else is down."** It isn't always true — there are years (2008) when long Treasuries beat cash — but cash is the most *reliably* positive asset in a crisis, because its return floor is its defining trait.

Why did 2022 break so badly? Because stocks and bonds fell *together*, which is supposed to be rare. Normally bonds rise when stocks fall (the diversification that makes 60/40 work). But in 2022 the thing hurting stocks — surging inflation and a hiking Fed — was the *same* thing hurting bonds. When the driver of a sell-off is rising rates, stocks and bonds become positively correlated and both sink, and the only place to hide is the asset that *benefits* from rising rates: cash. The stock-bond relationship is regime-dependent: it was a reliable hedge for two decades, then it flipped in 2022, and cash was the one safe asset that never depended on that relationship holding.

#### Worked example: cash as ballast in the 2022 drawdown

You start 2022 with \$10,000. Compare three places to have put it.

- **All stocks:** \$10,000 × (1 − 0.181) = **\$8,190.** You're down \$1,810.
- **All bonds:** \$10,000 × (1 − 0.130) = **\$8,700.** You're down \$1,300. (So much for "bonds are safe.")
- **All cash:** \$10,000 × (1 + 0.015) = **\$10,150.** You're *up* \$150.

Now a blended portfolio. Suppose you held 50% stocks, 30% bonds, 20% cash:

- Stocks leg: \$5,000 × (1 − 0.181) = \$4,095
- Bonds leg: \$3,000 × (1 − 0.130) = \$2,610
- Cash leg: \$2,000 × (1 + 0.015) = \$2,030
- Total = **\$8,735**, a loss of 12.6%.

Without the cash sleeve — say 70% stocks, 30% bonds — you'd have ended at \$8,633, a 13.7% loss. The 20% cash position cushioned the year by a bit over a percentage point *and* gave you spendable, stable money to rebalance with while everything else was on sale. The intuition: cash doesn't win the race, but it keeps you in it — ballast is most valuable exactly when every other asset is taking on water.

### Cash as rebalancing fuel

That last clause — "money to rebalance with" — is the most under-appreciated job cash does, so let's make it concrete. *Rebalancing* means periodically trimming what's gone up and topping up what's gone down to return to your target mix. It's a quiet, mechanical way of buying low and selling high, and over time it's worth real return. But you can only rebalance *into* a falling asset if you have something stable to fund the purchase with — and the only asset guaranteed to hold its value while everything else falls is cash.

#### Worked example: rebalancing with dry powder in a crash

You start with \$100,000: \$80,000 in stocks and \$20,000 in cash (an 80/20 mix). A crash hits and stocks fall 25%.

- Stocks: \$80,000 × (1 − 0.25) = **\$60,000.**
- Cash: \$20,000 × (1 + 0.00) = **\$20,000** (held its value).
- Total: \$80,000. Your mix is now \$60k / \$20k = 75% stocks, 25% cash — too defensive versus your 80/20 target.

To rebalance back to 80/20, you want 80% of \$80,000 = \$64,000 in stocks. So you move \$4,000 of cash into stocks, buying at the lows.

Now stocks recover their old level — a 33% rise from \$60k gets them back. Your \$64,000 of stock (the original \$60k plus the \$4k you deployed) rises 33% to about **\$85,300**, versus the \$80,000 you'd have if you hadn't rebalanced and just held \$64k... actually let's be precise: the \$64,000 invested at the bottom grows 33% to \$85,120, plus your remaining \$16,000 cash = **\$101,120** — already above where you started. Had you held no cash and been 100% in stocks, you'd have ridden \$100k down to \$75k and back to \$100k: break-even. The cash let you buy the dip and come out *ahead*. The intuition: in a crash, cash isn't just ballast that doesn't fall — it's ammunition that lets you turn someone else's panic into your discount.

## Cash as a regime asset: when it leads

Step back from any single year and the pattern is clear: cash is a **regime asset**. There are specific macro environments where it goes from afterthought to leader.

The cleanest framing is the classic growth-versus-inflation grid. Cash (alongside gold) is the asset that leads in the worst quadrant for everything else — **stagflation**, where growth is falling *and* inflation is rising. In that world, stocks are hit by weak growth, bonds are hit by inflation, commodities can be volatile, and the asset whose yield is being *raised* to fight the inflation — cash — quietly comes out on top. 2022 was a mild version of exactly this.

But there's a subtler regime where cash shines, and it's the one an allocator can actually act on: **late cycle with high short rates and an inverted yield curve.** When the Fed has hiked aggressively, three things line up in cash's favor at once:

1. **The yield is high.** You're paid 5% to wait, risk-free.
2. **The curve is inverted**, so you're giving up *no* income (or even gaining) by staying short instead of buying longer bonds.
3. **Risk assets are expensive and vulnerable** to a slowdown that the high rates are designed to cause.

In that setup, cash stops being "the absence of a decision" and becomes a *real option* — you collect a healthy yield while you wait for risk assets to get cheaper, with no opportunity cost in the meantime. That's the deepest version of "cash is king." More on the rates backdrop that creates it in [Interest rates: the price of money, the master variable](/blog/trading/macro-trading/interest-rates-the-price-of-money-master-variable).

## How cash correlates with everything else

An asset is interesting for what it does *alongside* the others, not just on its own — and here cash has a property no other asset can match. Its correlation with risky assets is essentially **zero**, and that is its superpower.

A quick definition: *correlation* measures how two assets move together, on a scale from +1 (they rise and fall in perfect lockstep) through 0 (their moves are unrelated) to −1 (one zigs exactly when the other zags). Diversification — the only "free lunch" in investing — works by combining assets that *aren't* highly correlated, so that a bad month for one is often an okay month for another, and the portfolio's ups and downs are smoother than any single piece.

Now plot cash's correlation against the major classes. Against stocks: about **−0.05** — effectively zero, a tiny touch negative. Against bonds: about **+0.15**. Against high-yield credit, commodities, gold, and real estate: all clustered around **0.00**. Cash barely budges when anything else moves, because its return is just a slow accrual of yield that has almost nothing to do with what stocks or oil or gold did this week.

Contrast that with the assets people *think* are diversifiers. Stocks and high-yield bonds correlate about **+0.75** — high-yield credit is "stocks in a bond wrapper" and falls right alongside equities in a sell-off. Stocks and real estate also run about +0.75. Even government bonds, the classic stock hedge, flipped from reliably *negative* correlation with stocks (the 2000–2021 era, when bonds rose as stocks fell) to *positive* in 2022, when rising rates sank both at once. That flip is exactly why 2022 hurt so much — the diversifier stopped diversifying. The one asset whose correlation with stocks did *not* betray investors in 2022 was cash, because cash's near-zero correlation is structural, not regime-dependent. It can't fall with stocks, because it can't really fall at all.

This is the precise, technical reason cash earns its place in a portfolio despite the drag: it's the most *dependable* diversifier you can own. Its diversification doesn't rely on a relationship that might break (the way the stock-bond hedge broke in 2022); it relies only on cash's return floor, which never breaks. When the bond-stock correlation flips against you — as it did in 2022 — cash is the backstop that holds, because its near-zero correlation is structural rather than a regime that can reverse.

#### Worked example: what a cash sleeve does to portfolio volatility

*Volatility* is the standard measure of how much a portfolio's value bounces around — higher volatility means a wilder ride. Let's see how cash tames it. Use round numbers: stocks have a volatility of about 16% a year; cash has a volatility of about 0%; and they're uncorrelated.

A handy fact: when you mix an asset with cash, and cash has zero volatility, the blend's volatility is just the asset's volatility scaled by its weight.

- **100% stocks:** portfolio volatility ≈ 16%.
- **80% stocks, 20% cash:** volatility ≈ 0.80 × 16% = **12.8%.**
- **60% stocks, 40% cash:** volatility ≈ 0.60 × 16% = **9.6%.**

So a 40% cash sleeve cuts the ride's roughness by about 40%, from 16% to 9.6%, at the cost of giving up roughly 40% of the stock market's *excess* return over cash. You're trading return for smoothness in a straight, predictable line — which is exactly why cash is the cleanest "dial" an allocator has for turning portfolio risk up or down. The intuition: cash is the volatility knob, and because its correlation is zero, the knob turns smoothly with no surprises.

## The opportunity cost: the cash drag

Now the other side, the reason cash can't be your whole portfolio. Cash's safety has a price, and over long horizons that price is steep. It's called the **cash drag** — the growth you give up by holding cash instead of riskier, higher-returning assets.

The picture is brutal and worth sitting with.

![Growth of 10000 dollars in cash versus stocks versus bonds 2014 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/cash-money-markets-the-underrated-asset-5.png)

\$10,000 invested at the end of 2014 and left alone for a decade became:

- **Stocks: ~\$34,300** — more than tripled.
- **Bonds: ~\$11,400.**
- **Cash: ~\$11,900.**

Over this particular decade cash actually edged out bonds (because 2022 wrecked bonds while cash was unscathed) — but both were crushed by stocks. The gap between the stock line and the cash line, roughly **\$22,000**, is the cash drag: real money you'd have earned by accepting risk, forgone by playing it safe.

#### Worked example: the long-run cost of "waiting in cash"

You're nervous about the market in 2014, so you keep \$10,000 in cash, telling yourself you'll deploy it "when things calm down." A decade passes.

- Cash compounded at its actual yearly returns (0.0%, 0.3%, 0.8%, 1.9%, 2.2%, 0.5%, 0.0%, 1.5%, 5.0%, 5.2%): your \$10,000 became about **\$11,900**. You earned ~\$1,900 over ten years.
- Had you simply bought the S&P 500 and held through every scary headline — including the 18% drop in 2022 — your \$10,000 became about **\$34,300**. You earned ~\$24,300.

The cost of "waiting" was roughly **\$22,400** of forgone wealth on a \$10,000 stake. And note: the market had a 34% COVID crash (2020) and an 18% bear market (2022) *inside* this window. You'd have ridden through both and still ended up nearly three times richer than the cash holder. The intuition: cash feels safe in the moment, but over long horizons the real risk isn't a market crash — it's the slow, certain erosion of *not being invested at all.*

This is the case **against** holding too much cash, and it's the necessary counterweight to everything positive we said. The very stability that makes cash great ballast makes it a terrible long-term *engine*. The growth in a portfolio has to come from the risky assets — cash's job is to make holding those risky assets survivable, not to grow your wealth. This is exactly why cash is the floor of the asset map and stocks are the engine; see [Equities: owning a slice of growth](/blog/trading/cross-asset/equities-stocks-owning-a-slice-of-growth).

There's an asymmetry worth naming: the cash drag is small and forgivable over months (you give up a little growth for a lot of flexibility) but compounds into a fortune-sized gap over decades. Cash is a *tactical* tool — held for a reason, for a while — not a *strategic* home for money you won't need for 20 years.

## Inflation erosion: the silent tax

The cash drag is about *opportunity* cost — what you forgo versus other assets. But cash has an *absolute* cost too, one that bites even if you never compare it to stocks: **inflation**.

Here's the key distinction, and it's the one beginners most often miss. There are two ways to measure a return:

- **Nominal return** — the number on your statement. "Cash paid 5%."
- **Real return** — what you earned after subtracting inflation. *Real return ≈ nominal return − inflation rate.* This is what actually happened to your *purchasing power* — how much more stuff you can buy.

A dollar that earns 5% in a year when prices rise 5% has bought you exactly nothing extra: you have more dollars, but each buys less, and it's a wash. Real return zero. This distinction between *nominal* (the headline number) and *real* (after inflation) is the single most important lens for judging cash, because cash is the asset where the two diverge the most.

Now look at what inflation did to cash in 2021–2022.

![Real cash return equals nominal yield minus inflation 2018 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/cash-money-markets-the-underrated-asset-4.png)

Each bar is cash's *real* return — its nominal yield minus that year's inflation. Green is positive (cash gained purchasing power); red is negative (cash lost purchasing power). The red years are the indictment:

- **2021:** cash yielded ~0%, but CPI (the Consumer Price Index, the standard inflation gauge) ran ~7% by year-end. Real cash return: about **−7%.** Holding cash in 2021 *cost* you 7% of your purchasing power.
- **2022:** cash yielded ~1.5%, CPI ran ~6.5%, peaking at **9.06% in June 2022** — a 40-year high. Real cash return: about **−5%.**

Then, as the Fed hiked and inflation cooled, cash's real return swung positive: roughly +1.6% in 2023 and +2.3% in 2024, because now the 5% nominal yield comfortably out-ran ~3% inflation. This is the regime that makes cash genuinely attractive — *positive real yield* — and it's relatively rare.

#### Worked example: the silent tax of holding cash through inflation

You sit in \$10,000 of cash through 2021, feeling safe. Inflation that year runs about 7%.

- Your cash earned ~0%, so you still have **\$10,000** on the statement. No loss, right?
- But a basket of goods that cost \$10,000 in January cost about \$10,700 by December.
- To buy the same stuff, you'd now need \$10,700. You have \$10,000. In real terms, your purchasing power fell by about **\$700** — roughly 7%.

You "didn't lose money," yet you got 7% poorer in everything that matters. The intuition: cash protects your *dollars* perfectly and your *wealth* not at all — inflation is a tax on cash that never shows up on a statement, which is exactly what makes it so easy to ignore.

Zoom all the way out and this isn't a 2021 fluke; it's cash's permanent condition. Over the very long run, cash is designed to roughly *match* inflation, not beat it.

The most authoritative dataset we have — the UBS / Credit Suisse Global Investment Returns Yearbook, which stitches together returns back to 1900 — gives the **real, after-inflation, annualized** returns over 1900–2023:

| Asset | Real return per year, 1900–2023 |
|---|---|
| US equities | **+6.5%** |
| US bonds | +1.7% |
| US bills (cash) | **+0.4%** |

Over **124 years**, cash beat inflation by four-tenths of one percent a year. That is essentially "ran in place." A century of holding cash roughly preserved purchasing power and did little more. Equities, by contrast, multiplied purchasing power many times over — that 6.5% real, compounded over a lifetime, is the engine of long-term wealth. The number to burn in: **cash barely beats inflation over a century; it is a store of value, not a creator of it.**

## Common misconceptions

**"Cash isn't really an investment — it's just sitting there."** It's an investment with a yield, a benchmark role, and a return profile — it just happens to have the *lowest* risk and lowest return. In 2023 and 2024 cash returned 5.0% and 5.2%, beating bonds both years. An asset that can be the best performer in a major year (2022) is not "nothing." Treating it as a non-asset is how people end up with too much of it (drag) or none of it (no ballast) by accident rather than by choice.

**"Cash is safe."** Cash is safe in *nominal* terms — your dollar count won't fall. It is genuinely *unsafe* in *real* terms: it reliably loses to inflation, dramatically so in 2021–22 (about −7% and −5% real). "Safe" depends on what you're protecting. Against a market crash, cash is the safest thing there is. Against the slow erosion of your purchasing power, cash is one of the *riskiest* things you can hold, because the loss is near-certain over time.

**"A high cash yield is just free money."** A 5% cash yield usually exists *because* inflation is high and the economy may be slowing — the Fed doesn't hike for fun. The 5% is partly compensation for the inflation eating your real return and partly a symptom of a late-cycle economy. It's a real and useful yield, but it's not a free lunch; it comes attached to the conditions that produced it.

**"Cash and bonds are the same 'safe' bucket."** They're opposites on the risk that matters most in practice. Cash has near-zero price risk and full reinvestment risk; bonds have high price risk and locked-in income. In 2022 that difference was the whole ballgame: cash +1.5%, bonds −13.0%. Lumping them together as "the safe part" of a portfolio hides the single most important choice in fixed income — how much duration to take.

**"If cash is dragging, I should always be fully invested."** The drag is real over decades, but cash's *optionality* has value precisely when assets are expensive or you'll need the money soon. Being 100% invested with no dry powder means you have nothing to deploy when assets go on sale — and forced selling in a crash (to raise cash you should have held) is far costlier than a little drag. The right amount of cash is rarely zero and rarely huge.

**"My money-market fund pays the same as my savings account — cash is cash."** Often wildly false. In 2023 a Treasury money-market fund paid about 5% while the average bank savings account paid under 0.5% — the same dollars, the same near-zero risk, a tenfold difference in yield. The deposit rate is set by your bank's profit motive (they pay you as little as competition allows); the money-fund rate is set by the market and tracks bills directly. "Cash is cash" is the belief that quietly costs savers tens of billions of dollars a year in forgone interest. *Where* you park cash is a real decision, not a formality.

## How it shows up in real markets

**2022 — the only green box.** Covered above, but it's the canonical case: stocks −18.1%, bonds −13.0%, the 60/40 −16% (worst since 1937), and cash +1.5%, the lone positive. The lesson allocators took away was that cash is a *distinct* diversifier from bonds — in the specific regime where rising rates drive the sell-off, cash protects when bonds can't, because cash is the only "safe" asset that *gains* from rate hikes.

**2021 — cash is trash.** The mirror image. The Fed pinned rates at 0%, so cash yielded nothing, while inflation surged past 6% by December (CPI 6.81%). Real cash returns were about −7%, the worst in decades. Meanwhile stocks returned 28.7%. This was the deepest "cash is trash" environment in a generation — zero nominal yield *and* punishing inflation. Anyone holding large cash balances "to be safe" got quietly poorer all year. It's the case study for why cash is a poor *strategic* holding when rates are at the floor.

**2023–24 — cash is king again.** The Fed's hikes to 5.50% turned cash into a serious asset. Money-market fund assets swelled past \$6 trillion as investors finally got paid to be patient. Cash returned 5.0% and 5.2%, beat bonds both years, and — with inflation cooling to ~3% — delivered a *positive real* return for the first time in years. This is the regime where the allocator's instinct to "hold dry powder" costs nothing: you're paid 5% to wait, with an inverted curve meaning you give up no income for staying short.

**2008 — when bonds beat cash.** An honest counter-example. In the Global Financial Crisis, stocks fell 37% — but *long Treasuries returned about +26%* as panicked investors fled to government bonds and the Fed slashed rates. Cash returned only its small positive yield. So "cash is the only thing up in a crisis" isn't universally true: in a *deflationary, flight-to-quality* crash (falling rates), long bonds are the better hedge. Cash wins when the crisis is *inflationary* (rising rates, 2022); bonds win when it's deflationary (falling rates, 2008). Knowing which kind of crisis you're in tells you which safe asset to hold — and is why a portfolio often wants some of both.

**The 2023 deposit lag.** When bills paid 5%, the average US bank savings account paid under 0.5%. Trillions sat in checking and savings earning almost nothing while the *same* cash in a money-market fund earned 5%. This sparked a wave of money moving out of bank deposits into money funds and T-bills (a "deposit flight" that contributed to the regional-bank stress of March 2023). The lesson: "cash" is not one yield. *Where* you hold cash — insured deposit, money fund, direct T-bills — can be a 4-percentage-point difference, which on \$50,000 is \$2,000 a year for the same near-zero risk.

**The 1970s — the original "cash is trash."** The decade investors of a certain age never forget. Inflation ran into the double digits, peaking near 14% in 1980. The Fed's policy rate eventually chased it up, so *nominal* cash yields looked enormous — but for years they trailed inflation, and *real* cash returns were deeply negative through the mid-1970s, much as in 2021–22 but sustained over years rather than months. Stocks went roughly nowhere in real terms for a decade and a half. The assets that protected purchasing power were the *real* ones — gold rose multiples, commodities surged during the oil shocks. The 1970s are the cautionary tale for the inflation-erosion section: holding cash through a sustained inflation isn't safety, it's a slow bleed, and it's why "cash preserves wealth" is only true when inflation is tame.

**2020 — the policy flood and the death of cash yield.** When COVID hit in March 2020, the S&P fell 34% peak-to-trough in five weeks. But unlike 2022, this was a *deflationary* shock met with overwhelming policy support: the Fed cut to 0.25% and flooded markets with liquidity, and by year-end stocks were *up* 18.4% and bonds up 7.5%. Cash, pinned at the new zero, returned just 0.5% — and then sat there through the 2021 inflation surge earning nothing while prices took off. The 2020 episode shows the bind cash holders face in a deflationary crash: the moment cash's ballast would matter most (the crash) is the same moment the Fed crushes its yield to zero, and if you don't deploy that cash into the rebound, you're left holding a zero-yield asset into an inflation. Cash is best in this regime as *fast-acting dry powder*, not a long-term perch.

## When to own it: the cash allocation playbook

Everything so far lands here — the allocator's decision. Cash does three jobs: **liquidity** (money you'll need soon, kept safe and spendable), **ballast** (stability that survives a crash and gives you something to rebalance with), and **optionality** (dry powder to buy assets when they're cheap). Size your cash to those jobs, and adjust the optionality piece with the regime.

The regime call is best seen as a 2×2, the same one in the figure: how much does cash *yield*, and how *cheap* are other assets right now?

![Cash is king vs cash is trash regime quadrant](/imgs/blogs/cash-money-markets-the-underrated-asset-6.png)

- **High rates + rich assets (late cycle, 2023–24): cash is king.** You're paid 5% risk-free, the hurdle for risk assets is high, and an inverted curve means you give up no income by staying short. *Raise cash, hold dry powder.* This is the regime where patience is literally rewarded.
- **High rates + cheap assets (deep panic, late 2008 / late 2022): start deploying.** Cash still pays well, but assets are on sale — the reason you held the dry powder. *Spend it, gradually.* Don't try to time the exact bottom; average in as spreads stay wide.
- **Low rates + cheap assets (post-crash recovery, 2009): deploy hard.** The worst possible time to sit in cash: it earns ~0% *and* assets are cheap. Every dollar in cash is a dollar not compounding in assets bought at a discount. *Be nearly fully invested.*
- **Low rates + rich assets (everything-rally, 2021): cash is trash, reluctantly.** Cash yields ~0% and inflation eats it, so holding it is costly — but assets are expensive too, so there's no easy win. Hold the *minimum* cash your liquidity and ballast jobs require, and accept that there's no comfortable place to hide.

### Rough sizing

This is education, not advice, and the right number depends entirely on your horizon, your need for the money, and your tolerance for drawdowns. But to make the framework concrete, here's how practitioners tend to think about it:

| Cash job | Rough size | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Liquidity / emergency | 3–12 months of spending | Money you can't afford to see fall 30% |
| Strategic ballast | ~5–15% of a long-term portfolio | Stability + rebalancing fuel; varies with risk tolerance |
| Tactical dry powder | 0–20% extra, regime-dependent | Raised when cash is king, spent when assets are cheap |

The strategic ballast number rises with how much *price risk* sits in the rest of your portfolio: an all-stock portfolio benefits from more cash ballast than a balanced one. The tactical sleeve is the part you flex with the 2×2 — near zero when rates are low and assets cheap, materially higher when rates are high and assets rich.

### What raises the case for cash, what lowers it

**Raise cash when:** short rates are high (you're paid to wait); the yield curve is inverted (no income given up); risk-asset valuations are rich and credit spreads are tight (little premium for taking risk); you're late in the cycle (the high rates may be about to bite). In this configuration cash is a *real option* with a positive carry — about as good as cash ever gets.

**Deploy cash when:** there's panic and credit spreads are wide (you're being paid a fat premium to take risk); assets are demonstrably cheap; or simply when rates are low and holding cash costs you both yield and inflation. The discipline is to spend the dry powder *into* weakness, when it's psychologically hardest — that's when the cheap assets are, and it's the entire reason you held the cash.

### How to deploy: average in, don't snipe the bottom

The mistake that wastes good dry powder is waiting for the *exact* low. Nobody rings a bell at the bottom; the cheapest day is only obvious in hindsight, and investors who hold out for it usually deploy too late — or never. The practical discipline is to **average in**: split the cash you intend to deploy into tranches and put them to work as the asset gets cheaper, on a schedule or at preset price levels, rather than betting everything on one call.

#### Worked example: averaging dry powder into a falling market

You have \$30,000 of dry powder and a stock has fallen and keeps falling. Instead of guessing the bottom, you deploy \$10,000 at three levels as it drops: at a price of \$100, then \$90, then \$80 a share.

- Tranche 1: \$10,000 ÷ \$100 = 100 shares.
- Tranche 2: \$10,000 ÷ \$90 ≈ 111 shares.
- Tranche 3: \$10,000 ÷ \$80 = 125 shares.
- Total: 336 shares for \$30,000, an **average cost of \$30,000 ÷ 336 ≈ \$89.30** per share.

If you'd dumped all \$30,000 at the first level (\$100), you'd own only 300 shares at \$100 average. By averaging down you bought 36 extra shares and lowered your average cost by nearly \$11 a share — and you didn't have to predict anything. If the stock recovers to \$110, your averaged position is worth 336 × \$110 = \$36,960 versus 300 × \$110 = \$33,000 for the all-at-once buyer. The intuition: dry powder's value isn't in timing the bottom perfectly — it's in having the patience and the ammunition to keep buying *while* an asset is cheap, which averaging makes automatic.

This is why the *cash* part of the playbook and the *deployment* part are inseparable. Holding cash you never spend is just permanent drag; spending it all at the first wobble wastes the option. Cash earns its keep only when you both hold it through the expensive times *and* deploy it through the cheap ones — the full round trip is the strategy.

### What invalidates the case

The case for a big cash position breaks when: **inflation is high and rising while the Fed is still at the floor** (cash bleeds real value with no nominal yield to compensate — the 2021 trap); your **horizon is genuinely long** and the money is for decades away (the cash drag, ~\$22,000 forgone on \$10,000 over a decade, swamps any ballast benefit); or **the curve is steeply positive** and longer bonds pay materially more than cash for risk you're willing to bear. In all three, cash is doing more harm than good, and the discipline is to *not* let "it feels safe" override the math.

The honest synthesis is the one we opened with: **"cash is king" and "cash is trash" are both true — in different regimes.** Cash is king when it pays you well to be patient and assets are expensive; cash is trash when it pays nothing, inflation rages, and you're giving up the long compounding that builds wealth. The skill isn't picking a side forever. It's knowing which regime you're in — which mostly means knowing what the Fed is doing and how cheap or rich everything else has become — and sizing the boring green bar accordingly.
