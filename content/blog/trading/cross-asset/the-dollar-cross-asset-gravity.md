> **TL;DR** — The US dollar is not just one currency among many; it is the *price of money for the whole world*. Because nearly everything global is borrowed, invoiced, and saved in dollars, the dollar's level sets global financial conditions. A rising dollar tightens conditions everywhere and pulls commodities, gold, emerging markets, and risk assets lower at once. A falling dollar loosens them and lights the green for the reflation trade.
>
> - **The dollar is the single most useful dial in cross-asset investing.** It is *negatively* correlated with almost everything risky — about **−0.40 with gold, −0.35 with commodities, −0.30 with stocks** over 2015–2024. A strong dollar is a headwind for the entire risk complex.
> - **A rising dollar is a global margin call.** Roughly **\$13 trillion** of dollar debt is owed by borrowers *outside* the US. When the dollar rises 20%, the local-currency cost of that debt can jump ~25% with no new borrowing — forcing the world to tighten and sell.
> - **The dollar trends for years and smiles at both extremes.** It is strong in a US boom *and* in a global panic, and weak only in the calm, synchronized-growth middle. That shape — the dollar smile — is why it works as a safe haven and a wrecking ball at the same time.
> - **The one number to remember:** when DXY spiked to **~114.8** in September 2022, 2022 became the year almost every asset class fell together. Watch the dollar, and you are watching the master switch.

In the autumn of 2022, an investor watching almost any screen could see the same thing happen at once. Global stocks were falling. The S&P 500 was down roughly 18% on the year. Bonds — supposedly the safe ballast — were having their worst year in over a century, down about 13%. Emerging-market currencies were in free fall. The Japanese yen had collapsed from 115 to past 145 per dollar. Gold, the thing that is supposed to glitter when everything else burns, was *also* down. Almost nothing worked. It looked like chaos, like every market had decided to break at the same time for no reason.

But there was a reason, and it was a single price. Over that year the US Dollar Index — a measure of the dollar against a basket of major currencies, ticker DXY — climbed from about 96 to an intraday peak near **114.8** in late September, its strongest level in two decades. That was not a side effect of the carnage. It *was* the carnage. The rising dollar was the gravity pulling everything else down. When you understand why one currency can do that — why a move in the dollar reaches into the price of oil in Riyadh, a factory loan in São Paulo, and a gold bar in a London vault — you have found the master switch of the entire cross-asset system.

This is the deep-dive on the dollar in the *Cross-Asset Playbook* series. Every other asset we study — stocks, bonds, [gold](/blog/trading/cross-asset/gold-money-insurance-or-just-a-rock), commodities, [emerging markets](/blog/trading/cross-asset/fx-currencies-the-relative-value-layer) — moves partly on its own story and partly on the dollar. The dollar is the one factor that touches all of them. Master it, and you have a single dial that tells you, most of the time, whether the wind is at the back of risk assets or in their face. The figure below is the mental model we will build for the whole post: the dollar at the center, with the rest of the risk complex orbiting it.

![The dollar at the center pulling commodities gold EM and risk lower when it rises](/imgs/blogs/the-dollar-cross-asset-gravity-1.png)

The diagram above is the thesis in one picture. A stronger dollar — DXY rising — is the same thing as tighter global financial conditions, and tighter conditions pull commodities, gold, emerging markets, and risk assets all lower together. US large-cap stocks are the one place that is *relatively* resilient, because that is where the world runs to hide. Flip the dollar the other way and the arrows reverse: a weakening dollar loosens conditions and turns the whole picture green — the reflation trade. By the end of this post you will be able to read a sharp move in DXY the way a sailor reads a falling barometer.

## Foundations: why the dollar is special

To see why one currency can move everything, we first have to be honest about something most people never stop to question: *the dollar is not a normal currency.* It is not just America's money. It is the world's money. And that is not a metaphor — it is a set of concrete, measurable facts about how the global financial system is plumbed.

Let us define our terms from zero before we build anything on them.

### What "the dollar" means here, and what DXY measures

When traders say "the dollar is up," they almost never mean the dollar against one specific currency. They mean the dollar against the *world* — against a basket of major currencies at once. The standard scoreboard for this is the **US Dollar Index**, written DXY. It is a single number that measures the dollar against a fixed basket of six currencies, weighted by how much trade the US does with each. The euro dominates the basket (about 58% of it), followed by the Japanese yen, the British pound, the Canadian dollar, the Swedish krona, and the Swiss franc.

The level of DXY is not a price in dollars of anything you can hold; it is an index, set to 100 at a starting point back in 1973. So when DXY reads 108, it means the dollar is about 8% stronger than that 1973 baseline against the basket. What matters for us is not the absolute level but the *direction and speed*: DXY rising means the dollar is strengthening against the rest of the developed world; DXY falling means it is weakening. A move from 96 to 108 — what happened in 2022 — is a roughly 12% strengthening of the dollar against the world's other major monies in under a year. That is enormous for a currency, and it is the kind of move that reorganizes every other market.

Hold that picture: **DXY = the dollar versus a basket, and a fast rise in DXY is the alarm bell for the whole risk complex.**

### Reason one: the dollar is the world's reserve currency

A *reserve currency* is the money that central banks and governments around the world hold as their savings — the cash they keep in the vault to pay for imports, defend their own currency, and weather a crisis. Think of it as the world's collective checking account. For decades, the overwhelming majority of that account has been held in dollars.

How dominant is it? According to the IMF's COFER data, which tracks the currency composition of official reserves, the dollar's share of disclosed global reserves stood at about **57.8% at the end of 2024**. The euro, in distant second, was about 19.8%. The Japanese yen was 5.8%, the British pound 4.7%, and the Chinese renminbi — despite China being the world's second-largest economy — was just 2.2%. The dollar's share has drifted down over the decades (it was 71.5% in 2001 and 63.8% in 2008), but at nearly 58% it still dwarfs everything else combined. No other currency is even in the conversation as *the* global store of value.

> [!note]
> **Why "still 58%" matters more than "down from 71%."** A common headline is that the dollar is "losing its reserve crown." The share *has* slipped. But a currency that is 58% of the world's official savings, when the next-biggest is 20%, is not being dethroned — it is being mildly diversified around the edges. For an allocator, the level that matters is that more than half the planet's reserves are still parked in dollars, which is why everyone still needs them.

This matters for cross-asset behavior because reserves are *demand*. The world has a permanent, structural need to hold dollars, which gives the dollar a floor of support that no other currency enjoys. And in a crisis, that demand spikes — when the world is scared, it wants more of its savings in the safest, most liquid money there is, and that is the dollar.

### Reason two: the dollar is the world's invoicing currency

When a Korean shipyard sells a tanker to a Greek owner, or a Brazilian farm sells soybeans to a Chinese buyer, or a refinery anywhere buys a cargo of oil, the price is almost always quoted in dollars — even though *no American is involved in the transaction*. The dollar is the world's *invoicing currency*, the unit in which a huge share of international trade is priced. Estimates put the dollar's share of global trade invoicing at roughly **half**, far above the US's share of actual world trade, which is closer to a tenth. Commodities are the extreme case: oil, copper, gold, wheat, and almost every other globally traded raw material is quoted in dollars per barrel, per pound, per ounce, per bushel.

This single fact is the mechanical heart of why a strong dollar pushes commodity prices down for the rest of the world — which we will work through in detail in a moment. If oil is priced in dollars and your currency just fell 15% against the dollar, then oil just got 15% more expensive *for you* even if the dollar price on the screen never moved. The dollar's level is therefore baked into the real, local cost of every commodity everywhere.

### Reason three: the dollar is the world's funding currency

Here is the reason that turns the dollar from a strong currency into a *systemic force*. When companies, banks, and governments outside the United States want to borrow — to build a factory, finance trade, or fund a budget — a very large share of them borrow in *dollars*, not in their own currency. They do this because dollar funding is deep, liquid, and often cheaper, and because the world's trade and capital flows run on dollars anyway. The Bank for International Settlements — the "central bank of central banks" — tracks this, and the stock of dollar credit owed by borrowers *outside* the US runs to roughly **\$13 trillion**. That is the dollar as the world's *funding currency*: the money the rest of the planet borrows in.

Put the three reasons together and you have the dollar's unique position. The world **saves** in dollars (reserve currency), **prices** in dollars (invoicing currency), and **borrows** in dollars (funding currency). No other money does all three. That is why the dollar's level is not just America's business — it is the setting on a dial that controls how tight or loose money is for the entire planet. For the deeper plumbing of how this system was built and how the Fed sits at the top of it, the macro post on [the dollar system and why USD rules markets](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy) is the companion to this one; here we focus on what it *does to other assets*.

It is worth pausing on *why* this arrangement is so sticky, because the instinct is to ask "couldn't another currency take over?" The honest answer is: not easily, and not soon. The dollar's dominance is a *network effect*, the same force that keeps everyone on the same messaging app even when a rival is technically better. The world prices oil in dollars because everyone else does, so doing anything else means swimming against the entire system. A country invoices in dollars because its trading partners expect dollars. A company borrows in dollars because that's where the deep, liquid lending market is. Each of these choices reinforces all the others. To dethrone the dollar, a rival would need deep and open capital markets, the rule of law, free convertibility, and the trust of the whole world — simultaneously. The euro has some of this but is fragmented across nations; the renminbi has scale but capital controls and a closed financial system. So the dollar's gravity persists not because America is uniquely virtuous, but because the entire world is locked into a system that runs on dollars, and switching costs are enormous. For an allocator, the practical takeaway is that the dollar's role as the master cross-asset signal is not going away on any horizon you're investing over.

#### Worked example: the dollar's three jobs, in one trade

Picture a single soybean cargo to make all three jobs concrete. A Brazilian farmer sells \$5,000,000 of soybeans to a Chinese mill. The contract is written in dollars (job one: invoicing). To grow the crop, the farmer borrowed \$2,000,000 from an international bank — in dollars, because that was the cheapest funding available (job two: funding). And the central bank of Brazil, sitting behind all of this, holds tens of billions of dollars in reserves to make sure the country can always settle its dollar obligations (job three: reserves).

Now the dollar strengthens 10%. The \$5,000,000 sale is still \$5,000,000 — but in Brazilian reais, the farmer's *costs* (local wages, local diesel) didn't fall, while the \$2,000,000 debt now costs 10% more reais to service. The single soybean shipment touches the dollar as invoice, as funding, and as the reserve buffer the whole country depends on — and a 10% dollar move quietly reshapes the economics of all three at once. The intuition: **the dollar is wired into the global economy at three points, so a move in it pulls three levers simultaneously.**

## The mechanism: how a stronger dollar tightens the whole world

We now have the *why* — the dollar is reserve, invoice, and funding currency. Let us turn it into the *how*: the three concrete channels through which a rising dollar reaches out and pushes other assets down. Get these three channels in your head and the dollar stops being a mysterious force and becomes a clear chain of cause and effect.

### Channel one: dollar-priced commodities get more expensive for everyone else

This is the most direct channel, and it follows straight from the invoicing fact. Commodities are priced in dollars. So when the dollar strengthens against the rest of the world's currencies, those commodities become more expensive *in every other currency* — which dampens demand for them, which pushes their dollar price down too. It works in both directions at once: a strong dollar both makes commodities pricier abroad and, because demand softens, drags the headline dollar price lower.

The cleanest way to see this is to imagine being a buyer outside the US.

#### Worked example: a strong dollar makes the same oil cost more

Suppose oil is \$80 a barrel. A European refiner buys it in dollars. At the start of the year, the euro is worth \$1.10, so one barrel costs the refiner \$80 ÷ 1.10 = **€72.7**.

Now the dollar strengthens. The euro falls from \$1.10 to \$0.95 — a roughly 14% stronger dollar. The screen price of oil hasn't budged; it's still \$80. But for the European refiner, that same barrel now costs \$80 ÷ 0.95 = **€84.2**. The oil just got 16% more expensive in euros *without the dollar price moving at all*.

Multiply that across every commodity buyer outside the US — and most of the world's commodity demand is outside the US — and you get less buying at the margin, which pushes the *dollar* price of the commodity down as well. So a strong dollar is a double headwind for commodities: dearer abroad, and softer in dollars. The intuition: **a commodity priced in dollars is, for everyone else, a leveraged bet against their own currency — and the dollar's strength is the thing leaning on the price.**

### Channel two: dollar debt gets heavier — the global margin call

The funding channel is where the dollar turns from a headwind into a force that can break things. Recall the ~\$13 trillion of dollar debt owed by borrowers outside the US. Those borrowers earn revenue in their own currencies — reais, rupees, rand, pesos — but owe their debts in dollars. When the dollar strengthens against their currency, their debt gets *heavier* in the only money they actually have. Nothing about the loan changed; the exchange rate did all the damage.

This is exactly like a *margin call* in a brokerage account — when the value of your collateral falls and the broker demands more money to cover your loan, forcing you to sell assets to raise cash. A rising dollar is a margin call on the whole world's dollar borrowers at once. They cut spending, sell assets, and pull back on investment to scrape together the now-bigger debt payments. That tightening is not gentle; it ripples through emerging-market economies and markets as a synchronized contraction.

![A rising dollar raises local debt costs and forces foreign borrowers to tighten and sell](/imgs/blogs/the-dollar-cross-asset-gravity-6.png)

The figure above traces the chain step by step: the dollar rises, the borrower's currency falls against it, the local-currency cost of the same dollar loan jumps, the borrower tightens and sells to fund the bigger payments, and — multiplied across \$13 trillion of dollar debt — the whole emerging-market and risk complex gets pulled lower together. This is the mechanism that turns a dollar rally into a global financial event. Let us put real numbers on the central node.

#### Worked example: a +20% dollar move is a +25% debt shock

An emerging-market company borrowed **\$100,000,000** in dollars back when DXY was 90 and its local currency bought dollars cheaply. Say at that time, one dollar cost 90 units of local currency, so the debt was worth 90 × \$100M = **9.0 billion** local units.

Now the dollar surges 20%, DXY going from 90 to 108 — and, as typically happens to emerging-market currencies in a dollar surge, the local currency falls about 20% against the dollar. One dollar now costs about 113 local units instead of 90.

The loan is *exactly the same* — still \$100,000,000, not a dollar more borrowed. But measured in the local currency the company actually earns, it is now worth 113 × \$100M = **11.3 billion** local units. That is a **~25% increase in the debt burden** with no new borrowing, driven entirely by the exchange rate. (The 25% comes from the currency falling: if the local currency buys 20% fewer dollars, the same dollar debt costs 1 ÷ 0.80 = 1.25× as much local money.)

For one company, that is a brutal year. Now multiply it across the **~\$13 trillion** of dollar debt owed outside the US. A rising dollar simultaneously inflates the real burden of trillions in obligations across dozens of countries. That is why a strong dollar is, mechanically, a *global margin call* — and it is the gravity that pulls emerging markets, commodities, and risk lower together. The intuition: **the dollar's level is set in New York, but the debt is paid in local money, so a dollar rally is a tax the whole world pays at once.**

### Channel three: the dollar is itself a risk-off asset

The third channel is the most subtle, because it is about *psychology and plumbing combined*. When the world gets scared — a financial crisis, a war, a pandemic — investors do not run toward risk; they run toward safety. And the safest, most liquid asset on earth is US government debt, paid in dollars. To buy US Treasuries, foreigners must first buy dollars. So a global panic creates a surge of *demand* for dollars, pushing the dollar up exactly when everything risky is falling.

This makes the dollar a **risk-off asset**: it tends to rise when risk assets fall. That is rare and valuable. Most things that go up in a panic (like long-dated Treasuries or, sometimes, gold) are bets on falling interest rates or fear. The dollar goes up for a more basic reason — the world's deleveraging machine runs on dollars, and a scramble for safety is a scramble for dollars. The dollar is the asset everyone owes and everyone wants when the music stops.

There is a second, mechanical layer to channel three that is worth spelling out, because it explains why dollar spikes in a panic can be so violent. Recall the funding channel: trillions of dollars of debt are owed by people who *don't* earn dollars. In normal times they roll that debt over smoothly — they pay off an old dollar loan with a new one. But in a panic, lenders pull back, and the borrowers suddenly have to find *actual dollars* to repay what's due. They sell whatever they can — local stocks, bonds, their own currency — to buy the dollars they owe. That forced buying is a *short squeeze on the dollar*: the people who are effectively "short" dollars (they owe more than they hold) all scramble to cover at once. It is why, in March 2020, even as the crisis hit the US directly, the dollar *spiked* — the whole world was short dollars and had to cover. A risk-off scare and a dollar squeeze are, at the deepest level, the same event.

These three channels — commodities dearer, debt heavier, safe-haven demand — all push the same way. That is why a rising dollar doesn't just hurt one asset; it leans on the entire risk complex at once. They are the three legs of the dollar's gravity. And critically, they *reinforce* each other in a feedback loop: a rising dollar pressures commodities and EM, which scares investors, which sends them fleeing to the dollar, which makes the dollar rise further. That is why dollar moves, once they start, tend to feed on themselves and overshoot — the very persistence we'll see on the chart.

#### Worked example: the dollar squeeze in a panic

Walk through a stylized crisis. A foreign bank has borrowed **\$500,000,000** short-term, which it has lent out to local businesses in its own currency. Normally, when the \$500M comes due, the bank simply borrows a fresh \$500M to repay it — a smooth roll.

Now a panic hits and dollar lenders refuse to roll. The bank must come up with **\$500,000,000 in cash** within days. It doesn't have the dollars; it has local-currency loans it can't quickly call in. So it sells whatever it can in the open market — local government bonds, equities, the local currency itself — and uses the proceeds to buy dollars. Say it has to dump \$500M worth of local assets and buy \$500M.

Multiply that one bank by hundreds of institutions worldwide doing the same thing in the same week, and you get the picture: a wall of forced *selling* of risk assets and forced *buying* of dollars, all at once. The dollar rockets up not because anyone is bullish on America, but because the world is short dollars and is being margin-called into covering. The intuition: **a dollar spike in a crisis is not optimism about the US — it is the global financial system being forced to repay what it borrowed in the one currency it can't print.**

## The cross-asset map: what the dollar does to each asset

Let us assemble the channels into a single map of how each major asset class behaves when the dollar moves. The pattern is remarkably consistent, and once you have it memorized you can read most cross-asset days from the dollar alone.

When the **dollar goes UP**, almost everything risky goes down:

- **Commodities** fall — dearer abroad (channel one), and the global growth scare that often accompanies a dollar surge cuts demand.
- **Gold** falls — it is priced in dollars (channel one again) and competes with the dollar as a store of value; a stronger dollar with higher real yields is gold's natural enemy.
- **Emerging-market equities and currencies** fall hardest — the debt channel (channel two) hits them squarely, and capital flees back to the dollar.
- **Risk assets broadly** — high-yield credit, smaller-cap stocks, crypto — come under pressure as global financial conditions tighten.
- **US large-cap stocks** are *relatively* resilient. They are not immune, but they are where global capital hides; a strong dollar can even reflect strength in the US economy that supports them. They go down least.

When the **dollar goes DOWN**, the map reverses into the **reflation trade**:

- Commodities rise (cheaper abroad, looser conditions).
- Gold rises (the dollar headwind becomes a tailwind).
- Emerging markets rally hard — the debt burden eases and capital floods back.
- Risk assets broadly catch a bid as global liquidity loosens.

The single most useful way to see this is not anecdotally but in the *correlations* — how each asset's monthly returns line up with the dollar's. The figure below shows them.

![Bar chart of how each asset class correlates negatively with the US dollar from gold to bonds](/imgs/blogs/the-dollar-cross-asset-gravity-4.png)

Read those bars. Over 2015–2024, monthly returns of nearly every risk asset were *negatively* correlated with the dollar. Gold was the most inverse at about **−0.40**, commodities about **−0.35**, stocks and high yield about **−0.30**, REITs about **−0.25**, and even bonds about **−0.20**. A correlation of −0.40 is not a perfect mirror — plenty of months break the pattern — but it is a strong, persistent tilt. The dollar is the rare asset that is negatively correlated with *almost the entire rest of the risk complex at once*. That is precisely what makes it the master signal: one variable that leans on everything.

A note on what the numbers mean. A *correlation* runs from −1.0 (the two move exactly opposite) to +1.0 (they move exactly together), with 0 meaning no relationship. So −0.40 for gold means that, on average, a strong-dollar month is a weak-gold month and vice versa — not always, but more often than not, and meaningfully so. The fact that the dollar's correlations are *negative across the board* — against stocks, commodities, gold, credit, and property — is the empirical fingerprint of cross-asset gravity.

The table below collapses the whole map into one reference card. For each asset, it names the *channel* through which the dollar reaches it, what happens when the dollar is strong, and what happens when it's weak.

| Asset class | Channel the dollar uses | Dollar STRONG | Dollar WEAK |
|---|---|---|---|
| Commodities | priced in dollars (invoicing) | dearer abroad, demand softens → down | cheaper abroad → up |
| Gold | priced in dollars + store-of-value rival | competes with dollar + real yields → down | tailwind → up |
| EM equities + FX | dollar debt burden (funding) | margin call, capital flight → down hard | debt eases, capital returns → up hard |
| High-yield credit | global risk appetite | conditions tighten → spreads widen, down | conditions loosen → up |
| US large-cap | safe haven + multinational earnings | relatively resilient (down least) | up with the risk tide |
| US Treasuries | safe haven (shared with the dollar) | both can rise in panic; mixed | mildly negative |

Notice the asymmetry in the EM row: it says "down *hard*" and "up *hard*." Emerging markets feel the dollar through *two* channels at once — the equity-risk channel that everything risky shares, plus the dollar-debt channel that is theirs alone. That double exposure is why EM is the highest-beta bet on the dollar cycle, and why getting the dollar right matters most for EM positioning.

#### Worked example: reading a day from the dollar alone

Suppose you wake up and the only thing you know is that DXY jumped 1.2% overnight — a big one-day move for the dollar. Before you look at anything else, what is your prior for the rest of the board?

Using the correlations: gold (−0.40) and commodities (−0.35) are probably down the most; stocks (−0.30) and high yield (−0.30) probably down; emerging markets, which combine the stock channel and the debt channel, probably down hardest of all; US large-caps down least. You'd guess a "risk-off" tape — red across commodities, EM, and gold, with the US holding up relatively better. If you then open the screens and that is roughly what you see, you have *explained the whole day with one number*. If instead everything is up despite the strong dollar, that divergence is itself information — something asset-specific is overriding the dollar, and it's worth finding out what. The intuition: **the dollar is the first thing to check, because most days, it sets the weather for everything else.**

## How the dollar behaves: trends for years, and the dollar smile

To use the dollar as a signal, you have to understand its *temperament* — how it tends to move through time. Two features dominate, and both are unusual.

### The dollar trends for years

Most people assume currencies bounce around randomly. The dollar does not. It moves in long, multi-year cycles — big bull runs and big bear runs that can last half a decade or more, driven by slow-moving forces like the gap between US and foreign interest rates, the relative strength of the US economy, and global risk appetite. The figure below shows the US Dollar Index from 2014 through 2025.

![Line chart of the US Dollar Index from 2014 to 2025 with the 2015 and 2022 surges marked](/imgs/blogs/the-dollar-cross-asset-gravity-2.png)

Trace the line. From a base around 80 in 2014, the dollar surged into the high 90s and low 100s through 2015–2016 as the Fed ended its post-crisis stimulus and began hiking — the *taper* and liftoff. It then chopped sideways and softened, bottoming near **89.9 at the end of 2020** in the weak-dollar reflation of the post-COVID recovery. Then came the 2022 wrecking ball: as the Fed hiked faster than in 40 years while the rest of the world lagged, DXY rocketed to a **103.5 year-end close** and an intraday peak near **114.8** in September. It stayed elevated through 2024 (108.5 close) before easing to 97.0 by the end of 2025.

The lesson for an allocator is that the dollar is *not* mean-reverting on a dime. When it starts a trend, it tends to persist — which means a dollar move is rarely a one-day event you can ignore. A dollar that breaks higher and keeps going is a multi-quarter headwind for commodities and EM; a dollar that breaks lower is a multi-quarter tailwind. The persistence is what makes the signal tradeable rather than noise.

What drives these multi-year trends? Above all, the *interest-rate gap* between the US and the rest of the world. Money chases yield: when US short-term rates are well above European, Japanese, and other developed rates, global capital flows into dollars to earn that gap, and the dollar trends up. When the gap closes — the Fed cuts while others hold, or others hike to catch up — the flow reverses and the dollar trends down. In 2022, the gap between US rates (heading above 4%) and Japanese rates (pinned near zero) was the widest in years, and the dollar surged accordingly. A second driver is *relative growth*: when the US economy outperforms, its assets earn more and pull in capital. A third is *risk appetite* — the smile's safe-haven pull. These forces are slow-moving, which is exactly why dollar trends last for years rather than days. For the full mechanism of why rates set the dollar, the macro post on [the dollar system and how to read DXY](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy) goes a level deeper.

#### Worked example: the rate gap that powers a dollar trend

Suppose you have **\$10,000** and you can park it in dollars earning 4.5% a year, or convert it to yen and earn 0.1% in Japan. Hold the dollars and after a year you have \$10,000 × 1.045 = **\$10,450**. Convert to yen and (ignoring any currency move) you'd earn just \$10,000 × 1.001 = **\$10,010** worth of yen. The dollar pays you **\$440 more** for doing nothing but sitting in the higher-rate currency.

Now imagine every global investor facing that same choice with trillions of dollars. The rational move is to hold dollars — so capital floods *toward* the dollar, pushing it up. The bigger the rate gap, the stronger the pull, and the longer it persists as long as the gap stays open. That is the engine under the dollar's multi-year trends: it is not sentiment, it is the cold arithmetic of where money earns more. The intuition: **a dollar trend is usually just the world's capital following the interest-rate gap, and the gap moves slowly — so the trend lasts.**

### The dollar smile

The second feature is the strangest and the most useful: the dollar tends to be strong at *both* ends of the global cycle and weak only in the middle. Picture the dollar's strength as a function of the global growth environment, and it traces a U-shape — a *smile*.

![The dollar smile showing the dollar strong in a global boom and a global panic and weak in the calm middle](/imgs/blogs/the-dollar-cross-asset-gravity-5.png)

The figure above is the dollar smile, read for cross-asset consequences. The two upturned corners are the two ways the dollar gets strong, and they could not be more different:

- **The right corner — a US boom.** When the American economy is outgrowing the rest of the world and the Fed is hiking rates, US assets pay more than foreign ones, so global capital chases dollars. The dollar rises on *strength*. This is the 2022 story: the Fed hiked aggressively, US yields soared above Europe's and Japan's, and money flooded into dollars. For the rest of the world, this corner means tighter conditions — EM debt gets heavier, commodities get pressured.

- **The left corner — a global panic.** When the world is terrified — a crisis, a crash — investors flee to safety, and safety means US Treasuries, which means buying dollars (channel three). The dollar rises on *fear*. This is the 2008 and March-2020 story: even when the crisis *originated* in the US, the dollar still rose, because the world's scramble for liquidity is a scramble for dollars. For the rest of the world, this corner *also* means tighter conditions — everything risky falls together.

- **The middle — calm, synchronized growth.** When the world is growing together and nobody is scared, capital leaves the safe-but-low-yielding dollar to chase higher returns abroad — in emerging markets, in commodities, in foreign stocks. The dollar *weakens*. This is the bottom of the smile, and it is the green-light regime: a falling dollar is the reflation trade, when commodities, EM, and gold all rise together.

The smile is why the dollar is uniquely useful. A strong dollar can mean "US is booming" *or* "world is panicking" — opposite states of the world. But for the *rest* of the risk complex, both corners feel the same: a strong dollar is a headwind either way. So when you see DXY rising hard, you don't need to know *which* corner you're in to know the cross-asset implication — risk assets are under pressure. The smile is also a caution: a strong dollar is not automatically a "growth is great" signal. The same chart shows it could mean the opposite. You read the *rest of the board* to tell which corner you're standing in.

#### Worked example: the same DXY level, two opposite worlds

DXY is 108 in two different scenarios. In **Scenario A (2024-style)**, the US economy is strong, the Fed is holding rates high, stocks are near record highs, and credit spreads are tight. A \$100 investment in US large-caps is doing fine; the strong dollar reflects US strength. You are on the *right* of the smile.

In **Scenario B (a hypothetical panic)**, DXY is also 108, but it got there in three weeks as a crisis erupted, equities are down 20%, and credit spreads have blown out. The same \$100 in US large-caps is now \$80; the strong dollar reflects fear. You are on the *left* of the smile.

Same dollar level, opposite worlds — and the way you tell them apart is not the dollar but the *speed of its move and the company it keeps*. A slow grind higher alongside rising stocks is the right corner; a violent spike higher alongside falling stocks is the left corner. Either way, for commodities and EM the implication is the same headwind — which is exactly why the dollar is the first read and the *rest of the board* is the confirmation. The intuition: **the dollar tells you conditions are tight; the rest of the cross-asset map tells you whether it's a boom or a bust doing the tightening.**

## How it shows up in real markets

Theory is clean; markets are messy. Here are the episodes where the dollar's cross-asset gravity was unmistakable — real dates, real numbers, the mechanism in action.

### 2014–2015: the taper tantrum's dollar surge crushes commodities and EM

In mid-2014, the Federal Reserve was winding down its post-crisis bond-buying and signaling rate hikes to come. The dollar took off. DXY rose from about 80 in mid-2014 to **98.7 by the end of 2015** — a roughly 20%+ surge in around 18 months. The cross-asset damage was textbook.

The Bloomberg Commodity Index, which tracks a broad basket of commodities, fell **−17.0% in 2014 and then −24.7% in 2015** — its worst run in a generation. Oil collapsed from over \$100 a barrel in mid-2014 to the \$30s by early 2016. This was partly a supply story (US shale was flooding the market), but the dollar amplified everything: as the dollar strengthened, every commodity got dearer abroad and cheaper in dollars at once. Emerging-market equities and currencies fell in sympathy — the MSCI EM index dropped about 15% in 2015 — as the debt channel tightened conditions across the developing world. The dollar's chart and commodities' chart were near-perfect mirror images.

![Dual axis chart of the US Dollar Index against a cumulative Bloomberg Commodity Index showing the inverse](/imgs/blogs/the-dollar-cross-asset-gravity-3.png)

The figure above puts the two series on one chart. The blue line is the dollar; the amber line is the commodity index, rebased to 100 at the start of 2014. The shaded window is the 2014–2015 strong-dollar period, and you can see commodities crater exactly as the dollar surges. Across the whole decade the two lines trace a rough mirror — when one rises, the other tends to fall. That is the inverse relationship in pictures, and it is the cleanest single proof of channel one.

### 2022: the dollar wrecking ball pressures everything at once

We opened with this one because it is the purest example in modern history. In 2022, the Fed raised rates from near zero to over 4% in a single year — the fastest tightening in four decades — while the European Central Bank lagged and the Bank of Japan kept rates pinned near zero. The interest-rate gap exploded in the dollar's favor, and the dollar did what the right corner of the smile predicts: it surged. DXY climbed from about 96 to an intraday peak near **114.8** in late September, closing the year at 103.5.

The cross-asset wreckage was total. The S&P 500 fell about 18%. The Bloomberg US Aggregate bond index fell about 13% — making 2022 the rare year both stocks and bonds fell hard together, a catastrophe for the classic 60/40 portfolio, which lost roughly 16%, its worst year since the 1930s. The yen collapsed past 145 per dollar, forcing Japan to intervene. Emerging markets were hammered. Even gold, which finished the year roughly flat at **\$1,824 an ounce**, spent most of the year *down* despite raging inflation — because the surging dollar and rising real yields overwhelmed gold's inflation-hedge appeal. The one notable winner was commodities themselves, where a genuine supply shock from the war in Ukraine pushed the Bloomberg Commodity Index up 16.1% — a reminder that a real-world supply story can sometimes beat the dollar headwind. But the *dominant* fact of 2022 was the dollar, and the dollar pulled almost everything else down.

### 2020 H2: the weak dollar lights the reflation trade

For the mirror image, look at the second half of 2020. After the COVID crash in March (when the dollar briefly spiked in a classic left-corner panic, DXY jumping toward 103 as the world scrambled for dollars), the Fed flooded the system with liquidity, cutting rates to zero and buying bonds at unprecedented scale. The dollar rolled over. DXY fell from that March spike to **89.9 by year-end 2020** — a meaningful weakening.

And the reflation trade lit up exactly as the map predicts. Gold surged to a year-end **\$1,898 an ounce** (a 25% gain on the year). Emerging-market equities, which had been crushed in March, rallied hard into year-end. Commodities bottomed and began the climb that would carry the Bloomberg Commodity Index up **27.1% in 2021**. Copper, oil, and industrial metals all turned up. The weak dollar was loosening global financial conditions, and the risk complex breathed out. If you had used the dollar as your master signal in mid-2020 — "the dollar is breaking down, that's a green light for commodities, EM, and gold" — you would have caught the entire reflation rally before it became consensus.

### 2024: a strong dollar that meant strength, not stress

One more, to keep us honest about the smile. By 2024, the dollar was strong again — DXY closing the year at **108.5** — but this was the *right* corner, not the left. The US economy was outperforming, the Fed was holding rates high while other central banks cut, and US stocks were near record highs (the S&P 500 returned about 25% in 2024). Here the strong dollar coexisted with strong US risk assets, because it reflected US economic strength rather than global fear. Gold *also* rose sharply in 2024 (to **\$2,625 an ounce**, up over 27%), partly on central-bank buying and geopolitical hedging — a reminder that the dollar-gold inverse is a strong tendency, not an iron law. The lesson: a strong dollar pressures the *rest of the world* most reliably; US large-caps, in the right corner of the smile, can thrive alongside it.

## Common misconceptions

The dollar attracts more confident wrong opinions than almost any market. Here are the ones that cost people money, each corrected with the mechanism.

### "A strong dollar is good for stocks because it means a strong economy."

Sometimes — in the right corner of the smile. But the relationship is *negative on average*: the dollar's correlation with US stocks over 2015–2024 was about **−0.30**. A strong dollar tightens global conditions, hurts the overseas earnings of US multinationals (which earn a big chunk of S&P 500 profits abroad), and pressures the rest of the world. The "strong dollar = strong stocks" story holds only when the strength comes from US *outperformance* rather than from global stress — and you can't tell which from the dollar alone. Treating a rising dollar as automatically bullish for stocks is a good way to be long into a tightening.

### "Gold and the dollar are basically the same safe-haven trade."

The opposite. Gold is priced in dollars and is *negatively* correlated with the dollar at about **−0.40** — the most inverse of any major asset. When the dollar is strong, gold usually struggles, because the two compete as stores of value and because a strong dollar typically comes with higher real yields, which make zero-yielding gold less attractive. In a panic, *both* can rise (left corner of the smile), which confuses people — but over full cycles they pull in opposite directions far more than they move together. They are rivals, not teammates.

### "If the dollar is so important, EM stocks must be a bad long-term hold."

EM is *cyclically* tied to the dollar, not permanently doomed by it. In weak-dollar regimes — the reflation trade — emerging markets are among the best performers anywhere, because the debt channel reverses into a tailwind and capital floods back. The mistake is owning EM at the *wrong point in the dollar cycle*, not owning it at all. The dollar tells you *when* to lean into EM (dollar falling) and when to back off (dollar surging) — which is exactly the kind of timing dial this whole series is built to give you.

### "The dollar's reserve status is collapsing, so it won't matter soon."

The dollar's reserve share has slipped — from 71.5% in 2001 to about 57.8% in 2024 — and that is a real, slow trend worth watching. But "slipped to 58%" is not "collapsing." No rival is remotely close: the euro is at 20%, the renminbi at 2.2%. The dollar is still where more than half the world's official savings sit, the currency half of trade is invoiced in, and the currency the world borrows \$13 trillion in. For an allocator's horizon — years, not decades — the dollar's gravity is intact. Betting that it has stopped mattering has been a losing trade for a very long time.

### "A weak dollar is bad — it means America is in decline."

For a cross-asset investor, a weakening dollar is usually a *loosening* of global conditions — the green light for the reflation trade, when commodities, EM, and gold all tend to rise. A controlled dollar decline in a synchronized global expansion (the bottom of the smile) is one of the most reliably bullish regimes for the broad risk complex. "Weak dollar = bad" confuses a national-pride frame with a financial-conditions frame. As a market signal, a softening dollar is more often a tailwind than a warning.

### "The dollar and US stocks always move together because they're both 'America.'"

They do not — and conflating them is one of the most expensive beginner errors. The dollar is the *price of money for the world*; US stocks are claims on US corporate profits. Their correlation is mildly *negative* (−0.30), not positive. A surging dollar can coincide with falling US stocks (2022) or with rising ones (2024) depending on whether the strength comes from global stress or US outperformance. The point is that "the dollar" and "US stocks" are answering different questions — one about global financial conditions, the other about earnings — and treating them as the same trade will get you long risk into a tightening or short risk into a boom. Keep them in separate mental buckets.

## When to own it: the dollar as the master conditions gauge

Here is the payoff. You don't usually *own* the dollar as a long-term holding — it earns only the US short-term interest rate and has no long-run "up." You use it as the **master conditions gauge**: a single dial that tells you whether to lean into or hedge the rest of the risk complex. The figure below is the playbook in one matrix.

![Matrix of how to position each asset class when the dollar is falling versus surging](/imgs/blogs/the-dollar-cross-asset-gravity-7.png)

The matrix above reads as the operating manual. Down the rows are the asset classes; the two columns are *dollar falling* (green light) and *dollar surging* (warning). Read it as your default playbook, then let the specifics of the moment adjust the sizing.

### The signal: watch DXY first, every day

Make the US Dollar Index the first thing you check. Not because it predicts everything — it doesn't — but because most cross-asset days are downstream of it. A useful discipline: before you form a view on commodities, EM, gold, or broad risk, ask "what is the dollar doing, and is it trending?" The direction and the *speed* are what matter. A slow drift is background; a sharp break is a regime signal.

### The green light: a breaking dollar (down)

When the dollar breaks *lower* and starts a trend — especially in a synchronized global expansion (the bottom of the smile) — that is the green light for the reflation trade. Historically this is when you want to lean *into* commodities, emerging-market equities and currencies, and gold. The debt channel reverses into a tailwind, conditions loosen, and the assets that suffer most under a strong dollar tend to outperform. The 2020 H2 weak-dollar turn that launched the 2021 commodity boom is the template: a falling dollar was the early, public signal of the rally.

### The warning: a surging dollar (up)

When the dollar *surges* — a fast, trending move higher — treat it as a warning across the whole risk complex, regardless of which corner of the smile you think you're in. Raise hedges, trim the most dollar-sensitive exposures (EM, commodities, high-yield credit), and lean toward the relatively resilient hideout of US large-caps and cash. The 2022 wrecking ball is the template: a dollar ripping toward 114.8 was the single best early warning that almost everything else was about to fall together. You did not need to forecast the recession or the war; you needed to respect the dollar.

### Rough sizing and what pairs with what

This is education, not individualized advice — but the broad shape of the playbook is well established. In a *weak-dollar* regime, allocators tend to add to commodities (often via a broad index), tilt toward EM equities, and hold gold as both an inflation and a weak-dollar play; these pair naturally because they all benefit from the same loosening. In a *strong-dollar* regime, the defensive move is to reduce the dollar-sensitive sleeve, hold more US large-cap and cash, and — for those who can — own the dollar itself or dollar-up hedges as the one thing that rises while the rest falls. The dollar is the rare asset that is *negatively* correlated with the whole risk book, which is exactly what makes a small long-dollar position such a clean portfolio hedge in a risk-off scare.

### What invalidates the read

The dollar is a *strong tendency, not a law*, and you have to know when it's not in charge. Three things can override it:

- **A genuine supply shock.** In 2022, commodities *rose* despite a surging dollar because the war in Ukraine was a real supply story strong enough to beat the dollar headwind. When a commodity has its own powerful fundamental driver, the dollar takes a back seat.
- **A specific asset story.** Gold rose in 2024 *alongside* a strong dollar, driven by central-bank buying and geopolitical hedging. When an asset has a structural buyer that doesn't care about the dollar, the inverse can break for a while.
- **A regime where the dollar isn't trending.** When DXY is chopping sideways in a tight range, it isn't sending a signal — and forcing a cross-asset call out of dollar noise is a mistake. The signal is in the *trend and the speed*, not every wiggle.

The discipline is simple: **the dollar is your first read and your default prior, but the rest of the cross-asset board is your confirmation.** When the dollar and the board agree — dollar up, risk down — you have a high-conviction regime. When they disagree — dollar up but risk also up — that divergence is the flag to dig into what asset-specific story is overriding the gravity.

The deeper context for all of this is global liquidity, the tide of money sloshing through the system. The dollar is the price of that money, and the two move together: a strong dollar and tightening liquidity are usually the same story told two ways.
