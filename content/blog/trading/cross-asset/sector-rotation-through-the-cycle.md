> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Even when the stock index goes nowhere, leadership *inside* it rotates with the business cycle. Cyclicals (technology, consumer discretionary, industrials, financials, materials) lead early and mid cycle; energy and materials lead the late-cycle overheat; defensives (consumer staples, utilities, healthcare) lead the slowdown and recession. Reading this rotation tells you both *where in the cycle you are* and *how to add return inside the equity part of your portfolio*.
>
> - The 11 GICS sectors sort into three behavior buckets: **cyclical / high-beta** (swing harder than the market), **defensive / low-beta** (swing less, demand-inelastic), and **rate-sensitive** (bond-proxies and long-duration growth that fall when yields rise).
> - The rotation map is: **early cycle** → financials, discretionary, industrials, small-caps; **mid cycle** → technology and growth, broad; **late cycle** → energy, materials, value; **recession** → staples, utilities, healthcare.
> - Leadership is also a **signal**. When defensives quietly start beating the index, the cycle may be turning down. When energy leads, you are late cycle with inflation in the system. You can read the regime off the sectors before the data confirms it.
> - The one number to remember: in 2022, the S&P energy sector returned about **+65.7%** while consumer discretionary fell **−37.0%** and communication services fell **−39.9%** — a leadership spread of more than **100 percentage points** *within the same flat-to-down index*.

In 2022, a US investor who owned "the stock market" had a miserable year: the S&P 500 fell about 18%. But that single number hides a civil war that was raging *inside* the index. One sector, energy, returned roughly **+65.7%** — it nearly doubled some holdings. At the same time, consumer discretionary fell about **−37.0%** and communication services fell about **−39.9%**. The gap between the best sector and the worst was more than **100 percentage points**, in a year when the headline index barely moved a fifth. If you owned the right sectors, 2022 was one of the best years of your investing life. If you owned the wrong ones, it was one of the worst. Same market. Same year. Opposite outcomes.

This is the thing that passive index investing quietly hides from you: the index is an average, and an average can be flat while its components are flying apart. The stock market is not one thing — it is eleven things bolted together, and those eleven things take turns leading depending on where the economy is in its cycle. Learning *which* sectors lead *when*, and *why*, is the difference between riding the cycle and being run over by it. It is also the cleanest way to add return inside the equity part of your portfolio without taking on a different asset class.

The diagram below is the mental model for this whole post: a clock face where leadership rotates clockwise through the cycle. Cyclicals lead as the economy recovers and expands; energy and materials lead as it overheats; defensives lead as it slows and contracts. By the end, you will be able to look at which sectors are quietly outperforming and say, with real confidence, "we are late cycle — I want more energy and value and less expensive growth," or "defensives are leading — the cycle is rolling over, time to play defense."

This post sits on top of the broader cycle map. If you have not read it, [the business cycle and the investment clock](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-business-cycle-and-the-investment-clock) lays out the four phases that we will tilt around here, and [equities, stocks: owning a slice of growth](/blog/trading/cross-asset/equities-stocks-owning-a-slice-of-growth) builds up what a stock actually is. This post is the "market within the market" — how the *internal* leadership of equities rotates, and how to trade it.

A quick honesty note before we start: this is educational, not financial advice. We are going to explain mechanisms, history, and tradeoffs so you can reason for yourself. Nothing here is a recommendation to buy or sell anything.

## Foundations: the 11 sectors and what "cyclicality" and "beta" mean

Before we can talk about rotation, we have to define the building blocks from zero. What *is* a sector? What makes one "cyclical" and another "defensive"? And what do the words "beta" and "duration" mean when we say a sector has them? Assume you have never seen a sector classification in your life — we will build it up carefully.

### What a "sector" is

The stock market contains thousands of companies. To make sense of them, the industry sorts every public company into one of **11 sectors** under a standard called **GICS** — the *Global Industry Classification Standard*, jointly maintained by index providers S&P and MSCI. A *sector* is just a bucket of companies that do broadly similar things and therefore tend to respond to the economy in similar ways. The 11 GICS sectors are:

1. **Information Technology** (we will call it "Technology" or "Tech") — software, semiconductors, hardware.
2. **Consumer Discretionary** — things people buy when they feel rich: cars, restaurants, travel, luxury, big-box retail.
3. **Industrials** — machinery, aerospace, railroads, construction, logistics.
4. **Financials** — banks, insurers, asset managers, exchanges.
5. **Materials** — metals, mining, chemicals, paper, building products.
6. **Energy** — oil and gas producers, refiners, pipelines, oilfield services.
7. **Communication Services** — telecom carriers plus, since a 2018 reclassification, large internet/media names (search, social, streaming).
8. **Consumer Staples** — things people buy no matter what: food, beverages, household products, tobacco.
9. **Utilities** — electricity, gas, and water providers; regulated, steady.
10. **Health Care** — drugmakers, medical devices, hospitals, insurers.
11. **Real Estate** — real estate investment trusts (REITs) and property companies (split out of Financials in 2016).

You do not have to memorize these. What matters is that they fall into a small number of *behavioral* groups, and those groups are what actually drive rotation. Let us define those groups, because they are the spine of everything that follows.

![Eleven GICS sectors sorted into cyclical, defensive, and rate-sensitive groups](/imgs/blogs/sector-rotation-through-the-cycle-2.png)

### Cyclical versus defensive: the core split

The single most important distinction in this whole post is **cyclical versus defensive**.

A **cyclical** sector is one whose fortunes rise and fall sharply *with the business cycle*. When the economy is booming, people buy cars, companies build factories, banks make more loans, and demand for steel and oil surges — so consumer discretionary, industrials, financials, materials, and energy do well. When the economy slumps, all of that demand evaporates, and those same sectors get hammered. Cyclicals are *economically sensitive*: their earnings are geared to growth.

A **defensive** (or *non-cyclical*) sector is one whose fortunes are relatively *insensitive* to the cycle. People buy food, take their medicine, and keep the lights on whether the economy is booming or in recession. So consumer staples, utilities, and health care earn steady cash flows through good times and bad. Their earnings do not surge in a boom, but they do not collapse in a bust either. The technical term for this steadiness is **demand-inelastic**: the quantity people buy barely changes when their income or the price changes.

That asymmetry is the whole game. Cyclicals offer more upside in good times at the cost of more downside in bad times. Defensives offer less of both — a smoother ride. Which one you want depends entirely on where you think the cycle is going.

### What "beta" means

The formal way to measure that sensitivity is **beta**. Beta is a number that tells you how hard a stock or sector tends to move *relative to the overall market*. The market itself has a beta of exactly **1.0** by definition.

- A sector with **beta 1.4** tends to move 1.4 times as much as the market. If the market rises 10%, it tends to rise about 14%; if the market falls 10%, it tends to fall about 14%. It *amplifies* the market.
- A sector with **beta 0.6** tends to move 0.6 times as much. If the market rises 10%, it rises about 6%; if the market falls 10%, it falls about 6%. It *dampens* the market.

The rule of thumb in this post: **cyclicals have high beta (roughly 1.2 to 1.5); defensives have low beta (roughly 0.5 to 0.8).** That is not a coincidence — it is the same economic sensitivity measured a different way. A sector geared to the cycle swings harder than the market (high beta); a demand-inelastic sector swings less (low beta).

> [!note]
> Beta is a *behavior*, not a *quality*. High beta is not "good" or "bad" — it is leverage to the market. You want high beta when the market is rising and low beta when it is falling. The art of sector rotation is matching your beta to the regime.

#### Worked example: beta turns one market move into different sector moves

Let us make beta concrete with round numbers. Suppose the overall stock market rises 10% over some period, and you are choosing between three sectors:

- **Consumer discretionary**, beta ≈ 1.4. Expected move ≈ 1.4 × 10% = **+14%**. On a \$10,000 position, that is about **+\$1,400**.
- **The market itself** (an index fund), beta = 1.0. Expected move = **+10%**, or **+\$1,000** on \$10,000.
- **Utilities**, beta ≈ 0.5. Expected move ≈ 0.5 × 10% = **+5%**, or **+\$500** on \$10,000.

Now suppose instead the market *falls* 10%. The same betas run in reverse: discretionary ≈ **−14%** (−\$1,400), the market **−10%** (−\$1,000), utilities ≈ **−5%** (−\$500). The high-beta sector made nearly three times as much in the up move *and* lost nearly three times as much in the down move. The one-line takeaway: beta is a multiplier on the market — it does not change your direction, it changes your *amplitude*, which is exactly why you want it high in a boom and low in a bust.

### The third axis: rate-sensitive sectors

There is one more grouping that cuts *across* the cyclical/defensive split, and it trips up a lot of beginners: **rate sensitivity**. Some sectors move strongly with **interest rates** — specifically with bond yields — regardless of where the economy is.

There are two flavors:

- **Bond-proxies.** Utilities, REITs, and parts of consumer staples pay steady, predictable, bond-like income. When safe bonds yield almost nothing, investors bid up these "bond substitutes" for their dividends. When bond yields rise, those same investors sell the proxies and buy actual bonds — so utilities and REITs fall when yields rise. They behave like long bonds wearing an equity costume.
- **Long-duration growth.** Technology and other high-growth sectors are valued mostly on *future* profits — earnings expected many years out. We will explain *duration* properly below, but the headline is: the more of a company's value sits far in the future, the more a rise in **real yields** (interest rates adjusted for inflation) shrinks the present value of those future profits. So expensive growth stocks fall when real yields rise, for the same mathematical reason a 30-year bond falls more than a 2-year bond when rates rise.

The one exception that runs the *opposite* way is **financials**, specifically banks. Banks borrow short and lend long; they make money on the *gap* between short and long rates. A steeper yield curve — long rates well above short rates — widens that gap and fattens bank profits. So banks tend to *like* rising long-term rates, the exact opposite of utilities and tech. We will return to this because it is central to early-cycle leadership.

Hold these three groupings in your head — cyclical, defensive, rate-sensitive — because the entire rotation is just these groups taking turns as the cycle's two dials (growth and inflation) move. The deep mechanics of why *real yields* — interest rates after subtracting inflation — price every asset are the same ones that decide which sectors lead, and we will return to them when we get to technology and duration below.

### The three buckets at a glance

It helps to see the buckets side by side, with the one fact that defines each. Notice that two sectors — utilities and parts of tech/financials — appear in more than one bucket, because the buckets describe *behaviors*, not exclusive categories. A sector can be both defensive *and* rate-sensitive (utilities), or both cyclical *and* rate-sensitive (banks, tech).

| Group | Member sectors | Typical beta | The defining fact | Loves | Hates |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cyclical | Discretionary, industrials, financials, materials, energy, (tech) | ~1.2–1.5 | Earnings geared to growth | A booming, accelerating economy | Recession, falling demand |
| Defensive | Staples, utilities, health care | ~0.5–0.8 | Demand-inelastic, steady cash | A slowdown (relative outperformance) | A roaring boom (it lags) |
| Rate-sensitive | Utilities, REITs, growth tech (and banks, opposite sign) | varies | Value moves with bond yields | Falling yields (banks: rising) | Rising real yields (banks: flat curve) |

The reason this table matters for rotation: each phase of the cycle moves *growth* and *inflation* (and therefore yields) in a particular direction, and that direction is exactly what picks the winning bucket. Early cycle: growth up, yields low and curve steep → cyclicals and banks. Mid cycle: growth steady, yields stable → growth tech. Late cycle: growth up, inflation and real yields up → energy/materials (cyclical inflation winners), and the long-duration growth bucket gets hurt. Recession: growth down, yields falling → defensives, especially the rate-sensitive bond-proxies. The buckets and the dials are the same story told twice.

### Value versus growth: the other axis that rides along

There is a parallel rotation that moves in lockstep with the sector cycle, and you will hear it constantly: **value versus growth**. A *value* stock is cheap relative to its current earnings or assets — often a mature, cyclical, short-duration business (a bank, an energy producer, an industrial). A *growth* stock is expensive relative to current earnings because investors are paying for *future* growth — often a long-duration tech or discretionary name. Because value stocks skew cyclical and short-duration while growth stocks skew long-duration, the value/growth rotation tracks the sector rotation closely:

- **Early and late cycle favor value** — early cycle because beaten-down cyclicals snap back, late cycle because rising real yields punish long-duration growth.
- **Mid cycle favors growth** — steady economy, stable-to-falling real yields, and investors willing to pay up for faster-growing companies.

So when you read "value is outperforming growth," translate it: cyclicals and short-duration sectors are leading, which is an early- or late-cycle tell. When growth leads, you are likely mid cycle with calm yields. The value/growth spread is just the sector rotation wearing a different label, and it is one of the most-watched relative-strength signals on the market.

## The rotation map: which sectors lead each phase

Now we can draw the map. The business cycle, as laid out in [the business cycle and the investment clock](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-business-cycle-and-the-investment-clock), moves through four phases, defined by two dials: **growth** (speeding up or slowing down) and **inflation** (accelerating or decelerating). Each phase has a leadership group, and the groups rotate in a fairly reliable clockwise order. Let us walk the clock.

### Early cycle (recovery): cyclicals, financials, and small-caps

The early cycle is the moment just after a recession ends. Growth is turning up from a low base, the central bank has cut interest rates to the floor, and there is enormous slack in the economy — lots of idle workers and factories. This is the phase of maximum *operating leverage*: when demand returns to a beaten-down company, its profits explode off a tiny base, because its costs are already cut to the bone.

So the early-cycle leaders are the most economically-sensitive, highest-beta sectors:

- **Consumer discretionary** — as confidence returns, people buy the cars and appliances they delayed.
- **Industrials** — companies restock and restart capital spending.
- **Financials** (banks) — loan demand recovers, loan losses stop rising, and a steepening yield curve (the central bank pins short rates low while long rates rise on recovery hopes) widens bank margins.
- **Materials** — early demand for raw inputs lifts metals and chemicals.
- **Small-cap stocks** — smaller, more domestic, more economically-sensitive companies snap back hardest.

The cleanest historical example is **2009**, the year after the global financial crisis. As the economy bottomed and turned, the cyclical and high-beta sectors snapped back violently. We will look at the numbers in detail later, but the pattern is unmistakable: the sectors that fell the most in the crash led the recovery. For the full phase, see [early-cycle recovery, what leads the rebound](/blog/trading/cross-asset/early-cycle-recovery-what-leads-the-rebound).

### Mid cycle (expansion): technology, growth, and broad participation

The mid cycle is the long, steady middle of an expansion. Growth is solid but no longer accelerating; inflation is stable; the central bank is neither slashing nor aggressively hiking. The easy snap-back gains are over. This is the phase where **leadership broadens** — most sectors do reasonably well — and where **technology and growth** stocks typically take the baton.

Why tech in the middle? Because mid cycle is when the economy is healthy enough to invest in the *future* rather than just survive the present. Companies and consumers spend on innovation, software, and equipment. And because growth is steady rather than booming, investors are willing to pay up for companies that can grow *faster than* the steady economy — which is the definition of a growth stock. Mid cycle is usually the longest phase and often the calmest, with low volatility and a grinding uptrend — the quiet middle that bookends the snap-back of the recovery and the heat of the late cycle. It sits between the two phases we link out to in detail: [early-cycle recovery, what leads the rebound](/blog/trading/cross-asset/early-cycle-recovery-what-leads-the-rebound) before it, and the overheat after.

### Late cycle (overheat): energy, materials, and value

The late cycle, or **overheat**, is when growth is still strong but the economy is running near full capacity, inflation is *accelerating*, and the central bank is *hiking* to cool things down. This is the one phase where the leadership flips hard toward **real assets** and **value**:

- **Energy** — strong demand plus inflation plus often a supply constraint sends oil and gas prices up, and energy company profits with them. Energy is the purest inflation play in the equity market.
- **Materials** — metals and commodities ride the same inflationary wave.
- **Value stocks** generally (cheaper, shorter-duration, often cyclical companies) beat **growth** stocks, because rising real yields punish the long-duration growth names hardest.

This is the phase that crushed expensive technology and lifted energy in **2022**, the headline example we opened with. The full mechanics — why real assets and short duration win when the economy overheats — are in [late-cycle overheat, when real assets win](/blog/trading/cross-asset/late-cycle-overheat-when-real-assets-win).

### Recession (slowdown): the defensives

Finally, the recession or slowdown. Growth is falling, often outright contracting; the central bank pivots from hiking to *cutting*; risk appetite collapses. Now the leadership rotates fully to the **defensives**:

- **Consumer staples** — people keep buying food and soap; earnings hold up while everything else's collapse.
- **Utilities** — demand for power is steady, and as the central bank cuts rates, the bond-proxy appeal of their dividends rises.
- **Health care** — people do not stop getting sick in a recession; drug and care demand is famously inelastic.

These sectors rarely go *up* in a deep recession — in 2008 everything fell — but they fall *far less* than the index, which is the entire point of defense. They cushion the drawdown so you have more capital left to redeploy at the bottom. See [recession playbook, defense and duration](/blog/trading/cross-asset/recession-playbook-defense-and-duration) for the full downturn allocation.

> [!note]
> Real cycles are messier than this clean clock. Phases overlap, blur, and occasionally skip (2020 compressed a whole cycle into months). The clock is a *map*, not a *timetable*. Use it to know which way the wind is likely blowing, not to predict the exact date leadership turns.

## Why each sector behaves the way it does

The rotation map is only useful if you understand the *mechanism* behind each sector's behavior — otherwise you are just memorizing a list, and you will be helpless the moment a cycle plays out a little differently. Let us go sector group by group and build the "why."

### Financials love a steep yield curve

A bank's core business is *maturity transformation*: it takes in short-term deposits (on which it pays a low rate) and makes long-term loans (on which it charges a higher rate). Its profit is roughly the gap between the two — the **net interest margin**. That gap is set by the **yield curve**, the line plotting interest rates from short maturities to long ones.

- When the curve is **steep** (long rates well above short rates), the bank's borrowing cost is low and its lending rate is high — fat margins. This is typical of early cycle, when the central bank pins short rates at the floor while long rates rise on recovery optimism.
- When the curve **flattens or inverts** (long rates near or below short rates), the margin compresses or vanishes. This is typical of late cycle, when the central bank hikes short rates faster than long rates rise.

This is why financials are an *early-cycle* leader and tend to struggle late cycle: their profitability is literally the slope of the yield curve, which is steepest early and flattest late. (The 2023 regional-bank stress was partly this: banks that loaded up on long bonds when rates were low got crushed when short rates blew past long rates.)

#### Worked example: how the curve shapes a bank's margin

Suppose a simple bank funds itself with \$1,000 of deposits and makes \$1,000 of loans. Walk two regimes:

- **Steep curve (early cycle).** It pays 0.5% on deposits and charges 4.5% on loans. Its margin is 4.5% − 0.5% = **4.0%**, or **\$40** of profit on the \$1,000 book.
- **Flat/inverted curve (late cycle).** The central bank has hiked short rates: now it pays 4.0% on deposits but, because long rates rose less, it only earns 4.5% on loans. Its margin is 4.5% − 4.0% = **0.5%**, or just **\$5** of profit on the same \$1,000.

The bank's loan book did not change, but its profit fell from \$40 to \$5 — an 87% collapse — purely because the curve flattened. The one-line takeaway: a bank's earnings *are* the yield curve's slope, so financials lead when the curve is steep (early cycle) and suffer when it inverts (late cycle).

### Energy and materials love inflation

Energy and materials companies sell physical commodities — barrels of oil, tons of copper, bushels of inputs. Their revenue is *price times volume*, and in an inflationary, late-cycle environment, *both* go up: strong demand lifts volume, and the inflation itself lifts the price of what they sell. Better still, much of their cost base is fixed — a producing oil well costs roughly the same to run whether oil is \$50 or \$100 — so every dollar of higher price drops almost straight to profit. This *operating leverage to price* is why energy earnings can explode in an inflationary boom.

That is the deep reason energy is the equity market's purest **inflation hedge**. When the cost of living is rising and eroding the value of cash and bonds, energy company profits are rising *because* of that same inflation. We explore why this inflation linkage makes energy and materials the leaders of the overheat in [late-cycle overheat, when real assets win](/blog/trading/cross-asset/late-cycle-overheat-when-real-assets-win). The flip side, which we will see in the 2020 numbers, is brutal: when demand collapses and the commodity price crashes, that same operating leverage works in reverse and energy is the *worst* sector.

### Staples and utilities are bond-proxies and demand-inelastic

Consumer staples and utilities share two traits that make them defensive. First, their demand is **inelastic**: you do not cut your electricity use or stop eating in a recession, so their revenue barely dips when growth falls. Second, they pay steady, reliable **dividends** that look and feel like bond coupons — which makes them **bond-proxies**.

That bond-proxy nature is a double-edged sword. In a recession, when the central bank is *cutting* rates, the steady dividend becomes more attractive relative to falling bond yields, and money flows into utilities and staples — they lead. But in a late-cycle environment, when the central bank is *hiking* and bond yields are rising, those same investors sell the proxies to buy actual bonds, and utilities/staples lag. So defensives are not uniformly "safe" — they are *rate-sensitive* defensives, best in a slowdown with falling rates, weaker when rates are rising.

### Technology is long-duration and hurt by rising real yields

This is the subtlest and most important mechanism in the post, so we will build it slowly.

A company's stock is worth the *present value* of all its future profits. "Present value" means we discount future dollars back to today, because a dollar in ten years is worth less than a dollar now. The rate we discount at is tied to interest rates — specifically **real yields** (interest rates after subtracting inflation).

Now, the key insight: **where in time a company's profits sit determines how sensitive its value is to that discount rate.** This is exactly the concept of **duration** from the bond world. A bond whose cash flows come far in the future (a 30-year bond) loses far more value when rates rise than a bond whose cash flows come soon (a 2-year bond). The same logic applies to stocks:

- A cheap, mature company (think a bank or an energy producer) earns most of its value from profits *now* and over the next few years. It is **short duration**. Rising real yields barely dent it.
- An expensive growth company (think a young software firm) earns most of its value from profits expected *many years* out — that is what "growth" means. It is **long duration**. Rising real yields slash the present value of those distant profits, so its stock falls hard.

That is the precise reason technology and other growth sectors get crushed when real yields rise (as in 2022), and why value beats growth in that environment. It is not that tech companies suddenly became worse businesses — it is that the *math of discounting* re-priced their far-future profits downward when the discount rate jumped. This is the single mechanism that ties the late-cycle sector flip together: the same rise in real yields that lifts short-duration energy and value simultaneously hammers long-duration tech, and we walk through it phase-by-phase in [late-cycle overheat, when real assets win](/blog/trading/cross-asset/late-cycle-overheat-when-real-assets-win).

![Sector beta ladder from high-beta cyclicals down to low-beta defensives](/imgs/blogs/sector-rotation-through-the-cycle-6.png)

The ladder above stacks the sectors by beta, which is just another view of everything we have built: consumer discretionary and tech at the top (they amplify the market), materials and energy in the middle (commodity-linked, around market beta), and healthcare, staples, and utilities at the bottom (they dampen the market). When you tilt toward high-beta sectors, you are dialing *up* your exposure to whatever the market does next; when you tilt toward low-beta defensives, you are dialing it *down*. Sector rotation, mechanically, is moving your portfolio up and down this ladder as the cycle turns.

#### Worked example: duration math on two sectors

Let us make the duration idea concrete with a stripped-down model. Take two stocks, each worth \$100 today, but earning their value at different times:

- **"Value Co."** earns most of its value soon — call its effective duration **5 years**.
- **"Growth Co."** earns most of its value far out — call its effective duration **25 years**.

A rough rule from bond math: a 1-percentage-point rise in the discount rate cuts present value by approximately (duration × 1%). So if real yields rise by 1 percentage point:

- Value Co. falls about 5 × 1% = **−5%**, from \$100 to about **\$95**.
- Growth Co. falls about 25 × 1% = **−25%**, from \$100 to about **\$75**.

In 2022, the 10-year real yield rose by roughly 2.8 percentage points. Run the same math at 2.8 points: Value Co. ≈ −14%, Growth Co. ≈ −70%. The real numbers were less extreme than this toy model (real companies have buffers), but the *direction and ranking* held exactly: tech fell −28.2% while energy (very short duration, plus an inflation tailwind) rose +65.7%. The one-line takeaway: rising real yields are a sledgehammer aimed squarely at long-duration growth, which is why late cycle flips leadership from growth to value.

## Sector leadership as a regime signal

Here is where sector rotation becomes more than a return-enhancing tilt: it becomes a **read on the cycle itself**. Markets are forward-looking. Investors do not wait for the official recession announcement to sell cyclicals — they sell them *in anticipation*, often months before the data confirms a downturn. So the *relative strength* of sectors — which ones are quietly outperforming the index — is a real-time signal of what the market collectively believes about the cycle.

**Relative strength** simply means a sector's performance *relative to the index*. If staples are up 2% while the S&P is flat, staples have positive relative strength even though the absolute move is small. The trick is to watch *which group is winning the relative-strength race*, because that often turns before the headlines do.

Two signals are especially powerful:

- **When defensives quietly start outperforming, the cycle may be turning down.** If you notice that utilities, staples, and health care are beating the index — not crashing, just steadily winning on a relative basis — while cyclicals lag, that is the market starting to price a slowdown. Defensive leadership inside a still-rising or flat market is one of the earliest, quietest warnings that the expansion is aging. It is the market whispering "play defense" before it shouts.
- **Energy leadership signals late cycle and inflation.** When energy is the top sector, the market is telling you that inflation is in the system and the cycle is in its overheat phase. Energy leadership is rarely an early-cycle phenomenon; it clusters in late-cycle inflationary regimes. If energy suddenly leads after years of lagging, the inflation regime has likely changed — a signal that ricochets across every asset class, and the one that defines the overheat phase covered in [late-cycle overheat, when real assets win](/blog/trading/cross-asset/late-cycle-overheat-when-real-assets-win).

The deeper point is that sector leadership is a *cross-check* on your macro read. If you think you are early cycle but defensives are leading, one of those two beliefs is wrong — and the market's money is usually a better judge than your narrative. The sectors are voting with real capital, in real time.

#### Worked example: reading the regime off relative strength

Suppose you are trying to figure out where you are in the cycle, and you have three months of sector data. Over that window, the S&P 500 is flat (0%). But underneath:

- Utilities **+6%**, consumer staples **+5%**, health care **+4%** (defensives leading by +4 to +6 points of relative strength).
- Technology **−5%**, consumer discretionary **−7%**, industrials **−4%** (cyclicals lagging by −4 to −7 points).

The index went nowhere, but the *internal* rotation is screaming: money is fleeing economically-sensitive sectors and hiding in demand-inelastic ones. That is the signature of a market pricing a slowdown. A reader of this rotation would lean defensive *before* the GDP or jobs data confirmed anything — which, historically, is exactly when defensive leadership shows up. The one-line takeaway: the index average can hide a regime change that the sector *spread* reveals, so leadership is a leading indicator the headline number is not.

## Common misconceptions

Beginners (and plenty of professionals) carry wrong beliefs about sectors that cost real money. Let us correct the most damaging ones, each with a number.

**Misconception 1: "Tech is always the best sector."** Recency makes this feel true, because tech dominated the 2010s and 2020. But in **2022**, technology fell **−28.2%** while energy rose **+65.7%** — a leadership gap of nearly 94 percentage points in tech's *worst* relative year in over a decade. Tech is a *mid-cycle, falling-real-yield* leader, not an all-weather one. When real yields rise, its long duration becomes a liability, not an asset.

**Misconception 2: "Defensive sectors are safe — they go up in bad times."** They go *down less*, not up. In **2008**, the best sector, consumer staples, still fell **−15.4%**. Defense is *relative* outperformance — losing 15% when the index loses 37% — not absolute safety. If you expect staples to rise in a deep recession, you will be disappointed; expect them to *cushion*, not to *profit*.

**Misconception 3: "Energy is a bad long-term holding, so I should always avoid it."** Energy is *regime-dependent*, not permanently good or bad. It was the **worst** sector in 2020 (**−33.7%**) and the **best** by a mile in 2022 (**+65.7%**). The same sector swung from worst to best in two years. The lesson is not "own energy" or "avoid energy" — it is "own energy in inflationary late cycles and underweight it when demand is collapsing."

**Misconception 4: "Sector rotation means constantly trading in and out."** This is the most expensive misconception. Leadership phases last *months to years*, not days. Over-trading sectors racks up transaction costs and taxes and usually underperforms a patient tilt. The index is the default; the tilt is the alpha; and the tilt should be *slow*, sized to your conviction about the regime — not a frantic weekly churn.

**Misconception 5: "If I just buy the index, sector rotation doesn't affect me."** It affects your *concentration* more than you think. A market-cap-weighted index like the S&P 500 mechanically tilts toward whatever sector has won recently — in 2024, technology and communication services were a huge share of the index. So "just buy the index" is itself a (large, passive) sector bet on the recent winners. Understanding rotation tells you what bet you are *already* making by default.

**Misconception 6: "The rotation clock is a precise timing tool — I can trade the exact handoffs."** The clock gives you the *order* of leadership, not the *dates*. In real time you almost never know you have crossed from mid to late cycle until weeks or months after the fact; the phases blur, and the occasional shock (2020) compresses or skips them entirely. Anyone selling you a model that calls the precise top of tech leadership or the exact day energy takes over is selling false precision. The honest use of the clock is *probabilistic*: it tilts the odds and tells you which way to lean, sized to your confidence — it does not hand you a calendar. A tilt that is right on the regime but a couple of months early or late still captures most of the move; a tilt that is precise on timing but wrong on regime captures nothing.

**Misconception 7: "Defensive and low-volatility are the same as low-risk."** Defensives carry their *own* risks that have nothing to do with the cycle. Utilities and REITs are rate-sensitive bond-proxies, so a sharp rise in yields can hit them hard even in a slowdown. Consumer staples can be squeezed when input-cost inflation outruns their ability to raise prices. Health care carries regulatory and patent-cliff risk. "Defensive" means *low beta to the economic cycle*, not *low risk overall* — you are trading cyclical risk for a different set of exposures, not eliminating risk.

## How it shows up in real markets

Now the proof. Four episodes, each a clean illustration of the rotation in action, with real GICS sector returns. (These are illustrative calendar-year total returns by S&P GICS sector, cross-checked against published year-end sector tables; values are within rounding of the official series.)

### 2009: the cyclical snap-back

Coming out of the global financial crisis, 2009 is the textbook early-cycle recovery. The economy bottomed in mid-2009, the central bank had rates at zero, and the highest-beta, most economically-sensitive sectors snapped back violently off their crash lows. The S&P 500 returned **+26.5%** for the year, but the *internal* dispersion tells the story: **technology +61.7%**, **materials +48.6%**, **consumer discretionary +41.3%** — the cyclicals roughly doubled the index. Meanwhile the defensives lagged: **consumer staples +14.9%**, **utilities +11.9%**, **energy +13.8%**. The pattern is exactly the early-cycle rule: the sectors that fell hardest in 2008 led the rebound in 2009, because their beaten-down earnings had the most operating leverage to recovering demand.

### 2020: the narrow tech dominance

2020 is a strange, instructive case. The COVID crash compressed a whole bear market into five weeks, and the recovery was equally violent — but it was *narrow*, dominated by technology and "stay-at-home" winners, because the pandemic split the economy in two. The S&P 500 returned **+18.4%** for the year, but the spread was enormous: **technology +43.9%**, **consumer discretionary +33.3%** (lifted by e-commerce), **communication services +23.6%** (streaming, social). At the same time, the cyclical sectors that depend on physical, in-person activity were *destroyed*: **energy −33.7%** (oil briefly went negative), and **financials −1.7%** (low rates and feared loan losses). This is a crucial nuance: 2020 *looked* like a tech-led recovery, but unlike a normal early cycle it did *not* lift all cyclicals — energy and financials were left for dead because their specific drivers (oil demand, the yield curve) were crushed by the pandemic and the central bank's response.

### 2022: energy and defensives, the overheat

2022 is the cleanest overheat in modern memory, and we opened the post with it. Inflation surged, the central bank hiked at the fastest pace in 40 years, and real yields jumped about 2.8 percentage points. The leadership flipped hard: **energy +65.7%** (inflation engine in overdrive), and on the defensive side **utilities +1.6%**, **consumer staples −0.6%**, and **health care −2.0%** all massively beat the index. The losers were the long-duration growth sectors that rising real yields punish most: **technology −28.2%**, **consumer discretionary −37.0%**, **communication services −39.9%**. The S&P 500 fell **−18.1%**, but the spread from best (+65.7%) to worst (−39.9%) was over **105 percentage points**.

![2022 sector total returns, energy leading and communication services lagging](/imgs/blogs/sector-rotation-through-the-cycle-3.png)

The bar chart makes the dispersion impossible to miss: one green bar (energy) towering above a sea of red, with the defensives clustered near zero and the long-duration growth sectors at the bottom. This is what an overheat does to the *inside* of the index — it is the single best argument for understanding rotation, because owning the index meant owning that whole spread averaged to −18%, while a late-cycle tilt could have turned the year positive (we do the math below).

### 2008: the defensive cushion

Finally, 2008, the recession year, to show what defense actually looks like. The S&P 500 fell **−37.0%** — one of the worst years on record. *Everything* fell. But the defensives fell far less: **consumer staples −15.4%**, **health care −22.8%**, **utilities −29.0%** all beat the index, while the cyclicals were destroyed: **industrials −39.9%**, **materials −45.7%**, and **financials −55.3%** (the epicenter of the crisis).

![2008 sector total returns showing defensives cushioning the recession](/imgs/blogs/sector-rotation-through-the-cycle-5.png)

The chart shows the cushion clearly: the green defensive bars are the shortest (least negative), the red cyclical bars the longest, with financials at the bottom. The lesson is precisely misconception 2 corrected: defense is *relative*. Staples still lost 15% — but losing 15% instead of 37% left you with far more capital to redeploy when the recovery came in 2009. A portfolio that rotated defensive going into 2008 and back to cyclicals for 2009 would have compounded dramatically better than one that just held the index through both.

Putting 2009 and 2020 side by side reveals a subtlety worth lingering on:

![2009 versus 2020 sector leadership comparison bars](/imgs/blogs/sector-rotation-through-the-cycle-4.png)

Both years were "tech-led recoveries," and in both, technology was the top sector (+61.7% in 2009, +43.9% in 2020). But the *breadth* differed completely. In 2009, the early-cycle recovery lifted *every* cyclical — materials, discretionary, industrials, even financials all rose double digits. In 2020, the recovery *split* the cyclicals: tech and discretionary soared while energy (−33.7%) and financials (−1.7%) were left behind, because the pandemic and zero rates broke their specific drivers. The chart's green bars (2009) are uniformly tall; the blue bars (2020) fan out from very high to deeply negative. The lesson: "tech is leading" does not always mean "we are in a normal early cycle" — you have to check whether the *rest* of the cyclicals are confirming. In 2009 they confirmed; in 2020 they did not, which warned that the recovery was narrow and unusual.

### What the four episodes have in common

Step back from the individual years and the same pattern repeats in all four. In every case, the *internal* dispersion of the index was enormous even when the headline move was modest, and in every case the leadership matched the cycle phase that the macro data confirmed *afterward*. The market did not wait for the National Bureau of Economic Research to date the recession or for the inflation print to top out — it rotated *first*, and the official confirmation came later. That is the deep reason sector rotation is worth learning twice over: once as a way to add return (own the leaders), and once as a way to read the regime (let the leaders tell you the phase).

There is also a humbling lesson hiding in these episodes. The textbook clock — cyclicals → growth → energy → defensives — is the *modal* path, but the actual path in any given cycle is shaped by what *kind* of shock hit. 2008 was a credit and financial shock, so financials were the epicenter and fell hardest (−55.3%). 2020 was a pandemic and demand shock concentrated in physical activity, so it spared the "stay-at-home" digital sectors and crushed energy. 2022 was an inflation and rates shock, so it inverted the usual tech leadership and lifted the inflation winners. The clock tells you the *general* order of rotation; the *nature of the shock* tells you which sectors get the extreme outcomes. A good rotation read combines both — "where are we in the cycle?" *and* "what kind of shock is driving this phase?"

One more pattern worth naming: notice how often the *worst* sector of one year becomes a leader soon after. Energy was the worst sector of 2020 (−33.7%) and the best of 2022 (+65.7%). Financials were the worst of 2008 (−55.3%) and a solid double-digit gainer in the 2009 recovery (+17.2%). This is the mean-reverting heartbeat of sector rotation: beaten-down cyclicals have the most operating leverage to a turn, so when the cycle flips, yesterday's worst sector often leads. That is *not* a reason to blindly buy the worst sector — many keep falling — but it is why a rigid "avoid the loser forever" rule is exactly backwards at cycle turns.

## When to own it: the sector-tilt playbook

This is the payoff. Everything above lands here: how to actually *use* sector rotation in the equity part of your portfolio. The governing principles first, then the phase-by-phase tilts, then the worked dollar example that proves the whole idea.

### The four governing principles

1. **The index is the default; tilts are the alpha.** Your baseline should be a broad, low-cost equity index — that captures the market's long-run drift and is impossible to beat reliably. Sector tilts are *adjustments around* that core, sized to your conviction about the regime. You are not abandoning the index; you are leaning it.
2. **Match your beta to the regime.** When you believe the cycle is early or mid, tilt toward higher-beta cyclicals to amplify the upside. When you believe it is late or turning down, tilt toward lower-beta defensives to dampen the downside. This is the beta ladder in action.
3. **Use relative strength as a regime read, not just a return tilt.** Let the sectors *tell* you where the cycle is. If your macro view and the sector leadership disagree, distrust the view — the market's money is the better judge.
4. **Do not over-trade.** Leadership lasts months to years. Size your tilt, set it deliberately, and let it run. Frantic rotation bleeds returns to costs and taxes. The discipline is patience, not activity.

![Sector tilt playbook matrix by cycle phase](/imgs/blogs/sector-rotation-through-the-cycle-7.png)

The matrix above is the whole playbook on one page: for each phase, what to *tilt toward* (the alpha), what to *underweight* (the trap), and the *relative-strength tell* that confirms the regime. It is worth keeping next to the rotation clock from the top of the post — the clock tells you the order, the matrix tells you the action.

### Phase-by-phase tilts and rough sizing

A practical way to think about sizing: start from the index weights and lean them by a modest amount — say, overweighting a favored group by 5 to 15 percentage points of your equity sleeve and underweighting the out-of-favor group by a similar amount. You are tilting, not betting the farm.

| Phase | Overweight (the tilt) | Underweight | The regime tell |
|---|---|---|---|
| Early cycle | Financials, discretionary, industrials, small-caps | Staples, utilities | High-beta cyclicals snapping back hardest |
| Mid cycle | Technology, growth, broad index | Deep value, pure commodity plays | Broad participation, low volatility |
| Late cycle | Energy, materials, value, short duration | Long-duration tech, expensive growth | Energy leading, real yields rising |
| Recession | Staples, utilities, health care | Financials, discretionary, high beta | Defensives quietly outperforming |

What invalidates each case? For a **cyclical tilt**, the invalidation is the yield curve flattening and inflation accelerating — that is the late-cycle warning to rotate out of early-cycle cyclicals. For a **late-cycle energy/value tilt**, the invalidation is demand collapsing and the commodity price rolling over (as in late 2008 or 2020) — the inflation trade dies when growth dies, and you rotate to defensives. For a **defensive tilt**, the invalidation is the central bank decisively cutting and the cyclicals starting to lead on relative strength — that is the early-cycle "all clear" to rotate back to high beta.

### The worked example that proves it

Now the dollar math that makes the whole post concrete — and shows both the upside *and* the trap.

#### Worked example: a late-cycle tilt in 2022 versus the same tilt in the wrong year

You have a **\$100,000** equity sleeve at the start of 2022. Consider three ways to hold it through that overheat year.

**Option A — plain index.** You hold the S&P 500. It returns −18.1%. Your sleeve ends at \$100,000 × (1 − 0.181) = **\$81,900**, a loss of **−\$18,100**.

**Option B — a late-cycle sector tilt.** You read the regime (inflation surging, central bank hiking, real yields rising) and tilt toward the sectors that lead an inflationary late cycle:

- **\$40,000 in energy.** Energy returns +65.7%, so this becomes \$40,000 × 1.657 = \$66,280, a gain of **+\$26,280**.
- **\$30,000 in defensives** (staples, health care, utilities), which averaged roughly −0.3% in 2022. This becomes \$30,000 × (1 − 0.003) = \$29,910, a loss of just **−\$90**.
- **\$30,000 left in the index** at −18.1%. This becomes \$30,000 × 0.819 = \$24,570, a loss of **−\$5,430**.

Total: \$66,280 + \$29,910 + \$24,570 = **\$120,760**, a *gain* of about **+\$20,760** on the same \$100,000.

The swing between Option A (−\$18,100) and Option B (+\$20,760) is roughly **\$38,860** — about \$39,000 of difference on a \$100,000 sleeve, *purely from rotating the equity sleeve toward the sectors that lead an inflationary late cycle*. Same money, same year, same asset class — opposite outcome.

**Option C — the same tilt in the wrong year.** Now run that *identical* energy-heavy tilt through **2020** instead, to prove that tilts must match the regime. In 2020, energy returned **−33.7%**:

- \$40,000 in energy → \$40,000 × (1 − 0.337) = \$26,520, a loss of **−\$13,480**.
- \$30,000 in defensives (2020 average for staples/health care/utilities ≈ +8%) → about \$32,400, a gain of **+\$2,400**.
- \$30,000 in the index at +18.4% → \$35,520, a gain of **+\$5,520**.

Total ≈ \$26,520 + \$32,400 + \$35,520 = **\$94,440**, a *loss* of about **−\$5,560** — in a year the plain S&P 500 *gained* 18.4% (which would have left you at \$118,400). The late-cycle tilt *underperformed the index by about \$24,000* in 2020 because the regime was wrong: 2020 was a demand collapse, not an inflationary overheat, so the inflation trade was exactly backwards.

The one-line takeaway this example exists to teach: sector tilts are not free money — they are *regime bets*. The same energy-heavy tilt that turned a −18% year into a +21% year in 2022 turned an +18% year into a −6% year in 2020. The leadership map only pays you if you read the cycle correctly; get the regime wrong and the tilt costs you exactly what it would have made. That is why principle 3 — let the sectors confirm the regime before you commit — is the most important rule in the playbook.

### How to actually express a tilt, and what it costs

In practice you do not buy individual stocks to tilt a sector. The standard vehicle is a **sector ETF** — an exchange-traded fund that holds all the companies in one GICS sector in their index weights, trades like a single stock, and charges a small annual fee (often 0.1% or so for the big sector funds). To overweight energy, you buy the energy sector ETF; to underweight it, you simply hold less of it than the index does. This keeps the tilt cheap, diversified within the sector, and easy to size and reverse.

The costs to respect are real but modest if you trade slowly: the ETF's annual fee, the bid-ask spread each time you trade, and — outside a tax-sheltered account — capital-gains tax when you sell a winner. These costs are exactly why over-trading is so corrosive. A patient tilt held for the duration of a cycle phase pays those costs a handful of times; a frantic weekly rotation pays them dozens of times and hands a chunk of the alpha to your broker and the tax authority. The math is unforgiving: if a tilt earns you 3% of extra return over a phase but you churn it enough to lose 1% a year to costs and taxes over two years, you have given back two-thirds of the edge.

A second practical point: **size the tilt to your conviction, and keep the core intact.** A reasonable discipline is to never let any single sector tilt move your equity sleeve more than, say, 10 to 20 percentage points away from the index weights. That keeps a bad regime read from being catastrophic — if you tilt 15 points toward energy and the regime turns out to be 2020 rather than 2022, you underperform, but you do not blow up, because 80%+ of your sleeve is still the diversified index. The index is the seatbelt; the tilt is the steering. You want both.

Finally, **decide in advance what would tell you you are wrong.** Before you put on a late-cycle energy tilt, write down the signal that would invalidate it — for energy, that the commodity price has clearly rolled over and demand is collapsing (the 2020 scenario). Before a defensive tilt, write down that the central bank has decisively pivoted to cutting and cyclicals are leading on relative strength again. Pre-committing to the exit signal is what separates a disciplined rotation from a stubborn one. The single most expensive mistake in sector rotation is not picking the wrong sector — it is refusing to rotate *out* when the regime that justified the tilt has already ended.

### Where this touches you

If you own a target-date fund or a broad index fund, you are already making a giant passive sector bet on the recent winners — understanding rotation tells you what that bet *is* and whether you are comfortable with it. If you actively manage even a slice of your equity holdings, the rotation clock and the playbook matrix give you a disciplined, history-grounded framework for leaning your portfolio with the cycle instead of guessing. And if you do nothing else, watching *which sectors are quietly leading* gives you a free, real-time read on where the economy is headed — often before the headlines catch up. The market is always voting; sector leadership is how you read the ballot.
