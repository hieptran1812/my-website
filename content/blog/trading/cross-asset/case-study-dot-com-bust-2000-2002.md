> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — When the dot-com bubble burst, the stock market was destroyed — the Nasdaq fell about **−78%** and the S&P 500 about **−49%** peak-to-trough. But because inflation was low, the Fed could cut interest rates from **6.5% to 1.0%**, so bonds *rallied* while stocks collapsed. A diversified investor — the classic 60% stocks / 40% bonds mix — lost far less than the all-equity investor, and the bond sleeve gave them dry powder to rebuy cheap stocks at the 2002 bottom. This is the cleanest case in modern history of diversification working exactly as the textbook promised.
>
> - The crash was brutal but slow: the Nasdaq peaked at **5,048** on March 10, 2000 and ground down for two and a half years to about **1,114** by October 2002 — a **−78%** collapse, with the S&P 500 falling from ~1,527 to ~777, about **−49%**.
> - The hedge worked because this was a *growth* shock in a *low-inflation* world. The Bloomberg US Aggregate bond index posted **positive** total returns every single year — roughly **+11.6% (2000), +8.4% (2001), +10.3% (2002)** — as yields fell.
> - The contrast that defines the whole series: in 2000-02 bonds saved you, in 2008 they saved you again, but in 2022 they *fell with stocks* — because in 2022 inflation was high and the Fed was *hiking*, not cutting. The regime decides whether the bond hedge fires.
> - The one number to remember: a **−49% stock crash did not mean a −49% portfolio**. A 60/40 investor's worst drawdown was closer to **−20% to −25%**, because the 40% in bonds *gained* while the 60% in stocks fell.

In the first quarter of 2000, a company called Pets.com — an online store that sold dog food and cat litter, shipped at a loss, and whose mascot was a sock puppet — ran a Super Bowl ad and went public at a valuation in the hundreds of millions of dollars. It had almost no revenue and no path to profit. Nine months later it was bankrupt, its stock worthless. It became the symbol of an era in which investors paid extraordinary prices for companies that made no money, on the theory that "eyeballs" and "the new economy" had repealed the old rules about earnings. The Nasdaq Composite — the index stuffed with these technology names — had quadrupled in five years. Then, in March 2000, it stopped, turned, and over the next two and a half years gave back nearly four-fifths of its value.

That part of the story is famous: the bubble, the mania, the spectacular wipeout. What gets told far less often is the *other* half — the half that actually matters if you are trying to learn how to protect money. Because while the Nasdaq was losing **−78%** and the broad S&P 500 was losing about **−49%**, an investor who had simply held a boring, diversified mix of stocks *and bonds* came through with a fraction of the damage. Bonds — loans to the US government and to companies — did not crash alongside stocks. They *rose*. The same calendar that cut the stock market in half handed solid gains to bondholders, three years running.

That is not a footnote. It is the single cleanest demonstration in modern financial history that diversification — owning assets that don't all move together — can work exactly the way the textbooks promise. The diagram above is the mental model we'll keep returning to: in 2000-2002, the all-equity investor and the diversified investor experienced two completely different crashes, and the difference was almost entirely the bonds.

![All-equity investor loses about half while a 60/40 investor loses far less because bonds rose as the Fed cut rates](/imgs/blogs/case-study-dot-com-bust-2000-2002-1.png)

This post is a case study, so we'll do it chronologically: first the *setup* (how the bubble inflated and why valuations got so absurd), then the *chronology* (the slow two-and-a-half-year grind down through a recession, 9/11, and the corporate scandals), then the *cross-asset scoreboard* (who got crushed, who survived, and crucially, who *gained*). Only then do we extract the lesson — *why* bonds were able to hedge here, when they so spectacularly failed to in 2022, and what that tells a diversified investor about when their safety net will actually catch them. (This is educational, not financial advice — we're explaining mechanisms and history, not telling you to buy or sell anything.)

## Foundations: the setup — what a bubble is and why this one inflated

Before we walk through what happened, we need to build the vocabulary from zero. A case study is only useful if you understand the *machinery* underneath the events. So let us define the pieces.

### What is a stock, and what is it worth?

A **stock** (also called a *share* or *equity*) is a small piece of ownership in a company. If a company is divided into a billion shares and you own one, you own one-billionth of the company — one-billionth of its profits, its assets, and its future. We cover this from the ground up in [Equities (Stocks): Owning a Slice of Growth](/blog/trading/cross-asset/equities-stocks-owning-a-slice-of-growth), but the one idea you need here is this: *a share's fundamental value comes from the cash the company will eventually return to its owners* — its profits, paid out as dividends or reinvested to grow. A share is, ultimately, a claim on a stream of future earnings.

That gives us a way to measure whether a stock is cheap or expensive: the **price-to-earnings ratio**, or *P/E*. You take the price of one share and divide it by the company's annual earnings (profit) per share. If a stock costs \$100 and earns \$5 per share each year, its P/E is 20 — you are paying \$20 for every \$1 of annual profit. A low P/E (say 10) means you're paying a little for a lot of earnings; a high P/E (say 40) means you're paying a lot for a little, betting that earnings will grow fast enough to justify the price. Historically, the US stock market as a whole has traded around a P/E of 15 to 18.

Hold that number — 15 to 18 — because the dot-com bubble is the story of that number going completely insane.

### What is a stock-market index?

You can't watch every stock, so we track baskets called **indexes**. Two matter for this story:

- The **S&P 500** — an index of the 500 largest US companies, weighted by size. It's the standard proxy for "the US stock market." When people say "the market was up 1% today," they usually mean the S&P 500.
- The **Nasdaq Composite** — an index dominated by technology companies (the Nasdaq is the exchange where most tech stocks list). Because it's tech-heavy, it swings harder than the S&P in both directions. In a tech boom it soars; in a tech bust it craters. It was the epicenter of the bubble.

### What is a bond, and why does its price move?

A **bond** is a loan. When you buy a bond, you lend money to a borrower — a government or a company — and in exchange they promise to pay you a fixed stream of interest payments (called *coupons*) and return your original money (the *principal*) on a set date (the *maturity*). A US Treasury bond is a loan to the US government, considered the safest borrower in the world. We build bonds from scratch in [Government Bonds: The Risk-Free Anchor and Duration](/blog/trading/cross-asset/government-bonds-the-risk-free-anchor-and-duration), but here is the single most important fact for this case study:

**Bond prices move opposite to interest rates.** When market interest rates *fall*, existing bonds — which were issued at the old, higher rates — become more valuable, because they pay more than newly issued bonds. So their price goes *up*. When rates *rise*, existing bonds become less attractive and their price goes *down*. This inverse relationship is the hinge on which this entire story turns. If you remember one mechanic from the foundations, make it this one: *rates down → bond prices up.*

How much a bond's price moves for a given change in rates is called its **duration** — roughly, the number of years of payments you're waiting for, and a measure of interest-rate sensitivity. A bond with a duration of 7 will gain about 7% if rates fall by 1 percentage point, and lose about 7% if rates rise by 1 point. Longer-dated bonds (20- and 30-year Treasuries) have higher duration and swing more; this matters because the *more* the Fed cuts, the more a long-duration bond gains.

### What is the "Agg," and what is a 60/40?

When we say "bonds" as an asset class, we usually mean a broad bond index. The standard one is the **Bloomberg US Aggregate Bond Index** — affectionately, "the Agg" — which tracks the whole US investment-grade bond market: Treasuries, high-quality corporate bonds, and mortgage bonds, blended together. Its total return in a year tells you how the bond sleeve of a normal portfolio did.

The **60/40 portfolio** is the most famous diversified mix in investing: **60% in stocks** (say, the S&P 500) and **40% in bonds** (say, the Agg). The whole idea is that the two halves tend to behave differently — when stocks fall, bonds often hold up or rise, so the combined portfolio is steadier than either piece alone. The engine that makes this work or fail is the **stock-bond correlation**, the subject of [Stock-Bond Correlation: The 60/40 Engine](/blog/trading/cross-asset/stock-bond-correlation-the-60-40-engine). Correlation measures whether two things move together (+1), opposite (−1), or independently (0). When stocks and bonds are *negatively* correlated, the 60/40 is a beautiful machine. When they become *positively* correlated — as in 2022 — the machine jams. The dot-com bust is the case where it ran perfectly.

### Drawdown, bear market, and the cruel math of recovery

Two more terms you need before the chronology. A **drawdown** is how far an asset has fallen from its most recent peak, measured peak-to-trough. If a stock goes from \$100 to \$60, that's a −40% drawdown. A **bear market** is the common name for a large drawdown in stocks — conventionally a fall of 20% or more from the peak. By that definition, the dot-com bust was a brutal, prolonged bear market: the Nasdaq's −78% and the S&P's −49% are among the deepest in modern history.

Drawdowns have a cruel arithmetic that every investor must understand, because it reframes why protecting against them matters so much. *The percentage you need to recover is always bigger than the percentage you lost.* If you lose 50%, you do not need a 50% gain to get back to even — you need a **100%** gain, because you're now growing a smaller base. Lose 78%, and you need a gain of about **+355%** just to return to where you started.

#### Worked example: the asymmetry of a deep drawdown

> [!note]
> Start with **\$100,000**. Take the broad-market −49% hit: you're down to \$100,000 × 0.51 = **\$51,000**. To climb back to \$100,000, your \$51,000 must roughly *double* — a gain of (\$100,000 − \$51,000) ÷ \$51,000 ≈ **+96%**. Now take the Nasdaq's −78%: your \$100,000 becomes \$22,000, and to get back to even it must grow to \$100,000 — a gain of (\$100,000 − \$22,000) ÷ \$22,000 ≈ **+355%**. The Nasdaq did not reclaim its March 2000 peak until 2015 — *fifteen years* later. The one-line takeaway: deep losses are not symmetric with the gains needed to undo them, which is exactly why a hedge that *shrinks* the drawdown in the first place — like the bond sleeve in 2000-2002 — is worth far more than its size suggests.

This asymmetry is the deepest reason diversification matters. The 60/40 investor whose drawdown was −20% instead of −49% needed only about a +25% gain to recover (because \$80,000 needs to grow back to \$100,000), versus the all-equity investor's +96%. A smaller hole is a vastly easier climb — and the bonds dug a smaller hole.

### The Fed: the dial that controls everything

The **Federal Reserve** (the "Fed") is the US central bank. Its main lever is the **federal funds rate** — the very short-term interest rate it sets, which ripples out into all other rates in the economy. When the Fed thinks the economy is overheating or inflation is too high, it *raises* the rate to cool things down. When the economy is weakening, it *cuts* the rate to stimulate borrowing and spending. Here is the chain that makes bonds a hedge: **Fed cuts rate → market rates fall → bond prices rise.** And here is the giant condition that decides whether the Fed *can* cut: **inflation.** If inflation is low, the Fed is free to cut hard. If inflation is high, the Fed must keep rates high to fight it — even if the economy is weak. That single fork, which we'll return to constantly, is why bonds rescued investors in 2000-2002 and abandoned them in 2022.

### How the bubble inflated

Now the setup proper. The late 1990s were the dawn of the consumer internet — web browsers, e-commerce, email for everyone. The promise was real and enormous: the internet *would* reshape the economy. But the market took a true story and priced it as if every internet company would win, immediately, forever. A few features defined the mania:

- **"Eyeballs over earnings."** Conventional valuation — paying for profits — was declared obsolete. Analysts valued companies on website traffic, "eyeballs," and "page views" instead of cash flow. A company losing money but growing users was treated as more valuable than a boring, profitable one. This is the tell of every bubble: the moment people invent new metrics to justify prices the old metrics can't.
- **Profitless IPOs.** An **IPO** (*initial public offering*) is the first time a company sells shares to the public. In 1999-2000, companies with little revenue and no profits went public and doubled or tripled on their first trading day. The point was to *list*, not to *earn*.
- **Extreme valuations.** At the peak, the Nasdaq's price-to-earnings ratio — for the companies that even *had* earnings — reached into the *hundreds*. Recall that the historical norm is 15 to 18. Some individual darlings traded at P/E ratios of 100, 200, even more, or had no earnings at all, making the P/E literally infinite. The whole index was priced for a future that no plausible reality could deliver.
- **A narrow rally.** By the late stages, the gains were concentrated in a shrinking handful of mega-cap technology names. Breadth — the number of stocks actually participating in the rise — was deteriorating even as the headline index climbed. A market that rises on fewer and fewer names is a fragile one, because when the leaders crack, there's nothing underneath to hold the index up.

And the Fed was tightening into all of it. Worried about overheating and a possible asset bubble, the Fed raised the funds rate through 1999 and into 2000, reaching **6.5%** by mid-2000. Higher rates make the speculative, profitless, far-future cash flows of growth stocks worth less today (because you discount distant cash flows more heavily when rates are high). The tightening was the pin looking for the bubble.

#### Worked example: how absurd was a P/E of 200?

> [!note]
> Let us make the valuation insanity concrete with round numbers. Suppose a hot internet stock trades at \$100 a share and earns just **\$0.50** per share in annual profit. Its P/E is \$100 ÷ \$0.50 = **200**. What does that mean in plain English? You are paying \$200 for every \$1 of current annual profit. Put differently, if the company never grew at all, it would take **200 years** of earnings to pay back your purchase price. A "normal" stock at a P/E of 16 pays you back in 16 years. For a P/E of 200 to make sense, the company's earnings would have to grow explosively — roughly *doubling every couple of years for a decade* — just to bring the effective P/E down to something reasonable. Now multiply that fragile bet across an entire index, and the reason a small disappointment in the growth story could halve, then quarter, the price becomes obvious. The one-line takeaway: a sky-high P/E isn't a price, it's a *promise* — and the dot-com bust is what happens when the market collectively stops believing the promise.

## The chronology: a slow-motion crash over two and a half years

Most people misremember the dot-com bust as a single crash, like a stock-market heart attack. It wasn't. It was a long, grinding, two-and-a-half-year illness — a series of false bottoms and failed rallies that wore investors down. Understanding the *shape* of it matters, because the slowness is part of the lesson: there was time, the whole way down, to be diversified and to rebalance. Let us walk the timeline.

![Nasdaq Composite climbs from 1,000 to a 5,048 peak in March 2000 then falls to about 1,114 by October 2002](/imgs/blogs/case-study-dot-com-bust-2000-2002-2.png)

**The peak: March 10, 2000.** The Nasdaq Composite closed at **5,048** — its all-time high, after roughly quintupling from around 1,000 five years earlier. The S&P 500 had peaked a fraction earlier in March at about **1,527**. This was the top. From here, the only direction was down, though almost nobody believed it at the time; the consensus was that any dip was a buying opportunity in "the new economy."

**Spring-summer 2000: the first leg down.** The Nasdaq fell sharply through spring, recovered, then rolled over again. The profitless dot-coms began running out of cash. Pets.com, Webvan, eToys, Boo.com — names that had been worth billions on paper — collapsed one by one as the money to fund their losses dried up. By the end of 2000 the Nasdaq had fallen to around **2,470**, already roughly cut in half from the peak. But the broad S&P 500 had fallen far less, because it held many old-economy, profitable companies that weren't part of the mania.

**2001: a real recession arrives.** What started as a stock-market correction became an economic downturn. Business investment in technology — which had been pulled forward by Y2K spending and dot-com exuberance — collapsed. The US entered a recession in March 2001 (as later dated by the NBER, the official recession referee). The Fed, watching the economy weaken with inflation under control, began cutting the funds rate *aggressively* — from 6.5% at the start of 2001 down through the year. This is the moment that matters most for our story, and we'll return to it: as the Fed cut, bonds began to rally.

**September 11, 2001.** The terrorist attacks shut US stock markets for the longest stretch since the Great Depression. When they reopened, stocks fell hard in a wave of fear and uncertainty, and the Fed cut rates again to stabilize the financial system. Markets found a short-lived bottom in late September 2001 and bounced into early 2002 — one of the many false dawns.

**2002: the corporate scandals.** Just as confidence was trying to recover, a wave of accounting frauds detonated. **Enron**, the energy-trading giant, had hidden enormous losses in off-balance-sheet entities; it collapsed into what was then the largest bankruptcy in US history, taking its auditor Arthur Andersen down with it. **WorldCom**, a telecom titan, was found to have fraudulently inflated its profits by billions; its bankruptcy soon surpassed Enron's as the largest ever. These scandals did something deeper than the dot-com flameouts: they made investors doubt the *honesty of the numbers themselves*. If you couldn't trust reported earnings, you couldn't value anything — so investors sold first and asked questions later. The S&P 500, which had held up better than the Nasdaq through 2000-2001, took its worst beating in 2002.

**The bottom: October 2002.** The grind finally exhausted itself. The Nasdaq bottomed around **1,114** in early October 2002 — a **−78%** collapse from its 5,048 peak. The S&P 500 bottomed near **777**, about **−49%** from its high. Two and a half years of selling had finally wrung the speculation out of the market. From that October low, a new bull market began.

Notice the texture of this: there was no single catastrophic day, no 1987-style one-day crash. It was a war of attrition — peak in March 2000, recession in 2001, 9/11, scandals in 2002, bottom in October 2002. And the *whole time*, an investor who held bonds alongside their stocks was being protected by a force quietly working in the background: the Fed was cutting, and bonds were rising.

### Why the slowness mattered

The grinding, multi-year shape of the bust is itself a lesson, and it cuts two ways. On one hand, the slowness was *merciful* to the disciplined investor: there was no single day on which you had to make the right call. Every month for two and a half years, a diversified investor could simply hold their mix, collect their bond gains, and — if they rebalanced — keep nudging money from the appreciating bonds into the cheapening stocks. The crash gave you time to do the right thing.

On the other hand, the slowness was *psychologically brutal* to the undisciplined one. A fast crash is terrifying but quick; a slow bleed is a form of water torture. Through 2000-2002 there were repeated, convincing rallies — sharp 20-30% bounces that felt like the bottom and then failed. Each false dawn lured investors back into the falling market, only to punish them on the next leg down. This is why the bust destroyed so many people who "knew" they should hold on: the market didn't crash and recover, it sagged and rallied and sagged again for thirty months, wearing down conviction until people capitulated and sold near the lows — the worst possible moment. The diversified investor was insulated from this trap not by superior willpower but by *structure*: because their bonds were rising, their total portfolio was falling far less, so the emotional pressure to capitulate was far weaker. Diversification doesn't just protect your money; it protects your *behavior*, which over a full cycle is worth just as much.

### How a bubble actually deflates

Underneath the headlines, the *mechanism* of the deflation is worth naming, because it recurs in every bubble. On the way up, rising prices are self-reinforcing: higher prices attract more buyers, who push prices higher, which seems to validate the buyers and attract still more. The profitless dot-coms could keep operating because the soaring stock market let them raise fresh cash whenever they ran low — the bubble *funded itself*.

The reversal runs the same loop backward. Once prices stop rising, the companies burning cash can no longer raise more (who buys shares in a falling stock?), so they fail. Their failures frighten investors, who sell, which pushes prices lower, which makes it even harder for the next company to raise money, which causes more failures. The Fed's rate hikes into 2000 were the initial shove; the self-reinforcing downward spiral did the rest. And critically, the *real economy* got pulled in: all that dot-com and telecom spending had been a real driver of growth, so when it collapsed, business investment fell and a recession followed. A financial bubble bursting became an economic downturn — which is exactly the *growth shock* that, in a low-inflation world, hands the Fed its cue to cut and hands bondholders their gains.

## The cross-asset scoreboard: who got crushed, and who got saved

Here is where a *cross-asset* lens — looking across all the asset classes at once, not just stocks — changes the entire story. If you only watched the stock market, 2000-2002 was an unmitigated disaster. If you watched *every* asset class, it was something far more instructive: a textbook example of diversification doing its job. Let us put the scoreboard up.

![Value of one hundred dollars in stocks falls to about fifty-one while one hundred in bonds rises to about one hundred thirty-two](/imgs/blogs/case-study-dot-com-bust-2000-2002-3.png)

**Equities: crushed.** This is the part everyone knows. The Nasdaq fell **−78%** peak-to-trough; the S&P 500 fell about **−49%**. An investor who had gone all-in on technology stocks at the top lost roughly four-fifths of their money. Even a broad-market S&P investor lost about half. These are devastating numbers — among the worst equity drawdowns of the post-war era.

**Treasuries and the Agg: rallied.** Now the part that gets left out. As the economy weakened and the Fed cut the funds rate from **6.5%** at the start of 2001 all the way to **1.0%** by mid-2003, market interest rates fell across the board. And remember the foundations: *rates down → bond prices up.* So bonds didn't just survive — they *gained*. The Bloomberg US Aggregate posted **positive total returns every single year** of the crash:

| Year | US Aggregate bond return | What stocks did |
|---|---|---|
| 2000 | about **+11.6%** | S&P −9.1% (total return), Nasdaq −39% |
| 2001 | about **+8.4%** | S&P −11.9% |
| 2002 | about **+10.3%** | S&P −22.1% |

Three straight years of solid, equity-like *gains* from the boring bond sleeve, while stocks bled. This is the heart of the case study. The chart above indexes \$100 in each: the stock line falls toward \$51 at the October 2002 low, while the bond line climbs steadily past \$130. The shaded gap between them is, quite literally, the value of diversification.

**Value and dividend stocks: outperformed.** Not all stocks are alike. The bubble was concentrated in expensive *growth* stocks — companies priced for explosive future earnings. The mirror image is *value* stocks — cheaper, profitable, often dividend-paying companies (utilities, consumer staples, old-economy industrials) that the mania had ignored. Because they never got bubbly, they had far less to give back. Value strategies and high-dividend stocks dramatically outperformed growth through 2000-2002 — in many measures, value was roughly *flat to positive* over a stretch in which growth was halved. Owning the boring, cheap half of the market was a hedge in itself.

**REITs: outperformed.** A **REIT** (*real estate investment trust*) is a company that owns income-producing property — apartments, offices, malls — and is required to pay out most of its rental income as dividends. REITs have almost nothing to do with internet eyeballs; they're about rents and interest rates. Through the dot-com bust, REITs *rose* while the Nasdaq collapsed, benefiting from steady rental income and falling interest rates (which lower their borrowing costs and make their dividends more attractive). We unpack this asset class in the series' real-estate post; here it's another example of an asset that zigged while tech zagged.

**Gold: bottomed and turned up.** Gold had been in a long, miserable bear market through the 1990s, bottoming near **\$255 an ounce** around 1999-2001 — about as unloved as it had ever been. Then, quietly, as the Fed slashed rates and the dollar softened, gold began the secular bull market that would carry it to roughly **\$1,900** by 2011. The dot-com era was its turning point.

![Gold price bottoms near two hundred fifty-five dollars in 1999 to 2001 then rises toward nineteen hundred by 2011](/imgs/blogs/case-study-dot-com-bust-2000-2002-6.png)

The lesson on the scoreboard is stark. *Within* the stock market, there was nowhere to hide if you owned the bubble. But *across* asset classes, there were several places to hide — bonds most of all, but also value, REITs, and eventually gold. A balanced portfolio touched most of them, and it survived. The all-equity gambler did not.

### The growth-versus-value split, in detail

It's worth dwelling on the growth-versus-value divergence, because it's the part of the scoreboard that lives *inside* equities and is most easily overlooked. The two styles are simple to define. **Growth stocks** are companies the market expects to grow earnings rapidly — in 2000, that meant technology, internet, and telecom names trading at enormous multiples on the promise of the future. **Value stocks** are cheaper, often slower-growing companies trading at low multiples of their current earnings or assets — in 2000, that meant the unglamorous old economy: utilities, banks, energy, consumer staples, industrials.

For most of the late 1990s, growth crushed value; owning boring value stocks felt like a mistake as the tech names tripled. Then the bust arrived and the relationship violently reversed. From the March 2000 peak through the October 2002 bottom, large-cap *growth* indexes fell far more than large-cap *value* indexes — in many measures value held roughly flat to modestly down while growth was cut in half or worse. The reason is mechanical: value stocks never inflated, so they had little air to lose, and their steady earnings and dividends provided a floor. A diversified equity investor who held *both* styles — rather than chasing only the hot growth names — already had a meaningful cushion built into their stock sleeve, before bonds even entered the picture.

This is a quieter form of the same lesson the bonds teach: *spreading across things that don't move together protects you.* Growth and value are both "stocks," but in 2000-2002 they behaved like different asset classes. The investor concentrated in one style took the full blow; the investor spread across styles softened it.

### Why rebalancing is the secret weapon

We touched on dry powder in the worked example, but the mechanism deserves its own treatment because it's where the diversified investor's advantage compounds over time. **Rebalancing** means periodically returning your portfolio to its target mix — if your 60/40 has drifted to 50/50 because stocks fell and bonds rose, you sell some bonds and buy stocks to get back to 60/40.

Notice what that *forces* you to do. When stocks have crashed and bonds have rallied, rebalancing makes you *sell the thing that went up* (bonds) and *buy the thing that went down* (stocks). It is a mechanical, emotion-free "buy low, sell high" rule — and it operates most powerfully at exactly the moment human psychology screams the opposite. At the October 2002 bottom, every instinct said "sell stocks, they're dangerous." Rebalancing said "buy them, they're cheap, and you have the bond gains to fund it." The diversified investor who simply rebalanced on a schedule bought equities near a generational low without having to be a hero or a market-timer. The all-equity investor had nothing to rebalance *with* — they were already 100% in the asset that had collapsed.

#### Worked example: the all-equity investor rides the bubble down

> [!note]
> Let's put a real dollar figure on the all-equity path. You start with **\$100,000** invested entirely in the S&P 500 at the March 2000 peak — you wanted "the market," nothing fancy, but also no bonds. The S&P falls about **−49%** peak-to-trough by October 2002. Your account: \$100,000 × (1 − 0.49) = **\$51,000**. You have lost **\$49,000** — nearly half your life savings — and that's if you owned the *broad* market. If you'd chased the hot Nasdaq names, down **−78%**, your \$100,000 would be worth just \$22,000, a **−\$78,000** loss. Worse than the dollar figure is the *position* it leaves you in: at the bottom, you have less money and, critically, no spare cash to buy the now-cheap stocks. You rode the whole thing down with nothing in reserve. The one-line takeaway: concentration feels brilliant on the way up and catastrophic on the way down, because there is no second asset to cushion the fall or to redeploy at the lows.

#### Worked example: the 60/40 investor survives — and has dry powder

> [!note]
> Now the diversified path. You also start with **\$100,000**, but split it: **\$60,000 in stocks** (the S&P) and **\$40,000 in bonds** (the Agg). Walk it through 2000-2002.
>
> Your **stock sleeve** of \$60,000 suffers the equity crash. Using the broad S&P's roughly **−49%** peak-to-trough hit, that \$60,000 falls to about \$60,000 × 0.51 = **\$30,600** — a loss of about \$29,400.
>
> Your **bond sleeve** of \$40,000 does the opposite. Compounding the three positive Agg years — roughly +11.6%, +8.4%, +10.3% — gives a cumulative gain of about **+32%** over the stretch: \$40,000 × 1.116 × 1.084 × 1.103 ≈ **\$53,300**. The bond sleeve *grew* by about \$13,300.
>
> Add them up at the depths: \$30,600 + \$53,300 ≈ **\$83,900**. Your blended drawdown is roughly **−16%** at the worst — versus the **−49%** the all-equity investor suffered on the broad market. The \$40k bond sleeve didn't just sit there; it gained about \$13,000 and *directly offset* a chunk of the equity damage.
>
> And here's the part that compounds for years afterward: at the October 2002 bottom, your bonds are worth more than you started with. You can *sell some bonds and buy stocks* — rebalancing back toward 60/40 — which mechanically forces you to buy equities at their cheapest in a decade. That dry powder is what turns a survived crash into a *won* recovery. The one-line takeaway: in a disinflationary bust, the bond sleeve is both a shock absorber *and* a war chest.

![All-equity, all-bond, and 60/40 calendar-year returns for 2000, 2001, and 2002 compared](/imgs/blogs/case-study-dot-com-bust-2000-2002-4.png)

The bar chart above makes the cushioning visible year by year. In 2002 — the worst year — the all-equity investor took a **−22.1%** hit on the S&P; the all-bond investor *made* +10.3%; and the 60/40 blend, computed as 0.6 × (−22.1%) + 0.4 × (+10.3%), came out to roughly **−9.1%**. The bond sleeve turned a brutal equity year into a flesh wound. That arithmetic — a −22% stock year becoming a −9% portfolio year — is the entire promise of the 60/40, paid in full.

## Why bonds hedged here (and not in 2022)

This is the analytical core of the case study, and it's the part most worth internalizing, because it generalizes. Bonds did not hedge stocks in 2000-2002 by luck or by magic. They hedged because of a specific *regime* — a specific combination of what was happening to growth and to inflation. Get the regime right and you know in advance whether your bonds will save you.

![A growth shock plus low inflation lets the Fed cut, so yields fall, bond prices rise, and the stock-bond correlation turns negative](/imgs/blogs/case-study-dot-com-bust-2000-2002-5.png)

The chain runs like this, and it's drawn in the figure above:

1. **The dot-com bust was a GROWTH shock, not a PRICE shock.** What broke was the economy's growth engine — overinvestment in tech reversed, business spending collapsed, a recession hit. But *inflation* — the rate at which consumer prices were rising — stayed low and well-behaved, around 2-3%. Nobody worried about runaway prices in 2001; they worried about a weak economy.

2. **Low inflation meant the Fed could cut freely.** This is the pivot. The Fed's job is to support the economy *and* keep inflation in check. When inflation is low, those two goals don't conflict — the Fed is free to slash rates as hard as it wants to revive growth, with no inflation penalty. So it did: from **6.5%** down to **1.0%**, one of the most aggressive easing cycles in its history.

3. **Falling rates pushed bond prices up.** Recall the master mechanic: rates down → bond prices up. As the Fed cut and growth fears pulled longer-term yields down too, bonds across the curve rallied. Longer-duration bonds rallied hardest, because they're the most sensitive to rate changes. The Agg gained three years running.

4. **Stocks falling + bonds rising = negative correlation.** When one asset zigs as the other zags, they're *negatively correlated*, and that's the magic ingredient of diversification. Through 2000-2002 the stock-bond correlation was firmly negative — the textbook growth-shock relationship. The bond sleeve of a portfolio did exactly what it's supposed to do: rise when stocks fall.

Now compare 2022, the regime where this *broke*. In 2022, the shock was different in kind: it was a **price shock**. Inflation surged to around 9%, the highest in four decades. Suddenly the Fed faced the opposite problem — not a weak economy needing stimulus, but an *overheating* price level needing to be cooled. So the Fed did the opposite of 2001: it *hiked* rates rapidly, from near zero toward 5%. And when rates rise fast, bond prices *fall*. In 2022 the Agg lost about **−13.0%** — its worst year on record — at the same time stocks fell **−18.1%**. The 60/40 lost roughly **−16%**, its worst year since the 1930s. Both halves fell together. The stock-bond correlation flipped *positive*. Diversification offered no shelter, because the very thing causing the equity damage — rising rates to fight inflation — was *also* crushing the bonds.

The series treats this regime dependence in depth in [Correlation and the Diversification "Free Lunch"](/blog/trading/cross-asset/correlation-and-the-diversification-free-lunch), but the one-line rule is worth memorizing:

> **In a disinflationary slump, bonds hedge stocks. In an inflationary slump, they don't.** The dividing line is whether the Fed can *cut* (low inflation, bonds rally) or must *hike / hold high* (high inflation, bonds fall). The regime decides the correlation, and the correlation decides whether your 60/40 is a fortress or a trap.

| Episode | The shock | Inflation | Fed's move | Bonds did | Did diversification work? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2000-2002 dot-com | Growth (tech bust) | Low (~2-3%) | Cut 6.5% → 1% | Rallied (+30% cumulative) | **Yes — beautifully** |
| 2008 GFC | Growth + credit | Low/falling | Cut 5.25% → ~0% | Rallied (long Tsy +25.9%) | **Yes** |
| 2022 | Inflation / rates | High (~9%) | Hiked 0% → ~5% | Fell (−13.0%) | **No — both fell** |

The table is the whole point of putting these three case studies in one series. 2000-2002 and 2008 are the disinflationary busts where bonds were your friend. 2022 is the inflationary bust where they weren't. Same 60/40, opposite outcomes, because the *regime* was opposite.

### What "the discount rate" has to do with all of this

There's a deeper layer worth unpacking, because it explains *why* low-inflation growth shocks are so kind to bonds specifically. Every asset is, in theory, worth the present value of its future cash flows — and to convert future cash into today's value, you *discount* it by an interest rate. Lower rates make future cash worth more today; higher rates make it worth less. That discount rate is the hidden hinge connecting stocks, bonds, and the Fed.

Here's the elegant part. A bond's cash flows are *fixed* — a set schedule of coupons and principal. So when the discount rate (interest rates) falls, a bond's value rises in a clean, mechanical, predictable way: that's duration, and it's *all* upside for the bondholder. A stock's cash flows are *not* fixed — they depend on the economy. In a growth shock, two things happen to a stock at once: the discount rate falls (which *helps* its value) but the expected future earnings also fall, often sharply (which *hurts* its value). In a recession, the earnings collapse dominates, so stocks fall despite lower rates. The bond gets the rate tailwind without the earnings headwind; the stock gets both, and the bad one wins.

That asymmetry is the engine of the hedge. *The same falling-rate environment that crushes stocks (because it accompanies collapsing earnings) is pure fuel for bonds (because their cash flows don't collapse).* It's why, in a disinflationary recession, the two assets reliably move in opposite directions — and why the magnitude of the bond rally roughly tracks how hard the Fed cuts.

Now you can see why 2022 broke the pattern with surgical precision. In 2022 the discount rate didn't *fall* — it *rose*, because the Fed was hiking to fight inflation. Rising rates hurt bonds directly (their fixed cash flows are now discounted harder, so prices fall) *and* hurt stocks (the same harder discounting compresses their valuations, especially the long-duration growth names). With the discount rate moving *against* both assets simultaneously, there was no hedge to be found inside a stock-bond portfolio. The dot-com bust and 2022 are the two faces of the same discount-rate coin: in 2000-2002 rates fell and bonds won; in 2022 rates rose and bonds lost. The direction of rates, set by inflation, decides everything. We trace this master variable through every asset class in [Correlation and the Diversification "Free Lunch"](/blog/trading/cross-asset/correlation-and-the-diversification-free-lunch).

#### Worked example: how much the Fed's cuts were worth to a bondholder

> [!note]
> Let's quantify the rate-cut tailwind in dollars. Suppose you own a Treasury bond fund with a **duration of 6** — meaning its price moves about 6% for every 1 percentage point change in interest rates. You hold **\$40,000** of it (the bond sleeve of our 60/40). Over 2001-2003 the Fed cut the funds rate from 6.5% to 1.0% — a drop of **5.5 percentage points** — and longer-term yields fell substantially too, call it about **2.5 points** on the medium-term rates that drive a duration-6 fund. Using the duration rule, the *price* gain from falling rates alone is roughly 6 × 2.5% = **+15%**, on top of the interest the bonds were paying. So your \$40,000 sleeve gains about \$40,000 × 0.15 = **\$6,000** in price appreciation, plus several thousand more in coupon income — landing near the ~\$13,000 total gain we computed earlier. The one-line takeaway: duration is the *measure* of how much a rate cut is worth to you, and in a disinflationary bust where the Fed cuts hard, longer duration is the engine that turns a defensive asset into a winning one.

## Common misconceptions

Case studies are where intuition goes to get corrected. Here are the beliefs about 2000-2002 — and about crashes in general — that the data quietly demolishes.

**Misconception 1: "A −49% stock crash means a −49% portfolio year."** This is the big one, and it's simply false for a diversified investor. The S&P fell about −49% peak-to-trough, but a 60/40 portfolio's worst drawdown was closer to **−20% to −25%**, because the 40% in bonds *rose* while the 60% in stocks fell. The headline crash number describes the *all-equity* experience, not the diversified one. Confusing "the stock market crashed X%" with "I lost X%" is the single most common error people make reading market history — and it leads them to either panic-sell or avoid stocks entirely, when the real fix was simply *owning a second asset.*

**Misconception 2: "Diversification doesn't work — everything crashes together."** People who lived through 2008 and 2022 sometimes generalize to "in a real crisis, correlations go to one and diversification fails." That's true in *some* regimes (a liquidity crisis, an inflation shock) but emphatically *not* true in 2000-2002. Here, diversification worked exactly as advertised: bonds rose by double digits for three straight years while stocks were halved. The correct statement isn't "diversification doesn't work" — it's "diversification's effectiveness depends on the regime, and you need to know which regime you're in." 2000-2002 is the proof that, in a disinflationary equity bust, the free lunch is real.

**Misconception 3: "Bonds are boring and pointless when you're trying to grow money."** During the bubble, holding bonds felt like leaving money on the table — why own a 6% bond when tech stocks were doubling? But that 40% bond allocation, which looked like dead weight in 1999, became the thing that saved the portfolio in 2000-2002 *and* provided the cash to rebuy stocks at the bottom. Bonds aren't there to win the bull market; they're there to keep you solvent and opportunistic through the bear market. Their value shows up precisely when stocks fail.

**Misconception 4: "The 2000-2002 bust proves stocks are too dangerous to own."** The opposite lesson is the right one. The investor who got hurt wasn't the one who *owned* stocks — it was the one who owned *only* stocks, *only* the bubble names, with *no* diversifier and *no* dry powder. A balanced investor came through the worst equity decline in a generation with a manageable drawdown and emerged able to compound for the next two decades. The bust is an argument for diversification and valuation discipline, not for abandoning equities.

**Misconception 5: "Bonds always hedge stocks."** This is the mirror error of the post, and the reason 2022 exists in this series. Bonds hedged in 2000-2002 and in 2008 *because inflation was low and the Fed could cut.* When inflation is high and the Fed is hiking — 2022, or the 1970s — bonds fall *with* stocks, and the hedge vanishes. "Bonds hedge stocks" is a regime-conditional statement, not a law of nature. The skill is in reading the regime, which is exactly what the next section gives you.

**Misconception 6: "You needed to be smart or lucky to come through 2000-2002 intact."** The diversified investor who survived did nothing clever. They didn't predict the peak, didn't dodge the scandals, didn't time the bottom. They held a boring, fixed 60/40 mix and, at most, rebalanced it on a schedule. The protection came from the *structure* of the portfolio, not from any forecast. That's the most encouraging lesson of the whole episode: surviving a historic crash didn't require genius or nerve, just diversification held with discipline. The investors who got destroyed were, more often than not, the ones who *tried* to be clever — concentrating in the hot names, abandoning their bonds as dead weight, and trading in and out of the false rallies.

## How it shows up in real markets: the episodes, side by side

Let's anchor the abstract regime logic in concrete, named episodes — the way an allocator actually carries this knowledge around.

**The dot-com bust itself (2000-2002).** The case in full. A growth shock with low inflation; the Fed cuts from 6.5% to 1.0%; the Agg gains roughly +11.6%, +8.4%, +10.3%; the S&P falls about −49% and the Nasdaq −78%; value, REITs, and (eventually) gold outperform; the 60/40 draws down maybe −20% to −25% instead of −49%. The cleanest textbook case of negative stock-bond correlation in modern history. The mechanism from this post — low inflation → Fed cuts → bonds rally → negative correlation → 60/40 cushioned — operated perfectly.

**The 2008 Global Financial Crisis.** The same regime logic, an even more violent expression. Growth and credit both collapsed, but inflation was *falling* (deflation was the fear), so the Fed cut from 5.25% to roughly zero and launched bond-buying programs. Long Treasuries returned a stunning **+25.9%** in 2008 while the S&P fell **−37%** — duration was the single best asset on Earth that year. Diversification worked again, for the same reason it worked in 2000-2002: a disinflationary shock let the Fed cut, so bonds rallied. We give this its own full case study in [Case Study — The 2008 Global Financial Crisis](/blog/trading/cross-asset/case-study-2008-global-financial-crisis); read the two together and the pattern jumps out. The difference: 2008 added a *credit freeze*, so the riskier corners of the bond market (high-yield, mortgages) fell *with* stocks — only the safest government bonds did the hedging.

**2022: the regime that broke the hedge.** The crucial counterexample. Inflation hit ~9%, the Fed *hiked* from ~0% to ~5%, and bonds had their worst year on record: the Agg fell **−13.0%** *alongside* a **−18.1%** drop in stocks. The 60/40 fell about **−16%** — its worst year since 1937. Same portfolio, opposite outcome, because inflation flipped the Fed from cutting to hiking and the stock-bond correlation from negative to positive. If you only knew 2000-2002 and 2008, you'd believe bonds always hedge. 2022 is the year that disproves it and teaches you to check the inflation regime first.

**The 1973-74 stagflation.** The deepest historical version of the 2022 lesson. Growth fell *and* inflation soared (the oil embargo), so the Fed couldn't ease — stocks fell about −37% over two years while bonds offered little protection. What worked instead was *real* assets: gold rose enormously and commodities surged. This is the original "bonds don't always save you" case, and it rhymes with 2022. It's why the disinflationary-bust playbook this post teaches comes with a giant asterisk.

**The 2020 COVID crash.** A lightning-fast version of the disinflationary pattern. Stocks fell about −34% in five weeks, but inflation was low, the Fed slashed rates to zero and flooded the system with liquidity, bonds rallied, and the diversified investor was protected before the V-shaped recovery. Same regime as 2000-2002, compressed into a single quarter. The speed was different; the mechanism — low inflation lets the Fed cut, bonds rally — was identical.

The throughline across all five: *the regime, defined by what inflation is doing, decides whether your bonds will catch you.* Three disinflationary busts (2000-02, 2008, 2020) where bonds rallied and diversification worked; two inflationary busts (1973-74, 2022) where bonds fell and only real assets helped. That's the map. The dot-com bust is the cleanest, slowest, most legible example of the good kind.

## When this matters to you: the disinflationary-bust playbook

We close where the series always closes — on the decision. What does the dot-com bust actually tell a diversified investor to *do*?

![A three by three playbook matrix mapping the setup, the duration hedge, and the discipline for a disinflationary equity bust](/imgs/blogs/case-study-dot-com-bust-2000-2002-7.png)

**1. In a disinflationary equity bust, duration is the hedge — and the 60/40 works.** When an equity bubble bursts while inflation is low, the playbook is clear: own high-quality bonds, and lean toward longer duration, because the Fed will cut and falling rates lift bond prices most for long-duration bonds. The 60/40 isn't a relic in this regime; it's a precision instrument. The bond sleeve cushions the equity loss *and* gains you dry powder. The entire mechanism is the negative stock-bond correlation we explored in [Stock-Bond Correlation: The 60/40 Engine](/blog/trading/cross-asset/stock-bond-correlation-the-60-40-engine).

**2. The regime is the whole ballgame — so check inflation first.** Before you assume your bonds will save you, ask the one question: *is inflation low and falling, or high and rising?* If low, the Fed can cut, bonds will likely rally, and duration is your friend — own it. If high, the Fed is trapped, bonds may fall with stocks, and you need a *different* hedge (cash, gold, real assets, commodities). The dot-com bust is the "yes" answer; 2022 is the "no." Never reach for duration without first reading the inflation regime — this is the single most important judgment in the whole cross-asset toolkit.

**3. Valuation discipline and avoiding concentration are the prevention.** The investor who got hurt in 2000-2002 wasn't unlucky — they were concentrated in extremely expensive assets with no diversifier. The lessons are unglamorous and durable: respect valuations (a P/E of 200 is a warning, not an opportunity), beware narrow rallies led by a shrinking handful of names, and never let a single hot theme become your whole portfolio. Diversification across asset classes (stocks *and* bonds *and* a real-asset sleeve) is the structural protection; valuation discipline is the behavioral one. Together they're what let you participate in booms without being destroyed by the bust.

**4. Use the bond sleeve as a war chest, not just a shock absorber.** The under-appreciated half of the playbook: a crash is also an *opportunity*, and the diversified investor is the only one positioned to take it. Because the bond sleeve held its value (and gained), the 60/40 investor at the October 2002 bottom could rebalance — sell some appreciated bonds, buy cheap stocks — mechanically buying low. The all-equity investor had no such option; they were fully invested in the thing that had collapsed. Rebalancing a diversified portfolio is a built-in "buy low, sell high" engine, and it's most valuable precisely at the bottom of a bust.

**5. Size it to your regime view, and know what would prove you wrong.** A rough framing: in a clearly disinflationary environment, a healthy bond allocation (the 40 in a 60/40, or more for the cautious) is doing real work and you can own duration with confidence. The thing that would *invalidate* the case — your tripwire — is a shift in the inflation regime: if inflation is accelerating and the Fed is hiking, the duration hedge is breaking, and you should expect bonds to fall with stocks and pivot toward cash, gold, and real assets. The history is unambiguous: the same 40% bond sleeve was a hero in 2000-2002 and a casualty in 2022, and the only thing that changed was inflation.

The dot-com bust earns its place as the foundational case study because it's the *clean* one — slow enough to learn from, severe enough to matter, and unmuddied by the credit freeze of 2008 or the inflation shock of 2022. It shows diversification working exactly as designed: stocks halved, bonds rallied, the diversified investor survived and rebuilt. Hold it next to the 2008 and 2022 cases and you have the full regime map — and the map, not any single crash, is what actually keeps your money safe across a lifetime of cycles.
