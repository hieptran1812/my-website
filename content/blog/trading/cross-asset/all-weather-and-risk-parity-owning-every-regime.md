> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — If you cannot reliably predict which economic regime is coming next, the alternative is to stop predicting and instead own a balanced basket that holds *something* built to win in each one. That is the all-weather idea. Risk parity is the engineering trick that makes the balance real: it weights assets by how much *risk* each one contributes, not by how many dollars you put in.
>
> - A classic **60/40** portfolio looks balanced — 60% stocks, 40% bonds — but it is roughly **88-90% equity risk**, because stocks are about three times as volatile as bonds. Your "balanced" portfolio rises and falls almost entirely on what stocks do.
> - **All-weather / risk parity** fixes this by holding far more bonds (often *levered* — borrowed against — to raise their punch) so each asset contributes a roughly equal share of the portfolio's wobble. A popularized version: ~30% stocks, ~40% long-term Treasuries, ~15% intermediate Treasuries, ~7.5% gold, ~7.5% commodities.
> - It worked beautifully for **three decades** (roughly 1990-2021) because rates were mostly falling and stocks and bonds usually moved opposite each other — a tailwind for a strategy that levers bonds.
> - It **failed in 2022**: rising rates made stocks *and* bonds fall together, and the leverage on the bond leg turned a bad year into a worse one. The one number to remember: the whole strategy assumes bonds diversify stocks — and in an inflation shock, they do not.

In the spring of 2022, the most sophisticated "balanced" portfolios on Earth were having their worst year on record — and the reason was almost philosophical. For thirty years, a quiet idea had spread out from one hedge fund in Connecticut into pension plans, sovereign wealth funds, and a generation of retail "lazy portfolios": you do not have to *know* what the economy will do next. You do not have to guess whether the next year brings a boom, a recession, an inflation spike, or a slow grind. You just have to own a basket assembled so that *whatever* happens, something inside it is winning enough to cushion whatever is losing. Stop forecasting the weather; dress for all of it.

That idea — popularized by Ray Dalio's Bridgewater under the name **All Weather**, and built on the engineering principle called **risk parity** — is one of the genuinely beautiful ideas in investing. It is also one of the most misunderstood, and in 2022 it taught a hard lesson about the difference between "balanced" and "balanced under one specific assumption." The funds that levered up their bonds to match the punch of their stocks watched both legs fall at once, and the leverage that had quietly added free return for two decades suddenly added very expensive losses. Some risk-parity funds fell more than 20%.

The diagram below is the mental model the whole post returns to. It is the four-box map of the economy — growth up or down, inflation up or down — with the asset that tends to win in each box. The all-weather bet is not on any one box. It is the decision to own all four at once, balanced so that no single box can wreck you. Everything that follows is an answer to two questions: how do you actually *build* that balance (the answer is risk, not dollars), and what is the one assumption that, when it breaks, takes the whole thing down with it?

![Four quadrant map of growth and inflation with the winning asset in each box and an all weather core](/imgs/blogs/all-weather-and-risk-parity-owning-every-regime-1.png)

By the end you will be able to look at your own portfolio and ask the question that matters: *am I balanced by dollars, which feels safe but is mostly an equity bet — or balanced by risk, which is genuinely diversified but rests on bonds doing their job?* That single distinction is the heart of modern portfolio construction, and it is worth more than any view on where the market goes next.

## Foundations: the idea you build when you admit you cannot time the regime

Before any math, let us be honest about the problem the all-weather idea is trying to solve, because the whole design follows from it.

### The thing nobody can reliably do

Every investor faces the same temptation: *time the regime*. If you knew a recession was six months away, you would pile into bonds. If you knew an inflation spike was coming, you would load up on commodities and gold. If you knew a boom was starting, you would go all-in on stocks. The trouble is not that these moves are wrong — they are exactly right. The trouble is the *if you knew*. Forecasting the economy a year out, consistently, well enough to bet your retirement on it, is something almost no one does reliably, including the professionals whose full-time job it is. (We trace why in [the business cycle and the investment clock](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-business-cycle-and-the-investment-clock) — the cycle is real, but its timing is maddeningly slippery.)

So the all-weather idea begins with a confession: *I do not know which regime is coming.* And then it asks a different question. Not "what should I own *if* a recession hits?" but "what should I own *given that I cannot tell* whether a recession, a boom, an inflation spike, or a slowdown is next?" That reframing changes everything. If you cannot pick the winning box, you stop trying to pick — and instead you make sure you own something that wins in *each* box.

### The four boxes of the economy

Strip the economy down and two forces explain most of what assets do: **growth** (is the economy speeding up or slowing down?) and **inflation** (are prices rising faster or slower?). Cross them and you get four boxes, and — this is the key insight — a *different* asset class tends to shine in each:

- **Growth rising, inflation falling** (the dream: a humming economy with cheap money). **Stocks win.** Company earnings grow, and the low discount rate that comes with low inflation makes those earnings worth more today. This is the late-1990s, the 2010s bull market.
- **Growth rising, inflation rising** (a hot, late-cycle boom). **Commodities win.** When the economy runs hot and prices climb, the raw materials feeding that economy — oil, copper, the inputs to everything — rise in price, and so do the companies and contracts tied to them.
- **Growth falling, inflation falling** (a recession or deflation scare). **Long-term bonds win.** A weakening economy pushes the central bank to *cut* interest rates, and falling rates push bond prices *up*. The longer the bond, the bigger the rally. This is 2008.
- **Growth falling, inflation rising** (stagflation — the nightmare box). **Gold and commodities win.** Stocks suffer (weak earnings), bonds suffer (high inflation erodes their fixed payments), and the assets that hold their value when paper money is losing it — gold above all — are the only refuge. This is the 1970s.

Notice that *every* box has a winner, and no single asset wins in all four. Stocks are crushed in stagflation. Bonds are crushed by inflation. Commodities are dead weight in a calm, low-inflation boom. Gold pays you nothing and can drift sideways for years. Each asset is the hero of one quadrant and a liability in another.

### The all-weather move: own one for each box

Here is where the idea lands on a decision. If each box has a different winner, and you cannot tell which box is coming, then the robust thing to do is **own something for every box at once.** Hold stocks (for the growth-up boxes), hold long bonds (for the growth-down, deflation box), hold commodities and gold (for the inflation-up boxes). Then, whatever regime actually arrives, *something* in your portfolio is winning, and that winner softens the blow from whatever is losing.

This is fundamentally different from a 60/40 portfolio, which only covers *two* of the four boxes well. A 60/40 is great when growth is up (stocks) or when growth is down and inflation is low (bonds). But it has almost nothing for the inflation-up boxes — and that gap is exactly where it got destroyed in 2022. The all-weather portfolio is the attempt to fill in the missing quadrants so that *no* regime is a catastrophe.

It is worth being honest about what the four-box map is and is not. It is a *simplification* — the real economy does not move in clean quadrants, regimes blur into one another, and an asset's "winning" box is a tendency, not a guarantee. Stocks usually win when growth rises, but they can fall in a growth-up year if rates spike; gold usually defends the stagflation box, but it can drift for a decade when real interest rates are high. The map is a way to make sure you have *not left a quadrant undefended*, not a precise forecast of which asset will win by how much. That is exactly why the all-weather investor holds all four and balances them, rather than trying to weight toward the box they think is coming: the map's value is in its completeness, not its precision. A portfolio that covers every box loses the ability to *win big* on a correct regime call, but in exchange it never gets *caught completely defenseless* by a regime it failed to predict — and for an investor who admits they cannot predict, that trade is the entire point.

But — and this is the engineering problem the rest of the post solves — owning one asset per box is not enough. You also have to own them in the right *proportions*. And the right proportion turns out to be the single most counterintuitive idea in this whole field: you balance by risk, not by dollars.

### What "balance" actually means

Let us define the word carefully, because everything hinges on it. A portfolio is **balanced** when no single thing dominates its outcome. The naive way to balance is by *money*: put equal dollars in each asset, or split it 60/40, and call it balanced. The problem is that equal dollars do not buy equal influence, because some assets are far wilder than others. A dollar in stocks moves around three times as much as a dollar in bonds. So a portfolio with equal dollars in stocks and bonds is *not* balanced in any meaningful sense — the stocks are driving, and the bonds are along for the ride.

True balance means each asset contributes an equal share of the portfolio's *risk* — its ups and downs, measured by **volatility** (the size of an asset's typical swings, usually quoted as the annual standard deviation of its returns; a higher number means a wilder ride). And to balance *risk* rather than dollars, you have to hold *very* different dollar amounts: a lot of the quiet assets, a little of the wild ones. That single shift — from dollar weighting to risk weighting — is what turns "own one for each box" into the strategy called **risk parity**. Let us build it from scratch.

## Risk parity vs dollar weighting: why your 60/40 is really a 90/10

Here is the fact that breaks most people's intuition about their own portfolio, so we will build it slowly with numbers.

### The hidden truth about the 60/40

A **60/40 portfolio** — 60% stocks, 40% bonds — is the default "balanced" portfolio of the entire investment world. By *dollars*, it looks moderately balanced: a bit more in stocks than bonds, but not wildly so. By *risk*, it is almost a pure equity bet. Roughly 88-90% of the portfolio's volatility comes from the stock sleeve, and barely 10-12% from the bonds. The "40% in bonds" that is supposed to be your ballast is contributing almost nothing to the actual ride. You feel balanced; you are not.

Why? Because the two assets are not equally wild. Stocks have a volatility of roughly 16% per year — meaning a typical year sees the stock sleeve swing about 16% up or down. High-quality bonds have a volatility of only about 5-6% per year. Stocks are about three times as jumpy as bonds. And risk does not add up like dollars; it is dominated by the wildest component. When one asset is three times as volatile as the other, even an equal-dollar split lets the volatile asset run the show.

The figure below makes the gap concrete. The same 60/40 dollars, measured two different ways: as a dollar split (60/40, looks balanced) and as a *risk* split (88/12, an equity bet wearing a balanced costume). The third bar shows what genuine risk balance — risk parity — requires: a roughly 50/50 split of risk, which forces you to hold far *more* bonds than stocks by dollars.

![Stacked bars comparing a 60/40 by dollars at 60/40 versus by risk at 88/12 versus risk parity at 50/50](/imgs/blogs/all-weather-and-risk-parity-owning-every-regime-2.png)

#### Worked example: how much risk each sleeve really carries

Let us prove the 88/12 number with arithmetic a beginner can follow. Take a \$100,000 portfolio split 60/40: \$60,000 in stocks, \$40,000 in bonds. Use round volatilities: stocks 16% per year, bonds 6% per year. We will measure each sleeve's risk as its dollar volatility — how many dollars it typically swings.

- The **stock sleeve** swings about 16% of \$60,000 = 0.16 × \$60,000 = **\$9,600** in a typical year.
- The **bond sleeve** swings about 6% of \$40,000 = 0.06 × \$40,000 = **\$2,400** in a typical year.

Now compare. If we ignore correlation for a back-of-envelope (a simplification, but it gets the proportions right), the stock sleeve's share of the total swing is \$9,600 ÷ (\$9,600 + \$2,400) = \$9,600 ÷ \$12,000 = **80%**, and the bonds carry just 20%. Account for the fact that risk adds in a slightly more complex way and the equity share rises toward the commonly cited **~88-90%**. Either way, the headline holds: in a 60/40, the stocks are doing nearly all the moving.

The takeaway in one line: putting 40% of your *dollars* in bonds buys you only about 10-20% of your *risk* in bonds, because each bond dollar is so much calmer than each stock dollar.

### What risk parity does instead

**Risk parity** is the rule: weight the assets so each contributes an *equal* share of the portfolio's risk. Not equal dollars — equal risk. To make a calm asset contribute as much risk as a wild one, you simply hold a lot more of it. If bonds are one-third as volatile as stocks, you need roughly three times the dollar amount of bonds to match the stocks' risk contribution.

Carried to a simple two-asset risk-parity portfolio, that means something like 25-30% in stocks and 70-75% in bonds by dollars — almost the mirror image of a 60/40. The stocks no longer dominate; each sleeve adds a roughly equal share of the wobble. *Now* the portfolio is balanced in the way that matters, because a bad year for stocks and a bad year for bonds hurt the portfolio by similar amounts, instead of stocks single-handedly steering it.

#### Worked example: rebalancing a 60/40 into risk parity

Suppose you start with the \$100,000 60/40 from before and want to convert it to two-asset risk parity, using the same volatilities (stocks 16%, bonds 6%). The recipe for equal risk: weight each asset inversely to its volatility.

- Stocks get a weight proportional to 1 ÷ 16 = 0.0625.
- Bonds get a weight proportional to 1 ÷ 6 = 0.1667.
- The two raw numbers sum to 0.0625 + 0.1667 = 0.2292. Normalize: stocks = 0.0625 ÷ 0.2292 ≈ **27%**, bonds = 0.1667 ÷ 0.2292 ≈ **73%**.

So your risk-parity split is about \$27,000 in stocks and \$73,000 in bonds. Check the risk: stock swing = 0.16 × \$27,000 = \$4,320; bond swing = 0.06 × \$73,000 = \$4,380. Nearly identical — **each sleeve now contributes about the same number of dollars of risk.** That is what "parity" means: equal risk, achieved by very unequal dollars.

The takeaway in one line: to balance two assets by risk, you hold far more of the calm one — risk parity is a portfolio that owns mostly bonds *precisely because* bonds are quiet.

### The leverage problem (and the leverage solution)

There is a catch hiding in that worked example, and it is the catch that makes risk parity controversial. A portfolio of 27% stocks and 73% bonds has *less total risk* than a 60/40 — and less risk usually means less expected return. The calm portfolio earns calm returns. For a big institution trying to hit a target like "7% a year," a sleepy 27/73 mix simply will not get there.

The fix is **leverage** — borrowing money to own more than your cash alone could buy. Specifically, risk-parity strategies often *lever the bond sleeve*: they borrow cheaply and use the borrowed money to hold more bonds, raising the bond sleeve's risk and return until it matches the stock sleeve's, while keeping the *balance* between the two intact. You end up with, say, equal risk from stocks and from a *levered* bond position, and a total portfolio risk (and expected return) cranked back up to whatever level you want.

This is the genuinely clever engineering at the heart of risk parity, and it is also its Achilles' heel. Leverage is a magnifier: it makes good years better and bad years worse. As long as the bonds behave — rallying when stocks fall — the leverage quietly adds return for almost free. But on the day the bonds *stop* behaving and fall *alongside* stocks, the leverage turns a loss into a bigger loss. Hold that thought. It is the whole story of 2022, and we will come back to it.

## A concrete all-weather sketch: what the popularized portfolio actually holds

The two-asset stocks-and-bonds version above is the engine. The full **All Weather** idea, as Ray Dalio's Bridgewater built it and as it was later popularized in a retail-friendly form, adds the inflation-fighting assets so that all four economic boxes are covered, not just two. Let us look at the actual allocation.

### The popularized All Weather mix

A widely circulated, simplified version of the All Weather portfolio — the one that spread into retail "lazy portfolio" guides — looks like this:

- **30%** US stocks
- **40%** long-term Treasury bonds
- **15%** intermediate-term Treasury bonds
- **7.5%** gold
- **7.5%** broad commodities

The figure below lays it out. Stare at it for a second, because the proportions are the entire point: this is a portfolio that holds **55% of its dollars in bonds** and only 30% in stocks — close to the inverse of a 60/40 — with a 15% sleeve of real assets (gold plus commodities) as the inflation hedge that a 60/40 lacks entirely.

![Horizontal bar chart of the popularized All Weather allocation with long bonds at 40 percent and stocks at 30 percent](/imgs/blogs/all-weather-and-risk-parity-owning-every-regime-3.png)

This is a *retail-friendly approximation*, not Bridgewater's actual fund — the real strategy uses leverage, a far wider set of instruments, and active risk targeting that no static allocation can replicate. But the popularized version captures the spirit perfectly, and it explains the proportions. The heavy bond weight is not a bet that bonds will outperform; it is the risk-parity logic in disguise. Because bonds are so much calmer than stocks, you need a *lot* of them by dollars for them to contribute a meaningful share of risk. The 55% in bonds and 30% in stocks is roughly what it takes to keep the *risk* contributions from being dominated by equities — even in this unlevered, retail form.

### Reading the allocation box by box

Map each sleeve back to the four-box diagram:

- **30% stocks** covers the growth-rising boxes. When the economy hums, this sleeve carries the portfolio.
- **40% long-term Treasuries + 15% intermediate Treasuries** covers the growth-falling, deflation box. In a recession, the Fed cuts rates, and *long*-term bonds rally the hardest because their long stream of fixed payments gains the most value when rates fall. (Why long bonds move more than short ones is the concept of *duration*, which we cover in [the stock-bond correlation: the 60/40 engine](/blog/trading/cross-asset/stock-bond-correlation-the-60-40-engine).)
- **7.5% gold** covers the stagflation box — growth falling *and* inflation rising — where stocks and bonds both suffer and gold is the classic store of value. (Whether gold actually deserves that role is the whole debate in [gold: money, insurance, or just a rock](/blog/trading/cross-asset/gold-money-insurance-or-just-a-rock).)
- **7.5% commodities** covers the inflation-rising boxes. When prices surge, the raw materials behind those prices surge too, and a basket of commodities is the most direct hedge.

Every box has a defender. That is the design. It is built to *lose less* in any single regime, not to *win the most* in the one regime that happens to occur. Which means — and this is essential to set expectations honestly — in a roaring bull market for stocks, the All Weather portfolio will *underperform* a stock-heavy portfolio, badly and for years. Its 30% stock weight cannot keep up with someone who is 100% in stocks during a bull run. That underperformance is not a flaw; it is the premium you pay for not getting destroyed when the regime turns. The all-weather investor trades upside in the good box for survival in the bad ones.

#### Worked example: a \$100,000 all-weather portfolio in a calm boom year

Let us see the trade-off in numbers. Take a \$100,000 popularized All Weather portfolio in a year like 2021, when stocks ran hard (S&P +28.7%) but bonds were flat-to-down (Agg −1.5%) and commodities surged (BCOM +27.1%) while gold slipped (−3.6%). Hold the two Treasury sleeves at roughly the Agg's behavior for simplicity.

- Stocks: 0.287 × \$30,000 = **+\$8,610**
- Bonds (\$55,000 total at ~−1.5%): −0.015 × \$55,000 = **−\$825**
- Gold: −0.036 × \$7,500 = **−\$270**
- Commodities: 0.271 × \$7,500 = **+\$2,033**
- Net: +\$8,610 − \$825 − \$270 + \$2,033 = **+\$9,548**, a return of about **+9.5%**.

A solid year — but a 100% stock investor made +28.7% that same year, three times as much. The all-weather portfolio left a fortune on the table in the good box.

The takeaway in one line: in a stock bull market, an all-weather mix deliberately underperforms, because most of its money is parked in the assets that protect you in the *other* boxes that did not happen this year.

## Why it worked for decades: the falling-rate, negative-correlation tailwind

For roughly thirty years, from the early 1990s through 2021, the all-weather and risk-parity approach did not just survive — it *thrived*, often with better risk-adjusted returns than a plain stock-bond mix. That track record is real, and it is worth understanding *why* it happened, because the why is also the warning.

### Two tailwinds that flattered the strategy

The era from 1990 to 2021 had two features that were nearly perfect for a strategy that levers bonds:

**First, interest rates fell, almost relentlessly.** The US 10-year Treasury yield went from around 8% in 1990 to under 1% in 2020. Remember the bond hinge: bond prices move *opposite* to rates. Three decades of falling rates meant three decades of rising bond prices — a structural, multi-decade bull market in bonds. A strategy that holds a lot of bonds, and *levers* them, was being paid a steady tailwind the entire time. Levering an asset that is in a thirty-year uptrend is, in hindsight, a wonderful idea.

**Second, stocks and bonds were usually negatively correlated.** Through most of 2000-2021, when stocks fell, bonds rose, and vice versa. This is the "60/40 dream" regime — bonds genuinely hedged stocks. For risk parity, a negative stock-bond correlation is even more valuable than for a 60/40, because risk parity *levers* the bonds: a levered hedge that reliably zigs when stocks zag is close to free risk reduction. You could crank up the bond exposure with borrowed money and trust it to cushion every equity drawdown.

The chart below shows the relationship that made it all work — and the moment it broke. The stock-bond correlation was solidly *negative* through the 2000s and 2010s (the green tailwind zone), then flipped sharply *positive* in 2022 (the red headwind zone). For two decades, the line sat where risk parity wanted it; then it jumped to where risk parity dreads it.

![Line chart of stock bond correlation negative at minus 0.40 and minus 0.30 then positive at plus 0.55 in 2022](/imgs/blogs/all-weather-and-risk-parity-owning-every-regime-5.png)

### Why levering bonds was nearly free risk in that era

To feel why the negative correlation mattered so much, you have to understand what leverage does to a *hedging* asset versus a *standalone* asset. (We unpack the deeper version of this in [correlation and the diversification free lunch](/blog/trading/cross-asset/correlation-and-the-diversification-free-lunch).)

When you lever an asset that moves *with* the rest of your portfolio, you are adding risk on top of risk — magnifying a position that is already correlated with everything else you own. That is dangerous. But when you lever an asset that moves *against* the rest of your portfolio, something different happens: you are magnifying the *hedge*. A bigger hedge cancels more of your other risk. So in a world where bonds reliably rallied when stocks fell, levering the bonds made the portfolio *more* stable, not less — a larger offsetting position that smoothed out the equity drawdowns. Leverage, which normally adds risk, was being used to *subtract* it. That is the magic trick that made risk parity look like a free lunch for two decades.

#### Worked example: the bond hedge doing its job in 2008

Take a \$100,000 popularized All Weather portfolio into the 2008 crisis, the textbook growth-shock recession, and watch the bonds save the day. Real 2008 numbers: S&P −37%, long Treasuries +25.9%, the Agg +5.2%, gold +5.5%, commodities −35.6%. Split the \$55,000 bond sleeve as \$40,000 long Treasuries and \$15,000 intermediate (call the intermediate sleeve ~+8% for the year).

- Stocks: −0.37 × \$30,000 = **−\$11,100**
- Long Treasuries: +0.259 × \$40,000 = **+\$10,360**
- Intermediate Treasuries: +0.08 × \$15,000 = **+\$1,200**
- Gold: +0.055 × \$7,500 = **+\$413**
- Commodities: −0.356 × \$7,500 = **−\$2,670**
- Net: −\$11,100 + \$10,360 + \$1,200 + \$413 − \$2,670 = **−\$1,797**, a loss of about **−1.8%**.

In the worst financial crisis since the Depression, the all-weather portfolio lost less than 2%, because the long-Treasury rally (+25.9%) almost exactly offset the stock collapse (−37%). A 60/40 lost about 20% that year; a 100% stock portfolio lost 37%. *This* is the all-weather portfolio at its absolute best.

The takeaway in one line: in a disinflationary recession, the long-bond leg rallies hard enough to cancel the stock loss almost dollar-for-dollar — the entire reason the strategy exists.

### The honest version of the track record

So the multi-decade record is genuinely strong, and the all-weather investors who sat through 2008 with a 2% loss instead of a 37% one were rewarded for their discipline. But notice what *made* that record: a thirty-year bond bull market and a reliably negative stock-bond correlation. Both were *features of an era*, not laws of nature. The strategy was not magic; it was beautifully adapted to a specific environment. And environments change. The fund managers who said "risk parity always works" were really saying "the last thirty years always happened" — which is a very different and much more fragile claim. In 2022, the environment changed, and the strategy met the one regime it was structurally unprepared for.

## The 2022 failure: when stocks and bonds fall together and leverage hurts

2022 was the year the all-weather idea collided with its own central assumption. To understand the damage, you have to see exactly which assumption broke and why the leverage made it worse.

### The assumption underneath everything

Strip risk parity to its foundation and you find one load-bearing belief: **bonds diversify stocks.** The entire case for holding a lot of bonds — and especially for *levering* them — depends on bonds zigging when stocks zag. If that holds, the bonds are a hedge, and a levered hedge is a powerful stabilizer. If it breaks — if bonds fall at the *same time* as stocks — then the bonds are no longer a hedge but a second source of loss, and the leverage that was magnifying a stabilizer is now magnifying a loss.

In 2022, it broke. The cause was an *inflation shock*. After a decade of dormant inflation, prices surged (US CPI peaked above 9%), and the Federal Reserve responded with the fastest rate hikes in decades. Recall the dual mechanism: rising rates crush bond prices directly (bond prices move opposite rates), *and* rising rates compress stock valuations (a higher discount rate shrinks the present value of future earnings). One force — rising rates driven by inflation — hit both assets at once. They fell *together*. The negative correlation that had defined two decades flipped positive, and the whole apparatus inverted.

### The numbers

2022 was a near-clean sweep of pain across the all-weather sleeves:

- **Stocks** (S&P 500): **−18.1%**
- **Bonds** (US Agg): **−13.0%** — and *long-term* Treasuries, the heart of the all-weather bond sleeve, fell roughly **−30%**
- **Gold**: **−0.3%** (essentially flat — a modest win)
- **Commodities** (BCOM): **+16.1%** (the one real winner)

Four of the five legs fell, and the two biggest legs — stocks and long bonds — fell hard *at the same time*. The bond sleeve, which in 2008 had *gained* 25.9% and saved the portfolio, in 2022 *lost* around 30% and sank it. The matrix below puts the two regimes side by side: 2008, where the bonds rallied and the portfolio barely scratched; 2022, where the bonds fell with the stocks and the design assumption failed.

![Matrix comparing all weather across 2008 recession green wins and 2022 inflation shock red losses by asset](/imgs/blogs/all-weather-and-risk-parity-owning-every-regime-4.png)

#### Worked example: the same \$100,000 all-weather portfolio in 2022

Now run the exact portfolio through 2022 and feel the difference. Use the simplified split the brief specifies: \$30,000 stocks, \$55,000 Treasuries (blended long-plus-intermediate at roughly −15% for the year, since long Treasuries fell ~−30% and intermediate fell less), \$7,500 gold, \$7,500 commodities.

- Stocks: −0.181 × \$30,000 = **−\$5,430**
- Treasuries (blended ~−15% on \$55,000): −0.15 × \$55,000 = **−\$8,250**
- Gold: −0.003 × \$7,500 = **−\$23**
- Commodities: +0.161 × \$7,500 = **+\$1,208**
- Net: −\$5,430 − \$8,250 − \$23 + \$1,208 = **−\$12,495**, a loss of about **−12.5%**.

A −12.5% year. Compare it honestly: a 60/40 lost about −16% in 2022, so the all-weather mix *did* do better — the commodity and gold sleeves earned their keep, and they are exactly what a 60/40 lacks. But −12.5% is still a serious, painful loss, and the reason is brutal: *four of the five legs fell together.* The diversification did not save the portfolio because, in an inflation shock, the assets stopped being diversified. They all answered to the same master — rising rates — and that master was hostile to nearly everything at once.

The takeaway in one line: an all-weather portfolio loses less than a 60/40 in an inflation shock because it has commodities and gold, but it still loses, because the inflation shock drags down stocks, long bonds, *and* intermediate bonds simultaneously.

### Why the levered funds did worse than the sketch

The unlevered, retail All Weather portfolio above lost around −12% to −20% depending on the exact bond mix. The *levered* risk-parity funds — the institutional versions that borrow to crank up the bond sleeve — did worse, with many down more than 20% in their worst year ever. The reason is the leverage we praised earlier, now working in reverse.

Recall the logic: levering a *hedge* subtracts risk; levering a *non-hedge* adds risk. For two decades the bonds were a hedge, so the leverage was subtracting risk. In 2022 the bonds stopped being a hedge — they fell *with* stocks — and overnight the leverage was magnifying a loss instead of cancelling one. A fund that had borrowed to hold, say, twice the bond exposure was now taking *twice* the bond drawdown, on top of its stock losses. The very mechanism that made risk parity look like a free lunch for thirty years made it a leveraged trap in the one regime where its assumption failed.

#### Worked example: how leverage turned a bad bond year into a worse one

Make the leverage concrete. Suppose a risk-parity fund holds its bond sleeve at **2x leverage** — for every \$1 of investor capital in bonds, it controls \$2 of bonds, financed by borrowing the other dollar. In 2022, long Treasuries fell about 30%.

- An *unlevered* \$10,000 long-Treasury position loses 0.30 × \$10,000 = **−\$3,000** (down 30%).
- A *2x-levered* \$10,000 of capital controlling \$20,000 of long Treasuries loses 0.30 × \$20,000 = **−\$6,000** on the position. Subtract that from the \$10,000 of capital and you are left with \$4,000 — a **−60% loss** on the capital, double the underlying drawdown. (Borrowing costs make it slightly worse still.)

That is why the levered funds posted their worst years on record while the unlevered sketch lost "only" −12% to −20%. The leverage that quietly added a few percent of free return every calm year took it all back, and then some, in the one year the bonds failed.

The takeaway in one line: leverage does not change *whether* you lose in a regime break — it changes *how much*, doubling a 30% bond drawdown into a 60% hit on the levered capital.

## Common misconceptions

A few beliefs about all-weather and risk parity are widespread and wrong. Each one is corrected by a number from this post.

**"Risk parity is conservative because it holds so many bonds."** It is *not* conservative — that is the trap. By dollars it looks bond-heavy and safe, but the *levered* versions target the same total risk as a stock-heavy portfolio; they just source that risk differently. And a heavily-bonded, *levered* portfolio is acutely exposed to one specific event — rising rates — which is exactly what hit in 2022, when levered funds fell more than 20%. "Lots of bonds" does not mean "low risk" when those bonds are levered and rates are climbing.

**"All-weather means you never have a bad year."** False, and 2022 proves it. The promise is *lose less across regimes*, not *never lose*. A popularized All Weather portfolio fell roughly −12% to −20% in 2022. What "all-weather" buys you is that no single regime is a *catastrophe* — a −12% loss instead of a stock investor's −18% or a long-bond investor's −30% — not that every year is positive.

**"Diversification protects you in every crisis."** It protects you in *most* crises — the ones driven by a growth shock, where bonds rally as stocks fall (2008, 2020). It fails in the crisis driven by an *inflation shock*, where stocks and bonds fall together. Diversification is not unconditional; it depends on the assets actually being uncorrelated, and in an inflation shock they correlate sharply. This is the deep lesson of [correlation by regime: growth and inflation](/blog/trading/cross-asset/correlation-by-regime-growth-and-inflation) — correlations are not constants, they are regime-dependent.

**"Risk parity beats a 60/40, full stop."** It beat a 60/40 *in the era it was designed for* — falling rates, negative stock-bond correlation, 1990-2021. Whether it beats a 60/40 in an era of higher, more volatile rates and positive stock-bond correlation is a genuinely open question, because the tailwind that powered its historical record (a thirty-year bond bull market) is gone. The track record is real but it is *conditional on its era*, and that condition no longer holds.

**"The All Weather portfolio you can buy is what Bridgewater runs."** No. The popularized 30/55/15 retail version is an *approximation*. Bridgewater's actual strategy uses leverage, a far wider instrument set, dynamic risk targeting, and active management that no static three-fund portfolio reproduces. The retail version captures the *philosophy* — balance by risk, cover every box — but not the machinery.

**"Leverage is always reckless, so risk parity is gambling."** This one is half right, and the half it gets wrong is the interesting half. Leverage applied to an asset that moves *with* the rest of your portfolio is genuinely reckless — you are doubling down on a bet you already hold. But leverage applied to a *hedge* — an asset that moves *against* the rest — actually *reduces* portfolio risk, because a bigger offsetting position cancels more of your other swings. For two decades, levering bonds did exactly that, and it made risk-parity portfolios *steadier*, not wilder. The recklessness only appears when the hedge stops hedging — which is the precise, narrow condition (an inflation shock turning the stock-bond correlation positive) that 2022 delivered. The honest verdict is not "leverage is gambling" but "levering a hedge is safe right up until the hedge fails, and you must know which regime can make it fail."

## How it shows up in real markets

Five episodes show the all-weather and risk-parity logic working, failing, and being argued over in the real world.

### The 2008 crisis: the strategy's finest hour

In 2008, the global financial system nearly collapsed, the S&P 500 fell 37%, and commodities and high yield were devastated. Yet the all-weather logic held beautifully, because 2008 was a *pure growth shock* — a deflationary recession, exactly the box long bonds are built for. As the economy cratered, the Fed slashed rates toward zero, and long Treasuries returned +25.9% for the year. A balanced all-weather sketch lost under 2%, because the bond rally offset the stock crash almost dollar-for-dollar (we computed −1.8% above). This is the episode every risk-parity advocate points to, and rightly: when the crisis is the *kind* the strategy expects, the protection is extraordinary.

### The 2010s: the quiet years that built the legend

The decade after 2008 was a near-ideal habitat for risk parity. Rates kept grinding lower (the 10-year yield fell from ~3.8% in 2010 to under 1% by 2020), so the levered bond sleeve was paid a steady tailwind. The stock-bond correlation stayed reliably negative, so the bonds kept hedging the occasional equity scare (2011, 2015-16, late 2018). Risk-parity funds compounded smoothly, and the assets under management in the strategy ballooned into the hundreds of billions. This is the stretch that built the strategy's reputation — and, in hindsight, the stretch that lulled everyone into forgetting the assumption underneath it.

### March 2020: a fast shock, a partial test

The COVID crash of March 2020 was violent — the S&P fell about 34% from its February peak to its March trough in five weeks. It was *mostly* a growth shock, and bonds initially did their job as Treasuries rallied on the flight to safety. But for a few terrifying days in mid-March, *everything* sold off at once, including Treasuries, as a global dash for cash forced even safe-haven holders to sell. Risk-parity funds, which lever bonds, were caught in that brief everything-falls window and had to cut positions, amplifying the move. The Fed's massive intervention restored the normal pattern within weeks and the funds recovered, but March 2020 was a preview: even a growth shock can briefly break the bond hedge when leverage forces selling into a liquidity vacuum. (The mechanism — correlations snapping to one in a panic — is covered in [when correlations go to one in a crisis](/blog/trading/cross-asset/correlation-and-the-diversification-free-lunch).)

### 2022: the regime the strategy was built to fear

2022 was the full inflation-shock test, and the strategy failed it as honestly as a strategy can fail. CPI ran above 9%, the Fed hiked from near zero to over 4% in a year, and stocks (−18.1%) and bonds (Agg −13.0%, long Treasuries ~−30%) fell *together*. The unlevered All Weather sketch lost around −12% (gold flat, commodities +16.1% softening the blow); the levered funds lost more than 20%, their worst year ever. Nothing about the strategy was *broken* — it did exactly what it was designed to do, which is hold a lot of (levered) bonds. The problem was that its core assumption, that bonds diversify stocks, is *false in an inflation shock*, and 2022 was the inflation shock the prior thirty years had not delivered.

### The 1970s: the regime the four-box map was built to remember

To see *why* the all-weather framework insists on inflation hedges at all, you have to go back further than 2022, to the original stagflation decade. Through the 1970s, the United States suffered the nightmare box — growth falling *while* inflation surged. Oil shocks drove prices up, the economy stalled, and the two assets that anchor a conventional portfolio both failed: stocks fell sharply in real terms (the S&P lost about a third of its value across 1973-74, and far more after inflation), and long bonds were eroded by inflation that made their fixed coupons nearly worthless. A 60/40 investor had nowhere to hide, because a 60/40 only defends two of the four boxes. The assets that *did* protect you were precisely the ones the all-weather mix adds: gold, which rose several-fold across the decade as confidence in paper money cracked, and commodities, which ride the very inflation that destroys bonds. The 1970s are the historical memory encoded in the 15% real-asset sleeve — a reminder that the inflation-up boxes are not hypothetical, and that a portfolio without a defense for them is one regime away from disaster. (The deeper mechanics of why gold wins exactly here are in [gold: money, insurance, or just a rock](/blog/trading/cross-asset/gold-money-insurance-or-just-a-rock).)

### The post-2022 debate: is the tailwind gone?

Since 2022, the investment world has been arguing about whether risk parity's golden era is over. The bull case for the strategy assumed two things that may not return: a falling-rate tailwind on levered bonds, and a negative stock-bond correlation. With rates higher and more volatile, and inflation a live risk again, the stock-bond correlation has stayed positive (around +0.5 through 2022-2024), which is the headwind regime. Some argue this is temporary and the negative correlation will return; others argue the 1990-2021 era was the *historical exception* and a positive correlation is the long-run norm (it was positive for most of the pre-2000 inflationary decades). The honest answer is that nobody knows — which is itself the argument for *not* betting everything on the assumption that bonds will reliably hedge stocks going forward.

## When to own it: the all-weather playbook

Here is the payoff — how to actually use the all-weather and risk-parity idea, with eyes open to its assumptions and failure mode. This is educational, not personalized advice; the point is the *framework*, not a recommendation to buy anything.

### Use an all-weather core for the part you will not actively manage

The single best use of the all-weather idea is as the **core** of the portfolio — the part you are *not* going to actively trade around the cycle. If you have a genuine edge in timing regimes, then yes, an active rotation across the cycle (overweight stocks early-cycle, rotate to commodities late-cycle, hide in long bonds in recession, as laid out in [the business cycle and the investment clock](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-business-cycle-and-the-investment-clock)) can beat a static mix. But *most* investors do not have that edge, and for them, the honest move is to admit it and hold a balanced core that does not require a regime forecast. The all-weather mix is the "if you can't time it, own all of it" portfolio — and that is most people, most of the time.

### Understand the leverage and the one assumption it rests on

If you use a *levered* risk-parity fund, you must know two things, or you do not understand what you own. First, *how much* leverage and at *what cost* — because the borrowing cost is a headwind, and rising funding costs (themselves a feature of the rate-hike regime) make the leverage more expensive exactly when it is also most dangerous. Second, the load-bearing assumption: the strategy *requires* bonds to diversify stocks. If you believe the stock-bond correlation will stay positive — that inflation, not growth, is the dominant risk ahead — then a levered-bond strategy is structurally fighting the regime, and you should size it accordingly or pair it with hedges.

### Add explicit inflation hedges so it survives an inflation regime

The single biggest lesson of 2022: a pure stocks-plus-bonds risk-parity portfolio has *no defense* against an inflation shock, because that is precisely the regime where both its assets fail. The popularized All Weather mix is wiser than a two-asset version exactly because it includes **commodities, gold, and (in many real-world versions) inflation-linked bonds (TIPS)** — the assets that were the *only* winners in 2022 (commodities +16.1%, gold −0.3% versus stocks and bonds down double digits). If you build or buy an all-weather sleeve, make sure it actually holds real-asset inflation hedges, not just stocks and Treasuries. That sleeve is what lets the portfolio *survive* the box that breaks everything else.

### Rebalance mechanically, not emotionally

The all-weather discipline only works if you **rebalance back to the target weights on a schedule**, not when it "feels right." Mechanical rebalancing forces you to sell whatever sleeve has run up and buy whatever has fallen — which is contrarian by construction, and harvests a small "rebalancing bonus" over time. More importantly, it keeps the *balance* intact: if you let the winners ride, one sleeve will come to dominate and you will drift back toward an undiversified, single-bet portfolio without noticing. The whole strategy is the balance; skipping the rebalance dissolves the strategy.

The reason rebalancing across uncorrelated assets adds a small bonus is worth seeing directly, because it is one of the few genuinely free things in investing. When assets move differently and you periodically reset them to fixed weights, you are systematically selling the one that just got expensive and buying the one that just got cheap — a small, automatic "buy low, sell high" that requires zero forecasting. Over many cycles, that mechanical contrarianism compounds into a return you would not have earned from simply holding the same assets and never touching them. It is largest exactly when the sleeves are volatile *and* uncorrelated — which is the whole design of an all-weather portfolio.

#### Worked example: the rebalancing bonus on two volatile, uncorrelated sleeves

Take a \$100,000 portfolio split 50/50 between two sleeves, A and B, that are uncorrelated and equally volatile. Over two years, A goes +30% then −20%, while B goes −20% then +30% (the same swings, opposite order, so neither sleeve is "better"). Compare *never rebalancing* with *rebalancing back to 50/50 at the end of year one*.

- **Never rebalance.** Sleeve A: \$50,000 × 1.30 × 0.80 = \$52,000. Sleeve B: \$50,000 × 0.80 × 1.30 = \$52,000. Total after two years = **\$104,000**, a +4.0% gain.
- **Rebalance after year one.** End of year one: A = \$50,000 × 1.30 = \$65,000; B = \$50,000 × 0.80 = \$40,000; total \$105,000. Reset to 50/50: \$52,500 each (you *sold* \$12,500 of the winner A and *bought* \$12,500 of the loser B). Year two: A = \$52,500 × 0.80 = \$42,000; B = \$52,500 × 1.30 = \$68,250. Total = **\$110,250**, a +10.25% gain.

The rebalanced portfolio ended **\$6,250 richer** on the same two assets and the same returns — purely from the discipline of resetting the weights. The act of trimming the winner to feed the loser, done mechanically, captured value that the buy-and-hold investor left on the table.

The takeaway in one line: rebalancing uncorrelated sleeves back to fixed weights is an automatic "sell high, buy low" that pays a real bonus over time — and it is the discipline that keeps an all-weather portfolio from quietly turning into a single-asset bet.

### The mechanism, one more time

It is worth fixing the central engineering trick in your mind, because it is the thing most people get wrong. The reason an all-weather / risk-parity portfolio holds so many bonds — and borrows to hold even more — is *not* a bet that bonds will outperform. It is the consequence of one rule: balance the portfolio by *risk*, and since bonds are so much quieter than stocks, equalizing risk forces you to hold (and often lever) a lot of bonds. The figure below traces that logic step by step: stocks are wild, bonds are calm, equal dollars let stocks dominate the risk, so you borrow to lever the bonds up to the stocks' risk level, and *only then* is each sleeve contributing equally.

#### Worked example: choosing between a 60/40, an all-weather mix, and a levered fund

Put yourself at a decision point with \$100,000 and three options, and reason through them with the numbers from this post.

- **A 60/40** earns the most in a stock bull market (it is ~88% equity risk, so it rides equities up) but is exposed to the inflation shock — it lost about −16% in 2022 and has *no* inflation hedge.
- **An unlevered popularized All Weather mix** gives up upside in stock bull years (we computed +9.5% in a +28.7% stock year) in exchange for losing less across regimes — −1.8% in 2008, about −12% in 2022. It is the survival-focused, hands-off core.
- **A levered risk-parity fund** targets a higher return than the unlevered mix by borrowing to lever bonds — which adds free return when the bond hedge works (the whole 2000-2021 record) but doubled the bond drawdown when it failed (we saw a 30% bond fall become a 60% loss on 2x-levered capital in 2022).

There is no universally "right" choice — it depends entirely on which regime you think is coming and how much you trust the bond hedge. The all-weather answer is the humble one: *if you genuinely cannot tell, the unlevered balanced mix gives up the least across all the regimes you might be wrong about.*

The takeaway in one line: the choice between these three is really a choice about how much you trust your own regime forecast — and the all-weather mix is the portfolio you build when the honest answer is "not much."

### What invalidates the all-weather case

Finally, the discipline of naming what would make you *wrong*. The all-weather case weakens if: (1) you have a genuine, tested edge in timing regimes — then active beats a static core; (2) you believe inflation is the dominant risk for years ahead and the stock-bond correlation will stay positive — then a levered-bond strategy is fighting the regime and needs heavier inflation hedges or less leverage; or (3) funding costs rise enough that the leverage on the bond sleeve becomes too expensive to carry. The strategy is not a faith; it is a bet on a specific structure — *balance by risk, hold one asset per box, and trust that no single regime dominates* — and like every bet, it has conditions under which it fails. Knowing those conditions is the difference between owning the strategy and being owned by it.
