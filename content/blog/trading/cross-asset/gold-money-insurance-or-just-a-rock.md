> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Gold has no earnings and no coupon, so it is not a productive asset; it is a store of value whose price is set by the *opportunity cost of holding money* (real interest rates), confidence in paper currencies, and fear. Its job in a portfolio is not to compound — it is to pay off in exactly the regimes where stocks and bonds both fail.
>
> - **Gold's "yield" is zero**, so it competes with cash and inflation-protected bonds. When *real* interest rates fall, gold rises; when real rates surge, gold stalls. That single relationship explains most of its big moves.
> - **It behaves in bursts**: decade-long dead stretches (1980-2000) and then explosive runs (the 1970s, 2001-2011, 2019-2024). Over a century it returned just **+0.8% per year after inflation** — it barely beats inflation, because it is insurance, not a growth engine.
> - **Its correlation with stocks is about +0.05** — essentially zero. That near-zero link, plus a **-0.40 correlation with the US dollar**, is the entire reason a multi-asset investor holds it.
> - The one number to remember: a 5-10% gold slice is a *diversifier*, not a bet. In 2008 stocks fell 37% and gold rose 5.5%; in 2020 stocks had a brutal spring and gold finished the year **+25%**. That is the trade.

In the autumn of 1979, an American who had kept their savings in a bank account was quietly being robbed. Inflation was running above 13% a year, the bank paid maybe 5% on a savings account, and so every dollar in the bank was losing purchasing power faster than it earned interest. People did the rational thing: they looked for somewhere to hide. A lot of that money ran into gold. The metal had started the decade near \$35 an ounce — a price fixed by the US government — and by January 1980 it touched \$850. A rock that pays you nothing, produces nothing, and cannot be eaten had outrun nearly every "real" investment of the decade.

Then it did almost nothing for twenty years. Anyone who bought gold at the 1980 peak and held it was still underwater, in plain dollar terms, in the year 2000 — and far further underwater once you account for two decades of inflation eating away at those dollars. The same asset that was a lifeline in 1979 was a slow-motion disappointment for the entire 1980s and 1990s. Both things are true, and the gap between them is the whole story of gold.

That contradiction — a "safe" asset that can lose money for twenty years, an "unproductive" rock that periodically outruns everything — is what makes gold the most misunderstood asset class an investor will meet. People want it to be one simple thing: money, or a inflation hedge, or a bubble, or a scam. It is none of those cleanly and all of them partly. The diagram above is the mental model we will build the whole post around: gold has no cash flow to anchor its price, so four forces do that job instead — real interest rates, the dollar, central-bank demand, and fear. Understand those four, and gold stops being mysterious. It becomes a tool with a specific, narrow, and genuinely valuable purpose.

![Gold has no cash flow so four forces set its price: real yields, the dollar, central banks, and fear](/imgs/blogs/gold-money-insurance-or-just-a-rock-1.png)

This is the deep-dive on gold in the *Cross-Asset Playbook* series — the post that sits next to [the map of asset classes](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-map-of-asset-classes-what-you-can-own), where we first laid out what you can own. Stocks are a claim on corporate profits. Bonds are a claim on a stream of interest. [Cash](/blog/trading/cross-asset/cash-money-markets-the-underrated-asset) earns the policy rate. Gold is the odd one out: it is a claim on nothing. And paradoxically, *that is the point.*

## Foundations: what gold actually is, and why a thing with no cash flow has a price

Let us start from absolute zero, because gold is the one asset where the usual valuation toolkit breaks completely.

### Most assets are valued by the cash they throw off

Almost everything you can invest in is, underneath, a promise of future cash. A *bond* — a loan you make to a government or company — pays you interest (the *coupon*) on a schedule and returns your principal at the end. A *stock* — a share of ownership in a company — gives you a claim on the company's future profits, paid out as *dividends* (cash the company sends shareholders) or reinvested to grow the business. Even a rental property pays rent. For all of these, there is a sensible way to estimate what they are worth: add up the future cash, discount it back to today (because a dollar next year is worth less than a dollar now), and you get a *fundamental value*. The phrase for this is *discounted cash flow*, and it is the backbone of how serious investors price things.

Gold breaks this completely. An ounce of gold sitting in a vault will, ten years from now, still be exactly one ounce of gold. It pays no coupon. It declares no dividend. It collects no rent. Its *yield* — the income you earn per year as a percentage of what you paid — is precisely **zero**, and in fact slightly *negative* once you count the cost of storing and insuring it. You cannot run a discounted-cash-flow model on gold, because there is no cash flow to discount. A famous investor once dismissed it exactly this way: dig it out of the ground, melt it, pay to guard it, and "it has no utility."

So how on earth does it have a price at all — and not a trivial one, but a market worth well over \$15 trillion in above-ground gold?

### A thing with no yield is priced by what people will pay to hold it

Here is the mental key. When an asset throws off cash, its price is tied (loosely, over time) to that cash. When it throws off *nothing*, its price is set entirely by **what the next person is willing to pay to hold it** — and that willingness depends on the alternatives.

Think about your own money. Suppose you have \$1,000 and two ways to park it. Option A: a savings account paying 5% a year, so after one year you have \$1,050. Option B: one ounce of gold (let us pretend gold costs \$1,000), which after one year is still one ounce of gold — \$1,000 of metal, no interest. Which do you choose? When the savings account pays a healthy *real* return, option A is obviously better; holding gold means giving up that 5%. The 5% you forgo is the **opportunity cost** of owning gold — the return you sacrifice by holding the thing that pays nothing.

Now flip it. Suppose the savings account pays 1% but inflation is running at 4%. Your \$1,000 in the bank becomes \$1,010 of dollars next year, but those dollars buy 4% less, so in *real* (after-inflation) terms you have *lost* about 3% of your purchasing power. The bank is a slow leak. Gold, meanwhile, lost you nothing in opportunity terms — there was no real return to give up, because cash itself had a negative real return. Suddenly the rock that pays nothing is *competitive with* the asset that pays something, because the something was worth less than nothing after inflation.

That is the single most important sentence in this entire post, so here it is plainly: **gold competes with cash and bonds, and what matters is not the headline interest rate but the *real* interest rate — the rate after inflation.** When real rates are high, holding zero-yield gold is expensive and gold tends to languish. When real rates are low or negative, holding gold costs you nothing relative to cash, and gold tends to shine. We will make this precise — and chart it — in a moment. (For the full machinery of how real yields are constructed and why they are the master signal, the macro series has a dedicated piece on [real vs nominal rates](/blog/trading/macro-trading/real-vs-nominal-inflation-real-yields-master-signal); we will lean on it rather than re-derive it.)

### Supply and demand: where gold comes from and where it goes

Gold's price is set by flows, so it helps to know the plumbing.

On the **supply** side, gold is unusual: very little new supply appears each year. Roughly 3,000-3,500 tonnes are mined annually, but the *total* above-ground stock — every ounce ever dug up, in bars, coins, jewelry, and central-bank vaults — is around 210,000 tonnes. So new mining adds only about 1.5-2% to the existing stock each year. Contrast that with oil, which is consumed and must be continuously replaced, or with a fiat currency, which a central bank can create at the press of a button. Gold's stock grows slowly and predictably, and almost nothing destroys it (it does not rust or corrode). This near-fixed supply is precisely why gold has been used as money for 3,000 years: you cannot conjure more of it to pay your debts.

On the **demand** side, three buckets matter:

- **Jewelry** (historically the biggest, especially in India and China) — roughly half of annual demand, though this is price-sensitive and behaves more like consumption.
- **Investment** — bars, coins, and gold-backed exchange-traded funds (*ETFs*, funds that hold physical gold and trade like a stock). This is the bucket that swings hardest with fear and real yields.
- **Central banks** — the official reserves of countries. This bucket has gone from a net *seller* in the 1990s-2000s to a heavy net *buyer* since 2010, and especially since 2022. We will come back to why this matters enormously.

A small amount of gold is used in electronics and dentistry, but industrially gold is almost an afterthought — unlike silver or copper, its price is barely an industrial story. Gold is a *monetary* metal first and an industrial metal a distant last. That is why it trades on interest rates and fear rather than on factory output.

#### Worked example: the opportunity cost of an ounce, in dollars

Let us put real numbers on the opportunity-cost idea, because it is the engine of everything.

Suppose you have \$2,000 and gold costs \$2,000 per ounce. You can hold one ounce of gold, or you can hold \$2,000 in one-year Treasury bills.

**Case 1 — high real rates.** The T-bill yields 5% and inflation is 2%. After one year:

- The bills give you \$2,000 × 1.05 = **\$2,100** in cash. In real terms, after 2% inflation, that is worth about \$2,100 ÷ 1.02 ≈ **\$2,059** in today's purchasing power — a real gain of about \$59.
- The ounce of gold is still one ounce. If its price merely tracked inflation, it would be worth about \$2,040 — a real gain of about \$0 (it just kept pace with prices).
- By holding gold instead of bills, you gave up roughly \$59 of real return. That \$59 is the *carrying cost* of gold when real rates are positive.

**Case 2 — negative real rates.** Now the T-bill yields 1% and inflation is 5%. After one year:

- The bills give you \$2,000 × 1.01 = \$2,020 in cash, but 5% inflation means that buys about \$2,020 ÷ 1.05 ≈ **\$1,924** in today's purchasing power — a real *loss* of about \$76.
- Gold, again, just needs to keep pace with inflation to hold its real value at \$2,000.
- Now holding gold instead of bills *saved* you about \$76 of real loss. The opportunity cost flipped sign.

The intuition: gold's appeal is mechanically the *inverse* of the real return on safe cash — when cash punishes you, gold's zero looks generous; when cash rewards you, gold's zero looks expensive.

## What actually drives the gold price

We now have the foundation: gold has no cash flow, so its price is what people will pay, which is governed by the alternatives. Let us turn that into the four concrete drivers from the cover figure, in order of importance.

### Driver 1: real yields — the master variable

If you remember only one driver, remember this one. Gold is in a constant tug-of-war with *real* government bond yields, most cleanly measured by the yield on **Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities** (*TIPS* — US government bonds whose principal rises with inflation, so their quoted yield is already a *real*, after-inflation yield). The 10-year TIPS yield is the purest market read on "what does safe money pay you, after inflation?"

The relationship is *inverse*: when the real yield falls, gold rises; when the real yield climbs, gold falls. The logic is exactly the opportunity-cost story from the Foundations. A high real TIPS yield means a risk-free, inflation-protected bond is paying you a real return — so why hold zero-yield gold? Money leaves gold for TIPS. A negative real TIPS yield means even the inflation-protected bond is guaranteeing you a real loss — so gold's zero is suddenly the *better* deal, and money flows in.

The chart below is the cleanest evidence in the whole post. It plots gold against the 10-year real yield, with the real-yield axis *inverted* so the two lines move together visually — which is the entire point.

![Gold price versus the inverted 10-year real yield, showing they move together](/imgs/blogs/gold-money-insurance-or-just-a-rock-3.png)

Read it left to right. In mid-2020, the 10-year real yield was about **-0.93%**, and by late 2021 it had fallen to roughly **-1.04%** — deeply negative, the most punishing real-rate environment for cash in decades. Gold was near its record highs, around \$1,900 an ounce. Then 2022 arrived: the Federal Reserve hiked interest rates aggressively to fight inflation, and the 10-year real yield ripped from negative all the way to **+1.74%** by October 2022, then **+2.48%** by late 2023. Cash and TIPS suddenly paid a real return again — and gold, predictably, stalled, chopping sideways for most of 2022 even though geopolitical fear was high. The opportunity cost of holding it had gone from "free" to "expensive" almost overnight.

This is why people who only watch the news are constantly surprised by gold. They expect war or inflation headlines to send it soaring. Often the headlines do not matter as much as the quiet, boring real-yield curve grinding in the background. (The mechanics of how the central bank moves these rates, and why the *real* rate — not the headline rate — is the one that bites, are spelled out in the [real vs nominal master-signal](/blog/trading/macro-trading/real-vs-nominal-inflation-real-yields-master-signal) piece, which is the prerequisite reading for this driver.)

#### Worked example: a 1-point move in real yields, in gold terms

Analysts often use a rough rule of thumb that has held loosely over the 2010s-2020s: a 1 percentage-point fall in the 10-year real yield has historically been associated with something like a 10-15% rise in gold over the following stretch (the relationship is noisy, not mechanical, so treat this as a sketch, not a law).

Suppose gold is \$2,000 and the 10-year real yield is +2.0%. Now say the economy weakens, the Fed signals cuts, and the real yield falls to +1.0% — a 1-point drop.

- Apply the rough 12% sensitivity: \$2,000 × 1.12 = **\$2,240**. Gold rises \$240 on a move that produced *no* news headline at all — just a quieter bond market.
- If instead the real yield rose from +2.0% to +3.0%, apply it the other way: \$2,000 × 0.88 = **\$1,760**. Gold falls \$240 even if nothing "scary" happened.

The intuition: most of gold's big swings are not driven by drama — they are driven by the unglamorous real-yield curve, and you can often see the move coming by watching TIPS rather than the news.

### Driver 2: the US dollar

Gold is priced in US dollars. One ounce of gold has a single global value, but it is quoted as a number of *dollars* — and that means the dollar's own strength changes the gold price even when gold's underlying value is unchanged.

Imagine gold is "really" worth a fixed basket of goods. If the dollar strengthens — if each dollar buys more of everything — then it takes *fewer* dollars to buy that same ounce, so the dollar price of gold falls. If the dollar weakens, it takes *more* dollars to buy the same ounce, so the dollar price of gold rises. On top of this mechanical effect, a weaker dollar makes gold *cheaper* for buyers in other currencies (a buyer paying in euros or rupees gets more gold per unit of their own money when the dollar is weak), which lifts their demand. Both effects push the same way: **a weaker dollar tends to lift gold, and a stronger dollar tends to weigh on it.**

We measure the dollar with the **DXY** (the US Dollar Index — the dollar's value against a basket of major currencies, mostly the euro). The link is inverse but loose. In 2022, the DXY surged to about **103.5** as the Fed hiked faster than everyone else; that strong dollar was part of why gold dipped that year even with a war in Europe. By contrast, when the dollar peaks and rolls over, gold often gets a tailwind. (The dollar is its own deep topic — why it sits at the center of the financial system, and how to read DXY — in [the dollar system](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy).)

A subtlety worth holding: the dollar and real yields are not independent. Often the *same* force — the Fed hiking — pushes real yields up *and* the dollar up at the same time, and both lean on gold together. That is why 2022 was a double headwind: real yields surged and the dollar surged in tandem. When those two move *against* each other, the gold signal gets muddier, and you have to weight real yields as the stronger of the two.

### Driver 3: central-bank demand

This driver was nearly dormant for two decades and then became one of the most important forces in the market, so it deserves real attention.

Central banks hold *reserves* — the official savings of a nation, used to defend its currency and settle international accounts. For most of the late 20th century, those reserves were overwhelmingly US dollars and US Treasury bonds, and central banks were net *sellers* of gold (the UK famously sold half its gold reserves around 1999-2002, near the bottom of the market). Gold was seen as a relic.

That has reversed dramatically. Since around 2010, and explosively since 2022, central banks — especially in China, India, Turkey, Russia, and across emerging markets — have been net *buyers* of gold, adding on the order of **1,000+ tonnes a year** in 2022, 2023, and 2024. Why the change? Two reasons. First, *diversification*: holding all your reserves in one country's currency and bonds is risky if your relationship with that country sours. Second, and more pointedly, the 2022 freezing of Russia's dollar reserves after its invasion of Ukraine taught every non-aligned central bank a lesson — dollar reserves can be switched off by the country that issues them, but a bar of gold in your own vault cannot. Gold is the one reserve asset that is *no one else's liability*. That makes it uniquely attractive to a central bank that wants savings nobody can freeze.

This matters for the price in a specific way: central-bank buying is **price-insensitive and persistent**. A central bank diversifying reserves does not care whether gold is \$1,800 or \$2,600 — it buys steadily for strategic reasons that have nothing to do with whether the chart looks expensive. A trader who buys gold and a central bank that buys gold are doing completely different things: the trader will sell if the price drops 15%, while the central bank will, if anything, buy *more*. That distinction matters enormously for the price, because it means a growing share of gold demand is *not* going to reverse on a bad month — it is a one-way structural flow. When a buyer of that size shows up and does not flinch, it puts a firm *floor* under the market and dampens the depth of selloffs.

This is precisely what explains the strangest stretch of recent gold history: why gold held up in 2022 and 2023 *despite* surging real yields, which the old playbook said should have crushed it. The traditional real-yield model predicted a 15-20% fall; instead gold was roughly flat in 2022 and rose in 2023 and 2024. The missing variable was the official sector. The general lesson for any asset, not just gold: when a usually-reliable relationship (real yields up, gold down) suddenly only half-works, do not assume the relationship is broken — look for a *new structural force* that is offsetting it. In gold's case from 2022 to 2024, that force was the world's central banks quietly building vaults of an asset that no other country can freeze, print, or default on. Recognizing that early was the single biggest edge in the gold market of the decade.

### Driver 4: fear and tail-risk hedging

The most famous driver is also the least reliable on its own. Gold is the classic **safe-haven** asset — the thing investors run to when they are frightened. Wars, banking panics, sovereign-debt crises, sudden inflation scares, contested elections: when the future looks genuinely uncertain and people doubt the institutions that stand behind paper money, demand for a hard, physical, 3,000-year-old store of value spikes.

But "fear drives gold" is true only in a specific way: fear drives the *timing and the spikes*, while real yields and the dollar set the *level and the trend*. A war can pop gold higher for a few weeks; if real yields are simultaneously high, the spike fades. The cleanest way to think about it: real yields and the dollar decide whether gold is in an uptrend or a downtrend over months and years, and fear decides whether next Tuesday it gaps up. A diversifier you hold for the rare catastrophe will spend most of its time looking boring — and that boredom is the premium you pay for the insurance.

## How gold behaves: dead decades and explosive runs

Knowing the drivers tells you *why* gold moves. Now we need its *temperament* — because gold's price path is genuinely strange compared with stocks or bonds, and misunderstanding it is how people get hurt.

### Gold moves in bursts, not a steady climb

The defining feature of gold's history is that it does almost nothing for long stretches and then moves explosively in short ones. Stocks, over the long run, grind upward most years with the occasional crash. Gold does the opposite: it grinds *sideways* for years, then erupts.

![Gold price from 1980 to 2024 showing dead decades and two explosive runs](/imgs/blogs/gold-money-insurance-or-just-a-rock-2.png)

The chart above tells the whole temperament in one picture. There are two dead zones and two booms:

- **1980-2000: a twenty-year dead loss.** Gold ended 1980 around \$590 (after the spike faded from the \$850 peak) and ended 2000 at just **\$273**. That is a *nominal loss of more than half* across two full decades — before you even count the inflation that ate the dollars. An entire generation of investors learned, correctly for their lifetime, that gold was a terrible investment.
- **2001-2011: the first great boom.** Real yields fell, the dollar weakened, and gold ran from \$273 to **\$1,531** — more than a 5x — culminating in the post-2008 crisis surge.
- **2011-2018: a flat plateau.** After the 2011 peak, gold drifted lower and then sideways for the better part of seven years as real yields rose and the crisis faded. It bottomed near \$1,061 at the end of 2015 — a roughly 30% drawdown from the high — and chopped around \$1,200-\$1,300 for years.
- **2019-2024: the second great boom.** Real yields collapsed (negative through 2020-2021), central banks piled in, and gold ran from about \$1,282 (end-2018) to **\$2,625** (end-2024), then extended above \$3,000 an ounce in 2025.

The lesson is brutal and important: **most of gold's lifetime return arrives in two or three bursts, with long flat stretches between.** If you are not holding it during the burst, you miss almost everything; and you cannot reliably predict the burst in advance. That is the central tension of owning gold, and it is exactly why the *sizing* discussion later matters more than the timing.

#### Worked example: \$10,000 in gold over 25 years

Let us trace a real holding period to feel both the payoff and the pain.

You invest **\$10,000** in gold at the end of 1999, when gold is about **\$290** an ounce. You buy:

- \$10,000 ÷ \$290 ≈ **34.5 ounces**.

You hold, doing nothing, for 25 years. At the end of 2024, gold is **\$2,625** an ounce. Your stake is now worth:

- 34.5 oz × \$2,625 ≈ **\$90,500**.

That is roughly a **9x** over 25 years — a compound annual growth rate of about (90,500 ÷ 10,000)^(1/25) − 1 ≈ **9.2% a year**, which sounds wonderful and beats many stock-market stretches. But look closer at *when* the money came: essentially all of it arrived in two bursts (2001-2011 and 2019-2024), and the middle of that period included the 2011-2015 drawdown where your stake fell roughly 30% from its peak and then went nowhere for years. You earned a great return, but you earned it by sitting through a lost half-decade in the middle without selling.

Now run the *cruel* version. Suppose you had instead bought at the end of **1980** at about **\$590** and measured at the end of **2000** at **\$273**:

- \$10,000 ÷ \$590 ≈ 16.9 ounces; 16.9 oz × \$273 ≈ **\$4,600**.

You *lost* more than half your money in nominal dollars over twenty years — and far more in real, after-inflation terms. Same asset, same "store of value," opposite outcome, decided entirely by which decade you happened to hold it.

The intuition: gold's return depends violently on your entry point and your patience, because it delivers in rare bursts separated by dead decades — which is the strongest possible argument for holding a *fixed small slice continuously* rather than trying to trade it.

### Year-to-year, gold's returns are lumpy

Zoom in to single calendar years and the lumpiness is just as clear. The chart below shows gold's annual returns over the most recent decade.

![Gold annual returns from 2014 to 2024 with up years green and down years red](/imgs/blogs/gold-money-insurance-or-just-a-rock-4.png)

The pattern: a brutal **-10.4%** in 2015 (the bottom of the 2011-2015 bear), several flat-to-down years, and then the big years clustering — **+18.3%** in 2019, **+25.1%** in 2020, and **+27.2%** in 2024. The eleven-year average of about +7% a year hides the truth: you did not earn 7% smoothly, you earned a string of small or negative years punctuated by a few huge ones. Notice that the two biggest up years, 2020 and 2024, were not evenly spaced — gold tends to deliver its best years in clusters, when real yields are falling and fear is rising at the same time.

### Volatility and drawdowns

Gold is *not* low-volatility. Its annual price swings run roughly 13-17% (a *standard deviation*, the typical year-to-year wobble around the average), which is in the same ballpark as the stock market and far above bonds or cash. And it has serious *drawdowns* — peak-to-trough falls. The 1980-2000 collapse was about an 80% real loss; the 2011-2015 fall was roughly 45% from the intraday high. So gold is not "safe" in the sense of "low risk of loss." A person who buys gold expecting a steady, calm store of value is in for a shock the first time it falls 30%.

There is a subtle but important point hiding here for anyone building a portfolio: an asset can be *individually* risky and still *reduce* the risk of the whole. Most people instinctively equate "lower-risk portfolio" with "add a calm asset," and so they reach for cash or short-term bonds. That works, but it also drags returns toward zero. Gold offers a different deal: it is *as volatile as stocks* on its own, yet because its swings are driven by entirely different forces (real yields, the dollar, fear) than the swings in stocks (earnings, growth, sentiment), the two sets of swings tend to *cancel* rather than compound when you hold them together. You are not buying calm; you are buying *independence*. And independence, mathematically, is what lowers portfolio risk — even when the independent asset is wild on its own. That is the single most counterintuitive and most valuable fact about gold, and it is worth sitting with before we make it precise.

So the takeaway on temperament is twofold. Gold is *not* a low-risk asset in isolation — it has 15%-plus volatility and has suffered 40-80% drawdowns. But its risk is *uncorrelated* with the risk of everything else, which makes it a powerful risk-*reducer* at the portfolio level. Those two statements sound contradictory and are both true; resolving them is exactly the work of the next section.

## How gold correlates with everything else — the entire reason to own it

Here is the heart of the matter for a multi-asset investor. We do not hold gold because we expect it to compound like stocks (it does not) or to pay income like bonds (it cannot). We hold it because of how it moves *relative to* the rest of the portfolio.

### Near-zero with stocks, negative with the dollar

*Correlation* measures how two assets move together, on a scale from +1 (they move in lockstep) through 0 (no relationship) to −1 (they move in perfect opposition). The single most valuable number in this entire post is gold's correlation with stocks.

![Gold correlation with stocks, bonds, commodities, and the dollar over 2015 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/gold-money-insurance-or-just-a-rock-6.png)

Over the decade 2015-2024, on monthly returns:

- **Gold vs US stocks ≈ +0.05.** Essentially *zero*. Gold's ups and downs have almost nothing to do with the stock market's ups and downs. This is the diversification gold.
- **Gold vs the US dollar (DXY) ≈ −0.40.** Meaningfully negative — gold is a built-in dollar hedge, rising when the dollar falls.
- **Gold vs bonds ≈ +0.30.** Mildly positive; both gold and bonds like falling real yields, so they often drift the same way.
- **Gold vs commodities ≈ +0.35.** Mildly positive; gold is a commodity by market classification, but it trades far more on monetary forces than on industrial demand, so the link is weak.

The near-zero correlation with stocks is the prize. To see *why* zero correlation is so valuable, you have to understand what it does to a portfolio's risk — which is not obvious, and is where most people's intuition fails.

### Why a near-zero-correlation asset reduces portfolio risk

The magic of diversification is that combining assets that do not move together produces a portfolio whose ups and downs are *smaller* than the average of its parts. Adding an uncorrelated asset can lower the whole portfolio's volatility even if that asset is volatile on its own. Gold's ~0 correlation with stocks is exactly the property you want.

#### Worked example: what a 10% gold slice does to portfolio risk

Let us make this concrete with round numbers. Suppose stocks have an annual volatility (standard deviation) of **16%** and gold has a volatility of **15%** — gold is *just as wobbly* as stocks on its own. Now build two portfolios:

**Portfolio A — 100% stocks.** Volatility = **16%**. Simple.

**Portfolio B — 90% stocks, 10% gold,** with a correlation between them of about **0** (which matches the data). The formula for the volatility of a two-asset portfolio is:

$$\sigma_p = \sqrt{w_s^2\sigma_s^2 + w_g^2\sigma_g^2 + 2\,w_s w_g\,\rho\,\sigma_s\sigma_g}$$

where $\sigma_p$ is portfolio volatility, $w_s, w_g$ are the weights in stocks and gold (0.90 and 0.10), $\sigma_s, \sigma_g$ are their volatilities (0.16 and 0.15), and $\rho$ is the correlation between them (≈ 0). Plugging in:

- $w_s^2\sigma_s^2 = 0.90^2 \times 0.16^2 = 0.81 \times 0.0256 = 0.020736$
- $w_g^2\sigma_g^2 = 0.10^2 \times 0.15^2 = 0.01 \times 0.0225 = 0.000225$
- $2\,w_s w_g\,\rho\,\sigma_s\sigma_g = 2 \times 0.90 \times 0.10 \times 0 \times \ldots = 0$ (the cross term vanishes because $\rho ≈ 0$)
- Sum = 0.020961; $\sigma_p = \sqrt{0.020961} ≈ 0.1448 = $ **14.5%**.

So adding a 10% slice of an asset that is *just as volatile as stocks* took the portfolio's volatility *down* from 16% to about 14.5% — a roughly 9% reduction in risk — purely because the cross term vanished. If gold had been *positively* correlated with stocks (say $\rho = +0.8$, like most other equities), the cross term would have been large and positive, and the risk reduction would have nearly disappeared. The whole benefit lives in that vanishing cross term.

The intuition: gold helps the portfolio not by being calm, but by zigging when stocks zag (or, more precisely, by not caring what stocks do) — and that independence is worth more than a higher return.

### The correlation is not constant — it improves exactly when you need it

Here is the property that elevates gold from "nice diversifier" to "insurance." Its average correlation with stocks is near zero, but in *genuine crises* the correlation often goes *negative* — gold rises while stocks fall. The diversification gets *better* precisely in the months you need it most. We will see this directly in the crisis case studies below. Compare that with most "diversifiers," whose correlations tend to spike *toward +1* in a panic (everything falls together when investors are forced to sell whatever they can). Gold is one of the few assets that frequently does the opposite.

## Gold as insurance: the regimes where it shines

We have built the theory. Now let us watch it work — in the three historical regimes where gold did its job, and the one where it failed, so you can see both sides honestly.

![Gold versus stocks across the 1973-74, 2008, and 2020 crises](/imgs/blogs/gold-money-insurance-or-just-a-rock-5.png)

The chart above is the case for gold in a single image: in three separate regimes where stocks were brutalized, gold either rose sharply or held its ground. Let us walk each one.

### 1973-74: stagflation, the regime gold was born for

The 1970s are the canonical case, because they showcase the one macro environment where gold beats nearly everything: **stagflation** — stagnant growth *and* high inflation at the same time. In a normal recession, inflation falls, so bonds rally (falling rates lift bond prices) and provide a cushion. But in stagflation, inflation is *rising* even as growth weakens, so bonds get hit by inflation *and* stocks get hit by weak growth — both the classic safe and risky assets fail together. There is almost nowhere to hide. Except gold.

Across 1973-74, the S&P 500 fell about **-37%** (a 14.7% drop in 1973 followed by a 26.5% drop in 1974), while gold rose roughly **+210%** compounded over the two years. Bonds were no help — long Treasuries returned only about +4% nominal while inflation ran over 22% cumulatively, a deep real loss. Gold was the asset that protected purchasing power when both halves of a normal portfolio were failing. This is the deepest reason gold exists in a portfolio: it is the hedge against the one regime — high inflation plus weak growth — where the stock-bond diversification that anchors most portfolios stops working. Picture the growth-versus-inflation grid that allocators call the "investment clock": in three of its four corners (recovery, expansion, deflationary recession) some combination of stocks and bonds works fine. Only in the stagflation corner — growth down, inflation up — do *both* fail at once, and that is the corner gold and cash were made for. A portfolio without a gold slice is implicitly betting that the stagflation corner never comes back. The 1970s, and the 2021-2022 inflation scare, are reminders that it can.

### 2008: the global financial crisis

The 2008 crash was a *deflationary* panic, not a stagflation — and gold's behavior was more nuanced, which is instructive. As Lehman Brothers collapsed and credit markets froze, there was a scramble for cash that briefly hit *everything*, gold included, because leveraged investors sold whatever they could to raise dollars. But over the full calendar year 2008, while the S&P 500 fell about **-37%**, commodities fell **-36%**, real estate fell **-38%**, and high-yield bonds fell **-26%**, gold finished **+5.5%** — modestly *positive*. The only other winners were US Treasuries (long Treasuries returned about +26% as investors fled to the safest government debt). Gold and Treasuries were the two assets that did their job. Notice the size of the gap: gold did not soar, but a +5.5% return while your stocks lost a third of their value is exactly what insurance looks like — a payout in the year of the disaster.

### 2020: the COVID shock

2020 is the modern textbook case, and it ties every driver together. When the pandemic hit in March 2020, the S&P 500 fell about 34% from its February peak to its March trough in a matter of weeks. The Federal Reserve responded with the most aggressive policy flood in history — cutting rates to zero and launching enormous bond-buying (*quantitative easing*, the central bank creating new money to buy bonds and push yields down). That flood did two things gold loved at once: it crushed real yields *deeply negative* (the 10-year real yield hit about -0.93%), and it raised fears about the long-term value of money being printed in such quantity. Gold ran to record highs above \$2,000 an ounce for the first time ever, and finished the full calendar year **+25.1%** — even though stocks, helped by the same policy flood, also recovered to finish +18.4%. Gold led on the way down *and* kept up on the way back. The driver stack — collapsing real yields, a softening dollar, and acute fear — all aligned, and gold did exactly what the model predicts.

### And when it fails: 2013, the rising-real-yield trap

Honesty requires the counterexample. In 2013, the US economy was recovering, inflation was low, and the Fed signaled it would slow its bond-buying (the "taper tantrum"). Real yields *rose* sharply — the 10-year real yield went from near zero to well over +0.7% in months. With the master driver moving hard against it, gold fell about **-28%** in 2013, one of its worst years ever, even though there was no crisis and stocks were having a great year (+32%). This is the mirror image of 2020: rising real yields, a firming economy, and fading fear are gold's kryptonite. A reader who buys gold "because inflation" without checking real yields will sometimes get exactly the wrong outcome — as 2013 and much of 2022 showed.

## Common misconceptions

Gold attracts more confident wrong beliefs than any other asset. Here are the ones that cost people money, each corrected with a number.

**"Gold is a great long-term investment / it always goes up."** Over the very long run, gold has returned about **+0.8% per year after inflation** (1900-2023, per the UBS/Dimson-Marsh-Staunton dataset) — it barely beats inflation across a century, compared with about **+6.5%** real per year for stocks and **+1.7%** for bonds. Gold is not a compounder; it is a store of value. Anyone holding it expecting stock-like wealth creation is misunderstanding the product. And as the 1980-2000 example showed, it can lose money for *twenty years* at a stretch.

**"Gold is a reliable inflation hedge — it tracks the CPI."** Only loosely, and not on any timescale you can count on. Gold protected purchasing power magnificently in the 1970s, but it *fell* during the modestly higher inflation of 2013, and it badly lagged the 2021-2022 inflation spike at first (because real yields were rising). Gold hedges *real yields and confidence in money*, which correlate with inflation in some regimes (1970s stagflation) but not others (2013, early 2022). "Inflation hedge" is too crude; "real-yield instrument" is accurate.

**"Gold has no use, so it is worthless / a bubble."** This confuses *industrial* utility with *monetary* utility. Gold's value comes from being a scarce, durable, divisible, universally recognized store of value that is no one's liability — exactly the properties that made it money for 3,000 years across unrelated civilizations. A thing can have near-zero industrial use and still have enormous *monetary* use; that is what "money" means. The \$15-trillion-plus above-ground stock is not waiting to crash to zero.

**"In a crisis everything falls together, so gold won't help."** Often true for risky assets — stocks, high-yield bonds, and real estate did all fall together in 2008. But gold's correlation with stocks is near zero *on average* and frequently goes *negative* in panics. In 2008 gold finished +5.5% while stocks fell 37%; in the 2020 crash gold held up far better than equities. Gold is one of the few assets whose diversification *improves* in a crisis rather than evaporating.

**"If I believe in gold, I should put a lot of my portfolio in it."** This is the most expensive mistake of all, and it follows from misunderstanding gold's job. Because gold can go nowhere (or down) for a decade-plus and is as volatile as stocks, a large gold allocation drags long-run returns badly while you wait for the rare burst. Gold's value is as a *small* diversifying slice — the insurance, not the house. We will size it precisely next.

## How it shows up in real markets

A tour of episodes where the gold model played out, beyond the headline crises, so the drivers feel real.

**The 1971 Nixon shock and the end of the gold standard.** Until 1971, the dollar was legally convertible to gold at \$35 an ounce, and gold could not float freely. When President Nixon ended convertibility in August 1971, gold was suddenly allowed to find its own price — and over the following decade it went from \$35 to its 1980 peak near \$850, a roughly 24x move. The lesson: gold's price is, in part, an inverse measure of confidence in the dollar as managed money. The moment the dollar was no longer anchored to it, the market repriced what people would pay for the anchor.

**The UK selling at the bottom (1999-2002).** Britain's government sold roughly half its gold reserves at an average price near \$275 an ounce, right at the two-decade low — an event sometimes called "Brown's Bottom." Gold then rose more than 8x over the next two decades. The lesson is not that the UK was uniquely foolish; it is that gold was *universally* hated at that moment, after twenty dead years, which is exactly when its opportunity cost was about to flip and the first great boom was about to begin. The most unloved moment for a diversifier is often the highest-payoff entry.

**2011: the parabolic top and the seven lean years.** Gold peaked near \$1,900 (intraday above \$1,920) in September 2011, at the height of the eurozone debt crisis and US debt-ceiling fears, with real yields near zero. Then the crisis eased, the US economy healed, the Fed began to normalize, real yields rose — and gold fell roughly 45% over the next four years and went nowhere until 2019. The lesson: when the fear fades *and* real yields turn up at the same time, gold's two strongest drivers reverse together, and the unwind is long and painful. Buying gold at the top of a fear spike, after a parabolic run, is how people get the twenty-year-dead-decade experience in miniature.

**2022: the year the old rule broke (a little).** By the old playbook, 2022 should have been catastrophic for gold: the 10-year real yield surged from about -1% to +1.7%, and the dollar ripped to multi-decade highs (DXY ~103.5). Gold "should" have fallen 20%+. Instead it finished roughly **flat (-0.3%)**. Why? The new structural buyer — central banks adding over 1,000 tonnes — and persistent fear (war in Ukraine, 9% inflation) offset the real-yield headwind. The lesson: when a reliable relationship only partly holds, look for a new force. In 2022 the missing force was official-sector demand, and recognizing it early was the edge.

**2024-2025: the breakout that puzzled everyone.** Gold rose **+27%** in 2024 and pushed above \$3,000 in 2025 — even though real yields stayed *high* (the 10-year real yield was around +2.2% at end-2024), which the model says should cap gold. The likely explanation is the same one from 2022, amplified: relentless central-bank buying, plus demand from investors hedging against large government deficits and geopolitical fragmentation, plus the eventual prospect of rate cuts. The lesson, and a humbling one: the real-yield relationship is the strongest single driver, but it is not the *only* driver, and when official-sector and fear demand are strong enough, gold can rise even with the master signal leaning against it. A model is a guide, not a guarantee.

## The allocation playbook: how much gold, and when

This is the payoff — the part that turns all the mechanism into a decision. After everything above, the single most important conclusion is this: **with gold, "how much" matters far more than "if."** Because gold's return arrives in unpredictable bursts and it can lie dead for a decade, you cannot reliably time it — so the right approach is a *fixed, small, continuous* allocation that you lean on the margins, not a tactical in-and-out bet.

![The gold playbook matrix: when to lean in and when to trim each driver](/imgs/blogs/gold-money-insurance-or-just-a-rock-7.png)

### Size it as a diversifier: roughly 5-10%

For most multi-asset investors, gold belongs as a **5-10% slice** of the portfolio. Below ~5%, even a big gold move barely moves the needle on the whole portfolio, so it does not provide meaningful insurance. Above ~10-15%, gold's own volatility and its long dead stretches start to drag the portfolio's long-run return materially while you wait for the rare payoff. The 5-10% band is the zone where gold provides real crisis protection without becoming a return-killer. The exact number inside that band is where you express your view on the drivers.

#### Worked example: what a 5% gold slice did in a 2008-style year

Let us price the insurance directly. Suppose you have a **\$100,000** portfolio, split 60% stocks, 35% bonds, 5% gold — and a 2008-style year hits, with stocks **-37%**, bonds **+5%**, gold **+5.5%**.

**Without gold (63% stocks, 37% bonds):**

- Stocks: \$63,000 × (1 − 0.37) = \$39,690 (a \$23,310 loss)
- Bonds: \$37,000 × 1.05 = \$38,850 (a \$1,850 gain)
- Total: **\$78,540** — a portfolio loss of about **-21.5%**.

**With a 5% gold slice (60% stocks, 35% bonds, 5% gold):**

- Stocks: \$60,000 × 0.63 = \$37,800 (a \$22,200 loss)
- Bonds: \$35,000 × 1.05 = \$36,750 (a \$1,750 gain)
- Gold: \$5,000 × 1.055 = \$5,275 (a \$275 gain)
- Total: **\$79,825** — a portfolio loss of about **-20.2%**.

The gold slice cushioned the loss by roughly 1.3 percentage points (\$1,285 on \$100,000) in this single year — modest, but it arrived exactly when everything else was bleeding, and a 10% slice would have roughly doubled the cushion. The intuition: a small gold slice will not transform a bad year into a good one, but it consistently softens the worst years — and softening the worst years is what lets you stay invested and avoid panic-selling the rest.

### Lean in or trim based on the four drivers

The matrix figure above is the working playbook. Use the four drivers as a dashboard, and shade your gold weight toward the top of the 5-10% band when the signals favor it, toward the bottom when they do not:

- **Lean toward 10% when:** real yields are *falling* or deeply negative (the master signal); the dollar is *weakening or peaking* (DXY rolling over); central banks are buying heavily; and tail risk is *rising* (war, inflation scares, debt fears). This is the 2019-2020 setup, and it is when gold's payoff is largest.
- **Trim toward 5% (but never zero) when:** real yields are *surging positive* (2013, 2022 — the clearest sell signal); the dollar is *strong and breaking out*; and markets are calm with low volatility. This is when gold has the least going for it. Crucially, you *trim* rather than *exit*, because the insurance value of holding *some* gold is precisely that crises arrive unannounced — the moment you sell your last ounce is the moment you most regret it.

The asymmetry matters: the cost of holding gold through a flat stretch is a modest drag on returns, while the cost of *not* holding it through a stagflation or a crisis is catastrophic. That asymmetry — small known cost, large rare benefit — is the textbook definition of insurance, and it is why the floor is 5%, not 0%.

A practical way to run this without overthinking it: pick a *base* weight inside the band — say 7% — and let one rule do most of the work. Watch the 10-year real yield. When it is *below* roughly +1% (cheap to hold gold), sit at the top of your band, near 10%. When it is *above* roughly +2% (expensive to hold gold), drift toward the bottom, near 5%. Then make two judgment overrides on top: nudge *higher* if central banks are clearly buying heavily and tail risk is elevated, and nudge *lower* if the dollar is in a powerful uptrend. That is it — one quantitative rule plus two qualitative overrides. The point of keeping it this simple is that it stops you from doing the thing that destroys gold investors: panic-selling the whole slice after a bad year (often right before the next burst) or doubling down out of conviction after a parabolic run (often right before a lost decade). A small, rules-based, continuously-held position sidesteps both traps. You are not trying to be brilliant about gold; you are trying to *always own some insurance* and pay slightly less for it when it is expensive.

### What gold pairs with, and what invalidates the case

Gold's natural partners in a portfolio are the assets it does *not* move with. It pairs especially well with stocks (near-zero correlation) and is a useful complement to [government bonds](/blog/trading/cross-asset/government-bonds-the-risk-free-anchor-and-duration): bonds are the better hedge in a *deflationary* recession (where rates fall and bonds rally), while gold is the better hedge in a *stagflationary* one (where inflation is rising and bonds fail). Holding both means you are covered in both kinds of bad outcome — which is the whole reason a thoughtful portfolio owns a little of each rather than betting on one type of crisis. Gold also pairs naturally with a [cash](/blog/trading/cross-asset/cash-money-markets-the-underrated-asset) buffer: cash is your insurance against a liquidity scramble, gold your insurance against a loss of confidence in money itself.

What *invalidates* the case for a heavier gold weight? A sustained regime of *high and rising real yields* with *falling inflation* and *calm markets* — a healthy, disinflationary expansion. In that world (much of the 1980s-1990s, and 2013), gold simply has no tailwind and a real opportunity cost, and you should be at the bottom of your range. The signal to watch above all others is the 10-year real yield: if it is climbing hard and you cannot identify a strong offsetting force (like heavy central-bank buying), respect the master driver and keep gold light.

To close where we started: gold is not money in the everyday sense (you cannot spend it at the store), it is not a productive investment (it earns nothing), and it is not "just a rock" (a 3,000-year, \$15-trillion store of value that no government can print is plainly more than that). It is *insurance* — a small, deliberately-held slice that does nothing in good times and earns its entire keep in the rare, terrible regimes where stocks and bonds fail together. Size it like insurance, hold it like insurance, and judge it like insurance: not by what it did last year, but by whether you will be glad to own it the year nothing else works.

*This is educational, not financial advice. Every asset that can gain can also lose; gold has fallen more than 40% from peak to trough and lost money for two decades at a stretch. Size positions to your own situation, not to a chart.*
