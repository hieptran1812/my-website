> **TL;DR** — Early cycle is the moment a recession ends and growth inflects up while policy is still maximally easy. It is the single highest-returning phase for risk assets, and the most beaten-down corners snap back the hardest.
>
> - The setup is four depressed springs released at once: cheap valuations, a near-zero discount rate, earnings about to inflect off a crushed base, and washed-out sentiment. Together they fire multiple expansion *and* spread compression at the same time.
> - The leaders are high-beta: cyclical and small-cap equities, high-yield credit, emerging markets, and industrial metals like copper. Government bonds *lag* — in 2009, long Treasuries lost -14.9% while high yield gained +58%.
> - You read the trough from credit spreads peaking and rolling in, the yield curve steepening, PMIs turning up, and unemployment claims peaking — not from the headlines, which are worst exactly at the bottom.
> - The one number to remember: in 2009 a \$100,000 tilt into the beaten-down corners would have gained roughly +\$59,000 — about double the +\$26,500 of the index — but only for an investor who bought when the news was at its darkest.

In March 2009, the financial world looked like it was ending. The S&P 500 had fallen more than 50% from its 2007 peak. Banks were being nationalized in everything but name. The headlines spoke of a second Great Depression, the unemployment rate was marching toward 10%, and every week brought a new record for jobs lost. A reasonable person reading the news that month would have concluded that the only sane thing to do with money was to hide it under a mattress.

That person would have missed one of the greatest entry points in the history of markets. From the March 9 low, the S&P 500 returned about +26.5% for the calendar year 2009 — and that was the *boring* number. High-yield bonds, the junk-rated corporate debt everyone assumed would default into oblivion, returned +58.2%. Emerging-market stocks returned +78.5%. Copper roughly *tripled* off its lows, ending the year up around +140%. The assets that had been hit the hardest in the crash came back the fastest and the furthest. The reward for buying when the headlines were at their worst was enormous — and it was not random. It was the early cycle doing exactly what the early cycle always does.

This post is the early-cycle chapter of the cross-asset playbook. The early cycle — the recovery phase, the rebound out of a recession — is the fat pitch of the entire business cycle: the phase where being early pays the most, where the most aggressive corners of the market lead, and where the discipline that matters is the discipline to lean in while everyone else is still frozen. The diagram below is the mental model we'll build the whole post around: at the trough, four depressed forces are coiled like springs, and when they release together they power the biggest rebound risk assets ever see.

![Early cycle setup with four depressed springs coiling at the trough then releasing into the rebound engine](/imgs/blogs/early-cycle-recovery-what-leads-the-rebound-1.png)

By the end you'll understand what "early cycle" actually means in mechanical terms, why this specific phase is the highest-returning one, exactly which assets lead and why, how to read the trough from real signals instead of the news, what the textbook episodes (2009, the 2020 second half, 2003) looked like, and — the payoff — how to lean into the rebound, how much, and what tells you you're too early or chasing a false dawn. None of this is investment advice; it's the mechanism, the history, and the decision framework.

## Foundations: what "early cycle" actually means

Let's build from zero. The economy does not grow in a straight line. It moves in *cycles*: long expansions punctuated by occasional *recessions* — periods when the economy actually shrinks, businesses cut back, and unemployment rises. The full loop from one expansion's peak, down through a recession to the bottom, and back up into the next expansion is the *business cycle*. If you want the full map of these phases, the companion piece on [the business cycle and the investment clock](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-business-cycle-and-the-investment-clock) lays out all four; this post zooms all the way into one of them.

The *early cycle* — also called the *recovery* or the *rebound* — is the phase that begins right at the bottom of a recession and runs through the first leg of the new expansion. It is defined by a specific cluster of conditions, all happening at once:

- **The recession is ending.** The economy has stopped shrinking and is about to start growing again. The very worst is in the past, even though it doesn't feel that way.
- **Growth is inflecting up from a low base.** This word *inflecting* is the key. The economy isn't strong — it's still weak — but the *rate of change* has turned positive. Yesterday things were getting worse; today they're getting less-bad; tomorrow they're getting better. Markets care enormously about that change in direction.
- **The central bank is maximally easy.** The Federal Reserve (the US central bank, which sets the country's key interest rate) has slashed its policy rate, often to near zero, and is doing everything it can to stimulate borrowing and spending. Money is cheap.
- **Credit is thawing.** During the recession, lenders panicked and stopped lending; the price of borrowing for risky companies spiked. In early cycle, that fear starts to drain out, and credit begins flowing again.
- **Inflation is low.** A recession crushes demand, which pulls prices down. So in early cycle, inflation — the rate at which prices rise — is typically low and falling. That matters because low inflation lets the central bank stay easy without worrying about overheating.
- **Unemployment is high but peaking.** The job market is the *last* thing to heal. Unemployment is still high and may even tick higher for a few months — but it is at or near its peak, and the rate of job losses is shrinking.

Put those together and you get a strange, counterintuitive picture: the *economy* is at its weakest, but the *setup for asset prices* is at its strongest. That gap is the whole point of the early cycle.

### Why "inflecting" beats "strong"

Here is the single most important idea in this entire post, and it's the one beginners get wrong: **markets are not paid by the level of the economy. They are paid by the change in the level.**

Think about what a stock price is. It is the present value of a company's future profits. When you buy a share, you're not buying this year's profit — you're buying a claim on the entire stream of profits stretching out into the future, discounted back to today. (We unpack this fully in the companion piece on [equities and owning a slice of growth](/blog/trading/cross-asset/equities-stocks-owning-a-slice-of-growth).) What moves that present value is not whether the economy is good or bad *right now*, but whether the future looks better or worse than the market had already priced in.

At the trough of a recession, the market has priced in catastrophe. Prices reflect a future that is grim. So the bar to clear is on the floor. The moment the data stops getting worse — the moment growth inflects from "collapsing" to merely "less-bad" — the market's grim assumption is proven too pessimistic, and prices have to be marked up. That re-pricing is the rebound. It happens while the economy is still objectively weak, because what's improving is the *direction*, and direction is what prices.

This is why people who wait for the "all clear" — for unemployment to fall, for the headlines to turn positive, for the economy to feel strong again — systematically miss the best returns. By the time it *feels* safe, the rebound is over. The fat pitch is thrown while everyone is still flinching.

### What "the fat pitch" means

In baseball, a "fat pitch" is an easy one right over the middle of the plate — the one you swing hard at. In investing, it's a setup where the odds are unusually stacked in your favor: where the potential reward is large, the downside is already largely realized, and the only thing standing between you and the return is the discomfort of acting while things look bad.

Early cycle is the fat pitch of the business cycle because three sources of return line up at the same moment, which we'll dissect in detail later:

1. **Multiple expansion** — valuations, crushed at the trough, expand back toward normal.
2. **Spread compression** — the extra yield on risky bonds, blown out in the panic, collapses back to normal.
3. **Earnings recovery** — profits, crushed at the trough, inflect upward.

In most phases of the cycle you get one of these, maybe. In early cycle you can get all three at once, which is why the returns are so outsized and so concentrated in the riskiest corners. Hold that three-part frame; it's the spine of everything that follows.

### The rhythm: big up years follow the drawdowns

Before we go deeper, it helps to *see* the cycle's basic rhythm in plain return data, because the early-cycle thesis is really a claim about a pattern that repeats. The chart below is the S&P 500's total return for each calendar year from 2014 through 2024 — eleven years that contain two clean drawdown-and-rebound pairs:

![Cycle context bar chart of S&P 500 calendar year total returns 2014 to 2024 with drawdown years in red and rebound years in green](/imgs/blogs/early-cycle-recovery-what-leads-the-rebound-6.png)

Notice the shape. The two down years — 2018 (-4.4%) and 2022 (-18.1%) — are each followed immediately by one of the biggest up years on the chart: 2019 returned +31.5% and 2023 returned +26.3%. That is the rhythm in miniature. The market falls when the future darkens, then rebounds hardest precisely off those darkened lows, because that's where expectations were lowest and had the most room to be exceeded. The 2018 and 2022 selloffs weren't full recessions (they were a growth scare and an inflation/rate shock, respectively), so the rebounds were "early-cycle-lite" rather than the full-blown 2009 version — but the geometry is identical. The biggest up years sit right next to the down years, not next to the calm years. A reader who internalizes only this one picture already understands the core of why early cycle pays: the rebound is largest where the drawdown was deepest.

The important caveat: this pattern is *not* a promise that every down year is followed by an up year (in a deep recession, the down year can be followed by *another* down year before the trough). It's a statement about what the recovery looks like *once the trough is actually in* — which is exactly why reading the trough correctly, the subject of a whole section below, is the entire skill.

## What leads the rebound — and why

If early cycle is the fat pitch, the natural question is: *what do you swing at?* The answer is consistent across every recovery in modern history. The assets that lead the rebound are the ones that were beaten down the hardest in the recession — the high-*beta* assets.

Let's define that term, because it's load-bearing. *Beta* is a measure of how much an asset moves relative to the overall market. A high-beta asset amplifies the market: when the market falls 10%, a beta-2 asset falls ~20%; when the market rises 10%, it rises ~20%. High-beta assets are the ones that get crushed worst in a downturn and snap back hardest in a recovery. Early cycle is when high beta is your friend.

Here is what actually led in 2009, the textbook early-cycle year. The chart shows total returns for the full calendar year:

![2009 total returns bar chart with copper plus 140 percent, EM plus 79, high yield plus 58, small-caps plus 27, S&P plus 27, bonds plus 6, long Treasuries minus 15](/imgs/blogs/early-cycle-recovery-what-leads-the-rebound-2.png)

Look at the shape of that. The biggest winners are the most economically sensitive, most beaten-down assets. The safe assets — high-grade bonds, and especially long-dated Treasuries — bring up the rear, with long Treasuries actually *losing* money. That inversion of the usual order is the signature of the early cycle. Let's walk through each leader and the mechanism behind it.

### Equities broadly — but especially the cyclical, high-beta corners

Stocks as a whole are the first big winner, because the early cycle is when the present value of future profits gets marked back up. But not all stocks are equal here. The leaders within equities are the *cyclicals* — companies whose fortunes swing hard with the economy.

- **Consumer discretionary** — companies selling things people buy when they feel confident: cars, restaurants, travel, retail. In a recession, discretionary spending is the first thing households cut, so these stocks crater. In recovery, it's the first thing they restore, so these stocks lead.
- **Financials** — banks and other lenders. They're hammered in a downturn (loan losses, fear of insolvency) and they're prime beneficiaries of the recovery: a steep yield curve (more on this below) lets them borrow cheap and lend dear, and loan losses fade as the economy heals.
- **Industrials** — companies that build physical things: machinery, construction equipment, transport. They're geared directly to the real economy restarting.
- **Small-caps** — shares of smaller companies, tracked by indices like the Russell 2000. Smaller firms are more fragile, more domestically exposed, and more leveraged, which makes them higher-beta. They fall harder and rebound harder. In 2009 the Russell 2000 returned +27.2%, roughly matching the S&P; in the steepest early-cycle rallies small-caps often *outrun* large-caps by a wide margin in the first leg.

The opposite of cyclicals are *defensives* — staples (food, household products), utilities, healthcare — companies whose demand barely moves with the economy because people buy toothpaste and electricity in any environment. Defensives are the heroes of a recession (they fall least) and the laggards of a recovery (they rise least). The early-cycle rotation is precisely a rotation *out of* defensives and *into* cyclicals.

#### Worked example: rotating from defensives into cyclicals at the trough

You enter the recession defensively positioned — \$50,000 in a basket of staples and utilities, which is exactly where you wanted to be while the market fell. At the trough you face a choice: stay defensive or rotate into cyclicals. Let's say cyclicals are down 55% from their peak and defensives are down only 20%, which is typical of how the two behave into a bottom.

- If you *stay* in defensives through the first year of recovery, you might earn the broad market's defensive return — call it +10%, so +\$5,000, leaving \$55,000.
- If you *rotate* the \$50,000 into cyclicals (discretionary, financials, industrials), which snap back from their deeper hole, you might earn +40% in that first year, so +\$20,000, leaving \$70,000.

The rotation earned \$70,000 versus \$55,000 — an extra +\$15,000 on \$50,000, simply for moving along the risk spectrum at the right moment. The mechanical reason is the deeper hole: cyclicals fell 55% and defensives only 20%, so cyclicals had far more lost ground to recover. The one-sentence intuition: the same beta that made defensives the right call into the recession makes them the wrong call out of it — at the trough you want to own what fell the most, not what fell the least.

### High-yield credit — the spread-compression engine

The second great early-cycle winner is high-yield credit: the bonds of riskier, lower-rated companies, also called *junk bonds*. We cover this asset in full in [corporate credit: investment grade, high yield, and the spread](/blog/trading/cross-asset/corporate-credit-investment-grade-high-yield-spreads), but here's what matters for the recovery.

A high-yield bond pays you the risk-free Treasury yield plus an extra slice called the *credit spread* — compensation for the risk the company doesn't pay you back. That spread is essentially the market's price tag on fear of default. In a recession, fear spikes, spreads blow out, and high-yield bond prices collapse as if half of corporate America were going bankrupt. In early cycle, the fear drains out, the spread *compresses* (narrows), and those bond prices rocket back up.

That spread compression is a profit engine. When a spread falls from, say, 1,100 basis points (a *basis point* is one hundredth of a percent, so 1,100 bps is 11%) back to 360 bps, the prices of those bonds rise sharply — and on top of that you've been collecting the fat coupon the whole time. In 2009, high yield returned +58.2%. That's not a typo; it's what spread compression off a crisis-wide level does. We'll quantify the engine in a moment with the spread chart.

### Emerging markets — the highest-beta equity

The third leader is *emerging-market* (EM) equities — stocks in developing economies like Brazil, India, China, South Korea. EM is, in effect, the highest-beta version of the global stock market. These economies are more sensitive to global growth, more dependent on commodity exports and trade, and more vulnerable to the cost and availability of dollars.

That sensitivity cuts both ways. In a global downturn, EM gets crushed. In a global recovery — especially one where the US dollar is weakening, which it tends to do when the Fed floods the system with easy money — EM screams higher. The weaker dollar is a double tailwind: it eases financial conditions for countries that borrow in dollars, and it boosts the commodity prices many EM economies depend on. (The dollar's gravitational pull across assets is its own chapter; the short version is that a falling dollar is rocket fuel for EM.) In 2009, EM equities returned +78.5%, the best of any major equity bucket.

### Industrial metals, especially copper — the real economy's pulse

The fourth leader is industrial metals, and copper above all. We give copper a full treatment in [metals: copper, silver, and the economy's pulse](/blog/trading/cross-asset/metals-copper-silver-the-economys-pulse), where it earns its trader's nickname "Dr. Copper" — the metal with a PhD in economics, because its price reads the real economy so well.

Copper is wired into the physical economy: it's in every building, every motor, every power line, every electronic device. When the real economy restarts — factories reopening, construction resuming, inventories being rebuilt — demand for copper inflects up hard, and because mining supply is slow to respond, the price moves violently. In 2009, copper returned roughly +140%, more than doubling. In the 2020 recovery it again roughly doubled off its March low. Copper is the cleanest commodity expression of the early-cycle thesis: the real economy is restarting, and copper is its pulse.

There's a second, subtler force at work in commodities during the recovery: *inventory restocking*. When businesses brace for a recession, they slash inventories to conserve cash — they stop ordering raw materials and run down whatever they have on the shelf. This makes the real demand for a metal like copper *fall faster than actual consumption*, because the supply chain is de-stocking on top of the underlying slowdown. Then, when the recovery begins, the process reverses with a vengeance: businesses have to both meet returning demand *and* rebuild the inventories they let run dry, so orders surge well above the level of end-consumption. That restocking surge is why the first leg of a commodity recovery is so sharp — the order flow temporarily overshoots, and prices overshoot with it. It's the same operating-leverage idea we saw in cyclical earnings, applied to the physical supply chain.

#### Worked example: why copper outruns the stock market in the rebound

You split \$20,000 evenly at the 2009 trough: \$10,000 into a broad stock index and \$10,000 into copper (via a miner or a metal fund). Both are "the recovery trade," but they're geared very differently.

- The \$10,000 in stocks rides the S&P's +26.5% → +\$2,650, leaving \$12,650.
- The \$10,000 in copper rides its ~+140% → +\$14,000, leaving \$24,000.

The copper leg made more than five times the dollar gain of the stock leg off the same \$10,000. The reason isn't that copper is "better" — it's that copper sits at the most operationally levered, most de-stocked point in the chain, so its demand inflection off the trough is the most violent. The one-sentence intuition: the further down the real-economy supply chain you go, the bigger the swing — copper amplifies the same recovery that lifts stocks, which is exactly why it leads and exactly why it's not for the faint of heart.

### What lags: government bonds and cash

The mirror image of all this is what *underperforms* in early cycle: safe assets. Government bonds — especially long-dated ones — and cash are the laggards, and that's not an accident, it's mechanical.

Long-dated government bonds are the great winners of a recession: when the economy collapses and the Fed cuts rates and investors flee to safety, long bond prices soar. But that's exactly what makes them losers in the recovery. As growth returns and long-term yields rise off their crisis lows (we'll see why with the yield-curve chart), bond prices *fall* — because bond prices move opposite to yields. In 2009, long Treasuries lost -14.9% while everything risky soared. The very asset that protected you in 2008 cost you money in 2009.

Cash is the other laggard. In early cycle the Fed has cut rates to near zero, so cash earns almost nothing, and meanwhile risk assets are compounding at double digits. Holding cash through a recovery is one of the most expensive forms of comfort there is — you feel safe while the rebound passes you by. Cash is a genuinely useful asset in other phases, but the recovery is the one phase where it is unambiguously the wrong place to be.

### The leaders at a glance

It's worth laying the whole leadership picture side by side, because the *order* is the lesson. The table below ranks the major buckets by their 2009 return and names the engine behind each:

| Asset | 2009 total return | Why it leads (or lags) the early cycle |
|---|---|---|
| Copper / industrial metals | ~+140% | The real economy restarts; slow mining supply means violent price moves |
| Emerging-market equities | +78.5% | Highest-beta equity, plus a weak-dollar and commodity tailwind |
| High-yield credit | +58.2% | Spread compression off crisis-wide levels, plus the fat coupon |
| Small-caps (Russell 2000) | +27.2% | High operating leverage and domestic, cyclical exposure |
| US large-cap (S&P 500) | +26.5% | The broad market re-rates as profits inflect off the trough |
| US bonds (Agg) | +5.9% | Mostly high-grade; modest coupon, little spread to compress |
| Long Treasuries | -14.9% | The recession's hero; long yields rise off the lows, so prices fall |

Read top to bottom and you're reading the risk spectrum from most aggressive to most defensive — and in early cycle, that is exactly the order of returns, with the safest asset actually losing money. No other phase of the cycle produces this clean a sort. In a *late*-cycle slowdown the order inverts (defensives and bonds win, cyclicals lose); in early cycle it lines up like this, which is why getting the phase right matters more than getting any single security right.

## The mechanism: why the rebound is so big and so concentrated

We've seen *what* leads. Now let's nail down *why* the returns are this large and this concentrated in the riskiest assets. The early-cycle rebound is so powerful because three independent sources of return fire at the same time, and they all fire hardest in the assets that fell furthest.

### Source 1: multiple expansion off depressed valuations

A stock's price can be written, very roughly, as its earnings times a *multiple* — the price-to-earnings (P/E) ratio the market is willing to pay. At a recession trough, the multiple is crushed: terrified investors won't pay much for a dollar of earnings when the future looks apocalyptic. As the early cycle unfolds and confidence returns, that multiple *expands* back toward normal. If a market trades at 10x earnings at the trough and re-rates to 16x as the recovery takes hold, that's a +60% gain from the multiple alone, before earnings even grow. The lower the starting multiple, the bigger the snap-back — which is why the most beaten-down, most hated assets expand the most.

### Source 2: spread compression off crisis-wide levels

The same logic applies to credit. The credit spread is, in effect, the multiple of the bond world — the risk premium the market demands. At the trough it's blown out to a crisis-wide level; in recovery it compresses back toward normal, and that compression is a price gain for whoever owned the bonds. This is the cleanest, most quantifiable early-cycle engine, so let's look at it directly:

![High-yield spread compression line chart from 2000 bps in 2008 and 1100 in 2020 down to 350-400 bps in recovery](/imgs/blogs/early-cycle-recovery-what-leads-the-rebound-3.png)

Look at the two great spikes and what follows each. In December 2008, the high-yield spread peaked near 2,000 bps — a level that priced in a catastrophic default wave. By mid-2009 it was already rolling down through 900 bps, and it kept compressing into the recovery toward the 350-400 bps band. In March 2020 the spread hit ~1,100 bps; by year-end it had compressed all the way back to ~360 bps. Each of those collapses *is* the +58%-style return on high yield, because every basis point of spread compression on a portfolio of bonds is a price gain on top of the coupon you're collecting.

#### Worked example: what spread compression does to a junk-bond position

You buy \$10,000 of high-yield bonds at the March 2020 trough. At that moment the spread is ~1,100 bps over Treasuries, and the bonds have an average *duration* of about 4 (duration is the rough percentage price change for a 1% move in yield). Over the rest of the year, the spread compresses from 1,100 bps back to 360 bps — a tightening of 740 bps, or 7.4 percentage points.

- Price gain from spread compression ≈ duration × spread tightening = 4 × 7.4% = +29.6%. On your \$10,000, that's about +\$2,960 of price appreciation.
- On top of that, you've been collecting a fat coupon. At the trough these bonds yielded well into the double digits; call it ~11% annualized, so roughly +\$1,100 over the year on \$10,000 (and more if you bought at a discount).
- Total: roughly +\$2,960 + \$1,100 ≈ +\$4,060 on \$10,000 — a return north of +40% in well under a year, even before counting that very few of the feared defaults actually arrived.

The one-sentence intuition: in a recovery, the spread doesn't just stop hurting you — its collapse becomes the single biggest driver of your return.

### Source 3: earnings recovery off a crushed base

The third engine is the actual recovery in profits. Earnings get destroyed in a recession, so they're starting from a low base. As the economy restarts, earnings grow — and growth off a crushed base produces enormous percentage gains. A company whose profit fell from \$100 to \$40 in the recession and recovers to \$90 has grown earnings +125% from the trough, even though it hasn't yet matched its old peak. High-beta, cyclical companies have the most operating leverage — their profits swing the most with revenue — so their earnings inflections are the most violent.

### Why the three stack

Here is the crucial point: these three engines don't add, they *compound*, and they compound hardest in the same assets. A cyclical company at the trough has a crushed multiple *and* crushed earnings *and*, if it has debt, a blown-out credit spread. As the recovery unfolds, all three reverse at once — the multiple expands, the earnings inflect, and the spread compresses — and the stock or bond captures the product of all three. That compounding is why the early-cycle returns in the high-beta corners are not 15% or 20% but 50%, 80%, 140%. The reward is enormous because three depressed forces release together, and you only get that alignment at the trough.

#### Worked example: the same trade in the calm middle of a cycle

To feel why early cycle is special, run the same high-yield trade in a boring mid-cycle year instead of at the trough. You buy \$10,000 of the same kind of high-yield bonds in 2014, when the spread is already sitting around 380 bps — near its long-run normal.

- There's almost no spread to compress. Over the year the spread drifts from ~380 to ~370 bps, a 10 bps move; price gain ≈ 4 × 0.10% = +0.4%, or about +\$40.
- You still collect the coupon, but it's now modest — say ~5.5% — so about +\$550.
- Total: roughly +\$590 on \$10,000, around +6%.

Same asset, same strategy, but +6% instead of +40%, purely because the spread had nowhere left to fall. The one-sentence intuition: the entire outsized early-cycle return comes from the *starting point* — wide spreads and crushed prices — not from the asset being special, which is why catching the trough is the whole game.

## Reading the trough: the signals that actually matter

Everything above assumes you can recognize the early cycle while it's happening. That's hard, because the trough is the moment the news is darkest — you cannot use the headlines, because the headlines are a *contrarian* indicator at the bottom. Instead, you read a handful of forward-looking, mechanical signals. None is perfect alone; together they form a mosaic. Here are the four that matter most.

### Signal 1: credit spreads peaking and rolling in

The single best early-cycle signal is the one we just charted: the high-yield credit spread. Spreads are forward-looking and they trade in real time, all day, every day. When the spread *stops widening, peaks, and starts to compress*, it is telling you that the credit market — the part of the market closest to actual corporate solvency — has decided the worst is priced in. Credit very often turns *before* equities at the bottom, because credit investors are obsessed with downside and are the first to sense when the default panic has overshot. A spread that has clearly peaked and is rolling down is the closest thing the market offers to a "the trough is in" bell.

### Signal 2: the yield curve steepening

The *yield curve* plots the interest rate (yield) on government bonds across different maturities — from 3-month bills out to 30-year bonds. Its *shape* is one of the most reliable cycle signals there is. Let's look at how it changes from the late-cycle peak to the early-cycle recovery:

![Yield curve diagram comparing a flat or inverted late-cycle curve with a steep early-cycle curve as short rates stay pinned and long yields rise](/imgs/blogs/early-cycle-recovery-what-leads-the-rebound-4.png)

Late in the cycle, the curve is typically *flat* or *inverted* — short-term yields as high as or higher than long-term yields — because the central bank has hiked short rates to fight inflation while the bond market sees a slowdown coming. That inversion is a famous recession warning. Then, as the recession hits, the central bank slashes the short-term rate, *pinning the front of the curve near zero*. Meanwhile, as the recovery begins, long-term yields *rise* off their crisis lows because the bond market starts to price in growth and an eventual return of inflation. Short end pinned + long end rising = a *steepening* curve. A steep curve is one of the cleanest early-cycle tells. It's also exactly why long bonds lag in the recovery (those rising long yields are falling bond prices) and why banks lead (they profit from the gap between cheap short funding and higher long lending rates).

### Signal 3: PMIs turning up

A *PMI* (Purchasing Managers' Index) is a monthly survey of business managers asking whether activity — new orders, production, hiring — is expanding or contracting. It's reported as a number around 50: above 50 means expansion, below 50 means contraction. Crucially, what markets watch is not just the level but the *direction*. A PMI that has bottomed and turned up — even if it's still below 50, even if the economy is still technically contracting — is the "less-bad" inflection that prices respond to. PMIs are timely (monthly, released fast) and forward-looking (they ask about orders, which lead production), which makes a turning PMI a core early-cycle confirmation.

### Signal 4: unemployment claims peaking

The job market heals last, so the unemployment *rate* is a lagging indicator — useless for catching the trough. But *initial jobless claims* — the weekly count of people newly filing for unemployment benefits — are far more timely and they *lead*. When weekly claims stop rising, peak, and start to fall, it signals that the bleeding in the labor market is slowing even though the unemployment rate is still high or even still climbing. A clear peak-and-roll in initial claims is one of the most reliable signs the recession is ending.

### The signal mosaic in one table

No single signal is reliable enough to bet the farm on — each fires false alarms — but they fail *independently*, so when several confirm at once the odds shift sharply. The table summarizes what to watch and why each one leads:

| Signal | What it measures | Early-cycle reading | Why it leads |
|---|---|---|---|
| HY credit spread | Priced-in fear of corporate default | Has peaked and is compressing | Credit investors sense overshoot first; trades in real time |
| Yield-curve slope | Short rates vs long rates | Steepening (front pinned, back rising) | Reflects easy policy now plus growth being priced ahead |
| PMI (direction) | Business managers on orders/output | Bottomed and turning up, even if below 50 | Surveys orders, which lead actual production |
| Initial jobless claims | Weekly new unemployment filings | Peaked and rolling down | Far timelier than the unemployment rate; leads the turn |

The skill is not in any one row; it's in waiting for the rows to *agree*. A steepening curve alone might just be a technical move in the bond market; PMIs turning alone might be a one-month blip. But a steepening curve *and* spreads rolling in *and* PMIs turning *and* claims peaking, all in the same few weeks, is a configuration that has marked the start of essentially every modern recovery. That is when the mosaic stops being noise and starts being a signal.

Put the mosaic together: **spreads peaking and rolling in, the curve steepening, PMIs turning up, and claims peaking.** When several of these align, you are very likely standing at or near the trough — regardless of what the headlines say. That divergence between the signals and the news is the whole opportunity. The early-cycle turn is the single cleanest, most durable "risk-on" signal the cycle produces, because it's backed by a genuine improvement in the real economy rather than a fleeting mood swing — which is exactly why the high-beta corners can run for months once it confirms.

## Common misconceptions

The early cycle is where the most expensive misunderstandings live, because acting correctly requires doing the opposite of what feels safe. Here are the beliefs that cost people the rebound, each corrected with a number.

**"Wait for the economy to look healthy before buying."** This is the single most costly mistake. The economy looks healthy *after* the rebound, not during it. In 2009 the S&P bottomed on March 9, while the unemployment rate kept rising until October — seven months *later* — and didn't peak until it hit 10%. An investor who waited for unemployment to fall missed the entire +26.5% S&P year (and the +58% in high yield, the +79% in EM). The signals lead; the economy lags; the headlines lag most of all.

**"The safest assets are the best place to be in a recovery."** The opposite is true. The assets that protect you in the recession — long Treasuries, cash, gold once the panic passes — are precisely the laggards of the recovery. In 2009, long Treasuries *lost* -14.9% and cash earned almost nothing while junk bonds gained +58%. Safety has a cost, and in early cycle that cost is the entire rebound.

**"High yield is too risky to buy when defaults are spiking."** This confuses the *level* of defaults with the *direction* of the spread. The right time to buy high yield is exactly when defaults are spiking *and* the spread has blown out to price in even more defaults than will actually occur. In 2009 the high-yield default rate peaked near 13% — and high yield still returned +58%, because the ~2,000 bps spread had priced in something even worse than a 13% default wave. You get paid for buying when the priced-in fear exceeds the realized damage.

**"Emerging markets are too unstable to own in a crisis aftermath."** EM is volatile, but its very sensitivity is what makes it the best early-cycle equity. The same beta that crushes EM in a downturn drives it in a recovery: +78.5% in 2009. Avoiding EM because it's volatile means avoiding precisely the asset whose volatility pays you most in this one phase.

**"I'll buy when the all-clear is sounded."** There is no all-clear. The market never rings a bell at the bottom; the bell is rung, if at all, by the signals (spreads, curve, PMIs, claims) while the mood is still grim. By the time a pundit declares the recovery underway, the easy money — the multiple expansion and spread compression off the trough — has already been made. The early cycle pays the early, by definition.

## How it shows up in real markets

The early-cycle pattern is not a theory; it has played out the same way in every modern recovery. Let's walk the three clearest episodes.

### 2009 — the textbook

This is the cleanest early cycle in living memory, because the recession was so deep and the policy response so large that every spring was coiled to the extreme. The S&P 500 bottomed on March 9, 2009, down more than 50% from its 2007 peak. At that moment the Fed had cut its policy rate to essentially zero and launched its first round of large-scale bond buying (*quantitative easing* — the central bank creating money to buy bonds and force interest rates down across the curve). The high-yield spread had blown out toward 2,000 bps. Every signal was screaming catastrophe.

Then the data inflected. Credit spreads peaked and rolled. The yield curve, already steepening, stayed steep. PMIs turned up from deeply contractionary levels. And the assets snapped back in textbook order: copper ~+140%, EM +78.5%, high yield +58.2%, small-caps +27.2%, the S&P +26.5%, high-grade bonds a modest +5.9%, and long Treasuries *down* -14.9%. The ordering — most beaten-down first, safest last — is the early cycle's fingerprint, and 2009 drew it as clearly as it has ever been drawn.

### 2020 second half — the fastest cycle ever

The COVID crash and recovery compressed an entire business cycle into a single year, which makes it a beautiful natural experiment. The S&P collapsed -33.9% from its February 19 peak to its March 23 trough in just over a month — the fastest bear market in history. At the bottom, the high-yield spread spiked to ~1,100 bps, and for a few days you could buy junk bonds yielding double digits.

Then the policy response — the Fed cutting to zero, buying bonds, and for the first time ever announcing it would buy corporate bonds directly — flipped the switch. From the March 23 low, the S&P rallied roughly +60% into year-end. Copper roughly doubled off its March low. The high-yield spread compressed from ~1,100 bps back toward ~360 bps within months, handing credit investors equity-like returns. It was the entire early-cycle script run at triple speed: spreads peaking and rolling, the most beaten-down assets leading, government bonds lagging the risk rally. The lesson of 2020 is that the early cycle can be ferociously fast — the window to lean in was weeks, not months.

### 2003 — the slow-burn recovery

Not every early cycle is a March-2009-style V. The recovery from the 2001 recession (the dot-com bust plus the shock of 9/11) was slower and messier — a so-called "jobless recovery" where output recovered but hiring stayed weak for a long time. The equity market actually made a final low in March 2003, well after the recession had technically ended, partly on the uncertainty around the Iraq war. But once that low was in, the same early-cycle pattern asserted itself: the high-beta corners led, cyclicals beat defensives, credit spreads compressed, and small-caps and emerging markets outran large-cap defensive stocks through the 2003-2004 recovery.

The 2003 episode teaches a different lesson than 2009 or 2020: the trough can come *later* than the official end of the recession, and it can arrive with a final, frightening washout. The signals still worked — spreads, the curve, the PMI turn — but the timing required patience and a tolerance for false dawns. Which brings us to the risks.

## The risk: being too early, and the false dawn

Everything in this post points at one action — lean into the rebound — so honesty requires being equally clear about how that action goes wrong. There are two main ways.

**Being too early.** The trough is genuinely hard to catch. Markets can fall a long way and then fall further; an asset down 50% can fall another 50% from there. If you buy what looks like the bottom and it isn't, you suffer real, painful losses before the rebound arrives, and you may not have the capital or the nerve left to participate when it finally does. "Cheap" is not the same as "done falling." The signals (spreads peaking, claims rolling, PMIs turning) exist precisely to keep you from buying on price alone, but even they are noisy and can give false readings.

**The false dawn.** In a bear market, sharp, convincing rallies happen *before* the real bottom — bounces of 10%, 20%, even more, that suck people back in and then roll over to new lows. These "bear-market rallies" are the cruelest part of the trough, because they look exactly like the start of the recovery and feel like the all-clear. The 2008-2009 bear had several violent rallies before the March 2009 low. Distinguishing the real turn from a false dawn in real time is the single hardest judgment in this phase, and nobody does it perfectly.

The practical response to both risks is not to abandon the thesis but to *manage the entry*: scale in rather than going all-in at a single price, demand confirmation from multiple signals rather than acting on price alone, and favor the slightly-less-aggressive expressions (high yield and quality cyclicals) over the most aggressive (distressed CCC-rated debt, the most leveraged small-caps) until the trough is more clearly confirmed. You're trying to be early enough to catch the rebound but disciplined enough to survive being wrong about the exact day.

There's a useful asymmetry that makes scaling in less costly than it feels. Because the early-cycle rebound runs for *months*, not days, you do not need to nail the single low to capture most of the return. Buying in thirds — a third when the signals first start to align, a third when they confirm, a third on the first pullback — gives up only the small slice of return between your average entry and the exact bottom, while dramatically reducing the risk of dumping all your capital into a false dawn that then makes new lows. The math is forgiving: if the rebound is a +50% move and your averaged-in entry is 8% above the absolute low, you still capture roughly +39% — you've sacrificed a fraction of the upside to buy a large reduction in the risk of ruin. Going all-in at a single price only looks smart in hindsight, once you know which dip was the last one; in real time, when several dips look identical, averaging in is how you stay solvent enough to be right.

The deeper point is psychological. The early cycle is the one phase where the correct action and the comfortable action are maximally opposed. Every instinct at the trough says *wait, protect, stay in cash* — and every instinct is wrong. The investors who capture this phase are not the ones with better forecasts; they're the ones who built a rule-based plan (these signals, this sizing, this rotation) *before* the panic, so that when the moment comes they can act on the signals instead of the fear. That pre-commitment is the real edge in early cycle, because the analysis is the easy part and the acting is the hard part.

## When to own it / the early-cycle allocation playbook

This is the payoff. You understand the setup, the leaders, the mechanism, the signals, and the risks. Now: what do you actually *do*? Here is the early-cycle playbook — concrete tilts, rough sizing logic, and what invalidates the trade. (Educational framework, not personal advice; the right answer for any real portfolio depends on circumstances this post can't see.)

The core move is a rotation along the risk spectrum: out of the assets that won the recession and into the assets that lead the recovery. The picture below is the two-sided rotation in one frame.

![Two column comparison of what to underweight versus overweight in early cycle](/imgs/blogs/early-cycle-recovery-what-leads-the-rebound-5.png)

### What to overweight

- **Cyclical and small-cap equities** — consumer discretionary, financials, industrials, and small-caps (Russell 2000). This is the highest-conviction early-cycle equity tilt: the high-operating-leverage, high-beta corners that capture the multiple expansion and earnings inflection most violently.
- **High-yield credit** — to harvest the spread compression off crisis-wide levels. The cleanest, most quantifiable early-cycle engine, and one that often turns *before* equities.
- **Emerging-market equities** — the highest-beta equity bucket, with a weak-dollar tailwind layered on top.
- **Industrial metals, especially copper** (and the miners that produce them) — the purest commodity expression of the real economy restarting.

### What to underweight

- **Cash** — it earns nothing while everything else compounds at double digits; the most expensive comfort in this phase.
- **Long-duration government bonds** — the recession's hero becomes the recovery's laggard as long yields rise and prices fall. In 2009 long Treasuries lost -14.9%.
- **Defensive equities** — staples, utilities, low-beta names. They protected you on the way down; they'll hold you back on the way up.

### How much, and how to decide

The honest sizing question isn't really "how much cyclical risk should I own" — it's "how confident am I that the trough is in, and how much has the market already moved?" The matrix below frames the decision along exactly those two axes:

![Early-cycle playbook matrix mapping trough signal confirmation against how much risk to add](/imgs/blogs/early-cycle-recovery-what-leads-the-rebound-7.png)

Read it like this. The **top-right cell is the fat pitch**: risk assets are still cheap and hated *and* the trough signals (claims peaking, spreads rolling, PMIs turning) are confirming. That's when you add cyclicals, small-caps, high yield, EM, and copper aggressively — it's the highest-return setup in the entire cycle, the 2009 / 2020-H2 / 2003 configuration. The **top-left cell is the false-dawn zone**: things are cheap but nothing has confirmed yet, so you scale in slowly and favor the less-aggressive expressions (high yield, quality cyclicals) over the most-aggressive (distressed debt, the most leveraged names). The **bottom-left** is the trap of chasing a rally that's run on hope with no confirmation — wait. And the **bottom-right** is the natural exit: the trough is confirmed but risk assets have already re-rated, which means early cycle is maturing and it's time to rotate toward [the steadier mid-cycle expansion](/blog/trading/cross-asset/mid-cycle-expansion-the-steady-grind), trimming the deepest beta and rotating into broader, higher-quality equities.

#### Worked example: the early-cycle tilt vs the index in 2009

Let's put real money on the 2009 setup and compare leaning into the beaten-down corners against simply owning the index. You have \$100,000 to deploy at the March 2009 trough.

First, the **early-cycle tilt**, spread across the four leaders:

- \$40,000 in cyclical and small-cap equities, which returned about +30% on the year → +\$12,000.
- \$30,000 in high yield, +58% → +\$17,400.
- \$20,000 in emerging markets, +79% → +\$15,800.
- \$10,000 in copper / miners, +140% → +\$14,000.

Total gain: \$12,000 + \$17,400 + \$15,800 + \$14,000 = **+\$59,200**, a +59.2% return, leaving you with about \$159,200.

Now the **all-index alternative**: the same \$100,000 entirely in the S&P 500, which returned +26.5% → +\$26,500, leaving you with \$126,500.

The tilt earned roughly **double** the index: +\$59,200 versus +\$26,500, a difference of about +\$32,700 on \$100,000. That is the reward for leaning into the high-beta corners at the trough instead of settling for the broad market. The one-sentence intuition: in early cycle the index is good, but the beaten-down, high-beta corners are roughly twice as good — *provided* you can stomach buying them when the headlines are at their absolute worst.

#### Worked example: the cost of waiting six months for the "all-clear"

Now price the most common mistake: waiting until it feels safe. Suppose that instead of buying at the March 2009 trough, you wait until September 2009 for confirmation — by which point the S&P has already rallied roughly +50% off its low and the high-yield spread has compressed from ~2,000 bps toward ~600 bps.

- By waiting, you've missed the first ~+50% of the equity move; entering in September, you capture only the remainder of the year's rally — a much smaller slice, and at far higher prices.
- In high yield, the bulk of the spread compression — from 2,000 bps down to ~600 bps — has already happened. The compression *left* to harvest is from ~600 to ~400 bps, perhaps a +8-10% price move on a duration-4 bond, versus the +40%+ available at the trough.
- On a \$100,000 high-yield allocation, that's the difference between roughly +\$40,000 (buying at the trough) and perhaps +\$8,000-\$10,000 (buying after the all-clear) — most of the return gone, simply for waiting until it felt comfortable.

The one-sentence intuition: in early cycle the return is front-loaded into the period when it's most uncomfortable to buy, so the cost of waiting for the all-clear is most of the rebound itself.

### What invalidates the early-cycle thesis

A thesis you can't invalidate is a religion, not a plan. The early-cycle setup is wrong, or over, when:

- **The signals re-deteriorate.** If credit spreads, after rolling in, blow back out to new highs; if the curve re-flattens; if PMIs roll back over; if jobless claims make a new high — then the "trough" was a false dawn, the recession is deepening, and the high-beta tilt is exactly the wrong place to be. This is your stop.
- **Inflation forces the central bank to reverse.** The early cycle depends on the Fed being easy. If inflation surges and forces the Fed to start *hiking* much sooner than usual, the discount rate stops falling and starts rising, kicking out one of the four legs of the rebound. (This is the bridge to the next phase, and why early cycle eventually gives way to mid-cycle and then to late cycle.)
- **The re-rating is done.** The cleaner way the thesis "ends" is success: spreads have compressed back to normal, valuations have re-rated, the easy money is made. That's not an invalidation, it's a graduation — the cue to rotate from the deepest-beta early-cycle trades toward the steadier compounding of the mid-cycle expansion.

The early cycle, then, is a phase you want to recognize, lean into hard, and then knowingly leave behind. It is the rarest and richest setup the business cycle offers: the moment when cheap valuations, a near-zero discount rate, inflecting earnings, and washed-out sentiment all release at once, and the assets everyone hates most become the assets that pay most. It comes with a recession attached, which is why so few investors capture it — the price of admission is buying when the news is darkest. But for the investor who learns to read the trough from the signals rather than the headlines, no phase rewards being early as richly as this one.
