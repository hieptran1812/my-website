> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Most macro correlations you will see are partly or wholly fake, and the single most dangerous one is the correlation that *looks* like nothing. Full-sample correlation between the 10-year real yield and gold is -0.01 — apparently no relationship — but that "0" is hiding a -0.96 in 2007-2021 and a +0.80 in 2022-2025. The relationship was never absent; it flipped, and the average buried the flip.
>
> - Six traps manufacture fake correlations: spurious trend, non-stationarity, the third variable, multiple testing, sample dependence, and overfitting / look-ahead. Each one has a specific fix.
> - The first reflex that saves you: correlate **changes** (returns, first differences), not **levels**. Two series that merely trend upward will show a high level correlation that means nothing.
> - Test 100 useless indicators at the 5% level and about 5 will look "significant" by pure chance. The defence is a Bonferroni-style cut: use 0.05 / 100, not 0.05.
> - The one number to remember: a sample correlation of r = 0.40 on only n = 24 observations barely clears the 5% noise threshold — and dies the moment you add a single control. Small samples make noise look like signal.

In late 2022 a strategist I know was certain he had found the trade of the decade. He had pulled twenty years of monthly data, run gold against the 10-year real yield, and the scatter was a beautiful downward line: real yields up, gold down, correlation around -0.8. The mechanism was textbook — gold pays no interest, so when the "real" return on a safe inflation-protected bond rises, the opportunity cost of holding a lump of metal rises with it, and gold should fall. He sized up a short-gold position into rising real yields. Real yields kept rising through 2023 and 2024. Gold went from \$1,800 to \$2,650.

The correlation had not vanished. It had *flipped*. Through 2007-2021 the relationship was as clean as anything in macro, around -0.96. Then central banks started buying gold by the hundreds of tonnes, a war and a sanctions regime made dollar reserves look politically risky, and gold began rising *with* real yields. Over 2022-2025 the correlation was about +0.80. If you computed the correlation over the whole 2007-2025 sample, you got -0.01 — a number that says "gold and real yields have nothing to do with each other," which is the single most misleading thing you could possibly conclude.

This post is the skeptic's chapter of the series, and it is the one I would make you read first if I could. Every other post in this series teaches you a real, useful correlation. This one teaches you why most of the correlations you will compute yourself, or read in a research note, or see on a chart someone tweeted, are partly or wholly fake — and gives you a checklist to catch each kind of fake before it costs you money. Six traps stand between a correlation you see and a relationship you can trust. Let us walk all six, from zero, with a number for each.

## Foundations: what a correlation actually is, and why it is so easy to fake

Before we can break correlations, we have to define them. A **correlation** is a single number, written r (the symbol mathematicians use for the Pearson correlation coefficient), that measures how tightly two quantities move together in a straight-line sense. It runs from -1 to +1. An r of +1 means the two move in perfect lockstep: every time one goes up by some amount, the other goes up by a proportional amount, exactly, with no scatter. An r of -1 means perfect *opposite* lockstep. An r of 0 means no straight-line relationship at all — knowing one tells you nothing, on average, about the other.

Here is the everyday version. Suppose you track, for thirty days, how many umbrellas a shop sells and how many cups of hot coffee a nearby cafe sells. On rainy days both go up; on sunny days both go down. Plot one against the other and you get an upward cloud of dots — a positive correlation, maybe r = 0.7. But the umbrellas do not *cause* the coffee. A third thing — the weather — drives both. That is the entire problem of this post, in miniature: a correlation is a description of co-movement, and co-movement has many possible sources, only one of which is "A genuinely moves B."

The arithmetic, so you know exactly what the number is made of: take two series of numbers, x and y (say, monthly changes in the real yield and monthly returns on gold). Compute each series' average. For each month, take how far x is from its average, multiply by how far y is from its average, and add up all those products. That sum is the **covariance** — it is positive when x and y tend to be above (or below) their averages together, negative when one is high while the other is low. Then divide by the spread of each series (the standard deviations) so the answer is unit-free and lands between -1 and +1. That division by the spreads is what makes r a *pure* number you can compare across gold, bonds, and FX. (If you want this built fully from scratch — Pearson, Spearman, and how beta differs — read [what correlation actually measures](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/what-correlation-actually-measures-pearson-spearman-beta), the companion foundations post.)

Now, the crucial part for a skeptic. Look at that recipe again. It is purely mechanical. You feed it *any* two columns of numbers and it returns a number between -1 and +1. It has no idea whether the two columns are returns or prices, whether they trend, whether you cherry-picked the sample, whether you tested a hundred other columns first, or whether any economic mechanism connects them. **The formula will happily hand you a confident-looking 0.85 for two series that have nothing to do with each other.** A correlation coefficient is not evidence of a relationship; it is a *measurement that is only meaningful if you have already ruled out the ways it can be manufactured*. Ruling those out is the whole job.

A famous illustration: over the 2000s, the per-capita consumption of margarine in the United States correlated about 0.99 with the divorce rate in Maine. Nobody thinks margarine ends marriages. Both series happened to drift downward over the same decade, and the formula rewarded that shared drift with a near-perfect r. That is trap number one, and it is the most common one in all of macro.

It is worth pausing on *why* this matters so much more in finance than in, say, a physics lab. A physicist measuring how a spring stretches under load can repeat the experiment a thousand times, control every other variable, and watch the relationship reveal itself with overwhelming evidence. A macro analyst cannot. There has been exactly one history. You cannot rerun 2022 with the Fed hiking more slowly to see what gold "would have" done; you get the single path the world actually took, with every variable moving at once and nothing held constant. This is the deep reason macro correlations are so treacherous: the data is observational, not experimental, the sample is short, the variables are tangled, and the relationships themselves change over time. Every trap in this post is a different way that observational, non-stationary, short, entangled data manufactures the *appearance* of a relationship. The skeptic's toolkit is what substitutes for the controlled experiment you can never run.

There is one more foundation to plant before we start, because it underlies almost every trap: the difference between a series' **level** and its **change**. The *level* of gold is its price: \$1,800, \$1,943, \$2,390. The *change* is how much it moved from one period to the next: +\$143, then +\$447. Levels of financial and macro series almost always **trend** — they drift persistently up (prices, money supply, GDP) or down over years. Changes do not trend the same way; they wobble around zero. This distinction is the hinge on which the first three traps turn, so hold onto it: **correlate changes, not levels** is the single most valuable habit in this entire post.

## Trap 1: spurious correlation from a shared trend

The first trap is the one that catches everyone, including professionals, because it produces correlations that look not just real but *strong* — 0.9 and up. It is called **spurious correlation**, and the mechanism is brutally simple: any two series that both trend in the same direction over the same window will correlate highly, by construction, whether or not they have anything to do with each other.

Think about why. The correlation formula measures whether the two series are *above their own averages at the same times*. If a series trends steadily upward, then it is below its average for the whole first half of the sample and above its average for the whole second half. Two upward-trending series therefore agree, mechanically, on a simple schedule: both "low" early, both "high" late. The formula sees that agreement and returns a high positive r. It cannot tell the difference between "these two genuinely move together period to period" and "these two both happen to grow over time." A shared trend is a free correlation.

This is why the divorce-rate-and-margarine and the umbrella-and-coffee examples work, and it is why so much macro analysis is quietly broken. The US money supply trends up. Gold trends up. Stock indices trend up. Nominal GDP trends up. House prices trend up. Pick any two of them, correlate the *levels*, and you will get a big number. It tells you almost nothing, because they would all correlate with a series of the integers 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 just as well — anything that goes up correlates with anything else that goes up.

The fix is the habit I planted above: **correlate the changes, not the levels.** When you take first differences (this period's value minus last period's) or returns (percentage changes), you strip out the trend. What is left is the period-to-period co-movement, which is the thing you actually care about: when the real yield jumped *this* month, did gold fall *this* month? A correlation on changes can only be high if the two series genuinely move together in real time — the shared-trend free lunch is gone.

![The spurious-trend trap: a high level correlation collapses on changes](/imgs/blogs/spurious-correlation-and-the-traps-of-macro-data-3.png)

The figure above makes the trap concrete with two real series that genuinely both trend up over recent years: the US M2 money stock and the annual gold price. On the left, plotted as levels, they rise more or less together — the level correlation is strongly positive and looks like a relationship. On the right, the year-over-year *changes* in the two scatter all over the place, and the correlation collapses toward something weak and noisy. The level number was mostly a shared upward drift; the changes show how little reliable period-to-period co-movement there actually is. Same two series, two completely different stories, and only the right-hand one is honest.

#### Worked example: a spurious level correlation that vanishes on changes

Suppose I tell you that over 2019-2024, the US money supply (M2) and the price of gold had a correlation of about +0.85 in *levels*. M2 went from roughly \$15.4tn in 2019 to about \$21.6tn in 2024; gold went from about \$1,393/oz to about \$2,390/oz. Both climbed, so the level r is high. A naive reader concludes: "Money printing drives gold — strong relationship, I should buy gold when M2 rises."

Now take year-over-year *changes*. The single biggest jump in M2 was 2019 to 2020 (about +\$3.7tn, from pandemic-era stimulus). What did gold do that year? It rose from about \$1,393 to \$1,770, roughly +\$377 — a solid year. But 2021 to 2022, M2 was essentially *flat* (about -\$0.1tn as the Fed tightened) while gold *also* rose modestly. And 2022 to 2023, gold jumped +\$141 with M2 falling. The change-on-change correlation is weak and noisy — nothing like the headline +0.85. The level correlation was almost entirely the two series sharing one upward decade-long drift. **The lesson in one line: if a correlation is computed on price levels rather than returns, treat it as fake until proven otherwise — the trend did the work, not the relationship.**

The practical rule that falls out of this: never trust a correlation, a scatter plot, or a regression that uses raw price *levels* of two trending series. The moment you see "S&P 500 level vs M2 level, r = 0.97," you should mentally discard it. The only correlations worth your attention in macro are computed on returns or changes — and even those have five more traps to clear.

## Trap 2: non-stationarity inflates the number

Trap 1's deeper cousin is **non-stationarity**, and understanding it is what separates someone who knows the "use changes" rule by rote from someone who knows *why* it works and when it is not enough.

A series is **stationary** if its statistical character does not change over time — roughly, its average stays put, its variability (how widely it swings) stays put, and the way it relates to its own past stays put. A series is **non-stationary** if any of those drift. Levels of macro series are almost always non-stationary: their average is not a fixed number you are wobbling around, it is a moving target that climbs (gold goes from a \$700-ish world to a \$2,600-ish world; its "average" is meaningless). The correlation formula assumes, implicitly, that each series has a stable average to measure deviations *from*. Feed it a non-stationary series and that assumption is violated, and the number it returns is contaminated by the drift rather than describing genuine co-movement.

Here is the subtle, important point that the simple "use changes" rule glosses over: taking first differences *usually* fixes non-stationarity, but not always, and not for every kind of drift. The most insidious form is when the *variance* drifts — when a series is calm for years and then violent. Correlations computed across a window that spans both a calm regime and a violent one get dominated by the violent part, because the violent observations are huge deviations from the average and the covariance sum is mostly made of them. Two assets can look almost uncorrelated for years and then, in a crisis, slam to a correlation near +1 (everything sells off together) — and a full-sample correlation that includes the crisis will report a misleadingly high number that describes the panic, not normal times. This is the heart of why [diversification fails in a crisis](/blog/trading/cross-asset/when-correlations-go-to-one-in-a-crisis): the correlation you measured in calm markets was real, but it was *regime-conditional*, and the regime changed.

There is a second, even sneakier route by which non-stationarity sneaks past the "use changes" defence: when *both* series share a slow-moving stochastic trend, differencing can leave a residual relationship that statisticians call *cointegration* — and the opposite failure, where two series each have a unit root but no real long-run link, produces what is literally named a *spurious regression* in the econometrics literature. Granger and Newbold's famous 1974 result showed that if you regress one random-walk series on another, completely independent random-walk series, you get a "significant" relationship with a high R-squared the overwhelming majority of the time — pure nonsense generated by two independent random walks. That is the formal, proven version of trap 1, and it is why econometricians insist on testing for unit roots before running any regression on macro levels. You do not need the full apparatus to be safe, but you should know it exists: the danger of correlating trending series is not a rule of thumb, it is a mathematical theorem.

So the rule upgrades from "use changes" to two rules: **(1) use changes to remove trend, and (2) check that the changes themselves are reasonably stationary** — that their swings are not wildly bigger in one part of your sample than another. If they are, your single correlation number is an average across regimes that may not describe any of them. The proper statistical tools here are the augmented Dickey-Fuller test for whether a series has a trend it needs differencing for, and simply splitting your sample and comparing the variance across sub-periods. The deeper machinery — unit roots, why differencing works, autocorrelation — is laid out in the math-for-quants treatment of [stationarity and autocorrelation](/blog/trading/math-for-quants/stationarity-autocorrelation-math-for-quants); for our purposes, the working knowledge is: drift inflates correlations, differencing usually but not always removes the drift, and a variance that explodes in part of the sample will hijack the whole number.

#### Worked example: how a variance shift inflates a correlation

Imagine two stocks whose *true* relationship is mild: in normal times their daily returns correlate about +0.2. Now take a 250-day sample where 230 days are normal (returns swinging about plus or minus 1%) and 20 days are a crisis (both stocks crashing together, returns swinging about plus or minus 6%, correlated about +0.9 during the panic because everyone is selling everything).

What does the full-sample correlation report? The 20 crisis days have returns roughly 6 times larger than normal days, so each crisis day contributes about 6 x 6 = 36 times as much to the covariance sum as a normal day. Twenty days at 36x weight = 720 "units" of crisis influence; 230 normal days at 1x = 230 units. The crisis, despite being 8% of the calendar, supplies about three-quarters of the covariance. The reported correlation lands far above the true 0.2 of normal times — maybe 0.6 — describing a panic that happens 8% of the time as if it were the everyday relationship. **The intuition: a few violent observations dominate a correlation, so a single full-sample number quietly becomes a number about the crisis, not about normal markets — and you only learn this by checking whether the variance is stable across your sample.**

## Trap 3: the third variable — the correlation is real but the cause is elsewhere

The first two traps produce correlations that are fake in the sense of being statistical artifacts. The third trap produces a correlation that is genuinely there in the data — the changes co-move, the series are stationary, everything checks out — and is *still* misleading, because the two things do not affect each other at all. A hidden third variable drives both, and your direct correlation is an illusion of a relationship that does not exist.

This is the umbrellas and coffee again, promoted to macro. The classic everyday version: ice cream sales correlate with drowning deaths. Both rise in summer; heat drives both; ice cream does not drown anyone. In macro, the "summer" is almost always one of a small handful of master variables — real yields, the dollar, liquidity, the business cycle — that move dozens of assets at once. When you correlate two assets that both load on the same master variable, you get a strong, real, completely uninterpretable correlation between them.

Concrete case. Gold and long-dated Treasury bonds are positively correlated. A naive read: "gold and bonds move together, so they are substitutes / they hedge the same thing." But why do they move together? Because both are priced heavily off **real yields**. When real yields fall, bond prices rise (bonds are worth more when the discount rate drops) and gold rises (lower opportunity cost of holding it). When real yields rise, both fall. The gold-bond correlation is not a relationship between gold and bonds; it is two separate relationships with real yields, showing up as an apparent link. If real yields were somehow held constant, the direct gold-bond correlation would largely evaporate. The whole edifice of cross-asset macro rests on this insight — that a handful of [master variables price everything](/blog/trading/cross-asset/real-yields-the-variable-that-prices-everything) — and missing it is how people build "diversified" portfolios that turn out to be the same bet six times.

The fix is **control for the common driver**: when you suspect a third variable, hold it constant and re-measure. Statistically this is a *partial correlation* (correlate the part of A that real yields do not explain with the part of B that real yields do not explain) or a multiple regression with the suspected driver included as a control. If the A-B correlation collapses once you control for real yields, you have learned that real yields were the actor and A-B was the shadow. The mechanics live in the [OLS regression](/blog/trading/math-for-quants/regression-ols-gls-regularized-math-for-quants) post; the discipline is: before you believe A moves B, ask "what third thing might move both?" and partial it out.

There is a darker version of this trap worth naming: the third variable does not have to be one you can see. Sometimes both A and B respond to *expectations* or *positioning* that you have not measured. The defensive habit is humility — a strong correlation between two assets is your cue to go hunting for what they have in common, not your cue to declare one the driver of the other.

This trap also explains a phenomenon that confuses many beginners: why a portfolio of "diversified" assets can crater all at once. If you own gold, long bonds, emerging-market equities, and growth stocks, you might think you hold four distinct bets. But look at the cross-asset correlation matrix and you will find that *all four* load heavily, in the same direction, on real yields — a real-yield spike hurts every one of them. You did not build a diversified portfolio; you built the same real-yield bet four times, wearing four costumes. The third-variable trap is not just an analytical curiosity; it is the mechanism by which people accidentally concentrate risk while believing they have spread it. The only cure is to think in terms of the underlying drivers — real yields, the dollar, liquidity, growth — and ask how many *independent* drivers your positions actually express, not how many tickers you hold. Two assets that both fall when real yields rise are, for risk purposes, one asset.

The general principle behind controlling for a common driver is sometimes called *confounding* in statistics, and it is the single most important idea in distinguishing correlation from causation. A confounder is a variable that causes both of the things you are studying, creating a correlation between them that has nothing to do with either causing the other. Detecting confounders is not a purely statistical exercise — you have to know enough economics to suspect which variable might be lurking — which is exactly why the mechanism requirement (checklist item 5) and the third-variable control (item 3) work together. Statistics tells you the A-B correlation is there; economics tells you to suspect real yields; the partial correlation confirms whether real yields explain it away.

#### Worked example: the gold-bond correlation is really a real-yield correlation

Suppose over some sample you measure: correlation(gold returns, bond returns) = +0.55. Looks like a clean relationship. Now you also have the change in 10-year real yields. You measure two more: correlation(gold returns, real-yield change) = -0.80 and correlation(bond returns, real-yield change) = -0.70. Both gold and bonds are strongly, negatively tied to real yields.

A rough partial-correlation calculation strips the shared real-yield piece out. The formula is: partial r(gold, bond | real yield) = [r(gold,bond) - r(gold,ry) x r(bond,ry)] / sqrt[(1 - r(gold,ry)^2)(1 - r(bond,ry)^2)]. Plug in: numerator = 0.55 - (-0.80)(-0.70) = 0.55 - 0.56 = -0.01. The denominator is some positive number. So the partial correlation between gold and bonds, *after removing real yields*, is essentially **zero**. The entire +0.55 was the shared exposure to real yields. **In one sentence: gold and bonds do not move together because they are related to each other — they move together because a third variable, the real yield, pushes both, and once you account for it the direct link disappears.**

## Trap 4: multiple testing — torture enough indicators and one will confess

Traps 1-3 are about a single correlation being contaminated. Trap 4 is different and, in the era of cheap data and fast computers, the most dangerous of all: **multiple testing**, also called p-hacking or data dredging. The trap is not in any one correlation but in the *search* — in how many correlations you tried before you found the one you are excited about.

Start with the foundation. When you test whether a correlation is "statistically significant," you compute a **p-value**: the probability of seeing a correlation at least this large purely by chance, if the truth were no relationship at all. The conventional threshold is 5% — if the p-value is below 0.05, the result is called "significant," meaning "this would happen by chance less than 1 time in 20." (The full machinery of p-values, what they do and do not mean, is in [hypothesis testing and p-values](/blog/trading/math-for-quants/hypothesis-testing-pvalues-math-for-quants).) The catch is right there in the definition: *1 time in 20*. If you run one test on two genuinely unrelated series, you have a 5% chance of a false "significant." If you run twenty such tests, you expect *one* false positive on average. If you run a hundred, you expect *five* — and every one of those five will have a p-value below 0.05 and look exactly as convincing as a real result.

![Test enough indicators and chance hands you edge](/imgs/blogs/spurious-correlation-and-the-traps-of-macro-data-4.png)

The figure shows this directly: 100 useless indicators, each tested against some asset at the 5% level. By the arithmetic of chance, about 5 of them clear the bar — the red squares — even though not one has any real relationship. This is exactly what happens, invisibly, when an analyst pulls "every macro indicator I could find" and runs each against the S&P 500, then reports the handful that "worked." The handful that worked are the false positives. The search manufactured them.

This is the engine behind an enormous fraction of published "signals," especially in the data-rich corners of finance. Someone screens hundreds of indicators against an asset, keeps the ones that crossed the significance bar, writes them up, and never mentions the hundreds that did not. Because they only report the survivors, the reader has no way to know that the survivors are exactly what you would expect from pure noise. The reported correlations are real numbers computed on real data — they are just the lucky tail of a search, and they will not repeat out of sample.

The defence is to **correct for the number of tests**. The simplest correction is **Bonferroni**: if you ran k tests and want an overall 5% false-positive rate across all of them, demand each individual test clear 0.05 / k, not 0.05. Test 100 indicators? The bar is 0.05 / 100 = 0.0005 — a result must be twenty times more extreme than the naive threshold to count. Most of the "5 of 100" survivors will not clear that bar, which is exactly the point: they were never real. Bonferroni is conservative (there are gentler corrections like Benjamini-Hochberg for controlling the false-discovery rate), but it has the right spirit, and for a skeptic the conservative default is the safe one.

#### Worked example: a Bonferroni adjustment kills a "significant" find

You screen 50 macro indicators against gold's monthly returns. One of them — say, "the change in the Australian trade balance" — comes back with a correlation whose p-value is 0.012. Significant! Below 0.05! You are tempted to write it up: "Australian trade flows lead gold."

Apply Bonferroni. You ran 50 tests, so your adjusted threshold is 0.05 / 50 = 0.001. Your finding's p-value is 0.012, which is *twelve times larger* than the 0.001 bar. After correcting for the fact that you went fishing through 50 indicators, this result is **not significant** — it is exactly the kind of thing you would expect roughly 0.012 x 50 = 0.6 of to show up by chance in a search this size (so finding about one is unremarkable). The honest conclusion is: there is no evidence here, and the naive 0.012 was an artifact of testing many things. **In a line: a p-value is only meaningful relative to how many tests you ran to get it — divide your threshold by the number of tries, and most exciting finds evaporate.**

A related, sneakier version is testing the *same* hypothesis many ways — different windows, different lags, different sub-samples, different definitions of the indicator — and reporting the specification that worked. Every variation is another test, even though you never wrote them down as separate tests. The honest practice is to decide your specification *before* looking at the result, and to count every variation you tried against your multiple-testing budget.

There is an even more invisible version that no individual analyst can fully escape, and it is worth understanding because it explains why so many published market "anomalies" fade after publication. Across the whole industry, thousands of researchers are screening thousands of indicators against the same assets. Even if every single one of them behaves honestly and Bonferroni-corrects their *own* search, the *profession as a whole* is running millions of tests, and the findings that get published, tweeted, and turned into products are disproportionately the false positives — because a result that "works" is exciting and gets shared, while the thousands that did not work are never mentioned. This is publication bias, and it means the base rate of fake findings in what you read is far higher than the nominal 5% would suggest. The defensive posture is to treat any single-study, single-indicator correlation you encounter as guilty until proven innocent, and to weight findings by whether they have an obvious mechanism and have survived genuinely out-of-sample, in live trading, for years — not by how clean the backtest looks.

The genuinely robust macro relationships — the handful this series will teach you — share a profile that the false positives lack: they have a clear mechanism, they show up across many sub-periods (with sign flips you can explain by regime, not random noise), they are large relative to the noise floor, and they have persisted out-of-sample for decades even after everyone knew about them. The real-yield-prices-everything relationship did not stop working when it became famous, because it is a mechanical feature of how assets are discounted, not a statistical fluke. That durability-with-a-mechanism is the signature of a real correlation, and it is what the multiple-testing trap can never manufacture.

## Trap 5: sample dependence — the full sample washes out a regime flip

We have arrived back at the opening story, now with the vocabulary to see it clearly. Trap 5 is **sample dependence**: the correlation you get depends entirely on which slice of history you measure, and a full-sample number can average away a relationship that is strongly present in every sub-period — just with different signs.

This is the deadliest trap for the overconfident, because it produces the most reassuring of all wrong answers: "there's no relationship here." A correlation of zero feels safe. It feels like the absence of a bet. But zero can be the *average of a strong negative and a strong positive* — a relationship that is intensely present and merely changes sign across regimes. If you trade on "they're uncorrelated, so this is a free diversifier," you are unknowingly long a coin flip on which regime you are in.

![The full-sample correlation lies: gold vs 10Y real yield](/imgs/blogs/spurious-correlation-and-the-traps-of-macro-data-2.png)

The figure is the killer demonstration of the whole post. Each blue dot is a year from 2007 to 2021: real yield on the horizontal axis, gold price on the vertical. They fall on a tight downward line — as real yields rise, gold falls — a correlation of about -0.96, one of the cleanest relationships in all of macro. Each amber square is a year from 2022 to 2025: they fall on a tight *upward* line, correlation about +0.80, because central-bank buying and reserve diversification began lifting gold even as real yields climbed. Two strong, real, opposite relationships. Now compute the correlation over the whole 2007-2025 sample, pooling blue and amber together: you get **-0.01**. A number that screams "gold and real yields are unrelated" — the single most wrong conclusion available, manufactured purely by averaging two regimes with opposite signs.

The same disease afflicts the stock-bond correlation, the most important relationship in portfolio construction. The figure below shows the rolling 24-month correlation between US stocks and Treasuries from 1990 to 2025. It was positive in the high-inflation early 1990s, turned strongly negative through the 2000s and 2010s — the "diversifying" era that made the 60/40 portfolio work, because bonds rose when stocks fell — then *flipped positive* in 2022 when inflation became the dominant risk and both fell together. The full-sample average (the dashed line) sits near zero, hiding the entire story. An investor who measured the full-sample stock-bond correlation and concluded "about zero, so bonds are a mild diversifier on average" would have been blindsided in 2022, when the diversification they were counting on reversed exactly when they needed it. This is why the [stock-bond correlation is a regime, not a constant](/blog/trading/cross-asset/stock-bond-correlation-the-60-40-engine), and why understanding [why today's correlations aren't yesterday's](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/structural-shifts-why-todays-correlations-arent-yesterdays) is a survival skill, not an academic nicety.

![Sample dependence: the full-sample average buries a sign flip](/imgs/blogs/spurious-correlation-and-the-traps-of-macro-data-6.png)

The fix is to **never trust a single full-sample correlation** — always look at it through a rolling window and across sub-periods conditioned on regime. A rolling correlation (computed over a moving window of, say, 24 months) shows you whether the relationship is stable or flipping, which a single number can never reveal. Conditioning on regime — splitting by the inflation level, by whether real yields are positive or negative, by whether you are in expansion or recession — shows you *when* each sign holds. The companion post on [rolling correlation and why the window matters](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/rolling-correlation-and-why-the-window-matters) is the practical toolkit for this; the discipline here is simply: a full-sample correlation near zero is a question ("is this absent, or is it flipping?"), never an answer.

#### Worked example: a -0.01 that is really a -0.96 and a +0.80

Take the 19 annual (real-yield, gold) points from 2007 to 2025. Computed over all 19 years, the Pearson correlation is **-0.01** — call it zero. Now split at 2021. Over 2007-2021 (15 points), the correlation is **-0.96**: real yields up, gold reliably down, almost a straight line. Over 2022-2025 (4 points), the correlation is **+0.80**: real yields up, gold *up*, because the buyer base changed.

How does a -0.96 and a +0.80 average to roughly zero? Because correlation is not additive, but the intuition is that the two clouds of dots point in opposite diagonal directions; when you pool them, the upward-sloping amber cloud sits up and to the right of the downward-sloping blue cloud, and the overall best-fit line through the combined mess is nearly flat. The pooled number describes a line that fits *neither* regime. **The takeaway you must internalize: a full-sample correlation of zero does not mean "no relationship" — it can mean "a strong relationship that flipped sign," which is a completely different and far more dangerous fact about the world.**

## Trap 6: overfitting and look-ahead — fooling yourself in a backtest

The final trap is the one that converts all the previous traps into real losses, because it is where you stop just *measuring* a correlation and start *trading* it. **Overfitting** is finding a relationship that fits the past beautifully but has captured noise rather than signal, so it falls apart on new data. **Look-ahead bias** is the subtler sibling: accidentally using information in your backtest that you would not have had in real time, which makes a useless rule look brilliant.

Overfitting is what multiple testing (trap 4) feels like from the inside when you are building a strategy. You try a 20-day window; it does not work. You try 50 days; better. You add a filter for high-volatility regimes; better still. You exclude 2020 as an outlier; now the backtest is gorgeous. Every one of those choices was fit to the specific wiggles of your historical sample. The more knobs you turned, the more your "strategy" became a precise description of the past's noise — and noise, by definition, does not repeat. A model with enough parameters can fit *any* history perfectly and predict *no* future.

Look-ahead bias is more insidious because it can creep in without you choosing anything. Examples that have burned people: using a quarter's GDP figure on the quarter-end date, when in reality it is released weeks later and revised for months afterward; using a stock index's *current* membership to backtest a strategy over the past (survivorship bias — the companies that went bankrupt and dropped out are missing, so the index looks far healthier than it was in real time); using an indicator's revised, final value instead of the messy first print you would actually have traded on. Each of these silently feeds your backtest information from the future, and a strategy built on future information will look spectacular and lose money the moment it has to run forward.

The fix is the discipline of **honest out-of-sample testing**: fit your relationship on one slice of history (in-sample), then test it, untouched, on a slice you never looked at while building it (out-of-sample). If it holds out-of-sample, you have evidence it is real. If it falls apart, it was overfit, and you just saved yourself the cost of finding that out with real money. For correlations specifically, the analogue is: measure the correlation in one period, then check whether it persists in the *next* period — a correlation that is real should show up out-of-sample, and one that was an artifact of the in-sample noise will not. This is important enough that the series devotes a whole post to [backtesting a correlation without fooling yourself](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/backtesting-a-correlation-without-fooling-yourself); the one-line version is: in-sample fit is worthless as evidence, and look-ahead bias makes garbage look like gold.

There is one more thing overfitting interacts with that closes the loop on this entire post: **sample size**. The smaller your sample, the more easily noise masquerades as a strong relationship, and the more dangerous overfitting becomes — because there is so little real signal to be found that anything you find is mostly luck.

![How big a correlation must be before it beats chance](/imgs/blogs/spurious-correlation-and-the-traps-of-macro-data-7.png)

The figure shows, for two genuinely unrelated series, how large a sample correlation has to be before it clears the 5% significance bar — and the red zone underneath is "this correlation could easily be pure chance." Notice how high the bar is for small samples. With only n = 10 observations you need an |r| above about 0.63 just to clear 5%; with n = 24 you still need about 0.40; only when you get to n = 100+ does a modest r = 0.20 become believable. Macro data is chronically small-sample — there have been only a handful of business cycles in modern history, only a few inflation regimes, only so many recessions — which means macro correlations sit much closer to that noise floor than people realize. The blue dot marks the worked example below.

#### Worked example: a 0.40 correlation on 24 months is basically noise

You have 24 monthly observations — two years of data, which feels like plenty — and you measure a correlation of r = 0.40 between some indicator and an asset's returns. That feels like a real, moderate relationship. Is it?

Test it. With n = 24, the degrees of freedom are 24 - 2 = 22. The two-sided 5% critical value of |r| at 22 degrees of freedom is about 0.404. Your r = 0.40 is *just below* that — it does not even clear the naive 5% bar. In plain terms: if the two series were truly unrelated, you would see a correlation this big or bigger purely by chance more than 5% of the time. There is no evidence of a real relationship at all. And remember trap 4: if you found this 0.40 by screening many indicators, the bar is far higher still. And remember trap 3: even if it cleared the bar, you would need to control for the common drivers. A "0.40, two years of data" finding survives none of these. **The number to carry forever: a 0.40 correlation on 24 observations sits right on the noise floor — impressive-looking and statistically meaningless — because small samples turn luck into the appearance of signal.**

## The honesty checklist

Six traps, six fixes. Rather than carry six separate rules, carry one checklist and run every macro correlation through it before you trust it. If a correlation fails any single item, you do not have a relationship — you have a number that fooled you, and you set it aside.

![The honesty checklist for any macro correlation](/imgs/blogs/spurious-correlation-and-the-traps-of-macro-data-5.png)

1. **Changes, not levels.** Did you correlate returns or first differences, or did you correlate raw trending price levels? Levels of two trending series correlate by construction. If it is a level-on-level correlation, discard it and recompute on changes. (Trap 1.)
2. **Check stationarity.** Are the changes themselves reasonably stable — mean near zero, variance not exploding in one part of the sample? If a crisis or a structural break is doing all the work, your single number is an average across regimes that describes none of them. (Trap 2.)
3. **Control for the common driver.** Could a third variable — real yields, the dollar, liquidity, the cycle — be moving both series? Partial it out or add it as a regression control. If the correlation collapses, the third variable was the real actor. (Trap 3.)
4. **Correct for multiple tests.** How many indicators, windows, lags, and definitions did you try before you found this one? Divide your significance threshold by the count (Bonferroni: 0.05 / k). Most exciting finds from a wide search evaporate. (Trap 4.)
5. **A mechanism must exist.** Can you name the economic channel — the actual reason one should move the other — *before* you trust the number? "No story, no trade." A correlation with no plausible mechanism is a coincidence until proven otherwise, no matter how strong. (Anti-spurious discipline.)
6. **Out-of-sample.** Does the relationship hold on data you did not look at while finding it? In-sample fit and a clean backtest prove nothing if you turned knobs or peeked at the future. (Trap 6 and sample dependence.)

The fifth item — *a mechanism must exist* — deserves emphasis because it is the human, non-statistical backstop that catches what the statistics miss. Statistics can tell you a correlation is unlikely to be chance; they cannot tell you it is not spurious in the deeper sense of "two things that drift together for unrelated reasons." Only economic reasoning can do that. Before you trust any correlation, you must be able to say, in one sentence, *why* one thing should move the other — the channel, the transmission, the mechanism. If you cannot, the burden of proof is not met. The companion series on [how policy moves every asset](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-policy-moves-every-asset-cross-asset-transmission-map) exists precisely to give you those mechanisms; this post exists to stop you trading correlations that have none.

## Common misconceptions

**"A strong correlation (like 0.9) must mean a real relationship."** No — a 0.9 is the *easiest* number to manufacture, because two trending series produce exactly that for free (trap 1). The strength of a correlation tells you nothing about whether it is real; only the checklist does. The margarine-and-divorce correlation was 0.99.

**"A correlation near zero means the two are unrelated, so it is safe to ignore."** This is the most dangerous misconception of all, because zero feels like the absence of a bet. A full-sample zero can be a strong negative and a strong positive averaged together (trap 5) — gold and real yields full-sample is -0.01, which is -0.96 and +0.80 in disguise. A near-zero correlation is a question, not an answer; look at it through a rolling window before you conclude anything.

**"More data is always better, so a 20-year sample beats a 2-year sample."** More data helps with sample size (trap 6), yes — but a 20-year sample is far more likely to span multiple regimes, and pooling regimes washes out flips (trap 5). Long samples reduce noise but increase the risk that you are averaging across structurally different worlds. The honest approach is *both*: enough data to beat the noise floor, but split by regime so you are not averaging a -0.96 with a +0.80.

**"If a backtest is profitable, the correlation it is built on is real."** A profitable backtest is the *expected* outcome of overfitting and look-ahead bias, not evidence against them (trap 6). The more you optimized the backtest, the less the in-sample profit means. Only out-of-sample performance — on data you never touched while building — counts as evidence.

**"Gold hedges inflation, so it should correlate with CPI."** This is the kind of plausible-sounding relationship that fails the mechanism test. Gold's cleanest correlation is with *real yields*, not CPI — and even that flipped after 2022. The cross-asset matrix in this series shows gold's correlation with a hot CPI surprise is near zero (about -0.10), because the inflation print's effect on gold runs *through* real yields, which can go either way depending on how the Fed is expected to respond. A correlation people are sure exists, that the data does not support, is a textbook trap.

## How it shows up in real markets

**Gold and real yields, 2022-2024 (the regime flip).** This is the case study that runs through the whole post. For fifteen years the gold-real-yield correlation was the most reliable relationship a macro trader had: -0.96, mechanism airtight (opportunity cost of holding non-yielding metal). Traders who shorted gold into the brutal real-yield rise of 2022-2023 were following a relationship that had worked for a decade and a half. It cost them dearly, because central-bank and official-sector buying changed the marginal buyer of gold and flipped the sign to +0.80. The lesson is not "the correlation was fake" — it was intensely real — but "a real correlation can flip when the structure underneath it changes," and the full-sample number that pooled both regimes (-0.01) hid the danger completely.

**The 60/40 portfolio, 2022 (the diversification that wasn't).** For two decades the stock-bond correlation was negative, around -0.3 to -0.5, and the entire 60/40 industry was built on it: when stocks fell, bonds rose and cushioned the blow. In 2022, inflation became the dominant risk, the Fed hiked aggressively, and stocks and bonds fell *together* — the correlation flipped to about +0.60, and the 60/40 portfolio had its worst year in modern history precisely because the diversification reversed when it was needed. Anyone who had checked the *rolling* correlation conditioned on the inflation regime (the data shows stock-bond correlation is about -0.45 when inflation is below 2% but about +0.50 when inflation is above 4%) would have known the diversification was inflation-regime-dependent and at risk. The full-sample average, near zero, told them nothing.

**Crypto's "macro correlation," 2021-2024.** During the 2022 macro stress, Bitcoin's rolling 90-day correlation with the Nasdaq spiked to about 0.65 — "Bitcoin is just a high-beta tech / liquidity trade," everyone declared, and built it into models. By 2024-2025 that correlation had faded back toward 0.20-0.30 as the buyer base shifted (spot ETFs, different holders). Anyone who locked in "BTC correlates 0.65 with Nasdaq" as a stable fact in 2022 was using a number that was true for one regime and decaying the moment they wrote it down — a sample-dependence trap in real time. The correlation was real; treating it as constant was the error.

**The endless parade of screened "indicators."** Every cycle produces a fresh crop of single-indicator "signals" — the Big Mac index predicts the dollar, the lipstick index predicts recessions, some obscure shipping rate predicts equities. Most are trap 4 (someone screened many things, reported the survivor) compounded by trap 1 (level-on-level on trending series) and trap 5 (it "worked" in one sample). The defence is reflexive skepticism: when you see a surprising single-indicator correlation, your first question is "how many indicators did they test, and is this on levels or changes, and over what sample?" Nine times out of ten, asking those three questions dissolves the finding.

## How to read it and use it

You will spend the rest of this series learning real, usable macro correlations. This post is the filter you run them all through. Here is the operating procedure.

**When you compute a correlation yourself:** run the six-item checklist in order before you believe it. Changes not levels; check stationarity; control for the obvious common driver (in macro, start with real yields and the dollar); count your tests and Bonferroni-correct; demand a mechanism you can state in a sentence; verify out-of-sample. A correlation that survives all six is worth acting on. One that fails any is a number, not a relationship.

**When you read someone else's correlation** (a research note, a chart, a confident tweet): you usually cannot rerun their analysis, so ask the three diagnostic questions that catch most fakes. *Is it levels or changes?* (If levels of trending series, discard.) *Over what sample, and is it stable in a rolling window?* (If one full-sample number with no rolling view, suspect a hidden flip.) *How many things did they test to find this?* (If a single indicator pulled from a wide screen, suspect a false positive.) If they cannot or do not answer these, treat the finding as unproven.

**The regime check that matters most:** because sample dependence (trap 5) is the deadliest and most common, make rolling-and-conditioned your default way of *looking* at any correlation. Never accept a single full-sample number as the answer to "are these two related?" Always ask "related *when*?" The answer is almost always "it depends on the regime," and knowing which regime you are in is the actual edge.

**What invalidates a correlation you are trusting:** a structural change in the buyer base or the mechanism (gold's official-sector buying flipped its real-yield link); a regime change in the master variable (inflation moving from below 2% to above 4% flips stock-bond); a sample that has quietly drifted (the relationship you measured five years ago may be decaying like crypto-Nasdaq). The defensive habit is to re-measure rolling correlations continuously, watch for the sign weakening or flipping, and treat every correlation as a regime-conditional fact with an expiry date rather than a law of nature.

**A note on tools, so you can actually do this.** None of the six checks requires exotic software. Computing returns instead of levels is one line of pandas (`series.pct_change()` or `.diff()`). A rolling correlation is `df['a'].rolling(24).corr(df['b'])`. A partial correlation is two short regressions and a correlation of the residuals, or one call to a stats library. A Bonferroni correction is dividing a number by another number. The augmented Dickey-Fuller stationarity test is one function in statsmodels. The barrier to running the checklist is not technical difficulty — it is discipline, the willingness to run the checks that might kill a finding you are excited about. That willingness is the entire skill. The analyst who *wants* the correlation to be real will find ways to skip the checks that would disprove it; the skeptic runs them precisely because the finding is exciting, knowing that the more thrilling a correlation looks, the more likely it is one of the traps.

The skeptic's mindset is not cynicism — it is what lets you use correlations *well*. Most of what you will see is fake, but not all of it, and the checklist is how you tell the difference. The trader who knows that gold-real-yields is real but flips, that stock-bond is real but inflation-conditional, that a screened single indicator is probably noise, and that a 0.40 on two years of data is meaningless — that trader can build on the correlations that survive, while everyone fooled by the traps blows up on the ones that do not. Honesty about the data is the edge.
