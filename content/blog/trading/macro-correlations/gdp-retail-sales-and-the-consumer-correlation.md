> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — GDP is the single most important number in the economy and one of the *least* market-moving, because it is lagging and already priced: by the time it prints, the market has nowcast it from monthly data, so the surprise is tiny and the correlation is near zero. Timeliness and surprise, not importance, drive correlation.
>
> - Stocks **lead** GDP by roughly two quarters; GDP does not lead the stock market, it confirms what the market already moved on.
> - The contemporaneous correlation between a quarterly GDP print and the same-session stock move is close to zero, because the monthly inputs (retail sales, trade, inventories) were already public and the market built a nowcast from them.
> - Retail sales and personal spending are the *timely* consumer reads — they come out two weeks after the month, carry a real surprise, and correlate with consumer-discretionary stocks and the front end of the yield curve.
> - The number to remember: about **70%** of US GDP is consumer spending, but the GDP print's incremental information by release day is near zero — that is why a strong GDP headline so often produces a shrug.

In late October 2023 the US Bureau of Economic Analysis released the advance estimate of third-quarter GDP. The number was a blockbuster: real GDP had grown at a 4.9% annualized rate, the fastest in nearly two years, roughly double the trend pace and well above what most economists had pencilled in a few months earlier. On paper this was the kind of growth surprise that should have set off fireworks. The economy was not just avoiding the recession everyone had been forecasting for eighteen months — it was *accelerating*.

The stock market's response was a yawn that turned into a selloff. The S&P 500 actually fell that day and kept falling into late October, bottoming a few days later before a sharp November rally. The "best growth number in two years" was met with red on the screen. To anyone who believes that strong growth is good for stocks, this looked insane. It was not. It was the single cleanest illustration of the thesis of this entire post: **GDP is the most important number that moves markets the least.**

The reason is not that growth does not matter — growth is the bedrock everything else stands on. The reason is that by the time GDP prints, the market has already seen the ingredients. Every month leading up to that 4.9% print, traders watched retail sales, the trade balance, inventory data, construction spending, and industrial production. The Atlanta Fed's GDPNow model and a dozen bank nowcasts were tracking a number near 5% for weeks. The print confirmed what was already in the price. And because the market is forward-looking, by the time the strong growth was *official*, attention had moved on to the next question — would all this strength force the Federal Reserve to keep rates high for longer? That is a bearish thought for a market addicted to rate cuts. The growth was old news; the rate implication was the live news.

![Monthly inputs build the GDPNow nowcast so the GDP print is old news while stocks lead GDP](/imgs/blogs/gdp-retail-sales-and-the-consumer-correlation-1.png)

This post builds the whole picture from zero. We will define GDP and its three estimates, explain why it is structurally backward-looking, introduce the idea of nowcasting and why it kills the GDP surprise, then turn to the consumer reads — retail sales, personal spending, consumer confidence — that come earlier and therefore carry the correlation. Along the way we will measure the correlations honestly: which assets actually move on a growth surprise, what *leads* GDP, and why two negative GDP quarters in 2022 did not produce a recession. The lesson generalizes far beyond GDP: in macro, an indicator's correlation with markets is set by how *timely* and *surprising* it is, not by how *important* it is.

## Foundations: what GDP is, and why it arrives late

Start with the term itself. **GDP — gross domestic product — is the total dollar value of all final goods and services produced inside a country over a period of time.** It is the broadest single measure of the size of an economy. When commentators say "the economy grew 2.5% last year," they mean real GDP grew 2.5%. It is, without exaggeration, the most important number in macroeconomics. It is also, for a trader, one of the least useful, and understanding that paradox is the point.

To build intuition before any numbers, here is an everyday analogy. Imagine you run a restaurant and you want to know how busy you were last quarter. The *truest* measure is the audited, fully reconciled revenue figure — every receipt counted, every refund netted, every gift card accounted for. That number is authoritative. But it takes your accountant three months to produce, and even then they revise it twice as late invoices arrive. Meanwhile, all quarter long, you already *knew* roughly how busy you were: you watched the daily till, the reservation book, the number of covers each night. By the time the audited number lands, it tells you almost nothing you did not already know from the daily reads. GDP is the audited quarterly number. Retail sales is the daily till.

### Real versus nominal

A first essential distinction. **Nominal GDP** is measured in current dollars; **real GDP** strips out inflation so that you are measuring actual *quantity* of output, not just higher prices. If the economy produces the exact same number of cars and haircuts this year as last, but every price rose 5%, nominal GDP rises 5% while real GDP is flat. Markets care about real GDP because it measures genuine growth. When you see a GDP growth number quoted, assume it is real unless told otherwise. In the US, real GDP growth is reported as a *seasonally adjusted annualized rate* (SAAR): a quarterly change scaled up as if it continued for a full year. A 1.2% quarter-on-quarter gain is reported as roughly 4.9% annualized — which is exactly how the Q3 2023 number got so eye-catching.

The conversion from inflation to real terms runs through the **GDP deflator**, a price index built from the whole basket of output. Subtract the deflator's growth from nominal GDP growth and you get real GDP growth. This matters for traders because in a high-inflation period, a big nominal GDP number can hide weak real growth: the dollars are growing because prices are rising, not because the economy is producing more. The 2022 episode is a perfect example — nominal GDP looked robust while real GDP stalled and even dipped, because inflation was doing the heavy lifting. A reader who only saw the nominal headline would have badly misjudged the economy's actual momentum. Always work in real terms when you are thinking about growth.

There is also a quieter sibling number worth knowing: **GDI, gross domestic income.** GDP measures the economy from the *spending* side (the sum of everything bought); GDI measures it from the *income* side (the sum of everything earned — wages, profits, interest, rents). In theory they should be identical, because every dollar spent is a dollar earned by someone. In practice they diverge because they are built from different source data, and the gap (the "statistical discrepancy") can be large. In 2022–2023 GDI ran notably weaker than GDP, fueling the recession debate: depending on which measure you believed, the economy was either fine or quietly stalling. The BEA now publishes an *average of GDP and GDI* to split the difference. The mere existence of two "true" measures that disagree by a percentage point tells you how much noise sits inside the most important number in macro — and why a trader should be humble about trading its tenths.

### The three estimates and the revision problem

Here is the structural fact that makes GDP late. The BEA does not publish GDP once. It publishes it three times for each quarter:

- The **advance estimate**, released about four weeks after the quarter ends, built on incomplete data. This is the number markets react to.
- The **second estimate**, about eight weeks after the quarter, folding in more complete trade, inventory, and services data.
- The **third estimate**, about twelve weeks after the quarter, based on nearly complete data — and *still* not final.

After that, GDP is revised again in annual updates and in comprehensive benchmark revisions that can rewrite *years* of history. The number you trade on release day is a draft. The truth, to the extent there is one, lands quarters or years later.

![Three GDP estimates and benchmark revisions show the headline you trade is provisional](/imgs/blogs/gdp-retail-sales-and-the-consumer-correlation-7.png)

This is the **revision trap**, and it is not a minor footnote. Revisions between the advance and final estimates routinely run several tenths of a percentage point and occasionally more than a full point. The advance estimate that printed 4.9% in October 2023 was later revised — the third estimate landed near 4.9% as well that quarter, but in other quarters the direction has flipped enough to change the narrative entirely. If the number itself is uncertain by half a point, then the "surprise" relative to consensus — which is what markets actually trade — is buried in measurement noise. You cannot build a tight correlation on a noisy signal.

It is worth dwelling on *why* the advance estimate is so revisable, because it explains the whole timing structure. When the BEA produces the advance estimate four weeks after the quarter, it does not yet have the final monthly data for the third month of the quarter. For categories like services spending, international trade, and inventories, it has to *estimate* the missing month using trend assumptions and partial reports. Those assumptions get replaced by actual data in the second and third estimates, which is where most of the revision comes from. So the advance estimate — the one markets react to — is precisely the one with the most guesswork baked in. The components most likely to be revised (inventories and net exports) are also the most volatile and the least economically meaningful quarter to quarter, which is a double curse: the noisiest parts of GDP are also the ones the early estimate is least sure about. A trader reacting to an advance print is reacting to the BEA's best guess about its own most uncertain components.

This also explains a recurring pattern: the *narrative* around the economy can lurch on a single GDP print that later gets quietly reversed. A weak advance number triggers recession headlines and a risk-off lean; two months later the revision erases the weakness, but the headlines have moved on and nobody re-trades the correction. The market that anchored on the advance number paid for a signal that turned out to be noise. This is not a reason to ignore GDP — it is a reason to weight it lightly, to wait for confirmation from the timely monthly data, and never to size a position on the advance estimate's tenths.

### What GDP includes, and why the consumer dominates

GDP is conventionally decomposed into four buckets, captured by the identity that gets drilled into every economics student:

```
GDP = C + I + G + (X - M)
   C = consumption (consumer spending)
   I = investment (business + residential)
   G = government spending
   X = exports, M = imports (so X - M = net exports)
```

In the United States, **consumption (C) is roughly 70% of GDP.** The American consumer is the engine; the other three buckets are smaller and, in the case of net exports, often a drag. This single fact is why "the consumer" gets so much airtime, and why the timely consumer indicators — retail sales and personal spending — are the most-watched monthly reads. If you can nowcast the consumer, you can nowcast about 70% of GDP. And the consumer data comes out monthly, weeks before the quarterly GDP print. That is the crux of the whole correlation story.

> If you take one thing from the foundations: GDP is a backward-looking, thrice-revised, consumer-dominated quarterly number whose ingredients are already public before it prints. Importance and timeliness are different axes, and markets pay for timeliness.

## Why "important" and "correlated" are different things

Beginners assume the most important indicator must be the most market-moving. It is an intuitive error, and it is wrong. The market is a discounting machine: it tries to price the future *now*. An indicator moves prices only to the extent that it delivers *new information* — information not already in the price. The technical word for new information is the **surprise**: the gap between what was released and what the market expected (the consensus).

If an indicator is important but fully anticipated, its surprise is small, and a small surprise produces a small move regardless of how important the underlying concept is. GDP is the textbook case: enormously important, almost fully anticipated by release day, therefore a small surprise and a small correlation with the same-session move.

We covered the surprise framework in depth in [the surprise, not the level](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/the-surprise-not-the-level-betas-to-data-surprises) and in event-trading's [why news moves markets](/blog/trading/event-trading/why-news-moves-markets-the-surprise-framework); the short version is that the price move on a release is approximately the *beta* (the asset's sensitivity) times the *surprise*. If the surprise is near zero, the move is near zero, no matter the beta.

![GDP is lagging and priced while retail sales is timely and surprising](/imgs/blogs/gdp-retail-sales-and-the-consumer-correlation-3.png)

The figure contrasts the two. GDP covers a quarter that has *already ended*, prints four weeks late, and is built from inputs the market already saw — so its surprise is tiny. Retail sales covers *last month*, prints in two weeks, and is the first real read on the live consumer — so it carries a genuine surprise and moves the consumer-sensitive corners of the market. Same economy, opposite correlation behavior, and the only difference is timeliness.

#### Worked example: why the GDP surprise is small by construction

Suppose consensus for an upcoming quarter is 2.5% annualized real GDP growth. Over the quarter, the monthly data come in: retail sales a touch soft, the trade deficit a bit wider than feared, inventories building. By the week of the print, the Atlanta Fed GDPNow model reads 2.6% and the average of bank nowcasts reads 2.4%. The market has effectively narrowed its expectation to a tight band around 2.5%. Now the advance estimate prints **2.7%**. Headlines say "growth beats expectations!" But the *market's* working expectation, informed by the nowcasts, was already ~2.5–2.6%. The genuine surprise is on the order of \$0.1–0.2 percentage points, not the half-point a naive reader infers.

Plug that into a beta. Even using a generous same-session equity beta of roughly −0.4% of S&P move per +1 percentage point of growth surprise (growth-up can read hawkish in a rate-fearful regime), a 0.2pp surprise implies a move of about 0.4 × 0.2 = **0.08%**, or eight basis points on the index — statistical noise. A trader who put \$100,000 on that "beat" would expect a directional edge worth about \$80 before costs, which the bid-ask spread eats. The intuition: GDP rarely surprises the *market* even when it surprises the *headline reader*, so the tradable move is tiny.

## Nowcasting: how the market reads GDP before it prints

The mechanism that flattens the GDP surprise has a name: **nowcasting.** A nowcast is a real-time estimate of a quantity that has not yet been officially measured — "now-casting" the present rather than forecasting the future. The most famous is the Atlanta Fed's **GDPNow** model, which updates its estimate of current-quarter GDP every time a relevant monthly indicator is released. The New York Fed runs a similar Staff Nowcast, and every major bank publishes its own GDP tracker.

The logic is mechanical and follows the C + I + G + (X − M) identity. GDP is built from monthly data: retail sales feeds consumption, the international trade report feeds net exports, the wholesale and business inventory reports feed the inventory component, construction spending and durable goods feed investment. As each monthly report lands, the nowcast model slots the new number into its bucket and recomputes the running estimate of the quarter. By the time the quarter ends and the BEA does its own arithmetic, the nowcasts have already converged on a number — because they are using the same inputs.

This is why the surprise dies. The "surprise" in a GDP print is *actual minus expectation*, but the expectation is itself a nowcast built from the same monthly data the BEA uses. The BEA is not revealing new information; it is publishing a number the market already assembled. The genuine surprises happen *upstream*, when each monthly input prints and the nowcast jumps. By the time the quarterly total is official, the information has long since been traded.

It helps to see how GDPNow actually behaves over a quarter to internalize this. At the start of a quarter, with almost no hard data in, GDPNow's estimate is anchored near a statistical prior — often close to trend — and it can be volatile, swinging on each early report. As the quarter fills in with data, the estimate tightens and converges toward what will become the official number. By the final weeks before the BEA release, GDPNow and the published number are usually within a few tenths of each other. The model is not magic; it is just disciplined arithmetic on the same C + I + G + (X − M) identity the BEA uses, fed with the monthly releases as they arrive. Anyone with a Bloomberg terminal or a browser can watch it. So can the entire trading desk community. That shared visibility is exactly why the GDP print rarely shocks the market: everyone has already been watching the answer assemble itself in real time.

The deeper point is that the *information content* of a quarter's growth is released gradually, spread across roughly a dozen monthly reports, not concentrated in the GDP print. Think of it as a slow drip of surprise rather than a single splash. Each monthly report delivers a small, tradable surprise that nudges the nowcast; by the time you sum all those drips into the quarterly total, the splash is gone because you already absorbed it drop by drop. This is the single most important structural fact about GDP for a trader, and it is why the rest of this post is really about the *inputs* — retail sales, personal spending, the surveys — rather than the aggregate they roll up into.

#### Worked example: nowcasting GDP from one monthly retail sales print

Walk through how a single report moves a nowcast, because this is where the real tradable surprise lives. Suppose a quarter's consumption is tracking +2.0% annualized in the nowcast. Then the monthly retail sales report lands far above consensus — control-group retail sales (the slice that feeds GDP consumption) rose +0.8% on the month versus +0.3% expected. Consumption is roughly 70% of GDP. A 0.5pp monthly upside in the control group, annualized and weighted, can lift the consumption contribution enough to push the whole nowcast up by several tenths — say from 2.0% to 2.4%.

Now think about the dollars. A consumer-discretionary basket worth \$500,000 with a beta of, say, +1.2 to a retail-sales upside surprise of this size might move +1.5% on the day the *retail sales* report prints — a gain of roughly \$7,500 — while moving essentially nothing when the *GDP* print arrives weeks later confirming the same strength. The tradable event was the retail sales report, not the GDP report. The intuition: trade the timely input that *changes the nowcast*, not the lagging total that merely confirms it.

## What GDP looks like, and why it is too slow to trade

Look at the actual series. US real GDP growth has spent most of the post-pandemic period oscillating around its ~2% trend, with two enormous outliers: the −2.2% pandemic contraction in 2020 and the +5.9% rebound in 2021. Those two years were violent. Yet even those moves were not GDP-print surprises — the COVID collapse and the reopening boom were both nowcast in real time from collapsing and then surging monthly data. Markets crashed in March 2020 and bottomed before the catastrophic Q2 GDP print, and they rallied through 2020 before the +5.9% 2021 number was anywhere near official.

![US real GDP growth 2019 to 2025 with the 2020 contraction and 2021 rebound](/imgs/blogs/gdp-retail-sales-and-the-consumer-correlation-2.png)

The chart makes the "slow-moving" problem visible. Outside the pandemic, GDP growth sits in a narrow 2.1%–2.9% band — a range so tight that the quarter-to-quarter changes are mostly within the revision noise. There is simply not much *signal* in a number that hugs trend and gets revised by tenths. Compare that with how much the S&P 500 moved over the same years: it fell 18% in 2022 with GDP at a perfectly respectable +2.5%, and it rose more than 25% in 2023 with GDP at +2.9%. The growth number barely budged while the market did its two biggest annual moves in a decade. If GDP drove stocks, those years would be impossible.

#### Worked example: the revision trap turns a "beat" into a "miss"

This is where the revision problem bites a real trader. Suppose you trade a quarter's advance GDP estimate, which prints +2.9% versus +2.5% consensus — a clean beat. You buy cyclicals expecting follow-through, deploying \$50,000. Two months later the second estimate revises the quarter down to +2.4%, and the third estimate to +2.2%. The "beat" you traded was a measurement artifact; the economy was actually running *below* consensus. Anyone who anchored on the advance number was positioned exactly wrong, and the cyclical rally faded as the revisions rolled in.

Quantify the damage. If the cyclical basket round-tripped a 3% move on the false signal — up 3% on the beat, then giving it all back plus a bit as the revisions corrected the narrative — a \$50,000 position that should have been flat instead chopped through roughly \$1,500 of gain and \$1,800 of loss, netting a loss to spreads and timing of perhaps \$300–\$500 with nothing to show for the risk. The intuition: a GDP "surprise" you can trade is only as real as the estimate's stability, and the advance estimate is the *least* stable of the three.

## What actually leads GDP

If GDP lags and confirms, what *leads* it? This is the productive question, because the leading indicators are where the tradable information lives. The lead/lag structure of the business cycle is well documented, and it is the backbone of the Conference Board's Leading Economic Index and of every recession model.

![What leads the GDP print across business-cycle indicators in months](/imgs/blogs/gdp-retail-sales-and-the-consumer-correlation-4.png)

Building permits lead GDP by roughly nine months: a builder pulls a permit long before the construction shows up in investment and before the resulting activity flows through the economy. ISM new orders lead S&P earnings growth by about six months. The yield curve's inversion leads recessions by over a year. Credit spreads lead equity drawdowns by a few months. Initial jobless claims lead the unemployment rate by a couple of months. Notice the pattern: every one of these *leads* something. GDP is conspicuously absent from the list of leaders — because GDP is the thing being led. It is the aggregate that the leaders are pointing toward.

Why does building permits lead GDP by such a long stretch? The chain is mechanical. A permit is filed; then ground is broken; then materials are ordered and workers hired; then the structure is built and finally sold or occupied — and only at the end of that chain does the activity register in GDP's investment component and ripple into the broader economy through construction employment and spending. Each link adds months. The permit is the earliest observable point in a process that takes the better part of a year to fully show up in the aggregate. The same logic explains why *new orders* lead production and earnings: an order placed today becomes revenue and profit one or two quarters later. The economy is a pipeline, and the leading indicators are simply the measurements taken at the *front* of the pipeline, while GDP is the measurement taken at the *end*. By the time water reaches the end of the pipe, you could have measured it at the front months earlier.

This is the practical reason a macro trader spends far more attention on the leading set than on GDP itself. If you want to anticipate where growth is headed, you watch permits, new orders, claims, the curve, and credit spreads — the signals at the front of the pipeline. GDP tells you where growth *was*, and it tells you with a lag and a revision. There is no edge in being told what already happened, especially when the telling is noisy. The edge is in the leaders.

For the cross-asset taxonomy of leaders, coincident indicators, and laggards, see the dedicated post on [leading, coincident, and lagging indicators](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/lead-lag-leading-coincident-and-lagging-indicators). The single takeaway for GDP: it is the canonical *lagging-to-coincident* aggregate. By the time it confirms expansion or contraction, the leading indicators — and the market — moved months earlier.

### Stocks lead GDP, they do not follow it

Here is the inversion that trips up beginners. People intuitively assume the economy leads the stock market: good economy, good stocks. The empirical relationship runs the other way at the margin. The stock market is itself one of the components of the Conference Board's Leading Economic Index. Stocks lead GDP by roughly two quarters, because equity prices are the discounted present value of *future* cash flows, and investors reprice those expectations the moment the outlook shifts — long before the production shows up in GDP.

This is why "strong GDP, falling stocks" is not a paradox. The stock market already moved on the *anticipation* of the strong economy months earlier. When the strong GDP finally prints, two things are true: the good news is already in the price, and the market has rotated to worrying about the *consequence* of strength — namely, that a hot economy keeps the central bank tight. In the 2022–2023 regime this produced the famous "good news is bad news" dynamic, where strong data was sold because it implied higher-for-longer rates. We unpack that regime in [NFP and asset prices](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/nfp-and-asset-prices-the-king-of-data-correlation), where the same good-news-is-bad sign flip is sharpest.

There is a deeper reason stocks lead the economy, and it is worth making explicit because it reframes how you should think about the whole relationship. A stock price is, in theory, the present value of all the company's future cash flows. The word that does the work there is *future*. When investors believe the economy is about to turn up, they reprice those future cash flows higher *immediately* — they do not wait for the cash flows to actually arrive. The repricing happens at the speed of belief, which is far faster than the speed of the real economy. So the market is constantly running ahead of GDP, pricing the next turn before the data confirms the current one. By the time GDP confirms the upturn, the market has often already started pricing the *next* risk — the overheating, the tightening, the eventual slowdown. This is why a strong GDP print can land on a market that has already moved on. The market is not reacting to GDP; it is several steps ahead of it.

This forward-looking nature is also why the stock market is a *noisy* leading indicator. It leads, but it also throws false signals — Paul Samuelson's old quip that "the stock market has predicted nine of the last five recessions" captures it exactly. The market sometimes prices a downturn that never materializes, and sometimes misses one entirely. But on average, and especially over the cycle, equity prices turn before GDP does. The asymmetry matters for trading: you will almost never get a tradable edge from the GDP print, but you can get one from correctly reading the leading indicators that *the market itself* is reacting to, before the consensus catches up.

## The consumer reads that carry the correlation

If GDP is the lagging aggregate, the timely consumer data is where the correlation lives. There are three reads worth knowing, in order of market sensitivity.

**Retail sales** (Census Bureau, ~mid-month, covering the prior month) measures spending at retail and food-service establishments. It is the first hard read on the consumer each month. The "control group" — retail sales excluding autos, gas, building materials, and food services — is the slice that feeds directly into the GDP consumption estimate, so nowcasters watch it obsessively. A control-group surprise moves nowcasts and moves consumer-discretionary stocks.

**Personal spending and personal income** (BEA, ~end of month, covering the prior month) is the broader consumption measure and is published alongside the PCE price index — the Fed's preferred inflation gauge. Because PCE inflation and consumer spending arrive in the same report, it is doubly watched. The spending side is timely; the inflation side is what the rates market keys on.

**Consumer confidence and sentiment** (the Conference Board's Consumer Confidence Index and the University of Michigan's Sentiment survey) are *soft* data — survey-based reads on how consumers feel. They are timelier still (sentiment has preliminary mid-month and final end-month readings) but noisier, and the relationship between how consumers *say* they feel and how they actually *spend* is loose. Sentiment can crater while spending holds up, as it repeatedly did in 2022–2023. Soft data leads, but it leads with a lot of false signals.

The distinction between *soft* (survey) and *hard* (transaction) data is one of the most useful frameworks in reading the consumer, and it deserves a moment. Soft data measures intention and mood; hard data measures actual behavior. In normal times the two move together and the soft data, being timelier, gives an early read on the hard data to come. But they can diverge spectacularly, and the divergence is itself a signal. In 2022, the University of Michigan sentiment index fell to its lowest level on record — lower than in the 2008 financial crisis — as inflation crushed how consumers *felt*. Yet retail sales and personal spending kept climbing, because consumers had pandemic savings and a strong job market and simply kept buying despite their gloom. A trader who shorted consumer-discretionary stocks on the basis of record-low sentiment would have been run over by a consumer who said one thing and did another. The rule that fell out of that period is durable: **when soft and hard data disagree, trade the hard data.** What people do moves markets; what they say they feel is a noisier leading hint that often does not cash out.

The Conference Board's measure adds one more useful wrinkle: it splits into a *present situation* sub-index and an *expectations* sub-index, and the gap between them is itself watched as a recession signal. When consumers feel fine about today but grim about the future (expectations far below present situation), it has historically preceded downturns. This is a soft-data *lead* on the cycle — another front-of-the-pipeline measurement — but, like all soft data, it carries false positives and should be confirmed by the hard spending numbers before you trade it.

The hierarchy is the whole point. The consumer is ~70% of GDP, and these three reads arrive *before* GDP, *carry the surprise*, and therefore *carry the correlation* with the consumer-sensitive parts of the market. GDP just totals them up later.

A subtlety worth understanding is the difference between **retail sales** and **personal consumption expenditures (PCE)**, because they measure overlapping but not identical things and a trader should know which is which. Retail sales captures spending on *goods* at retailers, plus food services — it is heavily a *goods* read and misses most of services. PCE, the broader measure that feeds GDP consumption, captures *all* consumer spending including services like healthcare, housing, and financial services, which together are the larger and stickier part of household budgets. Because the US economy is roughly two-thirds services, PCE is the more complete consumer picture, but retail sales is timelier and the goods component is where consumer cyclicality shows up most sharply. The market watches retail sales for the *first* read and personal spending for the *fuller* read a couple of weeks later — and it keys especially hard on the PCE *price* index that accompanies the spending data, because that is the Federal Reserve's preferred inflation gauge. In a regime where the rates market dominates, the inflation half of the personal-spending report can swamp the spending half: a strong-spending, hot-inflation report can sell off stocks because the inflation read implies tighter policy, even though the spending read implies a healthy consumer.

This is the same "two signals in one report" tension that runs through the whole series. The personal-spending report bundles a *growth* signal (spending) with an *inflation* signal (PCE prices), and which one the market trades depends on the regime. In a benign, low-inflation expansion, the market reads the spending number and treats a beat as risk-on. In an inflation-fearful regime, the market reads the price number first and a beat in spending can be bad news because it stokes the inflation worry. We trace the inflation side of this in [CPI and asset prices](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/cpi-and-asset-prices-the-master-inflation-correlation) and [PCE, breakevens, and the forward inflation correlation](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/pce-breakevens-and-the-forward-inflation-correlation); for the growth side, what matters here is that the consumer's *spending* surprise is timely and correlated, while the GDP total that eventually rolls it up is not.

#### Worked example: a consumer-discretionary trade on a retail-sales surprise

Make it concrete with dollars. Suppose retail sales control group is expected +0.3% month-on-month and prints **+0.8%** — a clear upside surprise that signals a stronger consumer than the market had nowcast. You want to express this where the consumer signal lands hardest: consumer-discretionary stocks (retailers, restaurants, travel) and the front end of the curve (a stronger consumer means the central bank can stay tighter, so 2-year yields rise).

You buy \$100,000 of a consumer-discretionary basket and, as a same-direction macro overlay, position for the 2-year yield to rise. Historically a retail-sales upside surprise of this size lifts a discretionary basket by roughly +1.2% and pushes the 2-year yield up by perhaps 5–7 basis points intraday. The discretionary leg gains about \$1,200. If you had instead waited for the GDP print to confirm a strong consumer, you would have captured *none* of that move — the GDP report, weeks later, would have shown strong consumption that everyone already knew about, and the basket would have shrugged. The intuition: the consumer correlation is real and tradable, but it lives in the *timely* retail-sales surprise, not in the lagging GDP confirmation.

#### Worked example: the two-signal personal-spending report

Now take the trickier report — personal spending, released with the PCE price index. Suppose spending prints +0.7% versus +0.4% expected (a strong consumer), but core PCE inflation also prints +0.4% versus +0.3% expected (hotter than hoped). You are holding \$200,000 of cyclical equity, expecting the strong consumer to be risk-on. In a benign regime, the +0.3pp spending beat would lift your basket maybe +0.8%, a gain of about \$1,600. But in an inflation-fearful regime, the market reads the hot PCE first: a +0.1pp core PCE upside, with a same-session equity sensitivity around −0.7% per 0.1pp, implies roughly −0.7% on the index, or about −\$1,400 on your position. The two signals net to nearly a wash, and which one wins depends entirely on the regime. The intuition: when a report carries both a growth surprise and an inflation surprise, you must decide *which signal the market is trading today* before you assign a sign — and in a higher-for-longer regime, the inflation signal usually wins.

## The empirical correlation: who moves on a growth surprise

Now measure the consumer-cyclical link directly. When growth surprises to the upside — captured cleanly by a rising ISM/PMI, the timely business survey — risk-on assets rise and defensives lag. Cyclical sectors, copper ("Dr. Copper," the metal whose demand tracks industrial activity), small caps, and the broad market all carry positive correlations with a growth surprise. Long Treasuries fall (yields rise with growth), and gold is roughly flat to slightly negative (a growth surprise pushes real yields up, and gold dislikes higher real yields).

![Correlation of assets and sectors with a positive growth surprise](/imgs/blogs/gdp-retail-sales-and-the-consumer-correlation-5.png)

The numbers tell the story. Cyclical sectors (industrials) carry the highest correlation with a growth surprise, around +0.65; copper around +0.60; small caps and the S&P 500 in the +0.55 range; the 10-year yield around +0.45 (yields rise with growth). On the other side, defensives (consumer staples) sit near −0.10, gold near −0.05, and long Treasuries clearly negative at around −0.45. This is the consumer-cyclical link in one chart: a growth surprise is risk-on, and it shows up in the parts of the market geared to the cycle.

But here is the crucial qualifier, and it is the same one that runs through this whole series. These are correlations with the *surprise*, captured through a *timely* indicator (ISM/PMI), not with the *level* of GDP. If you instead correlate same-session stock moves with the *GDP print itself*, you get something near zero — because, as we have established, the GDP print carries almost no surprise. The cyclical sensitivities are real; they just attach to the timely growth surprise, not to the lagging aggregate. Importance and timeliness, again, are different axes.

Read the structure of these numbers carefully, because the *spread* across assets is as informative as any single value. The most cyclical exposures — industrials, copper, small caps — cluster around +0.55 to +0.65. These are the businesses and commodities whose fortunes rise and fall most directly with the level of economic activity: factories run hotter, raw-material demand climbs, and smaller domestically-geared companies (which carry more operating and financial leverage) amplify the upturn. The broad S&P 500 sits a touch lower at +0.55 because it blends cyclicals with defensives and mega-cap technology, diluting the pure-cycle beta. The 10-year yield's +0.45 reflects a different channel: stronger growth raises the expected path of short rates and lifts the term premium, so bond yields rise (and prices fall) when growth surprises up.

On the negative side, the logic is just as clean. Defensives like consumer staples sit near −0.10 because their demand is inelastic — people buy toothpaste and electricity in good times and bad — so they neither benefit much from a boom nor suffer much in a bust; they are the part of the market least geared to the cycle. Gold at −0.05 is roughly neutral to growth but slightly negative because a growth surprise tends to push *real* yields up, and gold, which pays no yield, dislikes a higher real return on safe alternatives. Long Treasuries at −0.45 are the cleanest negative: a growth surprise raises yields, and long-duration bonds fall the most when yields rise. The whole table is a coherent map of cyclicality, and it is exactly the map you would use to build a growth-surprise trade — long the high-positive cluster, short or underweight the negative cluster.

A final honesty note, because this series insists on it: these are *representative* correlations synthesized from public research, not tick-exact estimates, and they are *regime-conditional*. A full-sample correlation that mixes expansions and recessions, calm and crisis, will be muddier than these regime-clean numbers suggest, and any of them can weaken or flip when the regime changes. We dig into why full-sample correlation numbers mislead in [rolling correlation and why the window matters](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/rolling-correlation-and-why-the-window-matters); for now, treat these values as the *sign and approximate strength* of each relationship in a normal expansion, not as constants you can lever blindly.

#### Worked example: turning a +0.55 correlation into a position size

A correlation is not a trade until you turn it into a position. Suppose your timely growth indicator (ISM new orders) surprises to the upside, and you want exposure to the cyclical leg, which carries a ~+0.65 correlation with growth surprises and the strongest beta. You have a \$200,000 risk budget and you want the cyclical tilt to contribute, say, 1.5% of expected move on a one-standard-deviation growth surprise.

If the cyclical basket's beta to a one-standard-deviation ISM surprise is roughly +2.5% (a strong-cycle name moves more than the index), then to get 1.5% of *portfolio* move you size the cyclical position at 1.5% / 2.5% = 60% of the budget, or **\$120,000**. The remaining \$80,000 stays in lower-beta or hedging exposure. If the surprise plays out as a +1 standard-deviation event, the cyclical leg contributes about \$120,000 × 2.5% = \$3,000 of gain. The intuition: a correlation tells you the *sign and reliability* of the relationship; the beta and your risk budget tell you the *size*. A +0.65 correlation justifies a real allocation; a near-zero GDP correlation justifies none.

## The investment clock: why the consumer-cyclical link rotates

The consumer-cyclical correlation is not constant — it rotates with the business cycle, which is the master regime variable for this whole family of relationships. The "investment clock" framework, popularized by asset allocators, maps which assets win in each phase: stocks lead the early recovery, commodities lead the late-cycle overheat, bonds and cash win the recession.

![Representative real returns by asset class across business cycle phases](/imgs/blogs/gdp-retail-sales-and-the-consumer-correlation-6.png)

The pattern in the chart is the rotation engine of cross-asset investing. In the **early/recovery** phase, stocks deliver the biggest real returns (~+20% in this representative mapping) as the market prices the upturn before GDP confirms it; commodities and bonds also do fine, cash lags. In **mid/expansion**, stocks still lead but moderate. In the **late/overheat** phase, commodities take over (~+14%) as growth runs hot and inflation bites, stocks slow, and bonds turn negative. In **recession**, government bonds win (~+10%) and cash holds value while stocks and commodities fall hard. The consumer-cyclical link — strong in expansion, when a growth surprise lifts cyclicals — *inverts* in recession, when even strong consumer data cannot save cyclicals against a deflating cycle.

This is why the same growth surprise can produce different asset reactions depending on *where you are on the clock*. A retail-sales beat in early recovery is unambiguously risk-on; the same beat in a late-cycle overheat can be read as hawkish (more inflation, more central-bank tightening) and weigh on stocks. The correlation is regime-dependent, exactly as the series thesis insists. For the mechanism of how the four phases drive every asset class, see macro-trading's [the business cycle, four phases for traders](/blog/trading/macro-trading/the-business-cycle-four-phases-for-traders); the dedicated map of how correlations themselves rotate by phase is the upcoming [business cycle correlation clock](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/the-business-cycle-correlation-clock).

## Common misconceptions

**"Strong GDP is bullish for stocks."** Not at the print, and not reliably. The contemporaneous correlation between a quarterly GDP release and the same-day stock move is near zero, because the print carries almost no surprise. Worse, in a rate-fearful regime, strong GDP can be *bearish* — the October 2023 4.9% print landed on a falling market because it implied higher-for-longer rates. Stocks already moved on the *anticipation* of growth, months before the print. The correct statement is "stocks lead GDP," not "GDP drives stocks."

**"GDP is the number to trade on data day."** It is one of the *least* tradable major releases precisely because it is the most anticipated. The tradable surprises happen upstream, in the monthly inputs — retail sales, the trade balance, inventories — that move the nowcast. By the time the quarterly total prints, the surprise has been arbitraged away. If you want a data-day GDP edge, you are usually trading noise plus the spread.

**"A growth surprise lifts everything risky equally."** No — the consumer-cyclical link is selective. A growth surprise lifts cyclicals (~+0.65 correlation), copper, small caps, and yields, while defensives (~−0.10), gold (~−0.05), and long Treasuries (~−0.45) lag or fall. And the whole pattern *inverts* in a recession phase. Treating "good growth" as uniformly good for risk ignores the rotation and the regime.

**"Consumer confidence tells you what consumers will spend."** Loosely, at best. Confidence is soft survey data; spending is hard data. The two diverged sharply in 2022–2023, when sentiment cratered toward record lows while consumers kept spending. Soft data leads but throws false signals; the hard retail-sales and personal-spending numbers are what actually move the consumer-sensitive trade.

**"Two negative GDP quarters means recession."** This is the popular rule of thumb, and 2022 broke it. The US posted negative real GDP in Q1 and Q2 of 2022 — the textbook "technical recession" — yet the NBER, which actually dates US recessions, never called one, because employment, income, and spending kept growing. The negative prints were driven by volatile inventory and trade components, not by a broad contraction. The episode is a clean lesson that GDP is a noisy, revision-prone aggregate, and that no single rule mechanically defines a recession.

## How it shows up in real markets

**October 2023 — the growth print markets ignored.** The advance Q3 2023 GDP estimate printed 4.9% annualized, the fastest in nearly two years and well above the recessionary forecasts that had dominated 2023. The S&P 500 fell that day and continued lower into late October. The "best growth in two years" produced red, because (1) the strength was fully nowcast and already in the price, and (2) in the higher-for-longer regime, strong growth read as hawkish — it meant the Fed had no reason to cut. This is the canonical "GDP is important and unmovable" case study, and the reason it leads this post.

**The 2022 "two negative quarters" non-recession.** Real GDP contracted in Q1 2022 (about −1.6% annualized) and again in Q2 2022 (about −0.6% annualized) — by the popular rule, a recession. Markets and the public debated whether the US was "in recession" for months. But the contraction was concentrated in inventories and net exports, while the consumer (70% of GDP) kept spending and the labor market stayed historically tight, with unemployment near 3.5%. The NBER never dated a recession. The lesson for correlation: GDP's volatile components can drag the headline negative even when the economy is expanding, which is exactly why a noisy aggregate makes a poor real-time trading signal.

**Retail sales surprises moving the consumer trade.** Through 2023 and into 2024, the months when control-group retail sales beat expectations were the months consumer-discretionary stocks and the 2-year yield jumped — and those moves happened on the *retail sales* day, weeks before the corresponding GDP print confirmed strong consumption. The market repeatedly demonstrated that it trades the timely consumer read, not the lagging total. When retail sales surprised soft, the same names fell and the front end rallied on hopes of earlier cuts. The consumer correlation is alive and tradable; it just lives upstream of GDP.

**The 2020 COVID swing nowcast in real time.** The −2.2% 2020 contraction and +5.9% 2021 rebound were the largest GDP swings in modern history, yet neither was a GDP-print surprise. Equity markets crashed roughly 34% into late March 2020 — before the catastrophic Q2 2020 GDP print — and bottomed and rallied hard through the rest of the year, well before the +5.9% 2021 number was anywhere near official. The market nowcast the collapse and the reopening from high-frequency data (mobility, jobless claims, card spending) and traded it months ahead of the quarterly aggregate. Even history's biggest GDP moves were old news by the time they printed.

The Q2 2020 print itself is the most extreme illustration of the whole thesis. When it landed in late July 2020, real GDP had collapsed at an annualized rate of roughly −30% — by far the worst quarterly contraction in the history of the series, a number that in any other context would have been apocalyptic. The stock market's reaction? It had already bottomed four months earlier and was, by late July, well into one of the sharpest recoveries on record. The single worst GDP number ever recorded coincided with a *rising* market, because the market was no longer pricing the depth of the collapse — it was pricing the recovery, the stimulus, and the reopening. If you needed one data point to prove that GDP confirms the past while markets price the future, the −30% print met by a rallying market is it. The correlation between the most important number and the market it supposedly drives was, at that moment, not just zero but negative — and entirely sensible once you understand that markets lead and GDP lags.

The COVID episode also showcased a new generation of *alternative* high-frequency data that has further eroded the GDP print's informational value. Card-spending trackers, restaurant-booking data, mobility indices, and shipping volumes now let nowcasters and traders read the consumer in near real time — daily, not quarterly. The more granular and timely the alternative data, the smaller the surprise left in the official aggregate by the time it prints. This is a one-way trend: as data gets faster, the lagging aggregates get *less* market-moving, not more. GDP's relevance as a tradable event has been shrinking for years and will keep shrinking, which only sharpens the lesson — the edge is in the timely inputs, and the inputs keep getting timelier.

## How to read it and use it

The playbook follows directly from the thesis. The signal you want is rarely GDP itself.

**The signal.** Watch the *timely consumer reads* — retail sales (especially the control group), personal spending — and the *timely business survey* (ISM/PMI). These carry the surprise and move the nowcast. When they surprise relative to consensus, the consumer-cyclical trade is live: cyclicals, copper, small caps, and the front end of the curve respond, defensives and long bonds fade. Trade the input that *changes the nowcast*, not the quarterly total that confirms it.

**The nowcast discipline.** Before any GDP release, check GDPNow and the bank trackers. The market's working expectation is the nowcast, not the official consensus headline. If GDPNow reads 2.6% and the print lands at 2.5%, that is not a "miss" to the market even if it is a miss to the published consensus — the nowcast already had it. The genuine surprise is the gap between the print and the *nowcast*, and that gap is almost always small.

**The regime check.** The sign of the consumer-cyclical correlation depends on where you are on the investment clock and on whether the market is in a "good news is good" or "good news is bad" mode. In early recovery and normal expansion, a growth surprise is risk-on. In a rate-fearful, higher-for-longer regime — or late-cycle overheat — a growth surprise can read hawkish and weigh on stocks even as yields rise. Identify the regime before you assign a sign. The same retail-sales beat is bullish or bearish for equities depending on the regime; it is reliably *bullish for the front-end yield* either way.

**Express it cross-asset, not just in equities.** A growth surprise is cleanest when you express it across several correlated legs at once, because the cross-asset map (the consumer-cyclical correlations above) tells you exactly which instruments carry the signal. The textbook risk-on growth expression is: long cyclicals and small caps (the high-positive cluster), long copper or a broad cyclical commodity, position for the front-end yield to rise (a stronger consumer keeps policy tighter), and underweight or short long-duration Treasuries and rate-sensitive defensives. Spreading the bet across legs that all key on the same growth surprise diversifies the *idiosyncratic* noise in any single instrument while concentrating the *macro* signal — which is the whole reason correlation maps are worth building. The same logic in reverse expresses a growth *disappointment*. Sizing each leg by its beta and your risk budget, as in the worked examples above, turns the correlation map into an actual portfolio.

**What invalidates the signal.** Revisions invalidate a GDP-print trade — the advance estimate is the least reliable of the three, so any edge built on it is fragile. A regime flip invalidates the *sign* of the consumer-cyclical correlation: if the market shifts into "strong data is hawkish," your risk-on growth trade can turn against you even as your growth thesis proves right. And the soft-data/hard-data divergence invalidates confidence-based reads: when sentiment and spending disagree, follow the spending. Above all, never size a position on the GDP level; size it on a timely, surprising input with a known beta.

The meta-lesson generalizes across this entire series. An indicator's correlation with markets is governed by **timeliness and surprise, not importance.** GDP is the most important number and one of the least correlated, because it lags and is fully nowcast. Retail sales and personal spending are less "important" in the textbook sense but far more correlated with the consumer trade, because they are timely and carry the surprise. When you meet a new macro indicator, do not ask "how important is this?" Ask "how timely is it, and how much of its content is already in the price?" That is the question that predicts whether it moves markets.
