In September 2022, a Vietnamese importer who had signed a contract to buy \$1,000,000 of machinery from Germany discovered that the bill had quietly gotten more expensive — not because the Germans raised the price, and not because the euro moved, but because the *dollar* had gone vertical. The US Dollar Index had just hit **114.8**, a two-decade high. The Fed had raised its policy rate from near zero to 3.25% in six months and was promising more. And the Vietnamese dong, which for years had been one of the most stable currencies in Asia, was slipping: USD/VND pushed from around 23,000 toward 24,000, and the State Bank of Vietnam was burning through its foreign-exchange reserves and hiking its own policy rate twice in a single month to defend it.

To a beginner, this looks like several unrelated problems landing at once: a US central-bank decision, a German invoice, a Vietnamese reserve drawdown, a rate hike in Hanoi. To anyone who understands the machinery, it was *one trade running through one variable*. The dollar is the world's denominator and its funding currency, and the gap between US interest rates and everyone else's is the price signal that pulls capital toward the higher-yielding currency. When the Fed hikes hard and the dollar surges while the SBV holds, the *rate differential* swings against the dong — and a managed-float emerging-market currency like the VND can only resist that gravity for so long before it gives, and before the move bleeds into domestic inflation, banks, and the stock market.

That is the thesis of this post, and it is worth stating plainly before we build the machinery underneath it: **the rate differential is the master correlation for the dong, and the dong is the channel into Vietnamese assets.** Read the DXY and the SBV-Fed gap, and you have a single dial that tells you whether the dong is about to be squeezed — and whether imported inflation, bank margins, and the VN-Index are about to feel it.

## Foundations: what an exchange rate is, and why a rate gap moves it

Before we can talk about *correlation* — the statistical measure of how two things move together — we have to be precise about what an exchange rate even is, and why an interest-rate gap on the other side of the world should move it.

### Correlation, in one minute

A **correlation** is a single number between -1 and +1 that summarizes how two things tend to move together. **+1** means they move in perfect lockstep (one up, the other always up by a proportional amount). **-1** means they move in perfect opposition (one up, the other always down). **0** means no linear relationship at all. The symbol statisticians use is *r* (Pearson's correlation coefficient). When we say "USD/VND and the DXY have a correlation of about +0.56," we mean: across the years 2019-2025, when the dollar rose against the world, the dong tended to weaken against the dollar too, and the relationship was moderately strong but far from mechanical. (For the precise machinery — Pearson vs. Spearman vs. beta, and why a single number hides regime shifts — see [what correlation actually measures](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/what-correlation-actually-measures-pearson-spearman-beta).)

The single most important thing to internalize, and the theme of this whole series, is that **correlation is a regime, not a constant.** The +0.56 between USD/VND and the dollar is a long-run average; in the calm pre-2022 years the dong barely moved at all, and in the 2022 stress it moved with the dollar almost step for step. We will return to this honesty again and again — and in Vietnam's case there is a special reason the correlation is *capped*, which is the central bank.

### What an exchange rate actually is

An **exchange rate** is just a price: how many units of one currency it takes to buy one unit of another. "USD/VND = 24,000" means it takes 24,000 Vietnamese dong to buy one US dollar. There is no single "value" of the dong floating in the ether — a currency is only ever worth something *relative to another currency*. When USD/VND **rises** (say from 23,000 to 25,000), it takes *more* dong to buy a dollar, which means the dong has **weakened** or **depreciated**. When USD/VND **falls**, the dong has **strengthened** or **appreciated**. A beginner must get this direction right and keep it straight, because it trips up almost everyone: *USD/VND up = dong down.* Throughout this post, "a weaker dong" and "USD/VND rises" mean the same thing.

A second distinction matters: an exchange rate has a **level** (is USD/VND at 23,000 or 25,000?) and a **rate of change** (is it rising or falling, and how fast?). Most correlations are with the *change*, not the level. The Vietnamese economy can live comfortably with a dong at 25,000 if it got there slowly and is stable; what frightens markets and central banks is a dong that is *moving fast* — a 3% slide in a month is a crisis signal in a managed currency, even though it would be a quiet day in a free-floating one.

There is also a third number lurking under any exchange rate: the **real** rate versus the **nominal** rate. The nominal rate is the one on the screen (24,000 VND per dollar). The *real* exchange rate adjusts for the difference in inflation between the two countries — it tells you whether Vietnamese goods are getting cheaper or more expensive *relative to American goods*, which is what actually drives trade competitiveness. If Vietnam's inflation runs persistently higher than US inflation, the dong needs to *depreciate in nominal terms just to keep the real rate flat* — otherwise Vietnamese exports get steadily more expensive and the trade surplus erodes. This is why a slow, managed nominal depreciation of the dong is not necessarily a sign of weakness: some of it is simply the nominal rate doing the work of keeping the *real* rate competitive when domestic inflation is a touch higher than America's. The market mistakes don't come from the slow grind; they come from the *fast* moves, when the nominal rate jumps far faster than any inflation gap can explain. That gap between "slow competitive drift" and "fast stress move" is exactly what the rate differential and the dollar help you tell apart. (For the deeper distinction between real and nominal — the master signal behind almost every macro correlation — see [real vs nominal: real yields are the master signal](/blog/trading/macro-trading/real-vs-nominal-inflation-real-yields-master-signal).)

### Why Vietnam manages the dong at all

It is worth pausing on *why* a country would choose to manage its currency rather than let it float. A free float (like the US dollar or the euro) lets the market set the price tick by tick, which is efficient but volatile. For a small, open, trade-dependent emerging economy like Vietnam, that volatility is genuinely dangerous: a sudden 20% currency swing can wreck an exporter's margins, blow up a firm with dollar debt, and ignite inflation overnight. Stability of the dong is treated as a near-policy-objective in its own right in Vietnam, because so much of the economy — exports, foreign direct investment, dollar-denominated contracts — depends on a predictable exchange rate. The cost of that stability is that the SBV must spend reserves and bend domestic interest rates to deliver it, and it must give up some independence: in the extreme, a country that pegs its currency tightly to the dollar effectively *imports US monetary policy*, because to hold the peg it must roughly track the Fed. Vietnam sits in the middle — a managed float, not a hard peg — which buys it *some* independence (it does not have to match the Fed move for move) at the cost of *some* dong volatility (the dong is allowed to drift). That middle position is exactly why the correlation with the dollar is real but capped: the SBV chose a system that lets the dollar through partway, on purpose.

This is a famous trade-off in international economics called the **"impossible trinity"** (or trilemma): a country can have at most two of three things — a fixed exchange rate, free movement of capital, and an independent monetary policy. It cannot have all three. If Vietnam wanted a rock-solid dong *and* fully open capital markets, it would have to surrender control of its own interest rates to the Fed. By choosing a *managed* (not fixed) float plus *partial* capital controls, Vietnam keeps a meaningful slice of monetary independence — which is precisely why the SBV could *cut* its policy rate in 2023 even as the Fed held high, accepting a weaker dong as the price. Every time you see the dong drift while the SBV does its own thing on rates, you are watching the trilemma being managed in real time. The correlation with the dollar is the *visible cost* of keeping that monetary independence.

### The rate differential and the carry idea: capital flows to the higher real rate

Here is the engine of the whole post. Money is mobile, and it chases yield. If you can earn 6% holding one currency and 1% holding another, and you think both are reasonably safe, you will tend to move money toward the 6% currency. That extra yield is called the **carry**, and the difference in interest rates between two currencies is the **rate differential**.

The logic, in everyday terms: imagine two savings accounts at two banks, one paying 6% and one paying 1%. If the money could flow freely and the deposits were equally safe, savers would pour into the 6% account until something gave — either the high-rate bank cut its rate, or the inflow itself changed the price (here, the exchange rate). In currency markets, that "something gives" shows up as the *exchange rate*: capital flowing into the high-yield currency *bids it up* (strengthens it), and capital fleeing the low-yield currency *pushes it down*. So the rate differential is a force on the exchange rate.

Now apply it to Vietnam. For most of the 2010s and into 2021, the SBV's policy rate sat well above the Fed's near-zero rate. A foreign investor (or a Vietnamese saver, or a corporate treasurer) could borrow dollars cheaply and hold dong deposits earning much more — pocketing the spread. That **carry inflow** was a tailwind for the dong: money wanted to be *in* VND, so USD/VND stayed remarkably flat. The differential was the dong's friend.

The danger of the carry trade is that it is *long the high-yield currency*, so it loses if that currency depreciates. The whole bet is: "I earn the rate spread, as long as the exchange rate doesn't move against me by more than the spread." When the Fed hikes and the dollar surges, two things happen at once — the *spread shrinks* (or reverses), and the *exchange rate moves against the carry* (the dong weakens). Both legs go wrong together. That is why carry trades "go up the stairs and down the elevator": years of quiet gains, then a sudden violent unwind. (The policy mechanism behind all of this — how a central bank raising rates pulls capital and strengthens its currency — is covered in depth in [how monetary policy moves currencies via rate differentials](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-currencies-rate-differentials); this post is about the measurable *correlation* that mechanism produces for the dong.)

There is a clean piece of theory worth knowing here, because it explains *why the carry both exists and is risky*. In a textbook world, **uncovered interest parity** would hold: a currency with a higher interest rate should be *expected* to depreciate by exactly that rate gap, so that on average no free lunch exists — the extra yield on the high-rate currency is precisely offset by its expected depreciation. If that held perfectly, the carry trade would earn nothing on average. The famous empirical fact is that it *doesn't* hold in the short and medium run: high-yield currencies tend *not* to depreciate as much as the rate gap implies (and often *appreciate*), so the carry trade earns positive returns for long stretches — the "forward premium puzzle." But — and this is the catch — those positive returns are punctuated by sudden, sharp losses when the high-yield currency crashes (a risk-off shock, a dollar surge). So carry is not a free lunch; it is *selling crash insurance*. You earn a steady premium for years and then pay it all back at once. For the dong, the managed float dampens both sides — the SBV smooths the steady drift *and* cushions the crash — which is why the dong's carry is steadier and its crashes milder than a free-floating EM currency's, but the *shape* of the trade (slow gains, fast losses) is the same.

A subtle but important point for Vietnam specifically: a lot of the "carry inflow" supporting the dong is not hot speculative money at all — it is **structural**. Vietnam runs a large trade surplus in many years (it exports more than it imports), it receives substantial **foreign direct investment** (factories built by Samsung, Foxconn, and others), and it gets sizable **remittances** from Vietnamese abroad. All three are persistent *dollar inflows* that the SBV typically absorbs by buying dollars and *building* reserves — which is the dong-supportive force in calm times. The carry-and-flow story is therefore two-layered: a structural surplus that supports the dong most years, plus a more fickle interest-differential flow that can reverse fast in stress. When people say "the dong is well-supported by fundamentals," they mean the structural layer; when the dong suddenly weakens anyway, it is usually the fickle layer (the differential and the dollar) overwhelming the structural one for a while. Knowing which layer is moving tells you whether a dong move is durable or a squeeze.

#### Worked example: the rate-differential carry on a \$100,000 position

Take the calm pre-2022 setup. Suppose a treasurer funds in dollars at about 2.5% and holds the proceeds in dong deposits earning about 6.5%. The **carry** is the difference: 6.5% - 2.5% = **4.0 percentage points** per year. On a \$100,000 position that is \$100,000 x 4.0% = **\$4,000 a year** in pure rate spread, *if the exchange rate doesn't move*.

Now stress it. If the dong depreciates 3% over the year (USD/VND rises 3%), the currency loss eats most of the carry: net = (4.0% - 3.0%) x \$100,000 = **\$1,000**. And in a 2022-style year where the dong slid about 3.5% even *after* heavy SBV defense, the net shrinks to (4.0% - 3.5%) x \$100,000 = just **\$500** — and a free-floating EM currency that fell 10% would have turned the \$4,000 carry into a roughly \$6,000 *loss*. The intuition: the carry pays you the rate gap, but the exchange rate can erase it in weeks, which is exactly why the differential *and* its direction matter so much.

### Why a managed-float EM currency still tracks the dollar

Vietnam does not let the dong float freely the way the US lets the dollar float. The SBV runs a **managed float** (sometimes called a crawling band): it sets a daily **central reference rate** and allows the market rate to trade within a band (currently about +/-5%) around it, and it *intervenes* — buying or selling dollars from its reserves — to keep the rate inside the band and to smooth moves. So you might ask: if the central bank controls the rate, why does it correlate with the DXY at all?

The answer is that the SBV is managing *against* the same gravity, not switching it off. The pressures — the rate differential, the dollar's strength, trade and capital flows — are real economic forces. The central bank can lean against them (slow the move, choose the timing, smooth the path) but it cannot make them disappear without paying a price in reserves or domestic interest rates. So the dong still *tracks* the dollar and the differential — the correlation is genuinely there — but the tracking is **damped and lagged**: the dong moves less than a free-floating currency would, and it moves later, because the SBV is actively braking. Think of the dollar as the current in a river and the SBV as a tugboat: the boat can hold position for a while and pick its moments, but it is fighting the current, and you can see the current in where the boat ends up.

### FX reserves and intervention: the SBV's ammunition

The tool the SBV uses to defend the dong is its **foreign-exchange reserves** — a stockpile of dollars (and other reserve assets) the central bank holds. To slow a weakening dong, the SBV *sells dollars and buys dong*: this adds dollar supply and dong demand to the market, pushing USD/VND back down. The catch is that reserves are finite. Every dollar sold to defend the currency is a dollar gone, and markets watch the reserve level like a fuel gauge. A standard rule of thumb (the IMF's) is that an economy should hold at least about **three months of imports** in reserves; falling below that is a red flag. So the SBV's defense is a balancing act: spend reserves to slow the dong, but don't spend so much that you run the tank low — at which point the only options left are to *let the dong go* or to *hike domestic rates hard* (which fights the differential but hurts the domestic economy). This is why reserve drawdowns are the **pressure valve** you watch to gauge how stressed the dong really is, beneath the managed surface.

## The empirical correlation: USD/VND vs the dollar

With the foundations in place, let's look at the actual data. The cleanest way to see the correlation is to put the dong and the dollar on the same chart.

![USD/VND and the DXY dollar index from 2019 to 2025 on a dual axis](/imgs/blogs/usdvnd-dxy-and-the-rate-differential-correlation-2.png)

The shape tells the story. From 2019 through 2021, the dong was almost flat — USD/VND actually *fell* slightly, from about 23,170 to 22,830, as carry inflows and a current-account surplus supported it while the dollar was weak (the DXY bottomed near 90 in 2020 amid the Fed's emergency easing). Then 2022 hit: the dollar surged to a two-decade high (DXY year-close 103.5, intraday peak 114.8), and the dong gave way, with USD/VND climbing to about 23,630. The slide continued through 2023 (24,270) and 2024 (25,300) as the Fed held rates high, reaching about 25,600 by 2025. Over the full 2019-2025 window, the correlation between USD/VND and the DXY works out to about **+0.56** — moderately strong and unmistakably positive: when the dollar rises against the world, the dong weakens against the dollar.

Two things are worth noting about that +0.56. First, it is *positive* because both series are quoted "dollars up": DXY up means the dollar is strong against the basket, and USD/VND up means the dollar is strong against the dong. Same direction, positive correlation. Second, +0.56 is *moderate, not 0.9* — and that is the SBV's fingerprint. A freely floating Asian currency over the same period (the Japanese yen, say) tracked the dollar far more tightly and fell far more, from about 108 to 157 per dollar — roughly a 45% depreciation, versus the dong's ~11%. The dong's tamer move and looser correlation are exactly what the managed float is *designed* to produce. The dollar's gravity is the same; the SBV is the brake.

It is worth being honest about a measurement subtlety here, because it is the heart of this whole series. A correlation computed on seven annual data points is a *teaching* number, not a precise estimate — with so few observations the figure is sensitive to the window you choose, and you should treat the **+0.56 as "moderate and positive," not as a decimal to trust to two places.** If you computed it on weekly data the number would jump around; if you computed it only over 2022 it would be much higher (the dong tracked the dollar closely in the acute stress); if you computed it only over 2019-21 it would be near zero (the dong barely moved while the dollar swung). This is the series' refrain made concrete: **the correlation is a regime, not a constant**, and a managed currency's correlation is *especially* regime-dependent because the central bank actively tightens or loosens the leash depending on conditions. The honest statement is directional: the dong tracks the dollar, the tracking is partial because of intervention, and the tracking *tightens in stress* — which is exactly when you most need to know it. (For why the window you choose changes the answer, and how to compute a rolling correlation that respects regimes, see [rolling correlation and why the window matters](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/rolling-correlation-and-why-the-window-matters).)

There is also a **lead/lag** wrinkle. The dollar and the differential do not move the dong instantly; the managed float introduces a deliberate *lag*. The DXY and the SBV-Fed gap tend to *lead* the dong, because the SBV first absorbs the pressure (selling reserves, smoothing the move) and only lets the rate adjust later, often in steps, when the pressure becomes too expensive to keep absorbing. So in practice you frequently see the dollar surge and the differential collapse *first*, with the dong's visible move arriving weeks or months afterward — which is precisely why the reserve drawdown is such a useful *leading* indicator: the reserves move *before* the exchange rate does, because they are what is being spent to delay the exchange rate's move. A trader watching only the USD/VND screen is watching the *most-lagged* variable in the chain; the dollar, the differential, and the reserve gauge all lead it.

### The scatter and the beta: how much dong per dollar point

A line chart shows the co-movement; a **scatter plot with a fitted line** quantifies it. Put the DXY on the horizontal axis and USD/VND on the vertical, one dot per year, and fit a straight line through the cloud.

![Scatter of DXY against USD/VND with a fitted regression line](/imgs/blogs/usdvnd-dxy-and-the-rate-differential-correlation-5.png)

The fitted line has a slope of about **0.10 thousand VND per DXY point** — meaning each one-point rise in the dollar index is associated with roughly **100 more dong** per dollar. The slope (in finance we'd call it the **beta** of USD/VND to the DXY) is the *sensitivity*: it answers "if the dollar moves this much, how much does the dong move?" The correlation (+0.56) tells you how *reliable* the relationship is; the beta tells you how *big* the move is. You need both — a high correlation with a tiny beta means the relationship is dependable but the dong barely budges, while a big beta with a low correlation means the dong sometimes moves a lot but not predictably.

#### Worked example: a USD/VND move from a DXY move

Suppose the dollar rallies 8 points — the kind of move that happened in a few months of 2022 (the DXY climbed from the mid-90s to the low-110s). Using the beta of about 0.10 thousand VND per DXY point, the implied move in the dong is 8 x 0.10 = **0.81 thousand VND**, or about **810 dong per dollar**. Starting from the 2023 level of about 24,270, that points the dong toward roughly **25,080 VND per dollar**.

Now translate it into a real bill. Our importer buying \$1,000,000 of machinery faces an extra 810 dong x 1,000,000 = **810,400,000 VND** in cost from the FX move alone — about **\$33,000** worth of dong, on a contract whose dollar price never changed. The intuition: a "small" 8-point move in an index most Vietnamese have never heard of quietly added tens of thousands of dollars to a single import contract, because the exchange rate is the ruler every cross-border price is measured with.

## The master driver: the SBV-Fed rate differential

The DXY is the dollar's strength against a rich-world basket; it is a *symptom* as much as a cause. The deeper driver of the dong, and the one a Vietnam watcher should put at the center of the dashboard, is the **policy-rate differential between the SBV and the Fed.** When you chart the two policy rates together, the 2022-23 stress jumps out.

![SBV refinancing rate Fed funds upper bound and the differential from 2019 to 2025](/imgs/blogs/usdvnd-dxy-and-the-rate-differential-correlation-3.png)

For 2019-2021, the SBV's refinancing rate sat well above the Fed's: the differential (SBV minus Fed) was a comfortable **+3.75 to +4.25 percentage points**. The dong yielded far more than the dollar, carry flowed in, and USD/VND was flat. Then the Fed hiked at the fastest pace in 40 years — from a 0.25% upper bound to 4.50% by the end of 2022 and 5.50% by mid-2023 — while the SBV moved more slowly. By the end of 2022 the differential had collapsed to **+1.5pp**, and in 2023 it went *negative*: with the Fed at 5.50% and the SBV cutting its refinancing rate to 4.50% to support a slowing domestic economy, the dong was earning *less* than the dollar for the first time in the period, a differential of about **-1.0pp**.

This is the crux. A negative differential means the **carry has flipped**: instead of being paid to hold dong, you are paid to hold dollars. Capital that had flowed in now had a reason to flow out, and the dong slid through 2023-24 even though the worst of the DXY's strength had passed. The differential explains *why* the dong kept weakening after the dollar peaked: the *level* of US rates stayed high relative to Vietnamese rates, so the carry stayed unfavorable. The DXY tells you about the dollar's broad strength; the differential tells you about the dong's *specific* incentive to hold or sell — and the differential is the variable the SBV can actually influence, by choosing how far to follow the Fed.

Notice the bind the SBV was in, because it explains why the dong had to give. In 2023 Vietnam's domestic economy was *slowing* — export demand was soft, the property sector was under stress, and growth needed support. The textbook domestic response is to *cut* rates to stimulate. But cutting rates *widens* the negative differential, which *weakens the dong further*. So the SBV faced two objectives in direct conflict: support domestic growth (argues for cuts) versus defend the dong (argues for hikes, or at least no cuts). It chose to prioritize the *domestic* economy and cut to 4.50%, accepting a weaker dong as the price. This is the trilemma from earlier made concrete: with the Fed at 5.50%, Vietnam could keep its monetary independence (cut rates for its own economy) *or* keep the dong strong, but not both. Every emerging-market central bank faces this fork in a Fed-tightening cycle, and which way it jumps tells you whether to expect currency defense (rate hikes, reserve sales) or currency depreciation (rate cuts, a softer FX line). Vietnam in 2023 chose the latter — which is *why* the dong kept sliding even as the dollar eased.

This also reframes which variable to watch *when*. The DXY is the broad, fast, global signal — it tells you about acute dollar surges that hit every EM at once. The differential is the slower, dong-specific signal — it tells you about the *sustained* incentive to hold or sell the dong, and it can keep the dong under pressure long after the dollar's broad move is over. In the dashboard later, the differential sits *above* the DXY in importance for exactly this reason: it is the variable that explains the persistent grind, and it is the one the SBV's own choices feed into.

#### Worked example: the implied carry when the differential flips

In 2019, a foreign investor holding \$10,000,000 in dong deposits earned the +4.0pp-ish carry: about \$10,000,000 x 4.0% = **\$400,000 a year** in rate spread (before any FX move). By 2023, with the differential at roughly -1.0pp, that *same* position now *costs* about \$10,000,000 x 1.0% = **\$100,000 a year** to hold relative to simply parking the cash in dollars — a swing of half a million dollars a year in the incentive, on a \$10 million book. No wonder the flow reversed. The intuition: the differential isn't an abstraction, it's a *cash carry* on every dollar of positioning, and when it flips sign the entire global investor base's reason to hold the dong flips with it.

### Putting the regime together

The differential and the dollar interact, and the cleanest way to see the *regimes* is a small matrix: what the differential was, which way capital flowed, and what happened to the dong.

![Matrix of the SBV minus Fed differential capital flow and USD/VND outcome by period](/imgs/blogs/usdvnd-dxy-and-the-rate-differential-correlation-6.png)

The three rows are three regimes. In **2019-21**, the differential was wide and positive (+3.75 to +4.25pp), carry inflows dominated, and the dong was firm near 22,800-23,100. In **2022**, the dollar surged (DXY to 114.8) and the differential narrowed fast to +1.5pp; flows turned and USD/VND pushed to 23,630 even as the SBV hiked and sold reserves to slow it. In **2023**, the Fed peaked at 5.50% while the SBV *cut* to 4.50%, driving the differential to -1.0pp; the carry went negative and the dong slid to 24,270, then on toward 25,600 by 2025. The regime you are in selects which force dominates — and the regime is set by the gap between two central banks, one of which (the Fed) Vietnam does not control.

## The pass-through chain: from the dong into Vietnam's assets

So far we have the *currency*. The reason a Vietnamese investor (or anyone with exposure to VN assets) must care is that the currency is the **channel** through which global conditions reach domestic markets. A weaker dong does not stay politely in the FX market; it passes through into inflation, banks, and equities. This is the second half of the thesis: the rate differential moves the dong, and the dong moves everything domestic.

![How a higher USD/VND splits into import cost foreign debt and outflow channels into VN assets](/imgs/blogs/usdvnd-dxy-and-the-rate-differential-correlation-4.png)

A rising USD/VND fans out into three channels:

1. **Imported inflation.** Vietnam is a deeply trade-integrated economy — it imports raw materials, machinery, oil, and intermediate goods, much of it invoiced in dollars. When the dong weakens, every dollar-priced import costs *more dong*. That feeds straight into producer costs and, with a lag, into consumer prices. The dong's slide is, mechanically, an upward push on **VN CPI**.
2. **Foreign-currency debt service.** Vietnamese firms (and the government) that borrowed in dollars must repay in dollars — and a weaker dong means each dollar of debt costs *more dong* to service. That stresses corporate balance sheets and the banks exposed to them.
3. **Foreign portfolio outflows.** Foreign investors holding Vietnamese stocks earn returns in dong; if they expect the dong to fall, their dong gains are worth fewer dollars when repatriated. A depreciating dong is therefore a reason for foreigners to *sell* HOSE-listed equities and take their dollars home, which directly pressures the VN-Index. (Foreign net buying correlates about +0.50 with the VN-Index in the data; a weaker dong tilts that toward net selling.)

All three channels converge on the same place — the SBV is forced to respond (hike rates, sell reserves), and Vietnamese banks and the **VN-Index** bear the pressure. This is why the imported correlation exists at all: it runs *through the currency*.

The **banking channel** deserves a closer look, because banks are the single largest sector in the VN-Index and the place where currency stress concentrates. A weaker dong hits banks in two ways. First, the SBV's defense — hiking the policy rate and letting interbank rates spike — raises banks' *funding costs* faster than they can reprice their loan books, squeezing their **net interest margin** (the spread between what they earn on loans and pay on deposits). Second, banks carry credit exposure to firms with **dollar liabilities** (importers, dollar-borrowers); when the dong weakens, those borrowers' debt-service costs jump, raising the risk of bad loans on the banks' books. Both effects are *pro-cyclical* with the currency: the same dollar surge that weakens the dong tightens bank margins and raises bank credit risk, all at once. Since financials are the heaviest weight in the VN-Index, this is a big part of *why* the index correlates negatively with the dollar — the currency stress lands hardest on the index's biggest sector. It is also why a Vietnamese banking-sector wobble and a dong-defense episode often coincide: they are two faces of the same tightening.

One more nuance on the foreign-flow channel, because it has a *reflexive* twist. When foreigners sell HOSE equities and convert the dong proceeds back to dollars, that conversion *itself* adds to USD/VND pressure — selling stocks means selling dong, which weakens the dong further, which gives the next foreigner more reason to sell. This is a small-scale version of the "dollar doom loop" that hits larger emerging markets: the currency move and the equity outflow feed each other. The SBV's job in a stress episode is partly to *break this loop* — by selling reserves to absorb the dong selling and by signaling that it will defend the currency, it removes the reason for the next investor to rush the exit. When you see foreign net selling on HOSE accelerating *alongside* a weakening dong, you are watching the loop spin; when you see the SBV step in with reserves and rate hikes, you are watching it try to break it.

### The correlation from the currency into equities

The summary statistic for the second half of the chain is how the VN-Index itself correlates with the relevant macro drivers.

![VN-Index correlations with US stocks emerging markets the dollar and SBV variables](/imgs/blogs/usdvnd-dxy-and-the-rate-differential-correlation-7.png)

The bars split neatly into an **imported leg** and a **domestic leg**. On the imported side, the VN-Index correlates about **+0.45 with the S&P 500** and **+0.55 with MSCI EM** (Vietnam is a high-beta emerging market that rises and falls with global risk appetite), and crucially about **-0.40 with the DXY** and **-0.25 with the US 10-year yield**. That negative dollar correlation *is* the currency channel: a stronger dollar weakens the dong, drives foreign outflows, and pressures Vietnamese stocks. On the domestic side, the VN-Index correlates about **-0.45 with changes in the SBV policy rate** (tighter SBV policy is a headwind for stocks) and about **-0.30 with rising VN CPI**. Notice how these connect: a strong dollar -> weaker dong -> higher imported inflation -> SBV tightens -> domestic headwind. The same shock travels the whole chain, which is why the legs reinforce each other in a stress episode. (For the imported-risk channel in full — VN-Index's beta to global risk and foreign flows — see [VN-Index and US macro: the imported correlation](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/vn-index-and-us-macro-the-imported-correlation); for the domestic leg in full — SBV, credit growth, and property — see [VN inflation, SBV, and the domestic-asset correlation](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/vn-inflation-sbv-and-the-domestic-asset-correlation).)

#### Worked example: imported-inflation pass-through from an FX move

Suppose the dong depreciates 5% over a year (USD/VND rises from, say, 24,000 to 25,200). Not all of that reaches consumer prices — many goods are domestic, contracts adjust slowly, and firms absorb some of the cost in margins. A reasonable teaching **pass-through coefficient** for Vietnam is about 0.15, meaning roughly 15% of an FX move shows up in consumer inflation within a year. So 5% x 0.15 = **0.75 percentage points** added to CPI. If underlying CPI was running at 3.2%, the FX move alone could lift it toward **3.95%** — pushing it toward the SBV's comfort ceiling of around 4%. The intuition: the dong's slide is a slow, partial, but real tax on every imported good, and it is exactly what forces the central bank's hand even when domestic demand is soft. (For how a Vietnamese CPI or SBV surprise actually moves the VN-Index on the day, see [Vietnam events: SBV, VN CPI, and how VN-Index reacts](/blog/trading/event-trading/vietnam-events-sbv-vn-cpi-and-how-vn-index-reacts).)

## Common misconceptions

A few myths cause more bad VN-macro takes than anything else. Each one is corrected with a number.

**Myth 1: "The SBV controls the dong, so the DXY doesn't matter."** No — the SBV *manages* the dong against the dollar's gravity; it does not switch the gravity off. The +0.56 correlation between USD/VND and the DXY over 2019-2025 is the proof: the dong still tracked the dollar, it just tracked it *less* (and later) than a free float would. The right way to say it is that the SBV controls the *path and the speed*, not the *direction*. When the dollar wanted the dong lower, the dong went lower — about 11% over the cycle — the SBV just made it orderly instead of violent.

**Myth 2: "A stable dong means there's no pressure."** Dangerous. A managed currency can look calm on the surface while the pressure builds *underneath* — in the reserve account. In 2022 the dong's *visible* move was modest (a few percent) precisely *because* the SBV spent an estimated \$20 billion-plus of reserves and hiked rates to keep it that way. The flat line on the FX chart was bought, not free. Always read the reserve gauge and the policy-rate response alongside the exchange rate; the calm surface can hide an expensive defense.

**Myth 3: "The dong weakened because of Vietnam's economy."** Mostly no — in 2022-23 the dong weakened because of *US* monetary policy. Vietnam ran current-account surpluses and solid growth through much of the period; the dong's slide lines up with the Fed's hiking cycle and the DXY's surge, not with a domestic crisis. This is the essence of an *imported* shock: the cause is exogenous (the Fed, the dollar), and the differential is the transmission. Blaming the local economy for a globally-driven currency move is a classic error.

**Myth 4: "The differential is just the headline policy rates."** Be careful — what really drives carry is the *real* and *market* differential, not only the two headline policy rates. Vietnamese deposit and interbank rates can sit well above the SBV's refinancing rate, and inflation differentials matter for the *real* return. The headline SBV-vs-Fed gap is the cleanest single proxy and it captures the regime well, but in a tight spot the SBV often lets *interbank* rates spike far above the policy rate to defend the dong without formally hiking — a move you'd miss if you only watched the headline rate. The policy-rate differential is the signal; the interbank rate is the fine print.

**Myth 5: "USD/VND and DXY move together one-for-one."** No — the beta is small (about 100 dong per DXY point) and the correlation is moderate (+0.56), not 1.0. The dong is *managed*, so it absorbs and lags the dollar. Expecting a tick-for-tick relationship will get you stopped out; the link is a *tendency over months*, capped by intervention, not a high-frequency tracker.

## How it shows up in real markets

Two episodes — back to back — show the correlation working in stress and then relaxing in relief. They are the same machinery running forward and then in reverse.

### 2022: the dollar surge, the dong's slide, and the SBV's defense

The 2022 episode is the textbook case. The Fed, fighting 40-year-high US inflation, raised its policy rate from a 0.25% upper bound in March to 4.50% by December — and signaled it would go higher still. The DXY surged to **114.8** in late September, a two-decade high. The SBV-Fed differential collapsed from a comfortable +4pp toward +1.5pp. Capital that had happily held dong for the carry now had a shrinking incentive, and USD/VND pushed up toward and through the top of its band.

The SBV's response was a three-pronged defense, and watching all three is how you read the *true* stress:

1. **It sold reserves.** Vietnam's FX reserves, which had built to roughly **\$110 billion** at the 2021 peak, were drawn down to an estimated **\$86-90 billion** through 2022 as the central bank sold dollars to slow the dong — a drawdown on the order of **\$20-24 billion**, or roughly 20% of the stockpile. That is the pressure valve, visible in the reserve gauge even though the FX rate stayed relatively orderly.
2. **It hiked rates.** The SBV raised its policy rates *twice* in a single month (September-October 2022), lifting the refinancing rate from 4.0% toward 6.0% — directly fighting the differential by raising the dong's yield.
3. **It widened the trading band**, from +/-3% to +/-5%, giving the market room to find a level without the central bank having to defend a tighter line at all costs.

The result: the dong's *visible* depreciation in 2022 was contained to a few percent — far less than the yen (-19%) or many EM peers — but only because the defense was expensive. This is the correlation *with the cap on*: the dollar's gravity was fully present, the SBV just paid (in reserves and domestic rates) to absorb most of it.

#### Worked example: sizing a reserve drawdown defense

Frame the reserve gauge in fuel-tank terms. With Vietnamese imports running around **\$30 billion a month**, the IMF's three-month adequacy floor is about **\$90 billion** of reserves. So a stock of \$86-90 billion in late 2022 was right at — or marginally below — the comfort floor: about **2.9 months** of import cover. That is why the SBV could not simply keep selling dollars indefinitely to peg the dong: spending another \$10-15 billion would have pushed reserves into the danger zone. The drawdown of roughly \$20-24 billion from the 2021 peak was the central bank deciding how much "fuel" it was willing to burn before switching to the other tools (rate hikes, a wider band). The intuition: a managed-float defense is *budgeted* — the correlation with the dollar is capped only as long as there is ammunition, and the reserve gauge tells you how much cap is left.

### 2023: the Fed pause and the dong's relief

By mid-2023 the Fed stopped hiking (the upper bound topped out at 5.50% in July 2023), and the market began to anticipate eventual cuts. The dollar came off its highs (DXY back toward 101 by year-end). That should have been pure relief for the dong — and partly it was, in the sense that the *violent* phase of pressure ended and the SBV could rebuild reserves and even *cut* its own rate to support domestic growth.

But here is the honest nuance: the dong did *not* snap back. USD/VND kept drifting up (to 24,270 in 2023 and 25,300 in 2024) even as the DXY fell. Why? Because the *differential* stayed unfavorable. The Fed held at 5.50% for a year, and the SBV *cut* to 4.50% to support a soft domestic economy — so the carry stayed negative (about -1.0pp) even after the dollar's broad strength faded. This is a critical lesson about which variable matters when: in the acute 2022 phase, the *DXY* (the dollar's broad surge) drove the dong; in the 2023-24 grind, the *differential* (US rates staying high relative to Vietnamese rates) drove it. The dong tracks *both* the dollar and the gap, and they don't always point the same way. When they diverge — dollar softening but differential still negative — the differential tends to win for the dong, because it is the *specific* carry incentive, not the broad basket.

### Historical context: how Vietnam used to handle it

The 2022-23 episode looks orderly *by Vietnamese standards*, and that is itself the story. Go back a decade or so and the dong's adjustments to dollar and external pressure were far cruder. In the years around the 2008 global financial crisis and the 2011 domestic inflation spike (when Vietnamese CPI ran into the high teens), the dong was repeatedly **devalued in step-jumps** — the SBV would defend a line, run low on the ammunition to hold it, and then reset the reference rate sharply lower in one move, sometimes several times in a year. Those step-devaluations were destabilizing: they came as a shock, they fed inflation expectations, and they taught Vietnamese savers to dollarize (hold dollars and gold instead of dong) at the first sign of trouble, which made the next defense harder.

The contrast with 2022 is the point. By 2022 the SBV had built a far larger reserve buffer (the ~\$110 billion peak versus a fraction of that in 2011), a credible inflation record (CPI held near 3-4%, not the teens), and a more flexible band mechanism. So when the dollar surged, Vietnam could absorb the pressure with a *managed glide* — reserve sales, two rate hikes, a band widening — rather than a panic step-devaluation. The dong fell, but it fell *smoothly and modestly*, and savers did not stampede into dollars. The lesson for reading the correlation: a managed float's ability to *cap and smooth* the dollar's gravity is not free or automatic — it is bought with reserves and earned with a track record of low inflation. The same external shock produces a crisis in a weakly-armed regime and a quiet drift in a well-armed one. When you assess how much the dong can resist the dollar in the *next* cycle, the question is always the same: how much ammunition (reserves) and how much credibility (inflation track record) does the central bank have? Those two stocks set the cap on the correlation.

### The carry/differential trade, in practice

For a trader or treasurer, the differential *is* the trade. When the SBV-Fed gap is wide and positive and the dollar is weak or stable (2019-21), the dong is a *carry magnet*: you're paid to hold it, and the FX risk is low because the SBV is happy to let inflows keep the dong firm. When the gap narrows or flips and the dollar is rising (2022), the carry trade is a trap: the spread shrinks while the FX moves against you, and the unwind can be sudden. The discipline is to *size the carry against the FX risk* — a 4pp carry is attractive only if you don't expect the dong to fall more than 4% — and to watch the differential's *direction*, not just its level, because the sign of the carry flips well before the policy rates fully cross.

## How to read it and use it

Here is the playbook the correlation implies, compressed into a routine you can run.

**The dashboard (in order of importance):**

1. **The SBV-Fed policy-rate differential, and its direction.** This is the master signal. Wide and positive (and widening) = dong supported, carry friendly. Narrowing or negative (and falling) = dong pressured, carry unfriendly. The *direction* leads the *level* — the carry incentive flips before the rates fully cross.
2. **The DXY.** A rising dollar is a broad tightening that pressures every EM currency, the dong included. Watch the *rate of change*: a dollar that is high but stable is far friendlier than a dollar that is lower but rising fast.
3. **FX reserves.** The pressure valve. A falling reserve level (especially toward the ~3-month-of-imports floor, around \$90 billion for Vietnam) tells you the dong's calm is being *bought*, and that the cap on the correlation is wearing thin.
4. **Interbank rates.** The SBV often defends via interbank rates *before* it touches the headline policy rate. A spike in overnight interbank rates is an early defense signal you'd miss watching only the refinancing rate.

**The regime check.** Decide which variable is driving. In an acute *dollar-surge* phase (DXY spiking), the DXY leads and the dong's risk is highest. In a *differential* phase (dollar calm but US rates high vs Vietnamese rates), the carry sign leads and the dong grinds in that direction. Match your expectation to the regime; don't fight the dollar in a surge or fight the differential in a grind.

**The pass-through read.** Once you have a view on the dong, you have a view on the chain. A weakening-dong regime tilts you toward: higher imported inflation (watch VN CPI), eventual SBV tightening (a headwind for the VN-Index and property), foreign outflows (a headwind for HOSE), and stress in dollar-borrowers and the banks exposed to them. A strengthening-dong regime (wide positive differential, weak dollar) tilts the other way — the classic risk-on setup for VN equities. (For the full domestic chain — SBV, credit growth, property, banks — see [VN inflation, SBV, and the domestic-asset correlation](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/vn-inflation-sbv-and-the-domestic-asset-correlation).)

**What invalidates the signal.** Three things break the playbook. First, **a large reserve war chest** lets the SBV cap the correlation harder and longer — a well-armed central bank can hold the dong against an unfavorable differential for a long time, so don't assume a negative carry mechanically means a falling dong *this quarter*. Second, **capital controls and band changes** are policy choices that can suddenly change how much the dong is *allowed* to move — a band widening (as in 2022) lets the correlation through, a tightening or new controls cap it. Third, **a domestic shock** (a banking-sector wobble, a property-credit event) can drive the dong independently of the global picture, decoupling it from the DXY for a stretch. In all three cases the lesson is the series' refrain: the correlation is a regime, not a constant — and in Vietnam's case, the regime is partly *chosen* by the central bank.

**The dollarization and gold escape valve.** One Vietnam-specific factor sits underneath everything: Vietnamese households have a long memory of currency instability, and when they lose confidence in the dong they shift savings into dollars and **gold**. This is a slow-moving but powerful force — if a dong-weakening episode is mishandled and households start to dollarize, domestic dollar demand rises *on top of* the external pressure, making the defense far harder and the correlation with the dollar more violent. It is precisely to prevent this that the SBV prizes a smooth, credible dong and low inflation: a population that trusts the dong does not rush to dollars at the first wobble, which keeps the correlation capped. So when you assess the dong, watch not only the external drivers (DXY, differential, reserves) but the *domestic confidence* signal — gold premiums, deposit dollarization, and the tone of policy communication. A loss of domestic confidence is the thing that turns a managed drift into a disorderly slide, and it is the one input that can make the dollar's gravity overwhelm even a well-armed central bank. (Gold's role as the Vietnamese household's inflation-and-currency hedge connects to the broader [inflation and gold: the real-yield story](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/inflation-and-gold-the-real-yield-story).)

**The one-line summary to carry around:** when the Fed out-yields the SBV and the dollar is rising, expect the dong to weaken, imported inflation to firm, and the VN-Index to feel the pull — and watch the reserve gauge to see how long the SBV can keep the lid on. (For the dollar's role as the global denominator that makes all of this possible, see [the dollar (DXY): cross-asset gravity](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/the-dollar-dxy-cross-asset-correlation) and [dollar system: why USD rules markets](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy); for the front-end rate that anchors the US side of the differential, see [the Fed funds path and the front-end correlation](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/the-fed-funds-path-and-front-end-correlation).)
