> **TL;DR** — A macro correlation will burn you in eight predictable ways, and each has a one-line fix; the master mistake is treating a number that is really a *regime* as if it were a *constant*.
>
> - The single most expensive error is the **static full-sample number**. The correlation between gold and the 10-year real yield over 2007-2025 is **-0.01** — which looks like "no relationship" but is the average of **-0.96** (2007-2021) and **+0.80** (2022-2025). The zero is a flip, not an absence.
> - **Diversification fails when you need it.** The average pairwise correlation across risk assets is ~**0.25** in calm markets but jumps to ~**0.80-0.85** in a crisis. The hedge you bought evaporates exactly in the crash.
> - **The asset reacts to the surprise, not the level.** A hot CPI print can rally markets if it came in *below* expectations; a +0.1pp core-CPI *upside surprise* moved the S&P about **-0.70%** and the 2-year yield about **+9bp** in 2022-23.
> - The one number to remember: **-0.01**. Whenever a correlation looks like "zero," ask whether you are averaging a +0.8 and a -0.96 into nothing.

In late 2013 a multi-strategy fund put on what looked like one of the safest trades in macro: short gold against rising real yields. The logic was airtight and the statistics were beautiful. For six years running, the 10-year inflation-protected (TIPS) yield and the price of gold had moved in near-perfect opposition — when the real yield rose, gold fell, and the correlation over that window was about **-0.9**. Gold pays you nothing; a real bond pays you a positive real return; so when the real return on a safe bond climbs, the case for holding the shiny rock that yields zero weakens, and its price drops. Mechanism, statistics, and the trade all agreed. The desk sized it as a core position.

For most of the next decade it worked, and then in 2022 it stopped working in the worst possible way. Real yields ripped higher — the 10-year TIPS yield went from **-0.95%** at the end of 2021 to **+1.95%** by 2024, one of the most violent real-rate selloffs in modern history. By the old correlation, gold should have collapsed. Instead gold *rose*, from about \$1,799 to \$2,390 an ounce, a gain of roughly **33%** straight into the teeth of the move that was supposed to kill it. The desk that was short gold against rising real yields lost money on both the level and the basis. The correlation had not weakened; it had *inverted*, flipping from about **-0.96** to about **+0.80** as central banks bought gold for reasons that had nothing to do with the real-yield arithmetic. The trade was not wrong about the past. It was wrong about assuming the past was permanent.

This post is the mistakes chapter of the series — the consolidated list of the recurring ways a macro correlation gets people hurt, each paired with the number that exposes it and the single habit that defuses it. Every other post in this series teaches you a *correlation*: what stocks do with bonds, what gold does with real yields, what crypto does with liquidity. This one is the negative image of all of them. It is the honesty thread of the whole series pulled into one reference you can keep open while you work. Read it as a pre-flight checklist: before you trade, write into a research note, or believe a chart, run the relationship through these eight gates. What survives all eight is worth sizing. What fails any one of them is, eventually, a way to lose money.

## Foundations: what a correlation is, and the three things it hides

Before we can catalogue the mistakes, we have to be precise about the object we are abusing. Let us build it from zero, because half the mistakes come from not understanding what the number *is*.

A **correlation** is a single number, written r, that measures how tightly two quantities move together in a straight-line sense. It runs from **-1** (perfect opposite lockstep: when one goes up by a step, the other goes down by a proportional step, every time) through **0** (no straight-line relationship at all) to **+1** (perfect lockstep up together). The arithmetic is mechanical. Take two columns of numbers, x and y. For each row, measure how far x sits from its own average and how far y sits from its own average. Multiply those two deviations together. Sum that product across every row — that sum is the **covariance**, the raw "do they tend to be high together and low together?" quantity. Then divide by the spread (the standard deviation) of each series, which strips out the units and the scale so the answer always lands between -1 and +1. If you want this built fully from scratch — Pearson versus Spearman versus a regression beta — see the foundations of this series. For the mistakes ahead, three facts about that formula matter more than the formula itself.

**Fact one: the formula has no idea where the numbers came from.** Feed it any two columns and it returns a confident number to two decimal places. It does not know whether the two series trend, whether a third thing drives both, whether you cherry-picked the window, or whether any mechanism connects them. The number's confidence is not evidence of a relationship. This is the root of mistakes one, four, and six.

**Fact two: the formula collapses time into a single average.** A correlation computed over a window gives you *one* number for the *whole* window. If the relationship was -0.96 for the first three-quarters of the window and +0.80 for the last quarter, the formula does not warn you; it quietly averages them and hands you something near zero. The single number erases the most important feature of the data — that it *changed*. This is the root of mistakes two, three, and eight.

**Fact three: the formula assumes the inputs are stable.** It implicitly treats each series as having a fixed average to measure deviations *from*. Macro and price series almost never have a fixed average — they trend, drift, and shift regimes. When the assumption is violated, the number is contaminated. This is the root of mistakes four and five.

Here is the everyday analogy that frames the whole post. Suppose you measure the correlation between "umbrellas open on the street" and "the pavement is wet." You will find a strong positive number, and you might conclude that opening umbrellas wets the pavement. The number is real; the conclusion is nonsense. A third thing — rain — drives both. Now suppose you measure that same correlation only on days you happened to sample, and you sampled mostly during a drought. You will get a different, weaker number, and if you quote it as "the" correlation you will mislead everyone. And suppose the climate shifts and it starts raining at night, after the streets are already wet from cleaning trucks — your old umbrella-pavement correlation breaks, even though nothing about your formula changed. Those three failures — *the third variable*, *the sampled window*, and *the shifting world* — are the spine of almost every correlation mistake a trader makes. We are about to give each of them a number and a fix.

One last piece of vocabulary, because it recurs in three of the mistakes: the difference between a **level** and a **change**. The *level* of gold is its price: \$1,800, then \$1,943, then \$2,390. The *change* is how much it moved between observations: +\$143, then +\$447. Levels of financial and macro series almost always trend — they drift persistently in one direction over years. Changes wobble around zero. Whether you correlate levels or changes is the single most consequential choice in computing a macro correlation, and getting it wrong is mistake number four. Keep the distinction in your pocket.

There is one more concept that turns a correlation from a number you observe into something you can reason about: the **r-squared**, which is simply the correlation squared, and which tells you what *fraction of the variation* in one series the other explains. A correlation of 0.7 sounds strong until you square it — 0.49, so the relationship accounts for under half the movement, with the majority left to other drivers and noise. A correlation of 0.3 squares to 0.09: the relationship explains barely **9%** of what happens, and the other **91%** is everything else. This single squaring is a built-in humility check. When a research note quotes an exciting 0.5 correlation, square it: the relationship explains **25%** of the moves, which means three-quarters of the time the "signal" is being overwhelmed by other forces. Most macro correlations that look tradeable on a chart have r-squareds in the 0.1-to-0.3 range — real, but weak, and easily swamped. Carrying the r-squared in your head is the cheapest defence against over-sizing a position on a correlation that sounds stronger than it is. It will matter directly in mistake four, where a fake 0.95 squares to 0.90 (90% explained — the giveaway that the number is too good to be real) while the honest 0.18 squares to 0.03 (3% explained — the sobering truth).

The reason any of this is hard — the reason macro demands more paranoia than, say, a physicist measuring a spring — is that the data is **observational, not experimental**. There has been exactly one history. You cannot rerun 2022 with the Fed hiking more slowly to see what gold "would have" done; you get the single path the world actually took, with every variable moving at once and nothing held constant. The sample is short (a few hundred monthly observations at most), the variables are tangled, and the relationships themselves change over time. Every one of the eight mistakes below is a different way that observational, non-stationary, short, entangled data manufactures the *appearance* of an edge — and every fix is a different way of refusing to be fooled by it.

## Mistake 1: confusing correlation with causation (the third-variable trap)

The oldest mistake in statistics is also the most dangerous in macro, because the macro world is a dense web of variables that all move at once, so almost everything correlates with almost everything.

**The wrong way.** You notice that two series move together — say, gold and the stock market both rose through 2024 — and you build a story in which one causes the other, or worse, you trade as if one predicts the other. "Gold is up, so risk appetite is high, so I'll buy stocks." The correlation is real on the screen. The causal claim is invented in your head. The danger is that you size a position on a relationship that has no engine, and when the unseen driver changes, the correlation evaporates and your trade with it.

**The number that exposes it.** The cleanest example in this whole series is gold and inflation. Retail lore says "gold is an inflation hedge," and over some windows gold and the consumer price index do co-move. But the correlation of a hot CPI *surprise* with gold is only about **-0.10** in the data for this series — essentially nothing. Meanwhile the correlation of the 10-year real yield with gold is about **-0.82** over 2007-2021. Gold is not tracking inflation; it is tracking the **real yield**, which is the nominal yield *minus* expected inflation. Inflation and real yields are themselves entangled, which is exactly why the naive "gold hedges inflation" correlation appears at all — it is the *third variable*, the real yield, showing through. The matrix data makes the point numerically: a hot CPI surprise correlates **-0.10** with gold but a real-yield rise correlates **-0.80** with gold. The bigger number names the real driver.

**The fix.** Before you trade a correlation, name the **third variable** and ask whether it explains both legs. The discipline is to write down the causal graph: what plausibly *drives* X, what plausibly drives Y, and is there a common parent? If a single parent (real yields, the dollar, liquidity, the growth-versus-inflation regime) drives both, then the X-Y correlation is an artifact of the parent, and the trade you actually want is on the *parent*, not on the spurious child relationship. There is a formal version of this test — the **partial correlation**, which measures how much X and Y co-move *after statistically removing* the influence of the suspected third variable. If the X-Y correlation is 0.6 but the *partial* correlation controlling for real yields is near zero, you have proof the relationship was the third variable all along, and the direct trade is hollow. You do not always need the formal machinery; often the causal graph and a moment's thought are enough to spot that the dollar, or the real yield, or liquidity is the puppeteer behind two strings you mistook for a single rope. The macro world is unusually prone to this because it has so few master variables and so many assets — the same handful of parents drive dozens of children, so spurious cousin-correlations between the children are everywhere. This is the central lesson of [spurious correlation and the traps of macro data](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/spurious-correlation-and-the-traps-of-macro-data): the macro world has a handful of master variables — the real yield above all — and most pairwise correlations you observe are those masters leaking through. For the deep mechanism of why the real yield prices nearly everything, see [real yields, the variable that prices everything](/blog/trading/cross-asset/real-yields-the-variable-that-prices-everything) and the series' own [real yields and the cleanest macro correlation](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/real-yields-and-the-cleanest-macro-correlation).

#### Worked example: pricing the third-variable trap

Suppose in 2021 you observe that gold and long-duration tech stocks have both rallied hard, with a rolling correlation of about **+0.6**. You conclude "gold confirms the risk-on tape" and you buy \$50,000 of gold as a momentum add to your \$50,000 tech position, treating them as the same trade. The hidden driver is the real yield, which was deeply negative (about **-0.95%** at end-2021) and lifting *both* assets. In 2022 the real yield rockets from -0.95% to +0.40%. Long-duration tech, whose valuation is exquisitely sensitive to the discount rate, falls hard. Your \$50,000 tech sleeve drops roughly **30%** to \$35,000, a **-\$15,000** loss. You expected gold to fall with it (same trade, right?) — but gold, driven by central-bank buying that has nothing to do with the real-yield arithmetic, *rose*. The "confirming" correlation was the real yield speaking through both, and once you understand that, you would never have treated the two as one position. **The third variable is not a footnote; it is the trade.**

## Mistake 2: trusting the static, full-sample correlation

If you remember only one mistake from this post, make it this one, because it is the most seductive: it looks like rigor. You computed a number over a long, honest sample. The problem is that the long sample is precisely what hides the truth.

**The wrong way.** You pull twenty years of data, compute one correlation, and quote it as "the" relationship. It feels more rigorous than a short sample — more data, less noise, surely more reliable. But a long sample averages across regimes, and if the relationship *flipped* somewhere in the middle, the single number is the average of two opposite truths and describes neither. You then size a trade on a number that has never actually been the prevailing correlation at any single moment in history.

**The number that exposes it.** This is the killer demonstration of the entire series. The correlation between the 10-year real yield and the price of gold, computed over the full **2007-2025** sample, is **-0.01**. To three significant figures, statistical "nothing." A naive analyst reads -0.01 and concludes "real yields and gold are unrelated; ignore the relationship." That conclusion is catastrophically wrong. Split the same data at 2022 and you find the correlation was **-0.96** over 2007-2021 (a near-perfect inverse relationship — one of the strongest macro correlations that exists) and **+0.80** over 2022-2025 (a strong *positive* relationship, the opposite sign). The full-sample -0.01 is not the absence of a relationship; it is the *collision* of two opposite, very strong relationships averaging to near zero. The "zero" is a flip.

![Two scatter clusters of gold against real yields showing a negative then positive relationship](/imgs/blogs/common-correlation-mistakes-and-how-to-avoid-them-2.png)

The scatter makes the lie visible. The blue cloud (2007-2021) slopes sharply down: higher real yield, lower gold, r = -0.96. The amber cloud (2022-2025) slopes *up*: higher real yield, *higher* gold, r = +0.80. Pool them into one regression and the two opposing slopes cancel, and you get a flat, meaningless line through the middle with r = -0.01. Every point on that chart is real; the single number summarising all of them is a fiction. A static correlation is a photograph of a sprinter that averages the moment they were at the start line with the moment they crossed the finish — the "average position" describes no instant of the race.

**The fix.** Never quote a static full-sample correlation for a macro relationship. **Roll the window.** Compute the correlation over a moving window (12, 24, or 36 months) and *look at the line*, not the average of the line. If the rolling correlation is stable, the static number is fine. If it swings — especially if it changes sign — the static number is a lie and you must condition on the regime instead. This is the entire thesis of [rolling correlation and why the window matters](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/rolling-correlation-and-why-the-window-matters): the window length you choose *is* a modelling decision, and a too-long window smears a flip into a flat average. For the structural reasons these flips happen, see [structural shifts, why today's correlations aren't yesterday's](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/structural-shifts-why-todays-correlations-arent-yesterdays).

#### Worked example: the dollar cost of trading a static correlation that flipped

You manage a \$200,000 macro sleeve. In early 2022 you read that the full-sample gold-real-yield correlation is essentially zero (-0.01), so you decide gold has no clean macro driver and you do not hedge your \$60,000 gold position when real yields begin to rise — "the relationship is noise." Had you instead rolled the window, you would have seen the rolling correlation was deeply negative (around -0.9) right up to 2021 and then *snapping toward positive* through 2022 — a regime change in progress, the single most tradeable event a correlation can show you. Acting on the static -0.01, you sit through the transition blind. Now run the counterfactual cost of the *opposite* error, the one the 2013 desk made: a trader who trusted the -0.9 number as permanent and shorted \$60,000 of gold against rising real yields in 2022 would have watched gold rally about **33%** while real yields rose, losing roughly **0.33 × \$60,000 = \$19,800** on the short — plus the carry. Either way, the static number — whether you read it as "zero" or as "-0.9 forever" — costs you. **The static correlation is the number that is true on average and wrong at every moment that matters.**

## Mistake 3: quoting a correlation without naming the regime

This is mistake two's sibling. Mistake two is about *time-averaging* a flip into nothing; mistake three is about *quoting* a correlation as if it were context-free, when its sign depends entirely on which macro regime you are in.

**The wrong way.** You say "stocks and bonds are negatively correlated, so a 60/40 portfolio is diversified," full stop, as if it were a law of nature. It is not a law; it is a regime-dependent fact. The stock-bond correlation has a *sign that depends on what the dominant macro risk is*. When the dominant risk is growth (recession fear), stocks and bonds move opposite: bad growth news sinks stocks but rallies bonds (rates fall), so bonds hedge stocks — the negative correlation that made 60/40 famous. When the dominant risk is *inflation*, both fall together: a hot inflation shock sinks stocks (higher discount rate) *and* sinks bonds (higher yields), so the correlation goes positive and the hedge fails. Quote the correlation without the regime and you will recommend a "diversified" portfolio that is secretly undiversified.

**The number that exposes it.** Condition the stock-bond correlation on the inflation regime and the number is not one value but four:

| Inflation regime | Stock-bond correlation |
|---|---|
| Below 2% | **-0.45** (bonds hedge stocks well) |
| 2-3% | **-0.30** |
| 3-4% | **+0.05** (the hedge breaks down) |
| Above 4% | **+0.50** (both fall together) |

The correlation does not merely weaken as inflation rises; it *changes sign*, from -0.45 to +0.50. There is no single "stock-bond correlation" to quote — there is one per regime. The same shows up across the four macro quadrants: in stagflation (growth down, inflation up) the stock-bond correlation is about **+0.55** (the worst case — both your sleeves fall), while in deflation (growth down, inflation down) it is about **-0.55** (bonds are the strongest hedge they ever are).

![Rolling stock-bond correlation 1990 to 2025 swinging from positive to negative to positive](/imgs/blogs/common-correlation-mistakes-and-how-to-avoid-them-4.png)

The rolling line tells the same story across history. Positive in the high-inflation early 1990s, persistently negative through the disinflation/QE era (1998-2021) — the "diversifying 60/40" decades that taught a whole generation that bonds hedge stocks — then violently positive again in the 2022 inflation shock, peaking near **+0.60**. The orange dashed line is the full-sample average of about -0.04 — which, just like the gold example, is a meaningless number that describes none of the three regimes. Quoting "the stock-bond correlation is about zero" or even "it's negative" without saying *which regime* is the error.

**The fix.** Before you read or quote any macro correlation, **tag the regime first.** Use the growth-versus-inflation quadrant: is growth accelerating or slowing, is inflation rising or falling? The quadrant you are in selects which correlations dominate and what their signs are. Read the conditional correlation, never the unconditional one. This is the entire framework of [correlation by regime, the four macro quadrants](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/correlation-by-regime-the-four-macro-quadrants) and, for the flagship pair, [the stock-bond correlation regime](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/the-stock-bond-correlation-regime). The mechanism — why inflation flips the sign — is in [the 60/40 engine](/blog/trading/cross-asset/stock-bond-correlation-the-60-40-engine).

#### Worked example: sizing a 60/40 book by the regime, not the average

You run a \$100,000 portfolio, \$60,000 stocks and \$40,000 bonds, and you believe the relationship is "negatively correlated, so diversified." In a *growth* shock (inflation under 2%, correlation -0.45), that belief is sound: stocks fall, bonds rally, the bond sleeve cushions the blow. Suppose stocks drop 20% (-\$12,000) and bonds rise 6% (+\$2,400); your net loss is **-\$9,600**, softened by the bond hedge. Now run the *same book* in a 2022-style inflation shock, where the correlation is +0.50 and both fall together: stocks drop 20% (-\$12,000) *and* bonds drop 12% (-\$4,800), for a net loss of **-\$16,800** — over **70%** larger, with no hedge at all, precisely when you assumed you were protected. The difference between -\$9,600 and -\$16,800 is the cost of quoting the correlation without its regime. **A correlation without a regime tag is a portfolio recommendation without a margin of safety.**

## Mistake 4: correlating levels instead of changes

This mistake manufactures more fake correlations than any other, and it is the easiest to fix once you see it. It is the difference between a number that describes a real relationship and a number that is an artifact of two trends.

**The wrong way.** You take two series in their raw *levels* — gold's price, the money supply, an index value, nominal GDP — and correlate them directly. Because nearly every macro and price level *trends* (drifts persistently up over years), any two upward-trending series will show a high positive correlation whether or not they have anything to do with each other. The correlation is real arithmetic; it just measures the shared trend, not the genuine period-to-period co-movement you care about.

**The number that exposes it.** The mechanism is worth seeing precisely. The correlation formula rewards two series for being above their own averages at the same times. A steadily upward-trending series is below its average for the whole first half of the sample and above it for the whole second half. Two upward-trending series therefore *agree by construction* — both low early, both high late — and the formula returns a big positive r on schedule. The classic demonstration: correlate the *levels* of two trending macro series (US money supply and gold, say, or any two indices) and you can routinely get **r ≈ 0.95**; correlate their *changes* and the number collapses to something honest, often **0.3 or 0.5 or even near zero**. The 0.95 is not a relationship; it is two trends sharing a clock. They would correlate just as highly with the integers 1, 2, 3, 4, 5.

**The fix.** **Correlate changes, not levels.** Use first differences (`.diff()`) for things measured in levels, or percentage returns (`.pct_change()`) for prices. Differencing strips out the trend and leaves the period-to-period co-movement, which is the thing you actually want to know: when the real yield jumped *this* month, did gold fall *this* month? The rigorous version tests *whether* a series needs differencing with an Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test rather than assuming it, but the working rule is unambiguous: if either series trends, difference both before you correlate. This is gate one of [backtesting a correlation without fooling yourself](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/backtesting-a-correlation-without-fooling-yourself), and the statistical machinery — unit roots, stationarity — is laid out in [stationarity and autocorrelation](/blog/trading/math-for-quants/stationarity-autocorrelation-math-for-quants).

```python
import pandas as pd

>>> df.columns.tolist()
['money_supply', 'gold']

## WRONG: correlate raw trending levels
>>> df[["money_supply", "gold"]].corr().iloc[0, 1]
0.95   # two trends sharing a clock; means almost nothing

## RIGHT: correlate CHANGES
>>> chg = pd.DataFrame({
...     "d_m2":      df["money_supply"].pct_change(),
...     "gold_ret":  df["gold"].pct_change(),
... }).dropna()
>>> chg.corr().iloc[0, 1]
0.18   # the honest, much weaker co-movement of monthly changes
```

#### Worked example: the fake 0.95 and the trade it kills

You read a research note claiming gold and the broad money supply have a correlation of **0.95** over twenty years, and the note recommends a long-gold position because "money printing drives gold." You allocate \$80,000 to gold on the strength of that 0.95. But the 0.95 is a *levels* correlation — both series trended up for two decades, so the number is mechanical. Difference both into monthly changes and the real co-movement is about **0.18**: a weak, barely-tradeable relationship. The honest correlation says gold's monthly moves are explained by money-supply changes only about **0.18² ≈ 3%** of the time (the r-squared) — the other 97% is real yields, the dollar, central-bank flows, and noise. Sizing \$80,000 as if the relationship were 0.95-strong when it is really 0.18-strong means you have built a position on a co-movement that is **five times weaker** than you think, and the first time money supply rises while gold falls (as happened repeatedly), you will be baffled — because the relationship you traded never existed at the change level. **A levels correlation answers a question you never asked; difference first and ask the real one.**

## Mistake 5: the diversification illusion (correlations spike to one in a crisis)

This is the mistake that does the most damage per occurrence, because it strikes exactly when you can least afford it. You spend years building a diversified book on the assumption that your positions are loosely correlated, and then in the one week that matters, every correlation snaps toward +1 and your diversification disappears.

**The wrong way.** You measure the correlations between your holdings in normal times — stocks, credit, EM, gold, crypto, all showing comfortably moderate pairwise correlations around 0.2 to 0.4 — and you conclude your book is well diversified and can survive a shock. The flaw: correlations are not constant through stress. In a genuine crisis, forced deleveraging and a universal flight to cash drive nearly all risk assets down *together*, and the gentle correlations you measured in calm markets are replaced by something close to perfect positive correlation. The diversification you paid for is a fair-weather friend.

**The number that exposes it.** The average pairwise correlation across major risk assets is about **0.25** in a calm market. In a normal selloff it rises to about **0.45**. In a genuine crisis — deleveraging, margin calls, dash for cash — it jumps to about **0.80**. Pin it to specific episodes and the pattern is unmistakable: calm 2017 about **0.20**; the 2022 rate shock about **0.60**; the Global Financial Crisis of 2008 about **0.80**; the COVID crash of March 2020 about **0.85**. The correlation you bought (~0.25) is not the correlation you get (~0.80) when you need diversification most.

![Average pairwise correlation across risk assets calm versus crisis episodes](/imgs/blogs/common-correlation-mistakes-and-how-to-avoid-them-3.png)

The bars say it in one glance: green for the calm and normal states where diversification is real, red for the crises where it failed. The gap between the green dashed line (the ~0.25 you bought) and the red dashed line (the ~0.80 you got) is the diversification illusion, and it is widest in exactly the GFC and COVID columns — the two moments a hedge had to work. Diversification is a fair-weather asset: it is there in the sunshine and gone in the storm.

**The fix.** **Stress-test the book at crisis correlations, not calm ones.** When you size risk, do not assume your measured calm-market correlations; assume that in a shock everything you own correlates at **0.8 or higher** and ask whether the portfolio survives *that*. The genuine diversifiers in a crisis are few — long Treasuries (in a deflationary/growth shock), cash, and occasionally gold — and even those flip (Treasuries failed as a hedge in 2022's *inflationary* shock, where the stock-bond correlation went positive). The deep treatment is [correlation during crises, when diversification fails](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/correlation-during-crises-when-diversification-fails) and the cross-asset companion [when correlations go to one in a crisis](/blog/trading/cross-asset/when-correlations-go-to-one-in-a-crisis). The VIX-and-risk-on/risk-off mechanism behind the spike is in [the VIX, risk-on risk-off, and the correlation spike](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/the-vix-risk-on-risk-off-and-the-correlation-spike).

#### Worked example: the diversification illusion on a \$100,000 book in a crisis

You hold a \$100,000 book spread across five "diversified" risk assets — \$20,000 each in US stocks, high-yield credit, EM equities, gold, and crypto — and you sleep well because in calm markets the average pairwise correlation is about 0.25, so the sleeves should zig and zag against each other. To estimate crash risk, naive diversification math says portfolio volatility scales roughly with the square root of the number of holdings when correlations are low: with five loosely-correlated sleeves, a 20% drop in any one *should* be cushioned by the others holding up. Now the crisis hits and the average pairwise correlation jumps to **0.85** (COVID March 2020). Every sleeve falls roughly together: if the average risk asset drops **30%**, your supposedly diversified book does not lose the cushioned amount you modelled — it loses close to the full **0.30 × \$100,000 = \$30,000**, because at 0.85 correlation there is almost no diversification left to do the cushioning. The "five uncorrelated bets" you owned behaved like **one** bet, five times over. The difference between the loss you modelled at 0.25 and the loss you took at 0.85 is the entire cost of the illusion. **Size your book for the correlation you get in the crash (~0.8), not the one you measured in the calm (~0.25).**

## Mistake 6: p-hacking the macro data (test a hundred, five look significant)

This is the quiet mistake — it does not feel like a mistake at all, because each individual test you ran was perfectly valid. The error is in the *count*, and in showing only the winners.

**The wrong way.** You have a dataset of a dozen macro indicators and a dozen assets, and you test all the pairwise relationships looking for a strong, significant correlation to trade. You find one — say a 0.71 correlation between a commodity index and an equity sector — with a clean p-value under 0.05, and you build a strategy around it. What you have forgotten is that you ran a *hundred* tests, and at the conventional 5% significance level, about **five of every hundred** useless relationships will pass by pure chance. The one you found and kept may simply be the luckiest of a hundred coin flips, with no relationship behind it at all. You then trade an edge that was never there, and it returns nothing or loses money live while you blame "the regime changing."

**The number that exposes it.** The arithmetic is brutal and exact. A significance test at the 5% level means "there is a 5% chance a truly-random relationship passes this test." Run **100** independent tests on pure noise and the expected number of false passes is **0.05 × 100 = 5**. So finding five "significant" correlations in a hundred-pair search is not evidence of anything — it is exactly what randomness predicts. The defense is the **Bonferroni correction**: to keep the *overall* false-positive rate at 5% across all your tests, divide the threshold by the number of tests. For 100 tests, the corrected threshold is **0.05 / 100 = 0.0005**. A correlation that passed at p = 0.03 in a single test fails decisively against a 0.0005 bar. Most "discovered" macro correlations do not survive the correction, which is the point.

![P-hacking on the left versus a Bonferroni-corrected workflow on the right](/imgs/blogs/common-correlation-mistakes-and-how-to-avoid-them-6.png)

The before-and-after says it cleanly. On the left, the p-hacking path: test 100 pairs, about 5 pass by chance, keep the best one, bury the 95 misses, and trade a signal that was never real. On the right, the honest path: count every test you ran, cut the threshold by that count, demand the survivor beat the corrected bar, and confirm it out-of-sample before risking a dollar. The discipline is not "run fewer tests" — it is "be honest about how many you ran and raise the bar accordingly."

**The fix.** **Count every test you run and correct the significance threshold for the count** (Bonferroni's 0.05 / N, or a less conservative false-discovery-rate control, or a deflated Sharpe ratio that penalises how many strategies you tried). Equally important: report the survivors *out of sample*, on data you did not use to find them. A relationship that survives both the multiple-testing correction and an out-of-sample test has earned a small position; one that only "won" an in-sample search has earned nothing. This is gates three and two of [backtesting a correlation without fooling yourself](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/backtesting-a-correlation-without-fooling-yourself), and the statistical foundation — what a p-value is and how multiple testing inflates false positives — is in [hypothesis testing and p-values](/blog/trading/math-for-quants/hypothesis-testing-pvalues-math-for-quants).

#### Worked example: a Bonferroni adjustment on a macro signal search

You search **120** macro-indicator-versus-asset pairs and find that **7** of them clear the conventional p < 0.05 bar. Excited, you prepare to trade the strongest, a correlation with a single-test p-value of **0.008**. Before you size it, run the Bonferroni math. At 120 tests, the expected number of false passes from pure noise is **0.05 × 120 = 6** — so finding 7 passers is barely above what randomness alone would produce; you have found essentially *nothing* beyond chance. The corrected threshold is **0.05 / 120 ≈ 0.00042**. Your best signal's p-value of 0.008 is about **19 times larger** than the corrected bar (0.008 / 0.00042 ≈ 19), so it fails the honest test decisively. Had you sized it at, say, \$25,000 on the strength of the uncorrected 0.008, you would have been betting real money on a coin that came up heads once in a search designed to find heads. **In a large search, the question is never "did this pass?" but "did this pass a bar raised for everything else I tried?"**

## Mistake 7: trading the level instead of the surprise

This mistake is specific to data releases, and it is the difference between understanding *why* markets move on data and being constantly baffled by it. It is the heart of why a "good" number can crash a market and a "bad" number can rally it.

**The wrong way.** You see a data print — "CPI came in at 3.2%, that's high, so I'll sell stocks" — and you trade the *level* of the number. You are then bewildered when a high CPI print is met with a stock *rally*, or a strong jobs number sinks the market. You conclude markets are irrational. They are not; you traded the wrong variable. Markets price in *expectations* continuously. By the time a number is released, the *expected* value is already in prices. What moves price is the **surprise** — the gap between the actual print and what was expected. A 3.2% CPI when the market expected 3.5% is a *downside* surprise (inflation lower than feared) and stocks rally; the same 3.2% when the market expected 2.9% is an *upside* surprise (hotter than hoped) and stocks fall. The level is identical; the surprise — and the reaction — is opposite.

**The number that exposes it.** The asset's reaction is a **beta to the surprise**, not to the level. In the 2022-23 inflation-fear regime, a **+0.1pp core-CPI upside surprise** (the actual coming in 0.1 percentage point above expectations) moved markets by these approximate amounts in the same session:

| Asset | Reaction to +0.1pp core-CPI upside surprise |
|---|---|
| S&P 500 | **-0.70%** |
| Nasdaq 100 | **-1.00%** (longer duration, more rate-sensitive) |
| US 10-year yield | **+7bp** (yields rise) |
| US 2-year yield | **+9bp** (front end reprices most) |
| US dollar (DXY) | **+0.35%** |
| Gold | **-0.80%** (real yields up) |
| Bitcoin | **-1.60%** (high-beta risk) |

Every one of these is keyed to the *surprise*, not the level. If the actual matched expectations exactly — a zero surprise — the beta-times-surprise reaction is roughly zero regardless of whether the level was 2% or 9%. The level is already in the price; only the surprise is new information.

![Bar chart of asset reactions to a 0.1pp core CPI upside surprise](/imgs/blogs/common-correlation-mistakes-and-how-to-avoid-them-5.png)

The bars show the same-session reaction function: equities fall (red), yields rise (blue, in basis points), the dollar firms slightly (green), gold and crypto fall. Note Bitcoin's -1.60% beta is more than double the S&P's -0.70% — crypto trades as the highest-beta risk asset to a hawkish surprise. And note the signs are *conditional on the regime*: in a "good-news-is-bad" regime, a strong jobs surprise sinks stocks; in a normal expansion the same surprise *lifts* them. The surprise is what you trade, but the sign of the reaction still depends on which regime you are in — mistakes three and seven compound.

**The fix.** **Always trade the surprise (actual minus expected), never the level**, and check the regime to know the sign of the reaction. Before a release, find the consensus expectation; after it, compute the surprise; size the trade as beta-times-surprise. This is the founding idea of the event-trading framework — [why news moves markets, the surprise framework](/blog/trading/event-trading/why-news-moves-markets-the-surprise-framework) and [CPI, the report that moves the world](/blog/trading/event-trading/cpi-the-report-that-moves-the-world) — and within this series it is the whole of [the surprise, not the level, betas to data surprises](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/the-surprise-not-the-level-betas-to-data-surprises) and [measuring beta to data surprises, an event study in Python](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/measuring-beta-to-data-surprises-an-event-study-in-python).

#### Worked example: level-versus-surprise sizing on a CPI print

Core CPI is released at **3.3%**. A trader who reads only the level thinks "still high, sell stocks" and shorts \$30,000 of S&P exposure. But the consensus expectation was **3.4%** — so the actual is a **-0.1pp downside surprise** (inflation came in *cooler* than feared). The correct beta math: a -0.1pp surprise (the opposite sign of the +0.1pp in the table) implies the S&P *rallies* about **+0.70%**, gold rises about **+0.80%**, and yields *fall* about **-7bp** on the 10-year. The level-trader, short into a relief rally, loses **0.70% × \$30,000 = \$210** on a tiny move — and far more on a larger surprise. Now flip it: the same 3.3% level against a **3.1%** expectation is a **+0.2pp upside surprise**, implying the S&P falls about **2 × -0.70% = -1.40%** and the 2-year yield jumps about **2 × +9bp = +18bp**. *Identical level, opposite trade, because the surprise is opposite.* Sizing \$30,000 by the surprise would have you long the first time and short the second; sizing by the level has you wrong half the time. **The print is the noise; the surprise is the signal.**

## Mistake 8: trading a decayed or structurally broken correlation

The last mistake is the subtlest, because the correlation you are trading was *genuinely real* — it had a mechanism, it held for years, it survived all the other gates. The error is assuming it is permanent. Correlations decay and break, and trading a dead one is how the most sophisticated practitioners lose money.

**The wrong way.** You found a clean, mechanism-backed correlation, it worked for years, and you keep trading it on autopilot long after the world that produced it has changed. Markets are adaptive systems: a profitable, well-known correlation attracts capital that arbitrages it away (the relationship decays), or a structural change in policy, market structure, or participants breaks the mechanism entirely (the correlation inverts or vanishes). You keep sizing the trade off the historical correlation while the live correlation has quietly gone to zero or flipped sign, and you bleed.

**The number that exposes it.** Return to the opening story, now as the eighth mistake. The gold-versus-real-yield correlation was **-0.96** over 2007-2021 — about as strong and clean as macro correlations ever get, with an airtight mechanism (gold's opportunity cost is the real yield). Then over 2022-2025 it became **+0.80** — a full sign inversion — as central-bank gold buying overwhelmed the real-yield channel. A trader still short gold against rising real yields in 2022, on the strength of that -0.96, was trading a *structurally broken* correlation. The same decay shows up in crypto: Bitcoin's correlation with the Nasdaq was near **zero pre-2020**, spiked to about **+0.65** in 2022 as it traded as a macro-liquidity asset, then *faded* back toward **+0.20-0.30** by 2024-25. A pair-trade sized off the 0.65 peak in 2022 would be trading a relationship that had since halved in strength. Correlations have a half-life.

**The fix.** **Monitor every traded correlation for decay and retire it on a break.** Keep the rolling correlation on a dashboard; set a rule for when the live number has drifted far enough from the historical number (or changed sign) that the trade is invalidated; and, crucially, demand a *mechanism* check — has the economic engine that produced the correlation changed? When central banks become structural gold buyers, the gold-real-yield mechanism is altered, and that is your signal to retire the trade *before* the loss, not after. There are two distinct flavours of breakage and they call for slightly different vigilance. **Decay** is gradual: a known, profitable correlation attracts capital that arbitrages the spread, so the relationship slowly weakens toward zero — the crypto-Nasdaq fade from 0.65 to 0.25 is this kind, a strength erosion you catch by watching the rolling number drift. **Structural break** is abrupt: a policy regime, a market-structure change, or a new dominant participant snaps the mechanism and the correlation can *invert* in a year — the gold-real-yield flip from -0.96 to +0.80 is this kind, a sign inversion you catch by watching the mechanism, not just the number. Decay quietly halves your edge; a structural break can put you on the wrong side entirely. A change-point detector on the rolling correlation flags both: it is the difference between noticing the world has changed and discovering it through your P&L. This is the lesson of [structural shifts, why today's correlations aren't yesterday's](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/structural-shifts-why-todays-correlations-arent-yesterdays) and the practical monitoring is built in [from correlation to signal, building a macro overlay](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/from-correlation-to-signal-building-a-macro-overlay) and [rolling correlation regimes and change-point detection](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/rolling-correlation-regimes-and-change-point-detection).

#### Worked example: the cost of riding a decayed crypto correlation

In mid-2022 you notice Bitcoin and the Nasdaq are correlated at about **+0.65**, and you build a \$40,000 pair trade — long Nasdaq, short Bitcoin (or vice versa) — sized on the assumption that the two move tightly together, so the spread between them is mean-reverting and low-risk. The trade depends on the correlation *staying* near 0.65. By 2024 the correlation has decayed to about **+0.25**. At 0.65, the two legs hedge each other well; at 0.25, they mostly do not, so your "hedged" spread trade is now carrying roughly **(1 - 0.25) / (1 - 0.65) ≈ 2.1 times** the unhedged risk you signed up for — the spread can blow out because the legs no longer track. If that wider spread moves **8%** against you on the \$40,000 notional, that is a **-\$3,200** loss on a position you believed was tightly hedged. You did nothing wrong at entry; you simply failed to notice the correlation had halved and re-size or retire the trade. **A correlation that worked is not a correlation that works; check its pulse before every roll.**

## Common misconceptions

Three beliefs about correlations survive in trading lore despite being wrong, and each is a compressed version of the mistakes above. Correcting them with a number is the fastest inoculation.

**"Gold is an inflation hedge."** No — gold tracks the **real yield**, not inflation. The correlation of a hot CPI surprise with gold is only about **-0.10**, while the correlation of the real yield with gold is about **-0.82** over 2007-2021. Inflation matters to gold *only* through its effect on the real yield (nominal yield minus expected inflation). When inflation rises but nominal yields rise faster, the real yield rises and gold *falls* — the opposite of the "inflation hedge" story. This is mistake one (the third variable) wearing a costume.

**"A 60/40 portfolio is always diversified because stocks and bonds are negatively correlated."** No — the stock-bond correlation has a *sign that depends on the inflation regime*: about **-0.45** when inflation is below 2%, but about **+0.50** when inflation is above 4%. In 2022 both legs fell together and the "diversified" portfolio had its worst year in decades. This is mistake three (ignoring the regime). The hedge is real in a growth shock and absent in an inflation shock.

**"Diversification protects you in a crash."** No — diversification is a fair-weather asset. Average pairwise correlation across risk assets is about **0.25** in calm markets but about **0.80-0.85** in a crisis (GFC 2008, COVID 2020). The correlations you measured to build the diversified book are precisely the ones that disappear in the crash. This is mistake five (the diversification illusion). Stress-test at crisis correlations, not calm ones.

**"A high correlation means a strong, tradeable relationship."** Not necessarily — square it first. A correlation of 0.4 squares to an r-squared of 0.16, meaning the relationship explains only **16%** of the moves and the other **84%** is other drivers and noise. Most macro correlations that look exciting on a chart explain less than a third of what actually happens, which is why even a "real" correlation can sit through long stretches where it appears to fail — the rest of the world is simply louder. The fix is to size by the r-squared, not the correlation: a 0.4 correlation deserves a fraction of the conviction a 0.8 (r-squared 0.64) does. This misconception compounds with all eight mistakes, because an over-estimated relationship strength makes every other error more expensive.

## How it shows up in real markets

The mistakes are not abstractions; each has a dated episode where it cost real money. Here are three, with the numbers.

**2013-2022, the gold–real-yield desk (mistakes two and eight).** A clean -0.9 correlation, an airtight mechanism, years of profit — and then a sign inversion to +0.80 as central banks became structural buyers. Anyone sized off the historical correlation as if it were permanent was short gold into a **33%** rally while real yields rose. The lesson is doubled: the full-sample number (-0.01) hid the flip (mistake two), and the strong historical number (-0.96) was structurally broken by 2022 (mistake eight). The desk that survived was the one watching the rolling correlation and the central-bank-flow mechanism, who saw the engine change and retired the trade.

**2022, the 60/40 portfolio (mistakes three and five).** For two decades the stock-bond correlation was reliably negative (about -0.40 across 1998-2021), and a generation of allocators learned that bonds hedge stocks. Then inflation became the dominant risk, the correlation flipped to about **+0.60**, and a standard 60/40 portfolio fell roughly **17%** in 2022 — its worst year since the 1930s — because *both* sleeves dropped at once. Allocators who tagged the regime (inflation above 4% implies positive stock-bond correlation) cut bond duration and added real assets; those who quoted "stocks and bonds are negatively correlated" as a law took the full hit.

**March 2020, the diversified hedge-fund book (mistake five).** In the COVID crash the average pairwise correlation across risk assets spiked to about **0.85**. Funds that had built "uncorrelated" multi-strategy books watched every sleeve fall together — equities, credit, EM, even some "safe haven" positions in the worst days of the dash for cash — and the diversification they had paid for did nothing. The funds that survived had stress-tested at crisis correlations and held genuine crisis hedges (cash, long Treasuries before the inflation regime, tail options), not merely calm-market diversification.

## How to read it and use it: the eight-gate checklist

Here is the whole post compressed into a workflow you can run on any correlation before you trade it. Treat it as a checklist; a relationship must clear all eight gates.

![The eight-gate pre-trade correlation checklist as a pipeline](/imgs/blogs/common-correlation-mistakes-and-how-to-avoid-them-7.png)

1. **Causation?** Name the third variable. If a common parent (real yields, the dollar, liquidity, the growth-inflation regime) drives both legs, trade the parent, not the spurious child. *(Mistake 1.)*
2. **Changes, not levels.** If either series trends, difference both (or use returns) before correlating. A levels correlation of 0.95 between two trends is an artifact. *(Mistake 4.)*
3. **Roll it.** Never quote a static full-sample number. Look at the rolling correlation line; if it swings or changes sign, the static number is a lie. Remember -0.01. *(Mistake 2.)*
4. **Tag the regime.** Read the conditional correlation for the growth-inflation quadrant you are in, not the unconditional average. The sign can flip across regimes. *(Mistake 3.)*
5. **Surprise, not level.** For data releases, trade the gap between actual and expected, sized as beta-times-surprise, with the sign set by the regime. *(Mistake 7.)*
6. **Correct for multiple testing.** Count every test you ran and raise the bar (Bonferroni 0.05/N), then confirm out-of-sample. *(Mistake 6.)*
7. **Crisis stress-test.** Re-size the book assuming everything you own correlates at 0.8 in a shock. Diversification you measured in the calm is gone in the storm. *(Mistake 5.)*
8. **Monitor decay.** Keep the rolling correlation on a dashboard; retire the trade when the live number drifts far from the historical one or the mechanism changes. *(Mistake 8.)*

**What invalidates the trade:** any gate failing. A correlation with no third-variable check, computed on levels, quoted statically, without a regime tag, on a level rather than a surprise, found in an uncorrected search, untested at crisis correlations, and unmonitored for decay, is not a trade — it is eight separate ways to lose money stacked into one position. Run the gates in order; each kills a different mirage, and what survives all eight has earned a small, monitored position.

The honest summary of this series is uncomfortable and freeing at once: **a macro correlation is guilty until proven innocent.** The default state of any number between -1 and +1 you compute from macro data is "probably fragile, possibly fake, and certainly regime-dependent." Your edge is not in finding correlations — anyone can find them, the formula prints one for any two columns. Your edge is in knowing which ones are real, in which regime, and for how long. That is the discipline this whole series exists to build, and the eight gates above are its checklist.
