> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The 10-year **real yield** (the TIPS yield, which is the nominal Treasury yield minus expected inflation) is the cleanest, most stable macro correlation there is: when it rises, gold, growth stocks, long bonds, and crypto fall together, and the dollar rises. It is the *true discount rate*, so it isolates the one channel — opportunity cost — that prices every risk asset.
>
> - The nominal yield mixes two stories (growth and inflation); the **real yield strips inflation out**, leaving just the rate that matters for valuation.
> - The real-yield signs are remarkably consistent: gold about −0.80, Nasdaq −0.65, long bonds −0.70, Bitcoin −0.55, EM equities −0.55, and the dollar **+0.40**. Gold is the strongest negative, growth/long-duration assets next, the dollar the mirror.
> - The cleanest single data pair in macro: gold versus the 10Y real yield ran a correlation of **r = −0.96** from 2007 to 2021. Then in 2022–24 it broke (central-bank buying lifted gold *with* real yields) — even the cleanest correlation is still a regime.
> - The one number to remember: roughly **−\$354 per ounce of gold for every +1 percentage point** the 10Y real yield rises, over the era when the relationship held.

In October 2022, the U.S. 10-year nominal Treasury yield punched above 4% for the first time in over a decade. That alone is a headline. But the headline missed the real story. The number that was actually doing the damage to portfolios was sitting one layer down: the 10-year **real** yield — the yield on Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities, or TIPS — had vaulted from roughly **−1.0%** in late 2021 to **+1.7%** by October 2022, and kept climbing toward **+2.5%** within a year. That is a swing of more than three and a half percentage points in the rate that prices everything.

Watch what happened in lockstep. Gold, which had floated near its all-time high while real yields were deeply negative, sagged. The Nasdaq 100 — wall-to-wall long-duration growth stocks — fell about a third from peak to trough. Bitcoin, the supposed "uncorrelated" asset, dropped over 60% in the same window. Long-dated Treasuries had their worst year in modern history. And the dollar ripped to a 20-year high. Five very different assets, one driver. That is what it looks like when the master variable moves.

This post makes a single argument: of all the macro variables a trader can track — CPI, payrolls, ISM, the nominal yield, the dollar — the **10-year real yield** has the cleanest and most stable correlation with the cross-section of risk assets. Not because it is magic, but because it is the closest thing markets have to a *true discount rate*. We will build it from zero (what a real yield even is, how TIPS price it, why it is the right rate), walk the cross-asset correlations one asset at a time, work the dollar math on real positions, and then be honest about the regime where it broke. By the end you should see the real yield the way a macro desk does: as the gravitational center the rest of the map orbits.

A note on what this post is *not*. It is not a primer on why the Fed moves rates or how monetary policy transmits to the economy — that mechanism lives in the macro-trading series, which we cite rather than repeat. This is a post about the *measurable statistical relationship*: the sign, the strength, the timing, and the regimes of the link between one variable and the price of everything else. The distinction matters because a correlation can be real, strong, and *useless* if you do not also know when it flips. The whole reason the real yield deserves a dedicated post is that its correlation is unusually durable — it holds across more regimes than almost any other macro relationship — which makes the one time it broke (2022–24) all the more instructive.

## Foundations: what a real yield actually is

Start with a question a beginner should ask: if a bond pays you 4% a year, are you really 4% richer? Only if prices stay flat. If inflation runs at 3%, your 4% buys you barely 1% more *stuff* than before. The 4% is the **nominal** yield — the number printed on the bond. The roughly 1% you keep after inflation is the **real** yield. It is the only one that buys groceries.

That distinction is the entire post. So let us define each piece carefully, because every term here gets used loosely in the press and precisely on a trading desk.

- **Nominal yield.** The quoted yield on an ordinary Treasury — say the 10-year note. It compensates you for two things bundled together: the real return you demand, *plus* the inflation you expect to lose over the bond's life. Nominal yield = real yield + expected inflation. That bundling is exactly the problem we are going to unbundle.
- **Expected inflation (the breakeven).** Markets have a live estimate of average inflation over the next 10 years, and you can read it directly. Take the nominal 10-year yield and subtract the 10-year TIPS yield; the difference is the **breakeven inflation rate** — the inflation rate at which holding a TIPS and holding a nominal note would leave you equally well off. In mid-2026 that breakeven sits near 2.3%. It is the market's collective inflation forecast, priced in real time.
- **Real yield.** What is left when you strip expected inflation out of the nominal yield. Formally, real yield ≈ nominal yield − breakeven inflation. You do not have to compute it yourself, because there is an instrument that quotes it directly: TIPS.

**TIPS — how the real yield gets priced.** Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities are bonds whose principal grows with the Consumer Price Index. If CPI rises 3% over a year, the face value of your TIPS rises about 3%, and the fixed coupon is paid on that grown principal. The effect: the inflation is handed back to you automatically, so the yield you negotiate when you buy a TIPS is a *real* yield — a return over and above whatever inflation turns out to be. The FRED series DFII10 is the 10-year TIPS yield, and it is the cleanest market-priced read on the real cost of money there is. When commentators say "real yields hit +2%," they mean DFII10 hit +2%.

Here is the relationship as one equation you should be able to write from memory:

```
nominal 10Y yield  =  real 10Y yield  +  10Y breakeven inflation
       4.5%         =      2.2%        +        2.3%
```

Read it left to right and you see why the nominal yield is a *blend*. A move in the nominal yield could be the real yield rising (tighter money, higher opportunity cost — bad for risk assets) **or** the breakeven rising (the market expecting more inflation — a different animal entirely). The nominal number alone cannot tell you which. The real yield can.

#### Worked example: decomposing a nominal move into the part that matters

Suppose the nominal 10-year yield climbs from 4.0% to 4.5% over a month — a half-point move that the press will call "rising rates." You hold a \$10,000 position in long-duration growth stocks and want to know if you should worry. The answer depends entirely on the decomposition. Case A: breakeven was flat at 2.3%, so the real yield went from 1.7% to 2.2% — the *whole* move was real. That is pure discount-rate tightening, the most hostile thing there is for a growth stock; on a position with the rate sensitivity of the Nasdaq, a +0.5pp real-yield jump has historically been worth roughly a 4–6% drawdown, so call it about **−\$500** on your \$10,000. Case B: the real yield was flat at 1.7% and breakeven rose from 2.3% to 2.8% — the move was pure inflation expectations. That is far less damaging to equities (often neutral-to-mildly-negative) and is actually *supportive* of gold. Same 0.5pp nominal move, opposite portfolio implications. The intuition: never react to a nominal yield move until you have split it into the real part and the inflation part — only the real part is the discount rate.

### How a TIPS actually delivers a real return

It helps to see the mechanism, not just the formula, because the mechanism is what guarantees the TIPS yield *is* a real yield. Say you buy a 10-year TIPS with a face value of \$1,000 and a fixed coupon rate of 2%. In year one, suppose CPI rises 3%. The Treasury *adjusts the principal up* by 3%, to \$1,030. Your 2% coupon is now paid on the inflated principal: 2% × \$1,030 = \$20.60 instead of \$20.00. In year two, if CPI rises another 3%, principal goes to about \$1,061, and the coupon scales again. At maturity you are repaid the *inflation-adjusted* principal (with a floor at the original face value, so deflation cannot cut your principal below \$1,000). Because the inflation is handed back to you automatically through the principal, the only thing left to negotiate at purchase is the return *over and above* inflation — and that is the real yield, the 2% in this example. Compare a plain nominal note: it pays 2% on a fixed \$1,000 forever, and if inflation runs at 3%, you quietly lose 1% of purchasing power a year. The TIPS structure removes that erosion, which is precisely why its quoted yield is "real."

Now you can see breakeven inflation as an arbitrage, not just a subtraction. If the nominal 10-year yields 4.5% and the TIPS yields 2.2%, the market is saying: "I am indifferent between the two only if inflation averages 2.3% over ten years." If you privately believe inflation will run *above* 2.3%, the TIPS is the better buy (you get the inflation upside for free); if you think inflation will run *below* 2.3%, the nominal note wins. The 2.3% breakeven is the price at which that bet is fair — a market-implied inflation forecast you can read off two yields. When breakeven *rises*, the market is raising its inflation forecast; when it *falls*, it is cutting it. That is a fundamentally different signal from the real yield, and conflating them is the single most common macro error a beginner makes.

### The Fisher relationship, and why subtraction is "good enough"

Economists write the link between nominal and real rates as the **Fisher equation**. The exact form is multiplicative — (1 + nominal) = (1 + real) × (1 + expected inflation) — but at the small percentages we deal with, the cross-term is tiny, so the simple subtraction nominal − breakeven ≈ real is accurate to a few basis points. That is why practitioners just subtract. The thing to internalize is the *causal direction* we care about for correlation: the breakeven floats with the inflation regime and the real yield floats with the *monetary-policy stance and growth expectations*. When the Fed is fighting inflation, it pushes the *real* yield up (tight money) even as it tries to push the breakeven *down* (anchoring expectations). So in a tightening cycle the two halves of the nominal yield can move in opposite directions — and the asset that cares about the real half (every risk asset) gets the bad news while the asset that cares about the inflation half (gold's inflation-hedge reputation) gets the *opposite* news. That tension is the whole reason gold and inflation have such a confusing reputation, and it resolves the moment you split the nominal yield into its two parts.

## Why the real yield is the *right* discount rate

To value any asset, you discount its future cash flows back to today. The discount rate is your opportunity cost: what you could earn risk-free instead. The deeper a cash flow sits in the future, the more a higher discount rate shrinks its present value. This is the single mechanism behind the entire cross-asset map, so it is worth slowing down.

Why the *real* rate and not the nominal one? Because a company's cash flows, a building's rents, gold's "yield" of zero — these are all most cleanly thought of in real, inflation-adjusted terms. If inflation rises and a firm can raise prices one-for-one, its nominal cash flows rise with inflation and the *nominal* discount rate that rose alongside them washes out. What is left, the thing that genuinely changes valuations, is the **real** discount rate — the return demanded *above and beyond* inflation. The nominal yield conflates the discount rate with the inflation that (partly) cancels against cash flows; the real yield isolates the part that does not cancel.

The deeper reading: the real yield is the price of *patience*. It is what the market pays you, in true purchasing power, to defer consumption for ten years. When that price is deeply negative — as in 2020–21, when DFII10 sat near −1.0% — the market is literally *paying you to take risk* rather than hold cash that loses purchasing power. When it is +2%, cash is a genuinely attractive alternative for the first time in fifteen years, and every risk asset has to compete against it. That competition is the correlation.

Gold is the purest case and the reason it shows the strongest negative correlation. Gold pays no coupon and no dividend; it just sits there. Its only "cost of carry" is the real return you forgo by not holding an inflation-protected bond instead. When the real yield is −1%, the opportunity cost of holding gold is *negative* — bonds are losing you purchasing power, so the zero-yielding rock wins, and gold rallies. When the real yield is +2%, you are giving up a guaranteed +2% real return to hold a rock; the rock loses. There is no growth story, no earnings, no inflation pass-through to muddy it — gold versus the real yield is almost the cleanest two-variable relationship in all of macro. We will see the −0.96 in a moment.

There is a second, subtler reason the *real* rate beats the nominal rate as a discount rate: it filters out the noise that has nothing to do with valuation. The nominal yield bounces around on every inflation print, every oil-price wiggle, every shift in the term premium investors demand for inflation uncertainty. Much of that motion is *inflation noise* that, as we saw, partly cancels against nominal cash flows and therefore should *not* move valuations. By stripping the breakeven out, the real yield removes that cancelling component and leaves a cleaner, lower-noise signal. This is why, empirically, asset correlations with the *real* yield are tighter than correlations with the *nominal* yield for the long-duration assets: you have removed a confounding variable. The nominal yield is two signals added together; the real yield is the one signal you actually want.

It also explains an apparent paradox beginners trip over: how can rising yields sometimes be *bullish* for stocks? When the nominal yield rises because *growth expectations* improved — real activity is picking up, earnings forecasts climb — equities can rally even as yields rise, because the numerator (cash flows) is rising faster than the discount rate is hurting the denominator. But when the nominal yield rises because the *real yield* is climbing on tighter policy with no growth offset, there is no numerator help, only denominator pain, and stocks fall. Same direction of nominal yields, opposite stock outcome. The real yield, read alongside whether growth is improving, tells you which world you are in. Pure real-yield rises with flat growth are the unambiguously hostile case — and they are exactly what 2022 delivered.

One more framing that makes the "master variable" claim click: think of the real yield as the *gravitational constant* of the asset universe. Every asset's valuation is a present-value calculation, and the real yield is the rate inside every one of those calculations. Change it and you change the gravity field that all assets fall through at once — which is why a real-yield move shows up everywhere simultaneously, while an oil move or a jobs surprise shows up in some places and not others. Nothing else in macro sits inside *every* valuation the way the real discount rate does. That ubiquity, not any special predictive magic, is what makes it the cleanest cross-asset correlation there is.

### Real versus nominal: a side-by-side

It is worth making the "real beats nominal" claim very explicit, because it is the crux of the whole post and the single thing that separates this correlation from the noisier one in the [bond-yields post](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/bond-yields-the-master-correlation-with-every-asset). Put the two side by side for the assets that matter most.

For **gold**, the contrast is stark. Gold's correlation with the *nominal* 10-year yield is real but loose — call it around −0.4 to −0.5 over long samples — because the nominal yield is contaminated by breakevens, and rising breakevens are *good* for gold even as rising real yields are bad. The two contaminants partly cancel, blurring the relationship. Gold's correlation with the *real* yield, by contrast, runs −0.8 and the scatter hits −0.96. Same asset, far cleaner read, because you removed the confounder. If you only had time to watch one number for gold, it is unambiguously the real yield, not the nominal one.

For **growth equities** the story is similar but with a wrinkle: the nominal yield occasionally correlates *positively* with stocks for short stretches when yields are rising on good growth news, which is exactly the contamination we discussed. The real yield, holding growth aside, is consistently negative for long-duration equities. That is why analysts who track the "real yield versus the Nasdaq" overlay get a cleaner picture than those staring at the nominal 10-year.

The general principle: whenever a variable is a *sum* of two components that affect an asset in *opposite* directions, the cleaner correlation will always be to the component, not the sum. The nominal yield = real yield + breakeven, and for risk assets the real part is hostile while the breakeven part is mixed-to-friendly. Decompose, and the signal sharpens. This is not a quirk of gold; it is a general lesson about reading any blended macro variable, and it is why a desk that watches DFII10 and T10YIE *separately* will out-read a desk that watches only the nominal 10-year. The policy-mechanism version of this argument — why the real yield is the master *signal* for the Fed's stance, not just for prices — is laid out in [Real vs nominal: inflation, real yields, and the master signal](/blog/trading/macro-trading/real-vs-nominal-inflation-real-yields-master-signal).

#### Worked example: same nominal yield, different gold outcomes

Two months, identical nominal-yield moves, opposite gold results — the contamination made visible. In month one, the nominal 10-year rises 0.4pp entirely because breakeven rose 0.4pp (an inflation-scare month); the real yield is unchanged. Gold, which loves rising breakevens and is indifferent to a flat real yield, *rises* — say +2%, about **+\$200** on a \$10,000 position. In month two, the nominal 10-year rises the *same* 0.4pp but entirely because the real yield rose 0.4pp (a hawkish-Fed month); breakeven is flat. Gold, facing a higher opportunity cost, *falls* — roughly 0.4 × \$354/oz ≈ −\$142/oz, about **−\$535** on the same \$10,000 (≈3.77 oz at \$2,650). Identical nominal-yield prints, a \$735 swing in opposite directions, decided entirely by *which component* moved. The intuition: a trader watching only the nominal yield saw "rates up" both months and could not explain why gold rose once and fell once — the trader watching the real yield knew instantly.

## The cross-asset map: one driver, the whole grid

Here is the claim that makes the real yield the *master* variable: it does not just drive gold. It drives the entire cross-section of risk assets, with consistent signs and a sensible ordering of magnitudes. The bar chart above is the real-yield row of the macro–asset correlation matrix — the correlation of each asset's return with a *rise* in the 10-year real yield. Walk it left to right, most-negative to least:

- **Gold: about −0.80.** The strongest negative, for the opportunity-cost reason above. No cash flows to muddy it.
- **US 10Y bond: about −0.70.** Mechanically a real-yield rise *is* (in part) a yield rise, and bond prices fall when yields rise. Long-duration bonds are the most rate-sensitive instrument there is.
- **Nasdaq (growth equities): about −0.65.** Growth stocks earn most of their value from cash flows far in the future, so a higher discount rate hits them hardest among equities. They are "long-duration equity."
- **Bitcoin: about −0.55.** Crypto trades as a zero-cash-flow, maximally-long-duration risk asset — pure discount-rate sensitivity plus a liquidity overlay. More on this below.
- **EM equities: about −0.55.** Emerging markets carry both growth-equity duration and a dollar-funding sensitivity; a higher U.S. real yield drains capital toward dollar assets.
- **S&P 500: about −0.50.** The broad index is less rate-sensitive than the Nasdaq because it has more near-term cash flows (banks, energy, staples), but the sign is the same.
- **US dollar: about +0.40.** The mirror. A higher U.S. real yield makes dollar assets more attractive relative to the rest of the world, so capital flows in and the dollar strengthens. It is the one *positive* in the row — the asset that *benefits* from the same move that hurts everything else.

Notice the structure. Every risk asset is negative; the magnitudes line up with **duration** (how far in the future the cash flows sit — gold and crypto being effectively infinite-duration); and the dollar is the lone positive. That is what a clean, single-factor relationship looks like. Contrast it with a messy driver like oil, whose correlation row flips sign across assets (good for energy stocks, bad for airlines, ambiguous for the index). The real yield does not do that. It pushes the whole grid one way.

The ordering is itself a teaching tool, because it is *not* random — it is a duration ladder. Rank the assets by how far their cash flows (or their "fair value anchor") sit in the future, and you recover almost exactly the order of the correlation magnitudes. Gold and crypto have no cash flows at all, so their value is pinned only to the opportunity cost — effectively infinite duration, the steepest sensitivity. Long bonds and growth equities have real but back-loaded cash flows — high duration, high sensitivity. The broad equity index mixes long- and short-duration cash flows, so it sits in the middle. And the dollar is on the *opposite* rung because it is the numeraire — the thing the higher real return is *paid in* — so it strengthens by the same logic that weakens everything else. When you can predict the ordering of a correlation row from a single concept (duration), you are looking at a genuine single-factor relationship, not a statistical coincidence. That predictability is the real meaning of "clean."

#### Worked example: the dollar and EM as the mirror leg

The dollar is the only positive in the row, and it is worth a dollar figure of its own because it is where the real yield's effect on *the rest of the world* shows up. Suppose the U.S. 10-year real yield rises 1pp while real yields abroad are unchanged — the U.S. now offers a full extra point of guaranteed real return relative to, say, the eurozone or an emerging market. Global capital chases that differential: it sells local-currency assets, buys dollars, and parks in U.S. real-yielding bonds. You hold a \$10,000 emerging-market equity position. EM equities carry both the growth-equity duration hit *and* this funding-outflow hit, which is why their real-yield correlation (−0.55) is as steep as Bitcoin's despite being ordinary stocks. A +1pp U.S. real-yield move, in a clean repricing like 2022, has historically been worth a double-digit drawdown for EM equity once the currency depreciation is layered on the equity decline — call it roughly **−\$1,200 to −\$1,500** on your \$10,000, of which a meaningful chunk is the currency, not the stocks. The flip side: a dollar-based investor holding the *dollar itself* (or short EM) made money on the same move. The intuition: a rising U.S. real yield does not just discount future cash flows harder, it physically *pulls capital* across borders toward the dollar — so the dollar is the asset on the receiving end of everything the real yield drains from the rest.

#### Worked example: the implied gold move from a real-yield change

Take the relationship that held from 2007 to 2021, when corr(real yield, gold) was −0.96. The fitted regression line had a slope of about **−\$354 per ounce for every +1 percentage point** the 10-year real yield rose. Now suppose you hold a \$10,000 gold position and the 10-year real yield rises 0.5pp — say from 1.5% to 2.0%. With gold around \$2,650/oz in 2025, your position is about 3.77 ounces. The implied price move is 0.5 × (−\$354) = **−\$177 per ounce**, so your 3.77 ounces lose about 3.77 × \$177 ≈ **−\$667**, a roughly **−6.7%** hit on your \$10,000. Flip the sign for a 0.5pp *fall* in real yields and you would expect about **+\$667**. The intuition: gold has no cash flows, so its entire valuation is the opportunity cost of holding it — move the real yield and you move gold almost mechanically, at least in the era when the relationship held.

#### Worked example: why growth stocks bleed more than value

Discounting math makes "duration" concrete. Take two \$10,000 stock positions. Stock V (value) is expected to return most of its cash to you within five years; Stock G (growth) back-loads its cash flows out to year fifteen and beyond. Raise the real discount rate by 1pp. The present value of a dollar arriving in year 5 falls by roughly 1% × 5 ≈ 5%; the present value of a dollar arriving in year 15 falls by roughly 1% × 15 ≈ 15%. So the same +1pp real-yield move costs Stock V about **−\$500** but costs Stock G about **−\$1,500** — three times the damage, purely because its cash flows sit further out. That is the entire reason the Nasdaq's real-yield beta (−0.65) is steeper than the S&P 500's (−0.50): the index with the longer-duration cash flows is the one the discount rate punishes hardest. The intuition: a real-yield rise is a tax on the future, and growth stocks own more of the future.

### Sign, strength, lead-lag: how to actually measure this correlation

Every relationship in this series has four properties — a sign, a strength, a lead/lag, and a tendency to flip — and the real yield is worth running through all four, because it is the cleanest case to learn the discipline on.

**Sign** is the easy part and the most stable: negative for every risk asset, positive for the dollar, and it essentially never flips *within* a normal regime. The opportunity-cost logic does not have a "good news" version for a zero-coupon asset — a higher real return on cash is unambiguously a higher hurdle. That sign-stability is exactly what earns the real yield its "cleanest" reputation; contrast a payrolls print, whose sign flips between "good news is good" and "good news is bad" depending on whether the market fears overheating.

**Strength** is what the correlation coefficient measures: how *tightly* the assets track the real yield, on a scale from 0 (no relationship) to ±1 (a perfect line). Gold's −0.80 and the gold-versus-real-yield scatter's −0.96 are unusually high for macro, where 0.3–0.5 is more typical. A useful way to read a coefficient: square it to get R², the fraction of variance explained. An r of −0.96 means about 0.92 of gold's annual variation lined up with the real yield over 2007–21 — astonishing for a single macro factor, and the reason desks treat the real yield as gold's master variable rather than one of many.

**Lead/lag** asks *which moves first*. For the real yield the honest answer is "roughly coincident, with the real yield a touch ahead" — TIPS are liquid, forward-looking markets, so the real yield tends to move *with* or slightly *before* the assets it prices, as both digest the same Fed signal. This is different from a slow macro indicator like building permits, which leads GDP by about nine months. The practical implication: you cannot use the real yield as an *early-warning* indicator the way you use the yield-curve slope; you use it as a *same-day attribution* tool — "gold fell today; was it the real yield?" — and as a *regime* gauge.

**Flips** are the fourth property, and the real yield's one famous flip (the 2022–24 gold decoupling) is covered below. The point of measuring all four is that "there is a strong negative correlation" is a lazy half-sentence. The full statement is: "the real yield is negatively correlated with risk assets (sign), strongly so for long-duration ones like gold and growth (strength ~0.6–0.8), roughly coincident in timing (lead/lag), and the sign holds across regimes except when a price-insensitive structural buyer overrides it (the flip)." That is a sentence you can trade on. The mechanics of *computing* these — rolling windows, why a single full-sample number lies — are the toolkit covered across the Foundations track of this series.

## The real-yield path and the regimes it created

Correlations only matter if the driver actually moves, and over 2020–26 the real yield moved more than it had in a generation. The chart below is DFII10, the 10-year TIPS real yield, across that window — and it tells the macro story of the decade in one line.

![10Y TIPS real yield path from minus one percent to plus two percent with regime shading](/imgs/blogs/real-yields-and-the-cleanest-macro-correlation-3.png)

Two regimes, shaded. From mid-2020 through late 2021 the real yield was **deeply negative**, bottoming near −1.06% in early 2021. The Fed had pinned policy rates at zero and was buying bonds while inflation expectations recovered, so nominal yields stayed low *and* breakevens rose — the real yield got crushed below zero. In that regime, cash was a guaranteed loser of purchasing power, the opportunity cost of every risk asset was negative, and the result was the **everything-rally**: gold near record highs, the Nasdaq up enormous amounts, Bitcoin to \$69,000, meme stocks, SPACs, the lot. Negative real yields lifted everything because there was nothing safe to lose by chasing risk.

Then the regime flipped. As inflation proved persistent, the Fed hiked aggressively through 2022 and the real yield rocketed from −0.14% in April 2022 to +1.74% by October — and on to roughly +2.2% by late 2024. That repricing is the second shaded band, and it is the mirror image: every risk asset that the negative real yield had inflated now deflated. The 2022 bear market was not really a "stock" story or a "crypto" story or a "bond" story. It was *one* story — the real yield going from −1% to +2% — wearing five different costumes.

![Bitcoin became a long-duration real-rate asset rolling correlation with Nasdaq](/imgs/blogs/real-yields-and-the-cleanest-macro-correlation-6.png)

The crypto chart drives the point home. Bitcoin's 90-day correlation with the Nasdaq — itself a real-rate proxy — was near zero before 2020, then spiked to about **0.65 in 2022** exactly as the real-yield shock hit, and faded back toward 0.2–0.3 by 2024–25 as the shock passed. During the regime change, the asset everyone called "uncorrelated" behaved like the *most* rate-sensitive thing on the screen: a zero-cash-flow, infinite-duration claim on future liquidity, repriced violently by the real discount rate. The lesson is not that Bitcoin is "really a tech stock." It is that when the real yield makes a generational move, it temporarily dominates every asset's return, dragging even the idiosyncratic ones into its orbit. (The full crypto-versus-macro treatment — liquidity, the dollar, the time-varying correlation — is in [Crypto as a macro asset: the liquidity correlation](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/crypto-as-a-macro-asset-the-liquidity-correlation).)

![Matrix of real yield falling versus rising flipping every asset at once](/imgs/blogs/real-yields-and-the-cleanest-macro-correlation-5.png)

The matrix above is the regime summary. One variable, two states, and the entire cross-section flips together — risk assets up and the dollar down when the real yield falls; risk assets down and the dollar up when it rises. That synchronicity is exactly why the real yield deserves the title "master variable": it is the rare driver that moves the whole board with one hand.

## The everything-rally, made concrete

It is worth dwelling on *why* negative real yields lifted everything, because it is the most counterintuitive part for a beginner. How can a single number push gold, growth stocks, junk bonds, and crypto up all at once when those assets seem to have nothing in common?

![When the real yield falls risk assets rally together bar chart](/imgs/blogs/real-yields-and-the-cleanest-macro-correlation-7.png)

The bar chart is the previous correlation row with the signs flipped — the response to a real-yield *fall*. It is the everything-rally drawn as a picture: every risk asset green, only the dollar red. The mechanism is the opportunity-cost channel running in reverse. When the safe real return collapses below zero, the *alternative* to taking risk becomes a guaranteed loss of purchasing power. Investors who would normally hold cash or short bonds are pushed out the risk curve in search of any positive real return, and the longest-duration assets — the ones whose value is most leveraged to the discount rate — rally the most. That is why gold (infinite duration, zero coupon) and growth/crypto (back-loaded cash flows) led the 2020–21 melt-up: they have the most discount-rate leverage, so a falling discount rate inflates them the most.

This is also why the everything-rally felt eerily *uniform* to anyone who lived through it — and why bear-market commentators kept reaching for the phrase "nowhere to hide" in 2022. Both observations are the same fact seen from opposite regimes. When a single variable sits inside every asset's valuation and that variable makes a large move, diversification across those assets stops working, because they are no longer independent bets — they are all the *same* bet on the real yield wearing different tickers. A 60/40 portfolio, a tech-and-crypto portfolio, a gold-and-stocks portfolio: in a real-yield repricing they all move together because the thing being repriced is common to all of them. The painful corollary for portfolio construction is that the assets you bought *for* diversification (long bonds, gold) are often the *most* real-yield-sensitive, so they fail you precisely in the regime — a rate shock — where you most wanted the protection. Real diversification against a real-yield shock comes from assets whose cash flows are *short*-duration or that benefit from the move (cash, the dollar, floating-rate exposure), not from spreading across many long-duration assets that share the same hidden factor.

#### Worked example: sizing the everything-rally on a \$10,000 sleeve

Imagine the late-2020 setup: the 10-year real yield is about to fall another 0.5pp (it went from roughly −0.55% in 2020 to −0.95% in 2021). You hold a \$10,000 risk sleeve split evenly across gold, the Nasdaq, and Bitcoin. Using the gold slope of about −\$354/oz per +1pp (so **+\$354/oz per −1pp**, or +\$177 for the 0.5pp fall), the gold third (about \$3,333, ≈1.86 oz at \$1,800) gains roughly 1.86 × \$177 ≈ **+\$329**, a ~10% move on that slice. The growth and crypto thirds, with even more discount-rate leverage, historically moved more than that in the same window. The point is not the precise figure — it is that **one** macro variable falling half a point lit up every slice of the sleeve at once. When you hear "a rising tide lifts all boats," in 2020–21 the tide was the real yield, and it was running out to sea. The intuition: negative real yields do not reward picking the right asset, they reward owning *duration* anywhere — which is exactly why the rally felt indiscriminate.

### The channel, asset by asset

The single opportunity-cost channel expresses itself a little differently in each asset, and understanding the variation is what lets you predict *which* assets will lead a given real-yield move.

- **Gold and other zero-coupon stores of value.** Purest case. The entire correlation is opportunity cost: a real-yielding bond is gold's direct competitor, so gold's "fair value" is essentially a falling function of the real yield. No earnings, no growth, no inflation pass-through to dilute the signal — which is why it has the strongest coefficient (−0.80) and the famous −0.96 scatter.
- **Long-duration / growth equities.** A stock is a claim on future cash flows; raising the real discount rate shrinks the present value of those flows, and back-loaded (growth) cash flows shrink most. The real yield therefore drives the *valuation multiple* (the P/E), separately from the *earnings* themselves. In a real-yield repricing, you can have *rising* earnings and *falling* prices at the same time because the multiple is collapsing — exactly what 2022's growth selloff was.
- **Long-dated bonds.** Almost tautological: a real-yield rise *is* part of a yield rise, and bond prices move inversely to yields with a sensitivity (duration) that grows with maturity. A 10-year bond loses roughly 9% for a +1pp yield move; a 30-year loses far more. The bond is the discount rate, so it is the most direct read of all.
- **Crypto.** A maximally long-duration, zero-cash-flow risk asset with a *liquidity* overlay. When the real yield falls, the opportunity cost of holding a non-yielding speculative asset drops and liquidity floods in; when it rises, both reverse. Crypto's coefficient (−0.55) understates how violently it moves in a *regime change* precisely because the correlation is time-varying — near zero in calm, spiking in a shock.
- **EM equities and the dollar.** These share a *funding* channel on top of the duration channel. A higher U.S. real yield makes dollar assets more attractive than the rest of the world, pulling capital home: EM equities and currencies weaken, and the dollar strengthens. The dollar's *positive* sign (+0.40) is the same coin's other face — it is the asset that *receives* the capital that everything else loses.

#### Worked example: the multiple-compression channel in growth equities

Make the P/E channel concrete. A growth stock trades at a forward P/E of 40 — investors are paying \$40 for every \$1 of next year's earnings because they expect the earnings to compound for years. A crude but standard approximation treats a stock's "duration" like a long bond's: a high-multiple growth name behaves like a 20–30-year-duration instrument. Now raise the real yield by 1pp. With ~25-year effective duration, the valuation should fall on the order of 25% from the multiple alone — the P/E of 40 compresses toward roughly 30, *even if next year's earnings estimate did not change at all*. On a \$10,000 position that is about **−\$2,500**, driven entirely by the discount rate, not by anything the company did. Hold that against a low-multiple value stock at a forward P/E of 10 (shorter effective duration, maybe 8–10 years): the same +1pp costs it only ~8–10%, about **−\$900**. The intuition: in a real-yield repricing, the most expensive, longest-duration growth names are not being punished for bad results — they are being punished for *when* their results arrive.

## Common misconceptions

**"Gold is an inflation hedge."** This is the big one, and it is mostly wrong as usually stated. Gold's correlation with the CPI level is weak and unstable (about −0.10 with a hot CPI *surprise* in the matrix). What gold actually tracks is the **real yield**, at about −0.80. Inflation *matters*, but only through the real yield — high inflation that the Fed chases with rate hikes drives real yields *up* and is *bad* for gold (see 2022); high inflation the Fed tolerates drives real yields *down* and is good for gold (see 2020–21). The 2022 case is the proof: CPI was running near 9% and gold went *nowhere*, because the real yield was screaming higher. "Gold hedges inflation" should be "gold hedges *falling real yields*."

**"The 10-year yield is what drives markets."** The *nominal* 10-year yield is a blend of the real yield and breakeven inflation, and the two halves have *opposite* implications for some assets. A nominal yield rising on higher breakevens (more expected inflation) can be *good* for gold and neutral for stocks; the same nominal rise driven by the real yield is bad for both. Watching the nominal yield alone is watching a sum without knowing the addends. The deeper why-it-moves treatment of the nominal-yield channel is in [Bond yields: the master correlation with every asset](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/bond-yields-the-master-correlation-with-every-asset); this post's point is that the *real* component is the cleaner signal.

**"Crypto is uncorrelated, so it diversifies my portfolio."** It is uncorrelated *until a regime change*, at which point it correlates with the real yield more strongly than your tech stocks do — its 90-day correlation with the Nasdaq hit ~0.65 in 2022. Diversification from a long-duration, zero-cash-flow asset evaporates exactly when the discount rate makes its big move, which is exactly when you needed the diversification.

**"A clean correlation is a reliable trade."** −0.96 over 2007–2021 is about as clean as macro gets, and it still broke. From 2022 to 2025 the gold-versus-real-yield correlation flipped to roughly **+0.6** as central banks bought gold for reserve-diversification reasons that had nothing to do with the real yield. A high historical correlation tells you the relationship *was* strong; it does not promise the relationship is *live*. Always condition on the regime.

**"Real yields only matter for gold and bonds."** They set the floor for *everything*, including things that feel unrelated — venture valuations, housing affordability, EM currencies, the price of unprofitable growth. Anything valued by discounting future cash flows is, by definition, a real-yield asset. The real yield is not a niche bond-market number; it is the price of the future.

**"A negative real yield means the bond market is broken."** It feels absurd that anyone would lock in a *guaranteed loss of purchasing power* by buying a −1% real-yielding TIPS, yet that was the world of 2020–21. It was not broken; it was the central bank deliberately holding the real cost of money below zero to force capital out of cash and into the real economy. Negative real yields are a *policy choice*, and recognizing them as such is the tell for the everything-rally regime — when the safe asset is engineered to lose, every risk asset is bid by construction. Far from a glitch, a deeply negative real yield is one of the most powerful bullish setups for duration assets there is, precisely because it punishes the alternative.

**"The full-sample correlation is the real correlation."** Compute corr(real yield, gold) across the entire 2007–2025 sample and you get roughly *zero* — the strongly negative 2007–21 period and the positive 2022–25 decoupling cancel out. A naive analyst would conclude "no relationship," which is exactly backwards: there is a *very* strong relationship that *changed regime*. This is the cardinal sin of macro correlation analysis — a single full-sample number averages across regimes and can hide a powerful, tradeable relationship inside a meaningless aggregate. Always look at the rolling correlation and the scatter, never just the one summary statistic.

## How it shows up in real markets

**2013 — the taper tantrum, a clean preview.** Before 2020 there was a smaller but textbook-perfect episode. In May 2013, the Fed merely *hinted* it might slow its bond buying, and the 10-year real yield jumped from roughly −0.6% to +0.6% over a few months — about a 1.2pp real-yield shock. Gold, which had spent 2011–12 floating on deeply negative real yields, fell from about \$1,670 (2012 average) toward \$1,410 (2013 average), one of its worst years in a generation. There was no inflation event, no growth collapse — just the real yield repricing higher, and gold mechanically following. It is the −0.96 relationship caught in a single before-and-after, and a reminder that the channel was operating long before 2020 made it famous.

**2020–21 — negative real yields, the everything-rally.** Real yield ≈ −1.0%. Gold near \$1,800, Nasdaq up ~40% in 2020 alone, Bitcoin from \$10k to \$69k, junk-bond yields at record lows. The unifying cause was not "stimulus" in the vague sense — it was the specific fact that the real cost of money was deeply negative, so every duration asset was bid. Note how the gold price barely moved from 2020 (\$1,770 average) to 2021 (\$1,799 average) even as the real yield fell further: gold was already near its ceiling for that level of real yields, having repriced hard in 2019–20. The everything-rally's *new* money flowed disproportionately into the longest-duration speculative assets — crypto, profitless tech, SPACs — that had the most room to inflate.

**October 2022 — the +2% repricing.** Real yield from −0.14% (April) to +1.74% (October). Peak-to-trough, the Nasdaq fell ~36%, gold dropped ~20% from its March spike, Bitcoin fell ~65% on the year, the dollar (DXY) hit a 20-year high, and long Treasuries lost ~30%. Five assets, one driver. If you had been watching only CPI you would have been baffled that gold did nothing during 9% inflation; if you were watching the real yield, it was the most predictable bear market in years.

**2023 — the real yield stays high, growth still leads (the twist).** Here is where the single-factor story needs nuance. Real yields stayed elevated (~+1.7% to +2.5%) through 2023, yet the Nasdaq *rallied* hard. Why? The AI narrative added a *growth* story strong enough to overpower the still-high discount rate — a reminder that the real yield is the dominant factor, not the *only* factor. The correlation is strongest when the real yield is the thing *moving*; when it is high-but-stable and a different story (AI earnings) is moving, that story can drive returns instead. This is the difference between the real yield as a *level* and as a *change*. A high but stable real yield is a known, priced-in hurdle that a fresh earnings story can clear. A real yield *in motion* — the 2022 surge — is an unpriced shock that nothing else can offset. The correlation you can trade on is the one to the *change*, which is why the cleanest readings come during real-yield repricings, not during plateaus. The October–November 2023 episode is the micro-version: when the real yield spiked toward +2.5% into late October, the AI rally stalled and growth wobbled; when it eased back through November and December, growth ripped again. Even with a powerful growth story running, the real yield's *moves* still set the rhythm.

**2022–24 — the gold decoupling.** The honesty case. Real yields rose to multi-year highs, which should have crushed gold — and yet gold ran from ~\$1,800 to over \$2,650, a brand-new all-time high, *with* real yields elevated. The clean −0.96 relationship broke. The cause was structural: aggressive central-bank gold buying (reserve diversification away from the dollar, accelerated after 2022 sanctions) added a price-insensitive buyer that overwhelmed the real-yield channel. The relationship did not become *wrong* — the opportunity-cost force was still there, just outweighed by a bigger force. This is the canonical lesson of the whole series, and it has its own deep dive: [Structural shifts: why today's correlations aren't yesterday's](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/structural-shifts-why-todays-correlations-arent-yesterdays).

![Gold versus the 10Y real yield near perfect negative line until 2022 scatter](/imgs/blogs/real-yields-and-the-cleanest-macro-correlation-4.png)

The scatter is the whole drama in one frame. The blue dots (2007–2021) fall along a tight downward line — r = −0.96, slope ≈ −\$354/oz per +1pp. The red diamonds (2022–2025) sit *above and to the right* of where the line says they should be: high real yields *and* high gold prices, the decoupling that turned the correlation positive (+0.6) over those years. The cleanest correlation in macro, and its cleanest break, on one chart. Treat it as a permanent warning label: a correlation is a regime, never a constant.

## How to read it and use it

If you take one operational habit from this post, make it this: **decompose the nominal yield before you react.** Every time you see "yields rose," ask whether it was the real yield or the breakeven. The real yield is the discount-rate signal; the breakeven is the inflation-expectations signal; they have different and sometimes opposite asset implications.

A practical routine:

1. **Pull DFII10 (the 10-year real yield) and T10YIE (the 10-year breakeven) together.** Nominal = the sum. Track which one is moving. A real-yield-driven move is the high-conviction cross-asset signal; a breakeven-driven move is an inflation-expectations signal.
2. **Read the level as a regime, not just the change.** Deeply negative real yields (below ~−0.5%) = the everything-rally regime, own duration. Positive and rising real yields = the repricing regime, the discount-rate tax is on, fade long-duration risk. Around zero and stable = no strong signal from this factor; look elsewhere.
3. **Rank assets by duration to size the response.** Gold and crypto have the most discount-rate leverage, then growth equities and long bonds, then the broad index, with the dollar on the *opposite* side. A real-yield move should hit your longest-duration holdings hardest.
4. **Check whether the real yield is the thing moving.** The correlation is cleanest when the real yield is in motion. When it is high-but-stable and a growth or liquidity story is dominant (2023's AI rally), expect the real-yield correlation to weaken.
5. **Know what invalidates the signal.** A price-insensitive structural buyer (central banks in gold, 2022–24), a regime where inflation rather than real rates is the dominant risk, or a flight-to-safety panic where correlations all go to one — any of these can override the opportunity-cost channel. When the scatter's dots leave the line, the regime has changed; do not keep trading the old slope.

A concrete way to operationalize step 5: keep a rolling correlation, not a static one. If you compute the correlation between gold and the real yield over a trailing one- or two-year window and watch it through time, you will *see* the relationship weaken before you lose money on it. The 2007–21 reading sat near −0.8 to −0.9 for years; the trailing window then drifted toward zero through 2022 and turned positive in 2023–24. A trader watching the rolling number had a live warning that the regime was shifting — the structural gold-buying story was overwhelming the rate channel — months before the full-sample number would have told them anything. The static −0.96 is a historical fact; the *rolling* correlation is the live regime gauge, and the gap between them is the whole reason this series insists that correlation is a regime, not a constant.

One more discipline worth naming: separate *attribution* from *prediction*. The real yield is superb for attribution — when an asset moves and you want to know why, checking whether the real yield moved resolves a huge fraction of cross-asset confusion on the spot. It is weaker for prediction, because TIPS are forward-looking and roughly coincident, so by the time the real yield has moved, the assets have usually moved with it. The mistake is to treat a coincident attribution tool as a leading indicator and try to "wait for the real yield to tell you" what gold will do next. It will not lead; it will move *with* gold. What it *will* do is tell you, durably, which regime you are in — and the regime is the thing that decides whether owning duration is rewarded or punished over the next year.

The real yield earns the "master variable" title not because it never fails, but because, when it moves, it is the one driver that reliably moves the whole cross-section the same way — and because it is mechanically the *right* rate, the inflation-stripped opportunity cost that prices every future cash flow. Learn to watch it instead of the nominal yield, and a lot of seemingly unrelated market moves resolve into a single, legible story: gold, growth, crypto, and the dollar are not five separate trades but four expressions of one number, the price of the future.
