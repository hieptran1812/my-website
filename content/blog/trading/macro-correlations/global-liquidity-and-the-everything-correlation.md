> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Central-bank liquidity is the tide under all risk assets: when net liquidity expands (QE, falling RRP/TGA) risk assets rise together, and when it drains (QT, rising RRP/TGA) they fall together — which is why cross-asset correlations among risk assets are positive, not zero.
>
> - The single biggest reason stocks, crypto, gold, and EM equities co-move is that they are all priced against **the same pool of money**. Liquidity is a *common factor*.
> - The trader's proxy is **net liquidity = Fed total assets − ON RRP − TGA** (all in USD trillions). It surged from about \$3.4T to \$6.8T in 2020–21 and drained toward \$5.65T in 2022.
> - Liquidity is a **slow tide, not a fast trigger** — it leads risk assets by weeks to months and is far better at explaining the *regime* than the next print.
> - The one fact to remember: **the everything-rally and the everything-selloff are liquidity phenomena.** Bitcoin has the highest liquidity beta (r ≈ +0.60); the US dollar has a negative one (r ≈ −0.40).

In November 2021, almost everything that could be called a "risk asset" was at or near an all-time high at the same time. The S&P 500 closed the year at 4,766. The Nasdaq 100 had nearly doubled off its 2020 low. Bitcoin had touched \$69,000. Gold sat near \$1,800. Junk bonds traded at the tightest spreads in a generation, and unprofitable tech companies were valued like utilities. A diversified investor looked at the screen and felt clever: everything was up. The uncomfortable truth was that everything was up *for one reason*, and that reason was about to reverse.

Through 2022, that one reason reversed. The S&P 500 fell about 19% on the year. Bitcoin fell about 64%. Long Treasuries had their worst year in modern history. Gold went nowhere. The supposedly diversified portfolio fell as a single block, because it had never really been diversified — it had been one bet, dressed up as many. The bet was on **liquidity**: on the price and quantity of money supplied by the central bank. When that tide came in, every boat rose. When it went out, every boat dropped.

This post is about that tide. We will build the idea from zero — what "liquidity" actually means, where it comes from, how quantitative easing and tightening change it, and why a trader's back-of-envelope measure called *net liquidity* tracks risk assets so closely. Then we will look the honest problems in the eye: liquidity *leads* assets by an uncertain lag, full-sample correlations flatter the relationship, and "it's all liquidity" becomes a lazy excuse if you can't measure it. By the end you should be able to compute net liquidity yourself, read it as a regime signal, and understand why the deepest layer under the whole [macro-asset correlation matrix](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/the-macro-asset-correlation-matrix) is a single number for the world's supply of money.

Why does this deserve its own post in a series about correlation? Because liquidity is not just *another* correlation among the dozens this series catalogues — it is the *reason so many of the others exist*. When you ask why stocks and crypto correlate, why a strong dollar pressures everything, why "risk-on/risk-off" days exist at all, the answer keeps bottoming out in the same place: these assets share a common driver, and that driver is the price and quantity of money. Liquidity is the factor that turns a portfolio of supposedly different bets into one big bet. Understand it, and a great deal of seemingly unrelated market behaviour suddenly has a single cause.

## Foundations: what "liquidity" actually means

The word *liquidity* gets used for three different things, and conflating them is the source of most confusion. Let's separate them carefully, because the rest of the post depends on getting this right.

**1. Market liquidity** is how easily you can buy or sell an asset without moving its price. A stock that trades \$50 billion a day is liquid; a tiny micro-cap that trades \$50,000 a day is not. This is a *property of a market*, not a quantity of money. It matters — illiquid markets gap violently in a crisis — but it is **not** what we mean here.

**2. Funding liquidity** is how easily a bank, dealer, or fund can borrow cash to finance its positions. When funding dries up, leveraged players are forced to sell, which is the mechanism behind most crashes. Funding liquidity is closer to our topic, but still a step removed.

**3. Monetary liquidity** — the subject of this post — is the *quantity of money the central bank has put into the financial system*, and the *price* at which money can be borrowed. This is the tide. When the central bank creates more money and pushes down its price (interest rates), there is more cash chasing the same set of assets, and asset prices rise. When it does the reverse, asset prices fall. Everything below is about monetary liquidity.

### Bank reserves, the monetary base, and broad money

To make "the quantity of money" precise, you need three nested layers. From narrowest to broadest:

- **Bank reserves** are deposits that commercial banks hold *at the central bank*. They are the most special form of money: a bank can't lend reserves to you and me, but it can use them to settle payments with other banks and to meet its regulatory requirements. When the Fed "creates money," it is literally crediting reserves to a bank's account. US bank reserves went from about \$1.5T in 2019 to a peak above \$4T in 2021 — that surge *is* QE.
- **The monetary base** (sometimes "high-powered money") is reserves *plus* physical currency in circulation. It is the raw material the central bank controls directly. The US monetary base jumped from about \$3.4T at the end of 2019 to \$6.4T at the end of 2021.
- **Broad money (M2)** is the money the public actually holds and spends: currency, checking accounts, savings accounts, small time deposits, and retail money-market funds. M2 is much larger and is created mostly by *commercial banks lending*, not by the central bank directly. US M2 went from \$15.4T at the end of 2019 to \$21.5T at the end of 2021 — a \$6 trillion expansion in two years, the fastest in the data's history.

Here is the key relationship, stated plainly: the central bank controls the base directly, the base influences (but does not mechanically determine) broad money, and the *flow* of new money — base and broad — is what we call liquidity creation. The chart below shows how dramatically the Fed's own balance sheet, which sits at the center of all three layers, has moved.

A common beginner trap is the old "money multiplier" textbook story, where the central bank prints a dollar of reserves and banks mechanically lend it out ten times over to create ten dollars of broad money. The modern reality is looser and more important to understand. Banks do not lend out reserves to the public — reserves stay trapped in the interbank system. When a bank makes a loan, it *creates a new deposit out of thin air* (broad money), and only then worries about funding and reserves. So QE reliably swells the *base* (reserves), but whether that base turns into broad-money growth depends on whether banks and borrowers actually want to lend and borrow. In 2020–21 they did, with help from fiscal stimulus, so M2 exploded. After the great financial crisis of 2008–09, by contrast, the Fed tripled the base through QE but broad money grew only modestly, because risk-averse banks sat on the reserves. The takeaway: **base growth is necessary but not sufficient for a broad-money boom — and the liquidity tide that lifts assets responds to both.** This is also why "money printing causes hyperinflation" is too simple: the 2008 QE did not, because it never reached broad money, while the 2020 episode coincided with the first serious US inflation in forty years precisely because it did.

There is one more layer worth naming, because it is where the rubber meets the road for asset prices: **the velocity of money** — how fast each dollar changes hands. A huge pile of money sitting idle in bank reserves or parked at the Fed does nothing for asset prices; the same money *circulating*, chasing assets, is what lifts the tide. This is exactly why the next two facilities we will meet — the RRP and the TGA — matter so much. They are giant pools where money can sit *idle*, removed from circulation, even though it technically exists. Liquidity that markets feel is not the total money in existence; it is the money actually sloshing around looking for a home.

### The correlation idea, stated once

Since this is a series about *correlation*, let's pin the term down. A **correlation** measures how two things move together. The Pearson correlation coefficient, written r, runs from −1 to +1: +1 means they move in perfect lockstep, −1 means they move in perfect opposition, 0 means no linear relationship. When we say two risk assets have a correlation of +0.6, we mean that in the historical sample, on days or months when one rose, the other usually rose too — about as often and about as much as 0.6 implies. (For the full machinery — Pearson versus Spearman, correlation versus beta, why a single number hides regimes — see [what correlation actually measures](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/what-correlation-actually-measures-pearson-spearman-beta).)

The thesis of this post in one line: **the reason risk assets have a high positive correlation with each other is that they all have a high positive correlation with the same hidden variable — liquidity.** When many things correlate with one common factor, they automatically correlate with each other. That's not magic; it's arithmetic.

## How the Fed adds and drains liquidity: QE and QT

The central bank changes liquidity through two levers: the *price* of money (the policy interest rate) and the *quantity* of money (the size of its balance sheet). This post is mostly about the quantity lever, because that is the one that maps onto "net liquidity." We will not re-derive the rate mechanism here — for that, read [interest rates: the price of money](/blog/trading/macro-trading/interest-rates-the-price-of-money-master-variable) and [QE vs QT](/blog/trading/macro-trading/qe-vs-qt-how-balance-sheet-policy-moves-markets). Here we focus on what the balance-sheet operations *do* to the supply of money.

### Quantitative easing (QE): pumping the tide up

In **quantitative easing**, the central bank creates new reserves out of nothing and uses them to buy bonds — usually Treasuries and mortgage-backed securities — from banks and dealers. The seller hands over a bond and receives newly created reserves in exchange. The central bank's balance sheet grows by the amount of bonds it bought; that growth is matched by an equal growth in bank reserves on the liability side. The net effect: the financial system now holds more cash and fewer bonds.

Walk through one transaction concretely. A pension fund owns a \$100 million Treasury bond. The Fed buys it. The fund's custodian bank receives \$100 million of newly created reserves; the bank credits the pension fund's deposit account with \$100 million of cash. The bond is now on the Fed's balance sheet; the cash is now in the system. The pension fund did not want cash — it wanted a return — so it goes shopping. It buys corporate bonds, which pushes corporate yields down, which makes stocks relatively more attractive, which pushes stocks up. Multiply this by trillions of dollars over two years and you have the 2020–21 melt-up.

Why does that lift asset prices? Two channels. First, the *portfolio-balance* channel: the seller now has cash that earns very little, so they reach for higher-yielding assets — corporate bonds, stocks, anything with a return. Their buying lifts prices. This is sometimes called the "hot potato" effect: the system as a whole *cannot* get rid of the new cash (it just moves from one holder to the next), so the only thing that adjusts is *prices* — assets get bid up until investors are willing to hold the larger cash pile at the new, higher valuations. Second, the *signalling and rate* channel: QE pushes down longer-term yields, which lowers the discount rate used to value every future cash flow, mechanically raising the present value of stocks, real estate, and long-duration anything. Both channels push in the same direction: more money, cheaper money, higher asset prices.

A useful way to hold this is the discount-rate identity that prices every asset: value = (expected future cash flows) ÷ (discount rate). QE attacks the *denominator* — it pushes the discount rate down — and a lower denominator raises the value of *everything with a future cash flow at once*. That "all at once" is the seed of the everything correlation: the same denominator sits under the stock, the bond, the building, and (through sentiment and the dollar) the gold bar and the coin. Change the denominator and you change every price in the same direction. We will see this fan-out explicitly in the transmission diagram later.

### Quantitative tightening (QT): draining the tide

**Quantitative tightening** is the reverse. The central bank lets bonds mature without replacing them (or, more aggressively, sells them). When a bond on the Fed's book matures, the Treasury pays it off, reserves are extinguished, and the balance sheet shrinks. The system now holds more bonds and less cash. The same two channels run in reverse: investors have less spare cash to reach for risk, and yields drift up, raising the discount rate and pressing asset values down.

QT is slower and gentler than QE for a structural reason: the Fed runs it "passively," by capping how much of its maturing bonds it replaces (the so-called runoff cap), rather than by actively selling into the market. So QE can be a firehose — the Fed bought \$120 billion a month at the 2020–21 peak — while QT is a steady trickle, around \$60–95 billion a month. The asymmetry matters for the correlation story: the tide comes in fast and goes out slowly, which is part of why the everything-rally felt explosive and the everything-drain felt like a grinding bear market rather than a single crash.

The US story since 2020 is the cleanest case study you will find. The Fed's balance sheet exploded from about \$4.2T in early 2020 to a peak of **\$8.96T in April 2022** — call it \$4.7 trillion of new money in two years. Then QT began, and the balance sheet drained toward \$6.66T by mid-2025, removing about **\$2.3 trillion**. As you would expect from the tide model, the everything-rally happened during the inflation of the balance sheet and the everything-selloff began as it stopped growing and started to shrink. The three earlier QE waves on the long-run chart — QE1 after the 2008 crisis, QE2/QE3 through 2010–14, and the COVID infinite-QE — each coincided with a multi-year risk-asset rally, while the in-between pauses (the 2015–18 stretch when the balance sheet was flat to falling) were choppier. One balance sheet, decade after decade, has tracked the broad mood of risk assets.

## Net liquidity: the trader's proxy

Here is where the topic gets practical. The Fed's headline balance sheet (the "WALCL" series, for "Wednesday-level Assets, Less ...") is not the same as the cash that actually reaches markets. Two large accounts sit *between* the Fed's balance sheet and the banking system, and they can soak up or release liquidity even when the headline balance sheet barely moves. Traders correct for them with a simple formula:

```
Net liquidity = Fed total assets − ON RRP − TGA
```

All three are in US dollar trillions. Let's define the two subtractions, because they are the whole reason "net liquidity" is more useful than the raw balance sheet.

![The plumbing showing Fed assets draining into RRP and TGA leaving net liquidity](/imgs/blogs/global-liquidity-and-the-everything-correlation-5.png)

### The ON RRP: a cash parking lot

The **overnight reverse repo facility (ON RRP)** is a place where money-market funds and other eligible institutions can park spare cash at the Fed overnight and earn a fixed rate. When cash goes into the RRP, it leaves the banking system and sits sterile at the Fed — it is liquidity *removed* from circulation. When cash comes *out* of the RRP, it flows back into the system and into markets.

Why did the RRP balloon? In 2021, QE and fiscal stimulus had flooded money-market funds with cash, but there were not enough safe short-term assets (T-bills) for them to buy, and the yields on what existed were near zero. The Fed's RRP offered a clean, risk-free place to stash the excess overnight at a guaranteed rate, so cash piled in. The RRP launched in earnest in 2021, ballooned to a peak of **\$2.55T in December 2022**, and then drained almost to zero by late 2025 as the Treasury issued a flood of higher-yielding bills that money funds preferred. That drain matters enormously: even as the Fed ran QT and shrank its balance sheet through 2023–24, money pouring *out* of the RRP partly refilled the system. This is why subtracting the RRP is essential — the raw balance sheet was shrinking, but the cash reaching markets was cushioned.

The mechanical bookkeeping is worth seeing once. The RRP, bank reserves, and the TGA are all *liabilities* on the Fed's balance sheet — they are the three big ways the money the Fed created can be "held." Because the liability side must equal the asset side, if the asset side is fixed and the RRP shrinks, the other liabilities must grow — and the one that grows is bank reserves, the form of money that reaches the banking system and supports lending and asset prices. So "RRP drains" and "bank reserves rise" are two sides of the same coin. Net liquidity captures this automatically by subtracting the RRP: cash moving from the RRP into reserves leaves net liquidity unchanged in level but means *more usable* liquidity, which is the correct reading.

### The TGA: the government's checking account

The **Treasury General Account (TGA)** is the US government's checking account, held at the Fed. When the Treasury collects taxes or sells bonds, cash moves *into* the TGA and out of the banking system — liquidity drained. When the government spends, cash moves *out* of the TGA into the economy and banks — liquidity added. The TGA swings violently around tax dates and especially around debt-ceiling episodes, when the Treasury is forced to run its account down to nearly zero (it hit just \$0.05T in June 2023) and then rebuild it after the ceiling is lifted.

The debt-ceiling dynamic deserves a moment, because it produces some of the sharpest liquidity swings. When Congress lets the Treasury bump against the debt ceiling, the Treasury cannot issue new debt, so it funds the government by spending down the TGA — which *injects* liquidity (a stealth tailwind for risk assets). The moment the ceiling is lifted, the Treasury must rebuild the TGA by issuing a wall of new bonds, which *drains* liquidity (a stealth headwind). In mid-2023, the TGA fell to \$0.05T during the standoff and then was rebuilt by hundreds of billions of dollars over the following months — a meaningful liquidity drag that, crucially, was *offset* by the RRP draining at the same time. If you only watched the headline balance sheet you would have missed both the TGA drain and the RRP cushion that netted them out. This is the everyday reality of why the plumbing, not the headline, is what moves markets.

So the logic of the formula is: start with the gross money the Fed created (its assets), then subtract the two big pools that have *trapped* some of that money away from markets (RRP and TGA). What's left is the cash that actually circulates — the tide level that markets feel. It is a crude proxy — it ignores foreign central banks, ignores the Fed's other smaller facilities, and treats every dollar of reserves as equally "usable" when in reality some sits idle — but it is crude in the way a tide table is crude: good enough to tell you which way the water is going, and free to compute from public data.

#### Worked example: computing net liquidity from the components

Take the snapshot for **June 2023**, in the middle of the QT-plus-debt-ceiling episode:

- Fed total assets: **\$8.34T**
- minus ON RRP: **\$1.95T** (still huge — money funds were hoarding cash)
- minus TGA: **\$0.05T** (drained to nearly zero by the debt-ceiling standoff)

Net liquidity = 8.34 − 1.95 − 0.05 = **\$6.34T**.

Now contrast with mid-2025: Fed assets \$6.66T, RRP just \$0.20T, TGA \$0.40T, so net liquidity = 6.66 − 0.20 − 0.40 = **\$6.06T**. Notice the headline balance sheet fell by \$1.68T (from \$8.34T to \$6.66T) but net liquidity fell only \$0.28T — because the \$1.75T that drained out of the RRP nearly offset the QT. **The intuition: the Fed's stated policy and the liquidity markets actually feel can diverge by trillions, and net liquidity is the bridge between them.**

The waterfall below makes the June-2023 arithmetic visual: a \$8.34T Fed balance sheet, minus the \$1.95T sitting in the RRP and the \$0.05T in the TGA, leaves \$6.34T of net liquidity reaching the system. The same picture for a different date would have differently-sized drains and a different remainder — which is the whole reason the components must be tracked separately rather than trusting the headline number.

## The centerpiece: net liquidity and the S&P 500

The chart above puts net liquidity and the S&P 500 on the same timeline. The co-movement is the whole thesis in one picture: both surged through 2020–21 (the everything-rally), both fell in 2022 (the everything-selloff), and both recovered through 2023–25 as the RRP drain cushioned QT. Over this short window the correlation between the two annual series is **r ≈ +0.51** — and over higher-frequency (weekly) data through the 2020–23 regime, researchers have measured the relationship even higher.

Treat that +0.51 with appropriate humility. It is computed on a handful of annual points in a single, unusually QE-dominated decade, so it is a teaching number, not a law of nature. But the *direction* and the *episode structure* are robust across many studies: liquidity up, risk up; liquidity down, risk down.

There is a deeper reason the *level* of the two series tracks so well even when short-term wiggles diverge. Equities, in the long run, are valued off two things: earnings and the multiple investors are willing to pay for those earnings. Earnings grow with the economy at a fairly steady pace; the *multiple* is where liquidity does its work. When the discount rate is crushed and cash is plentiful, investors pay 22 times earnings; when liquidity drains and rates rise, they pay 16 times the same earnings. That re-rating of the multiple is the channel through which a slow-moving liquidity tide shows up as large, persistent swings in the index level — and it is why the net-liquidity line and the S&P line trace such similar arcs over a multi-year window even though, on any given week, earnings news or a headline can pull them apart. The liquidity tide sets the multiple; the economy sets the earnings; the index is their product.

It is worth stressing why we use the *S&P 500* as the headline risk asset here rather than a broader basket. The S&P is the deepest, most-watched risk barometer in the world, and almost every other risk asset — credit, EM, crypto — is at least loosely benchmarked or hedged against it, so its co-movement with liquidity is the cleanest single window into the everything correlation. If liquidity moves the S&P, it moves the things that trade off the S&P, and the chain propagates outward through the whole risk complex.

#### Worked example: a dollar risk-asset move on a liquidity swing

Fit a simple line of the S&P 500 on net liquidity across the 2019–25 sample and the slope comes out around **535 index points per \$1 trillion of net liquidity** (with an intercept near 1,634). That is a crude, in-sample relationship — but it lets us sanity-check magnitudes.

In 2022, net liquidity fell from \$6.8T to \$5.65T, a swing of about **−\$1.15T**. Multiply by the slope: −1.15 × 535 ≈ **−615 index points** of "liquidity drag" on the S&P 500. The index actually fell from 4,766 to 3,840, a drop of 926 points (about −19.4%). So the liquidity model "explains" roughly two-thirds of the move, with the rest coming from the earnings hit and the rate shock that traveled alongside the liquidity drain. **The intuition: liquidity is not the only driver, but on this episode it accounted for the majority of the index's fall — exactly what "the tide under everything" should look like.**

## The everything correlation: one factor, many assets

Now we can answer the question the series keeps circling: *why are risk assets positively correlated with each other in the first place?* The conventional answer — "they all respond to the economy" — is incomplete, because in 2020–21 the economy was a mess and risk assets soared anyway. The deeper answer is that they share a **common factor**: the price and quantity of money.

Statistically, if asset A and asset B each load positively on a single common factor F, then A and B will be positively correlated *with each other*, even if they have nothing else in common. Crypto and gold have almost opposite fundamental stories — one is a digital bearer asset with no cash flows, the other a 5,000-year-old metal — yet both rallied in 2020–21 and both stalled in 2022, because both load on liquidity. The liquidity factor is the invisible string tying the puppets together.

The arithmetic is simple enough to write down. Suppose two assets each have the form *return = β × (liquidity factor) + (idiosyncratic noise)*. If asset A loads on liquidity with βA and asset B with βB, and the factor has variance σF², then the *covariance* between A and B coming purely from the shared factor is βA × βB × σF². As long as both betas have the same sign — and for risk assets they are both positive — the two assets are positively correlated, no matter how unrelated their day-to-day stories. The correlation they share is *manufactured by the common factor*. This is the precise sense in which the everything correlation is not a fluke: it is the mathematical shadow of one driver standing behind many assets. And it tells you something subtle about diversification — adding a second liquidity-sensitive asset to a portfolio of the first does far less risk reduction than its surface "difference" suggests, because you are doubling down on the same hidden factor.

It also explains why the everything correlation is *strongest when the factor is moving most*. When liquidity is stable, the idiosyncratic noise terms dominate each asset, so they look weakly related — stocks do their earnings thing, gold does its real-yield thing, crypto does its adoption thing. But when the tide is surging or draining hard, the βF term swamps the noise, and suddenly *everything* lines up. This is the same phenomenon as a crowded theatre: when nothing is happening, people move independently; when the building shakes, everyone runs for the same door at once. Correlation is a regime, and the liquidity regime is the deepest one of all — a theme that runs through [correlation is a regime, not a constant](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/correlation-is-a-regime-not-a-constant).

![Risk assets ranked by correlation with changes in net liquidity from Bitcoin to the dollar](/imgs/blogs/global-liquidity-and-the-everything-correlation-3.png)

The bar chart ranks assets by their liquidity beta — how strongly each one correlates with changes in net liquidity. The ordering is intuitive once you see it:

- **Bitcoin: r ≈ +0.60.** The highest liquidity beta of any major asset. Crypto has no earnings, no coupon, and no fundamental anchor, so its price is almost pure discount-rate-and-liquidity sentiment. When the tide rises, nothing rises faster; when it drains, nothing drops faster. This is the heart of [crypto as a macro asset](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/crypto-as-a-macro-asset-the-liquidity-correlation).
- **Nasdaq: r ≈ +0.55** and **S&P 500: r ≈ +0.50.** Long-duration equities — especially the high-growth, no-profit kind — behave like leveraged bets on the discount rate, which is downstream of liquidity. The Nasdaq's higher beta versus the broad S&P is the same duration story.
- **EM equities: r ≈ +0.50.** Emerging markets borrow in dollars and depend on global capital flows, so they boom when liquidity is plentiful and a weak dollar invites money abroad.
- **Gold: r ≈ +0.35.** Positive, but weaker. Gold's main driver is *real yields* (see [real yields: the cleanest macro correlation](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/real-yields-and-the-cleanest-macro-correlation)), and liquidity is only one input into real yields. Gold rides the tide, but it has its own current too.
- **US dollar: r ≈ −0.40.** The one negative. More dollar liquidity means more dollars sloshing around, which tends to *weaken* the dollar. The dollar is the denominator of every other price, so when it falls, dollar-priced assets rise — yet another channel through which liquidity lifts the fleet. The dollar's negative loading is what links liquidity to the entire [dollar cross-asset correlation](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/the-dollar-dxy-cross-asset-correlation): a draining tide and a rising dollar are usually the same regime described from two angles.

Notice what is *not* on the high-beta list: short-dated Treasury bills, cash, and other safe-haven instruments. They have low or negative liquidity betas because they *are* the liquidity — when the tide drains, money flows *into* them, not out. This is the deep structural reason a portfolio of "stocks plus crypto plus gold plus high-yield" is far less diversified than it looks, while a portfolio of "stocks plus cash plus short Treasuries" actually diversifies the liquidity factor: the second basket holds both ends of the same driver. If you want genuine protection against a draining tide, you need assets with a *negative* loading on liquidity, not just a different story but a positive one.

Long government bonds are an instructive special case. In a *growth* shock they hedge stocks beautifully (yields fall, bond prices rise, the classic 60/40 diversifier). But in a *liquidity-and-inflation* shock like 2022, they fall *with* stocks, because the same rising-rate force that drains liquidity also crushes bond prices. That sign-flip is the whole subject of [the stock-bond correlation regime](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/the-stock-bond-correlation-regime), and it is, at bottom, a liquidity story: when the dominant shock is the price and quantity of money, even bonds join the everything correlation.

![Why one tide produces the everything correlation across stocks crypto and gold](/imgs/blogs/global-liquidity-and-the-everything-correlation-7.png)

The transmission diagram above traces the whole mechanism: a liquidity swing fans out through the *price of money* (discount rate, risk premium) and the *quantity of money* (cash chasing assets), hits stocks, crypto, and gold individually, and the three merge into one positive cross-asset correlation. The everything correlation is not a coincidence — it is what you get when one factor drives everything.

### Why this makes diversification weaker than it looks

The practical sting of the common-factor story lands on diversification. The whole promise of holding many different assets is that their idiosyncratic risks cancel out — when one zigs, another zags, and the portfolio is steadier than any single holding. That promise holds *only to the extent the assets are genuinely independent*. The moment they all load on the same factor, the cancellation breaks down: the shared factor moves them in lockstep, and the portfolio behaves like one big position in that factor.

A portfolio of US stocks, EM stocks, crypto, high-yield bonds, and gold *looks* diversified — five different asset classes on three continents. But every one of them has a positive liquidity beta, so when the tide drains, all five fall together, and the investor discovers the diversification was an illusion exactly when it was needed. This is the liquidity-factor version of the broader truth that [diversification is not a free lunch](/blog/trading/cross-asset/correlation-and-the-diversification-free-lunch): you only get the free lunch for risks that are actually uncorrelated, and a shared macro driver quietly correlates them. The everything correlation is the reason a "diversified risk-asset portfolio" is closer to a single leveraged liquidity bet than most investors realize. Genuine diversification against the liquidity factor requires holding things with the *opposite* loading — cash, short-dated government debt, and in some growth-shock regimes, long Treasuries — not just a wider variety of the same kind of risk.

#### Worked example: the liquidity beta of Bitcoin versus gold

Suppose net liquidity expands by **+\$0.5T** in a quarter and you want a rough sense of the relative moves. A clean way to compare is through each asset's *beta to liquidity* — the slope of its return on liquidity changes — but even with just the correlations we can rank the sensitivities, because beta = r × (asset volatility ÷ liquidity volatility), and Bitcoin's volatility dwarfs gold's.

Bitcoin has r ≈ +0.60 and roughly 4–5× gold's volatility, so its *beta* to liquidity is on the order of **six to eight times** gold's. Concretely: in the 2022 drain, net liquidity fell about \$1.15T, gold ended the year roughly flat (it has its own central-bank-buying current), while Bitcoin fell about **64%** — from \$46,306 to \$16,548. Same tide, wildly different boats: the one with no anchor and the most leverage to sentiment moved the most. **The intuition: correlation tells you the sign and how reliably two things move together; beta tells you how *much* — and on liquidity, Bitcoin is the high-beta end and gold the low-beta end of the same factor.**

## Why liquidity is a slow tide, not a fast trigger

Here is the most important honest caveat, and the place most "it's all liquidity" commentary goes wrong. Liquidity is a **tide**, not a **trigger**. Tides move slowly and predictably; triggers are fast and sharp. Confusing the two will get you run over.

A CPI surprise is a trigger: it hits in the same second the number prints, and the market reprices in minutes (that intraday reaction is the [event-trading](/blog/trading/event-trading/cpi-the-report-that-moves-the-world) world). Liquidity is the opposite. The Fed's balance sheet changes by small weekly increments. The RRP drains over months. The effect on asset prices is cumulative and lagged — liquidity typically *leads* risk assets by anywhere from a few weeks to a few months, and the lag itself is unstable.

This has two consequences. First, **liquidity is far better at explaining the regime than the next print.** It will tell you whether you are in an everything-rally environment or an everything-drain environment; it will not tell you whether stocks are up or down tomorrow. Second, because the lag is variable, the *full-sample correlation understates the true relationship* — if liquidity leads by an unknown amount, a same-period correlation smears the signal. Studies that align liquidity with risk assets at the right lead consistently find a tighter fit than the contemporaneous number.

#### Worked example: the lead/lag, and why the contemporaneous correlation lies

Imagine liquidity leads the S&P 500 by, say, two months. If you compute the correlation of *this month's* liquidity change with *this month's* stock return, you are comparing a cause with an effect that has not fully happened yet, so you measure something like +0.4. But if you shift liquidity *back* two months — comparing two-month-ago liquidity with today's stock return — you might measure +0.6. The relationship did not change; your alignment did. Concretely, in the 2020–23 sample the contemporaneous annual figure we computed was about **+0.51**, while weekly studies that lag the series properly have reported numbers in the +0.6 to +0.7 range. **The intuition: a low correlation between two series can hide a strong *lagged* relationship — always test whether the leading series lines up better when you shift it, or you will dismiss a real driver as weak.** This is the single most common way people under-rate liquidity: they regress it contemporaneously, get a middling number, and conclude it "doesn't matter much."

So use liquidity as a *backdrop* indicator, the way a sailor uses the tide table: it tells you which way the water is going over the coming weeks, not whether the next wave is up or down. The corollary is that liquidity is most dangerous to *trade* and most useful to *position around*. People who try to scalp the weekly net-liquidity print get whipsawed by the noise and the variable lag; people who use it to decide whether to be broadly risk-on or risk-off over the coming quarter tend to be on the right side of the big regime shifts.

### The honest causation question

We should be candid about a hole in the story: does liquidity *cause* asset prices, or do they sometimes move together for a third reason? The mechanism is real — the portfolio-balance and discount-rate channels genuinely transmit money into prices — but central banks also *react to* markets and the economy, which muddies the picture. The Fed launched infinite QE in 2020 *because* markets were collapsing; it began QT in 2022 *because* inflation was raging. So part of the observed correlation is the central bank responding to conditions, not just driving them, and a naive reading risks confusing the two. The defensible position is this: liquidity is a genuine, mechanically-transmitted driver *and* a partly endogenous policy variable, so the correlation overstates the pure causal effect somewhat — but the effect is large enough, and the channels concrete enough, that ignoring liquidity is a far bigger error than over-crediting it. As always in this series, the discipline is to hold the correlation and the causation as separate facts: the co-movement is well-measured; the precise causal share is not, and anyone who claims to know it to two decimal places is selling something.

## Common misconceptions

**Myth 1: "More money printing always means higher stock prices, immediately."** No. The transmission runs through a variable lag, and it can be overwhelmed in the short run by other forces — an earnings collapse, a credit event, a geopolitical shock. In March 2020 the Fed was creating liquidity at a record pace *while* stocks were still falling; the tide had turned but the wave hadn't arrived yet. Liquidity sets the direction of the current, not the timing of the next wave.

**Myth 2: "The Fed's balance sheet is all that matters — just watch WALCL."** No. As the 2023–24 episode proved, the headline balance sheet fell by over \$1.6T while net liquidity barely moved, because the RRP drain refilled the system. Watching WALCL alone would have had you bearish into a rally. You must net out the RRP and the TGA. **The plumbing is the point.**

**Myth 3: "Diversifying across stocks, crypto, and gold protects me."** Only partially. Because all three load on the liquidity factor, their correlation rises toward each other exactly when the tide is moving — which is exactly when you want diversification. This is the same lesson as [when correlations go to one in a crisis](/blog/trading/cross-asset/when-correlations-go-to-one-in-a-crisis): a basket of liquidity-sensitive assets is less diversified than its surface variety suggests, a point we return to under "real markets."

**Myth 4: "Liquidity and the rate cut are the same thing."** Related but distinct. Rate cuts change the *price* of money; QE changes the *quantity*. In 2020 both happened together, so they got conflated. But the Fed can hold rates steady and still drain liquidity via QT (as in 2024), or cut rates while the balance sheet is flat. The two levers can point in different directions, and net liquidity captures the quantity lever specifically.

**Myth 5: "Global liquidity is just the Fed."** The US dominates, but the *global* tide is the sum of the major central banks — the Fed, the ECB, the Bank of Japan, the People's Bank of China — plus the dollar's strength (a strong dollar tightens global financial conditions even if the Fed does nothing). The fullest version of the model watches all of them; see [global liquidity: the world's money tide](/blog/trading/macro-trading/global-liquidity-the-worlds-money-tide) for the international picture. This post uses the US net-liquidity proxy because it is the cleanest and most data-available slice of the global whole.

## How it shows up in real markets

### 2020–21: the infinite-QE everything-rally

In March 2020, the Fed cut rates to zero and announced open-ended QE — "infinite QE," in the market's phrase. Over the next 21 months it created roughly \$4.7 trillion of new money, M2 expanded by \$6 trillion (from \$15.4T to \$21.5T), and net liquidity climbed from about \$3.4T to \$6.8T. The result was the purest everything-rally in living memory: the S&P 500 rose from a March-2020 low near 2,237 to 4,766 at the end of 2021; the Nasdaq nearly doubled; Bitcoin went from under \$5,000 to nearly \$69,000; gold made new highs; junk-bond spreads collapsed; SPACs, meme stocks, and unprofitable tech all soared together. A traditional analyst trying to explain each asset on its own fundamentals was baffled. The liquidity model explained all of them at once: the tide was coming in faster than ever, so every boat rose.

The tell that it was liquidity and not fundamentals: the assets that rose the *most* were the ones with the *least* connection to current cash flows — meme stocks, story stocks, and crypto, the purest plays on the discount rate and on excess cash looking for a home. When the longest-duration, most-speculative assets lead a rally, that is the fingerprint of a liquidity-driven melt-up rather than an earnings-driven bull market. It is the same fingerprint, in reverse, that warns you a liquidity drain is coming when those same assets start leading the way down.

### 2022: QT, the RRP build, and the everything-selloff

In 2022 the tide turned. The Fed stopped buying, began QT in June, and cash flooded *into* the RRP — it peaked at \$2.55T that December, pulling liquidity out of the system. Net liquidity fell from \$6.8T to \$5.65T over the year. As the model predicts, everything fell together: the S&P 500 −19.4%, the Nasdaq worse, Bitcoin −64.3% (from \$46,306 to \$16,548), long Treasuries down sharply, and the dollar *up* (DXY from 95.7 to 103.5, consistent with its −0.40 liquidity correlation). The "diversified" 60/40 portfolio had its worst year in decades because both legs were really the same liquidity bet. This is the everything-selloff: one tide going out, the whole fleet grounding at once.

The ordering of the damage is the tell. The assets with the highest liquidity beta fell first and fell hardest — speculative tech and crypto peaked in November 2021, *before* the Fed had even raised a rate, sniffing out the coming drain. Then the broad index rolled over in early 2022, and finally the most defensive corners. That "highest-beta-first" cascade is the everything-selloff's signature, the mirror image of the highest-beta-first melt-up of 2021. A liquidity-aware investor who saw the most speculative assets top out in late 2021 had a warning, months ahead, that the tide was about to turn for the broader market — exactly the kind of regime read the liquidity lens is good for, and exactly the kind of timing precision it is *not*.

Note also that M2 itself behaved consistently: broad money *fell* in 2022 (from \$21.49T to \$21.36T) for essentially the first time in the modern data, confirming that the liquidity contraction reached the broad-money layer, not just the base. When the broadest measure of money in public hands actually shrinks, you are in a genuine liquidity drain, and 2022 was the rare year that registered on every gauge at once.

### 2023: the RRP drain that cushioned QT

The 2023 episode is the most instructive, because it shows why net liquidity beats the headline balance sheet. The Fed ran QT all year, shrinking its assets from \$8.55T to \$7.68T. A naive WALCL-watcher would have been bearish. But money poured *out* of the RRP — from \$2.55T at the end of 2022 toward \$1.02T by year-end 2023 — refilling the banking system almost as fast as QT drained it. Net liquidity barely fell, and risk assets staged a strong recovery: the S&P 500 rose about 24% on the year, and Bitcoin more than doubled. The lesson is exact: **QT was running, but the tide the market actually felt was roughly flat, because the RRP drain offset it.** If you only watched the balance sheet you missed the rally; if you watched net liquidity you saw it coming.

This episode is also the cleanest refutation of the lazy bear case that dominated commentary in early 2023. "The Fed is doing QT, liquidity is shrinking, risk assets must fall" was everywhere — and it was wrong, because it watched the wrong variable. The headline balance sheet was indeed shrinking, but the RRP drain meant the *usable* liquidity in the system was roughly flat to rising. The people who got 2023 right were the ones tracking the components, not the headline. There is a generalizable lesson here: any time a popular macro narrative rests on a single headline number, ask what is happening one layer down in the plumbing. The plumbing is where the surprises live.

### 2024–25: an empty RRP and the global tide

By late 2024 the RRP had drained almost to zero — it hit just \$0.16T at the end of 2024 and effectively emptied in 2025. That changes the regime in an important way: once the RRP is empty, it can no longer cushion QT. The same balance-sheet runoff that was painless in 2023 starts to bite bank reserves directly, which is one reason the Fed slowed and then planned to end QT as reserves approached the level it considers "ample." Meanwhile the *global* tide — the combined balance sheets of the Fed, ECB, Bank of Japan, and People's Bank of China, plus the dollar's level — became the thing to watch, because the US-only proxy was no longer the whole story. The dollar's swings (DXY ran from 95.7 at the end of 2021 to 103.5 in 2022, then 108.5 in 2024) tightened or loosened global financial conditions on top of whatever the Fed was doing. The takeaway for the careful reader: **the net-liquidity proxy is a tool, not a constant — its components shift, the plumbing evolves, and the global layer matters more as the US facilities normalize.** Re-derive it from current data; do not trust a formula you memorized two years ago.

#### Worked example: the QT drag, sized

How big is QT as a headwind, in liquidity terms? From the April-2022 peak of \$8.96T to mid-2025's \$6.66T, the Fed drained about **\$2.3 trillion** of assets — call it roughly \$60–95 billion a month over the run. Using the rough slope from earlier (≈535 S&P points per \$1T), \$2.3T of pure balance-sheet drain would imply about **−1,230 index points** of headwind. Yet the S&P *rose* over 2023–25, because (a) the RRP drained \$2.4T+ back into the system, almost fully offsetting the QT, and (b) earnings grew. **The intuition: the gross QT number looks terrifying in isolation, but the net liquidity the market felt was far gentler — which is exactly why the everything-selloff did not continue into an everything-depression.**

## How to read it and use it

You don't need a Bloomberg terminal to track the tide. Every component is published free by the Fed and the Treasury. Here is the practical playbook.

**The signal.** Compute net liquidity weekly: Fed total assets (FRED series WALCL) minus the ON RRP (RRPONTSYD) minus the TGA (WTREGEN), all in trillions. The data publishes on the Fed's H.4.1 release (Thursdays) and the Treasury's Daily Statement; all three series are free on FRED. Watch the *direction and slope* of the result, not the level. Rising net liquidity is a tailwind for risk; falling is a headwind. The signal is about the regime over the coming weeks to months, never about tomorrow. A practical refinement: smooth the series with a four- or eight-week moving average before reading its slope, because the raw weekly print is noisy around tax dates and settlement days, and you care about the trend, not the wiggle.

**Reading the level versus the change.** A subtle but important point: it is the *change* in net liquidity, not its absolute level, that correlates with asset *returns*. A flat \$6T of net liquidity, however large, is just the status quo already reflected in prices; a \$6T that is rising at \$50 billion a week is a tailwind. This is why "there's still a lot of liquidity in the system" is not, by itself, a bullish argument — what matters is whether that pile is growing or shrinking at the margin. Markets price the second derivative of policy, and net liquidity is most useful read as a rate of flow.

**The regime check.** Ask three questions. (1) Is the Fed in QE or QT — which way is WALCL trending? (2) What are the *plumbing flows* doing — is the RRP draining (adds liquidity) or building (drains it), and is the TGA being spent down (adds) or rebuilt, e.g. after a debt-ceiling fight (drains)? (3) What is the dollar doing — a strengthening dollar tightens global liquidity on top of everything else. When all three point the same way, the tide is strong and the everything correlation is at its highest. When they conflict (QT *and* an RRP drain, as in 2023), the net tide can be near flat even though the headlines scream "tightening."

**What invalidates it.** The liquidity model fails or gets overwhelmed in three situations. First, a *fast trigger* — a credit event, a war, a sovereign default — can crater risk assets in days regardless of the tide; liquidity is slow, triggers are fast. Second, when an asset's *own* dominant driver moves hard against the tide: gold can rally on central-bank buying even as net liquidity falls (it partly decoupled in 2022–24), and a single stock can soar on idiosyncratic earnings regardless of liquidity. Third, *structural plumbing shifts* — once the RRP is empty, it can no longer cushion QT, so the same balance-sheet drain bites the system harder. Always re-derive net liquidity from current components rather than assuming last year's relationship still holds.

**The trade, honestly stated.** Liquidity is a position-sizing and regime overlay, not an entry signal. In a rising-tide regime, lean into risk and expect diversification to work *less* well than the brochure claims — your stocks, crypto, and high-yield are more correlated than they look. In a draining-tide regime, raise cash, reduce gross exposure, and remember that "diversification" across liquidity-sensitive assets will fail you precisely when the tide goes out. The single most useful thing the liquidity lens gives you is humility about correlation: when you understand that one factor sits under everything, you stop being surprised that everything falls together.

**A final synthesis.** Step back and the whole post collapses into one sentence: risk assets are positively correlated with each other because they are all, at bottom, claims on the future priced against the same pool of money, and a central bank that changes the price and quantity of that money changes every claim in the same direction at once. The liquidity tide is the deepest common factor in markets — deeper than the business cycle, deeper than inflation, deeper than any single indicator this series studies — because it sits in the denominator of every valuation. Inflation, growth, and rates all matter, but they matter substantially *through* their effect on the liquidity tide and the discount rate it sets. That is why this post is the floor beneath the rest of the series. When you have internalized that one number for the world's supply of money sits under the entire risk complex, the everything-rally and the everything-selloff stop looking like mysteries and start looking like the tide doing exactly what tides do. Master this, and you will rarely be the investor surprised to learn, mid-crash, that the diversified portfolio was one bet all along.

The discipline that ties it together is the same one this whole series preaches: a correlation is a *regime*, not a constant. The liquidity correlation is strong in a QE/QT-dominated decade and could weaken in some future world where central-bank balance sheets matter less. The plumbing evolves — the RRP that cushioned QT in 2023 was empty by 2025 and cannot do it again. The honest practitioner re-measures, re-derives the proxy from current components, and treats every number here as a snapshot of a relationship that will itself drift. What does not drift is the underlying logic: one pool of money, many claims on it, moving together as the pool fills and empties.
