It is positively correlated with global risk (S&P 500 about +0.45, MSCI Emerging Markets about +0.55), negatively correlated with a stronger US dollar (DXY about −0.40) and rising US 10-year yields (about −0.25), and the transmission belt is foreign net buying and selling on the Ho Chi Minh exchange (HOSE), which correlates about +0.50 with the index. >
> - **Why a domestic market imports a global correlation:** Vietnam is a frontier/emerging market funded partly by foreign capital, priced in a dollar world, so when global risk appetite turns, foreign money moves and drags the VN-Index with it.
> - **The dollar is the master dial:** a rising DXY tightens global conditions, pulls capital home to the US, weakens the dong, and triggers foreign selling on HOSE — the same channel that hits all emerging markets (the dollar's correlation with EM equities is about −0.55).
> - **The correlation is real but weaker than a developed market's, and regime-dependent:** in calm markets domestic factors (SBV policy, credit growth, margin) lead; in a global risk-off the imported correlation spikes and dominates.
> - **The one number to remember:** in 2022, the dollar surged about 15% to a two-decade high, the Fed hiked from near zero to 4.5%, and the VN-Index fell roughly **−33%** with heavy foreign selling — a textbook imported drawdown.

In late 2022, two charts that look like they have nothing to do with each other told the same story. The first was the US Dollar Index, the DXY, climbing to **114.8** in September, a twenty-year high, as the Federal Reserve raised interest rates at the fastest pace in forty years to fight inflation that had peaked at 9.1%. The second was the VN-Index — the headline stock index of Vietnam, a country eight thousand miles away whose companies sell coffee, steel, bank loans, and apartments mostly to Vietnamese customers in Vietnamese dong. Over 2022 the VN-Index fell from about 1,500 to under 1,000, a drawdown of roughly **−33%**. Foreign investors were net sellers for much of the year, pulling money out of Vietnamese blue chips week after week.

To a beginner those two facts look unrelated. What does an American central bank fighting American inflation have to do with the share price of a Vietnamese steelmaker? The answer is the entire subject of this post. Vietnam is a *domestic* market — most of its trading volume is local retail investors, most of its companies earn dong — but it is plugged into the global financial system through a small number of wires, and the most important wire is foreign capital. When the dollar rises and US yields jump, global money flows *out* of risky emerging markets and *home* to the safety of US dollars and Treasuries. Vietnam, as a frontier/emerging market, is on the receiving end of that tide. The VN-Index didn't fall in 2022 because Vietnamese earnings collapsed. It fell, in large part, because it *imported* a global risk-off.

That is the thesis: **the VN-Index has an imported correlation to US and global macro.** It is positively correlated with global risk appetite, negatively correlated with the dollar and US yields, and the foreign-flow tap is the mechanism that carries the signal across the ocean. This correlation is genuine and tradeable, but it is also *weaker* than a developed market's and *regime-dependent* — domestic credit, policy, and margin matter enormously too. By the end you'll understand why a Vietnamese investor has to keep one eye on a US inflation report, and exactly how strong that imported link really is.

## Foundations: what "imported correlation" means and why an emerging market has one

Before we measure anything, we have to build the idea from zero, because "the VN-Index imports a US correlation" packs three separate concepts into one phrase: *correlation*, *what the VN-Index is*, and *why an emerging market is exposed to global capital in the first place*.

### Correlation, in one minute

A **correlation** is a single number between −1 and +1 that summarizes how two things tend to move together. **+1** means perfect lockstep (one up, the other always up proportionally); **−1** means perfect opposition (one up, the other always down); **0** means no linear relationship. The symbol is *r* (Pearson's correlation coefficient). When we say "the VN-Index and MSCI Emerging Markets have a correlation of about +0.55," we mean: across a long history of weekly or monthly moves, when global emerging-market stocks rose, the VN-Index usually rose too, and the relationship was moderately strong but far from mechanical. (For the precise machinery — Pearson vs. Spearman vs. beta, and why a single number hides a lot — see [what correlation actually measures](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/what-correlation-actually-measures-pearson-spearman-beta).)

The single most important thing to internalize, and the theme of this whole series, is that **correlation is a regime, not a constant.** The +0.55 between the VN-Index and emerging markets is a long-run average; in a global panic it spikes toward +0.8, and in a quiet, domestically-driven year it can fade toward +0.3. We will return to this honesty repeatedly, because for Vietnam the regime-dependence is the whole game.

### What the VN-Index actually is

The **VN-Index** is the benchmark stock index of Vietnam. It is a market-capitalization-weighted index of the companies listed on the **Ho Chi Minh City Stock Exchange (HOSE)**, the larger of Vietnam's two main bourses (the other is Hanoi's HNX). "Market-cap-weighted" means each company's influence on the index is proportional to its total share value, so a handful of giants — the big banks (Vietcombank, BIDV, Techcombank), the conglomerate Vingroup and its property arm Vinhomes, the dairy company Vinamilk, the brewer and the gas distributor — drive most of the index's daily move. There is also a sub-index, the **VN30**, of the thirty largest and most liquid names; it is the part of the market foreign funds and exchange-traded funds (ETFs) actually buy, which is why it matters so much for the imported correlation.

A few facts about the market structure that make the imported correlation possible:

- **It is retail-dominated but foreign-influenced.** On a typical day, local individual investors are the majority of *volume*. But foreign institutions own a meaningful slice of the float of the biggest, highest-quality names, and crucially, foreign flows are the **marginal** buyer/seller of blue chips. The marginal player sets the price.
- **It is classified as a frontier market, edging toward emerging.** Index providers (FTSE, MSCI) currently treat Vietnam as a *frontier* market, with a long-discussed upgrade to *emerging* status. That classification governs which global funds are *allowed* to own it, and a future upgrade would mechanically pull billions of passive dollars in — a structural version of the same foreign-flow channel.
- **The currency is managed, not free-floating.** Vietnam's central bank, the **State Bank of Vietnam (SBV)**, manages the dong (VND) within a controlled band against the dollar. This matters: it means the dollar's pressure on Vietnam shows up partly in the *currency* and partly in *capital flows and rates*, rather than in a freely crashing exchange rate the way it might in a fully floating EM.

### Why an emerging market imports global risk appetite

Here is the core mechanism, and it is the same for Vietnam, Brazil, Indonesia, or South Africa. A developed market like the US is, to a first approximation, the *source* of global capital. An emerging market is a *recipient* of it. That asymmetry creates the import.

Global investors — pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, ETF providers, hedge funds — allocate a small slice of their portfolios to "emerging and frontier markets" as a basket. When global risk appetite is high (a "risk-on" regime), they *add* to that basket, and money flows into places like Vietnam. When risk appetite collapses (a "risk-off" regime), they *cut* the basket first, because it's the riskiest, least liquid, most marginal part of the portfolio — and money flows *out*. This is the "risk-on/risk-off" cycle, and an emerging market is on the volatile end of it. Vietnam doesn't generate the global risk cycle; it *receives* it, amplified.

The phrase "risk-on/risk-off" (sometimes shortened to RORO) deserves a precise definition because the whole imported correlation hangs on it. **Risk-on** is a market state in which investors are willing to hold risky, higher-return assets — equities, emerging markets, crypto, high-yield bonds — and are *selling* safe assets like Treasuries and the dollar. **Risk-off** is the reverse: a flight to safety, where investors *sell* risk and *buy* the dollar, Treasuries, and other havens. The key feature of a RORO regime is that *correlations collapse onto a single axis.* In a strong risk-off, the question stops being "what do I think about Vietnamese banks versus US tech versus copper" and becomes simply "risk, or safety?" — and everything risky moves together, down, while everything safe moves together, up. The VN-Index, being unambiguously a risk asset, gets sold in that single sweep regardless of its domestic fundamentals. That collapse-onto-one-axis is precisely why the imported correlation *spikes* in stress and *fades* in calm, and it's the single most important behavioral fact in this post.

A natural objection: if every allocator follows the same risk-on/risk-off playbook, why isn't every emerging market identical? Because the *size* of each market's wire to the global system differs. A deeply integrated, freely-floating, MSCI-index member transmits the full current; a frontier market with capital controls and a managed currency transmits a fraction. Vietnam is toward the *muted* end of the spectrum — which, as we'll quantify, is exactly why its imported correlations sit around 0.4–0.55 rather than the 0.7+ of a fully-wired EM. The risk cycle is the same everywhere; the gauge of the wire is what differs.

What turns the risk cycle on and off? Overwhelmingly, **US monetary conditions** — the dollar and US yields:

- **The dollar funding channel.** A huge amount of global finance is borrowed and priced in dollars. When the dollar rises, dollar funding gets scarcer and more expensive worldwide, financial conditions tighten *everywhere*, and the riskiest assets get sold first. A rising dollar is a global tightening, and emerging markets feel it most. (For the deep mechanism of why the dollar holds this role, read the dedicated [dollar system: why USD rules markets](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy) post; here we measure the *correlations* that role produces, and how they reach Vietnam.)
- **The yield/return channel.** When US 10-year Treasury yields rise, the "risk-free" return an investor can earn in dollars goes up. That raises the bar for taking risk in a far-away frontier market. Why fly to Ho Chi Minh City for an uncertain equity return when you can clip 4.5% risk-free in Treasuries? Higher US yields *pull* capital home.
- **The relative-rate channel.** When the Fed hikes and the SBV doesn't keep pace, the *gap* between US and Vietnamese rates narrows the reward for holding dong assets, pressuring both the currency and inflows. (The currency leg of this — USD/VND and the SBV–Fed rate differential — gets its own treatment in [USD/VND, DXY, and the rate-differential correlation](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/usdvnd-dxy-and-the-rate-differential-correlation).)

Put those together and you have the picture in the figure above: US CPI and the Fed set the dollar and US yields; the dollar and yields set the global risk regime; the risk regime sets foreign flows into and out of HOSE; and foreign flows move the VN-Index. The correlation is *imported* through that chain, link by link. Now let's measure each link.

### The dollar funding channel, made concrete

It's worth slowing down on *why* a rising dollar tightens conditions in a country that doesn't even use dollars domestically, because it's the least intuitive link and the most important. The mechanism is the global plumbing of credit. Banks, corporations, and governments outside the US borrow trillions of dollars — the Bank for International Settlements tracks roughly \$13 trillion of dollar credit to non-bank borrowers outside the United States. Those borrowers earn local currency (dong, rupiah, real) but owe dollars. When the dollar rises, the local-currency cost of that debt rises mechanically: a Vietnamese importer or a regional bank that owes \$10,000,000 suddenly owes more dong to settle the same obligation, even though nothing about the loan changed.

So a rising dollar is a *tightening of credit conditions for the whole world at once.* Lenders pull back, borrowers scramble for dollars, and the scramble itself bids the dollar higher — a self-reinforcing squeeze. Risk assets get sold to raise the needed dollars, and the riskiest, most liquid-on-the-way-out assets get sold first. A frontier equity market is high on that sell list. None of this requires a single Vietnamese company to have dollar debt; it's enough that Vietnam sits inside a global system whose funding currency just got more expensive. The dollar is the *price of global credit*, and when that price rises, every leveraged risk position on earth gets a margin call — Vietnam included.

### Why Vietnam's imported correlation is weaker than a "real" EM's

A fair question: if Vietnam is so plugged into the dollar/EM channel, why is its dollar correlation only −0.40 and its US-yield correlation only −0.25, when the dollar's correlation with the *broad* EM equity basket is −0.55? Three structural reasons keep Vietnam's imported link looser than a fully-integrated emerging market's, and a beginner should hold all three:

- **Frontier classification and capital controls.** Vietnam is a *frontier* market, not yet in the main MSCI Emerging Markets index. That keeps a large pool of passive, index-tracking foreign money *out* — money that would otherwise transmit the global risk cycle mechanically. Foreign ownership limits, currency-conversion friction, and a smaller free float all loosen the wire. Paradoxically, being *less* integrated makes the imported correlation *weaker* — which is exactly why a future MSCI upgrade would *raise* it.
- **The managed currency absorbs shocks.** As we'll see in the dollar section, the SBV smooths the dong, so the currency leg of the dollar's force is damped. A floating-currency EM transmits the dollar straight into a crashing exchange rate; Vietnam converts part of that force into reserves spending and rate policy instead, blunting the equity-market correlation.
- **A huge domestic retail base provides a counterweight.** When foreigners sell, Vietnamese retail investors are often *buying* (or vice versa). That two-sided market means foreign flows don't always get their full price impact — local demand can partly absorb a foreign sell-down in a calm regime. In a panic the retail base flips to a seller and the counterweight disappears, which is exactly why the correlation *rises* in stress.

The lesson: Vietnam's imported correlation is real and large, but *partial*, because the country is only partially wired into the global capital system. That partial wiring is a feature, not a bug — it's what gives a domestic stock-picker room to add value in calm regimes. But it is *not* a force field; in a genuine global risk-off the wires that exist carry the full current.

## The centerpiece: how much of the VN-Index's correlation is imported

The cleanest way to see the imported correlation is to lay out the VN-Index's correlation with every major driver and split them into two groups: **imported** (global) drivers and **domestic** drivers.

![VN-Index correlation bars split into imported global drivers and domestic drivers](/imgs/blogs/vn-index-and-us-macro-the-imported-correlation-2.png)

Read the bars from the figure. The imported drivers:

- **MSCI Emerging Markets: about +0.55.** This is the single strongest link, and it is the heart of the thesis. The VN-Index moves *with* the global EM basket because it *is* part of the global EM/frontier risk trade. When EM is bid, Vietnam is bid.
- **S&P 500: about +0.45.** Vietnam correlates with the US stock market too, but more weakly than with EM — because the S&P is partly a "US-specific" story (US tech, US earnings) while the EM basket is closer to Vietnam's actual risk bucket.
- **Foreign net buy on HOSE: about +0.50.** This is the *mechanism* showing up as a correlation: when foreigners are net buyers, the index rises; when they net sell, it falls. We'll dissect this belt in its own section.
- **DXY (the dollar): about −0.40.** A stronger dollar is a headwind. This is the dollar funding/tightening channel reaching Vietnam.
- **US 10-year yield: about −0.25.** Rising US yields pull capital home and raise the global discount rate; the VN-Index leans down. It's the *weakest* of the imported links, partly because Vietnam's own rates and the SBV's management blunt the pass-through.

And the domestic drivers (shown in a muted color in the figure, because they are the *home* leg, not the imported one):

- **SBV policy rate change: about −0.45.** When Vietnam's central bank raises rates, the VN-Index leans down (tighter domestic money, higher discount rate); when it cuts, the index leans up. This is as strong as the dollar link — a reminder that *the imported correlation is not the whole story.*
- **Vietnam CPI year-over-year change: about −0.30.** Rising domestic inflation pressures the index, mostly through the rate channel (it forces the SBV to tighten).

The pattern is unmistakable: a frontier/emerging market like Vietnam has a *meaningful* set of imported global correlations — EM +0.55, foreign flows +0.50, S&P +0.45, dollar −0.40 — sitting *alongside* a comparably strong set of domestic ones (SBV −0.45). The imported correlation is real and large, but it shares the steering wheel. That balance is the difference between Vietnam and a pure satellite of US macro, and it's why the honest answer to "does the VN-Index just follow the S&P?" is *no — about half of it does, and the regime decides which half is driving.*

Notice an internal consistency in the imported bars that confirms they are measuring one underlying force rather than five unrelated ones. The S&P, EM, and foreign-flow correlations are all *positive* and clustered around +0.45 to +0.55, while the dollar and US-yield correlations are *negative* — and the dollar tends to *rise* when stocks *fall*. So all five point the same way: when global risk is on (stocks up, dollar down, foreigners buying), the VN-Index rises; when global risk is off (stocks down, dollar up, foreigners selling), it falls. They are five thermometers in the same room. That coherence is what lets us collapse the whole imported side into a single statement — *the VN-Index is long global risk* — and it's why watching any one of these (most practically, the foreign net-buy number) gives you most of the imported signal.

It also tells you something about *position concentration*. A Vietnamese investor who also owns global EM funds, or who is long crypto, or who holds US tech, does not have five diversified bets. They have one bet — long global risk — expressed five ways. In a risk-off, all five lose together, because they share the same +0.5-ish exposure to the global risk factor. The imported correlation is, quietly, a *concentration* warning: the "diversification" of holding Vietnamese stocks *plus* other risk assets is far thinner than it looks, and it gets thinner exactly when markets fall (the crisis-correlation lesson again).

### Why the S&P link is weaker than the EM link

One detail in the bars rewards a second look: why is the VN-Index's correlation with *emerging markets* (+0.55) stronger than its correlation with the *S&P 500* (+0.45)? After all, the S&P is the world's most-watched index and the source of most global risk signals. The answer is about *what bucket Vietnam lives in.* The S&P is increasingly a bet on a handful of mega-cap US technology companies whose fortunes — AI spending, US consumer strength, US-specific regulation — have little to do with a Vietnamese bank or steelmaker. The MSCI EM basket, by contrast, *is* Vietnam's peer group: other dollar-funded, export-driven, foreign-flow-sensitive economies. When a global allocator decides "more EM" or "less EM," Vietnam is inside that decision in a way it is not inside a decision about US tech. So the EM correlation is the *direct* membership link, and the S&P correlation is the *indirect* spillover from S&P moves to global risk appetite generally. When you want the cleanest read on the imported signal, watch EM, not just the S&P.

#### Worked example: the imported beta in dollars

Let's turn a correlation into a number you can size a position with. Suppose a foreign fund holds a Vietnamese equity position worth **\$1,000,000** (about 25.6 billion dong at a 2025 USD/VND of 25.6). The VN-Index's correlation with MSCI EM is about +0.55. Correlation isn't the same as *beta* (the move-per-unit-move), but for a quick estimate, suppose the VN-Index's beta to a 1% EM move is roughly **0.7** (a moderately high-beta member of the basket). Now global EM sells off **−5%** in a risk-off week.

Expected VN-Index move ≈ beta × EM move = 0.7 × (−5%) = **−3.5%**.

Expected mark-to-market loss on the position ≈ \$1,000,000 × (−3.5%) = **−\$35,000** — *before* any extra hit from the dong weakening against the dollar. The intuition: an investor who "only owns Vietnamese stocks" still carries a global-risk exposure worth tens of thousands of dollars on a routine EM down-week, because the imported beta is doing real work on the portfolio.

## The dollar channel: the master imported dial

Of the imported drivers, the dollar deserves its own section, because it is the *master* dial that sets the others in motion. The DXY's −0.40 correlation with the VN-Index isn't a coincidence; it's the same gravity that pulls down *every* emerging market.

![Correlation of a stronger dollar with each asset showing emerging markets hit hardest](/imgs/blogs/vn-index-and-us-macro-the-imported-correlation-4.png)

This figure shows the dollar's correlation with a range of assets (from [the dollar (DXY) cross-asset correlation](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/the-dollar-dxy-cross-asset-correlation)). Notice where **emerging-market equities** sit: about **−0.55**, the *strongest* negative link in the whole chart, tied with gold. The reason EM is the dollar's biggest victim is exactly why Vietnam imports the correlation:

- **EM borrows in dollars.** When the dollar rises, the local-currency cost of servicing dollar debt rises, balance sheets tighten, and EM assets get sold.
- **EM is the marginal risk trade.** A rising dollar = global tightening = sell the riskiest, most marginal bucket first = EM and frontier.
- **EM currencies fall.** A foreign investor's return is the *local* stock return *plus* the currency move. A weakening EM currency subtracts from dollar returns, so foreigners pre-emptively sell when the dollar is rising.

Vietnam is wired into that EM channel. So the dollar reaches the VN-Index *twice*: directly (the −0.40 DXY correlation) and indirectly through the broader EM trade it drags down (the +0.55 EM correlation). They are two views of one force.

There's a third, slower channel worth naming: the **carry and flow** channel. Vietnam, like most emerging markets, attracts capital partly because it offers *higher* interest rates than the developed world — investors borrow cheaply in low-yielding currencies and park money in higher-yielding EM assets to earn the spread (the "carry trade"). A rising dollar and rising US yields *shrink* that spread: suddenly the US itself pays a competitive risk-free rate, so the reward for taking Vietnamese risk to earn a few extra points of carry collapses. Carry trades unwind, capital flows back to the dollar, and the unwind is self-reinforcing because everyone heads for the exit at once. This is the same machinery that periodically blows up the yen carry trade and the broader EM carry complex; Vietnam sits inside it. The carry channel is slow and invisible day-to-day, but it's a big part of why a *sustained* rise in US yields produces a *sustained* drift of capital out of frontier markets, beyond any single risk-off episode. (The currency and rate-differential mechanics get their own deep treatment in [USD/VND, DXY, and the rate-differential correlation](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/usdvnd-dxy-and-the-rate-differential-correlation).)

The currency leg of this is visible in Vietnam's own exchange rate. When the dollar strengthens globally, the dong weakens against it — but in a *managed* way, because the SBV smooths the move.

![A stronger dollar pulls the dong weaker shown by DXY against USD per VND](/imgs/blogs/vn-index-and-us-macro-the-imported-correlation-3.png)

The scatter plots the DXY's year-end level against USD/VND (the number of thousand dong per dollar) for 2019–2025. The fitted line slopes *up* — a stronger dollar means more dong per dollar, i.e. a weaker dong — with a correlation of about **+0.56** and a slope near **0.10**, meaning each 1-point rise in the DXY is associated with roughly **0.1 thousand dong** (100 dong) of additional USD/VND. The relationship is clear but *loose*, and that looseness is the SBV's fingerprint: a fully floating EM currency would track the dollar far more tightly. Vietnam's central bank deliberately damps the pass-through, spending reserves and adjusting rates to keep the dong stable. So the dollar's pressure that *would* show up as a crashing currency instead leaks out partly through the currency and partly through capital flows and domestic rates — which is precisely how it reaches the equity market.

#### Worked example: the dollar move's pass-through to the dong

In 2020 the DXY closed near **89.9** and USD/VND was about **23,100** dong per dollar. By the end of 2022 the DXY had climbed to about **103.5** — a rise of **(103.5 / 89.9 − 1) = +15.1%** — and USD/VND had risen to about **23,630**, a dong depreciation of only **(23,630 / 23,100 − 1) = +2.3%**.

So a 15% dollar surge produced barely a 2% dong move. Why so little? Because the SBV absorbed the pressure — burning foreign-exchange reserves and, in late 2022, hiking its policy rate from 4.0% to 6.0% to defend the currency. The intuition for a Vietnamese investor: *the dollar's full force doesn't hit the dong, it gets converted into tighter domestic policy and foreign outflows — and those are what hit your stocks.* The damped currency is not the absence of the imported correlation; it's the imported correlation routing through a different pipe.

This re-routing is the single most counterintuitive feature of an emerging market with a managed currency, so it's worth stating the trade-off plainly. A country can choose, in a dollar squeeze, to let *one of three things* take the strain: the currency (let the dong fall), the reserves (sell dollars to defend it), or domestic rates (hike to make dong assets more attractive). It cannot fully escape all three — this is the "impossible trinity" of open-economy economics, the rule that a country can't simultaneously have a fixed exchange rate, free capital movement, and an independent monetary policy. Vietnam, like most managed-currency EMs, chooses a mix: a *little* dong weakness, *some* reserves, and *meaningful* rate hikes. For the equity investor, the punchline is that the dollar's force never disappears — it just shows up as whichever lever the central bank pulled. In 2022 it showed up as a 200-basis-point SBV hike, and *that* — a higher domestic discount rate, on top of the foreign selling — is a big part of why the VN-Index fell so hard. The managed currency didn't protect the stock market; it transmitted the shock through the rate door instead of the currency door.

## The foreign-flow belt: the physical mechanism

Correlations describe *what* moves together. The foreign-flow tap explains *how*. If you want to watch the imported correlation actually transmit in real time, you watch **foreign net buying and selling on HOSE** — the single line that translates a global risk shock into a Vietnamese index move.

![The foreign flow transmission belt from a global shock to net selling to a VN-Index fall](/imgs/blogs/vn-index-and-us-macro-the-imported-correlation-5.png)

Trace the belt in the figure:

1. **A global shock arrives.** US CPI comes in hot, the Fed sounds hawkish, the dollar rises, US yields jump. Nothing has changed inside Vietnam.
2. **Global funds cut their EM/frontier weight.** A risk-off signal makes the marginal allocator trim the riskiest bucket and raise cash in dollars. Vietnam, as a frontier name in that bucket, gets a sell order.
3. **Foreigners net-sell on HOSE.** The selling lands on Vietnamese blue chips — the VN30 names foreigners actually own. This is the *net foreign buy/sell* number Vietnamese traders watch every single day.
4. **The index effect concentrates the damage.** Because the VN-Index is market-cap-weighted and foreigners own the biggest names, foreign selling hits the *heaviest-weighted* stocks hardest, which mechanically drags the whole index down more than the breadth of selling would suggest. Concretely: the top handful of names — the big banks, Vingroup, Vinhomes, the gas and beverage giants — make up a large share of the index's total weight, and they are precisely the liquid blue chips foreign funds hold. So when a global allocator trims "Vietnam," the sell order lands on the stocks that move the index the most. A foreign sell-down that is *narrow* by stock count can be *broad* by index weight. Passive ETF flows make this sharper still: when money leaves a Vietnam ETF, the ETF must sell its entire basket pro-rata, hammering every big weight at once. (Vietnam's foreign flows and the index effect get a dedicated treatment in the vietnam-stocks series — see [foreign flows, ETFs, and the index effect](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam).)
5. **Domestic margin amplifies it.** Vietnam's market runs on heavy retail margin (borrowed money). When prices fall, margin calls force more selling, which pushes prices lower, which triggers more calls — a domestic loss spiral that *amplifies* the imported shock. This is the crucial point: the trigger is imported, but the amplifier is home-grown.
6. **The VN-Index falls** — imported beta plus domestic amplifier.

The foreign-flow correlation of about **+0.50** with the index is the statistical shadow of this belt. And the index-effect/margin amplifier is why Vietnam's drawdowns in a global risk-off are often *larger* in percentage terms than the foreign selling alone would predict: a frontier market is high-beta on the way down because its own leverage and liquidity make the move convex.

#### Worked example: sizing a foreign outflow

Suppose a global risk-off triggers a **\$100,000,000** net foreign outflow from HOSE over a month — a large but historically realistic figure for a bad stretch. HOSE's average daily trading value is on the order of **\$700,000,000** (roughly 18 trillion dong at a 25,600 USD/VND rate). So the foreign outflow is about **\$100M / (\$700M × 21 trading days) ≈ 0.7%** of the month's total turnover.

That sounds tiny — and on raw volume it is. But foreigners are the *marginal* buyer of the heaviest-weighted blue chips, and they sell into a market where domestic margin is forced to sell *with* them. A 0.7%-of-turnover outflow can map to a high-single-digit-percent index drop once the index effect and the margin spiral are added on top. The intuition: *foreign flows punch far above their volume weight because they hit the index's pressure points and set off the domestic amplifier.* This is why a Vietnamese day-trader checks the foreign net-buy number before checking almost anything else.

### Lead, lag, and the time zone advantage

Correlation has a *direction in time* as well as a sign and a strength. For the imported correlation the lead/lag is overwhelmingly one-way: **US macro leads, the VN-Index follows.** Vietnam is a small open economy receiving the global signal, so it almost never *leads* the dollar, US yields, or global EM. This is not a symmetric relationship where each side informs the other; it is a downstream market reacting to an upstream shock.

The lead shows up most cleanly across the international date line. The US closes its trading day, and major US releases — CPI at 8:30 a.m. New York time, the FOMC decision at 2:00 p.m. — land in the *evening* in Vietnam, after HOSE has already closed. So the overnight US session is a *preview* of the next Vietnamese open. A Vietnamese trader who watches the US tape after HOSE closes has, in effect, a several-hour head start on how the VN-Index is likely to gap the following morning: a hawkish US CPI and a red Wall Street night reliably set up a soft Vietnamese open. The imported correlation, run through the clock, becomes a *leading* indicator you can actually act on before your own market opens.

There's a subtler lag too. The fast leg — the same-day gap reaction to a US surprise — is the *trigger*. But the slow leg — sustained foreign repositioning over weeks as global funds gradually shift their EM/frontier allocation — is the *trend*. A single hot CPI print gaps the index down a percent or two; a *regime* of rising US yields and a strengthening dollar produces months of patient foreign selling that grinds the index lower far beyond any one session. When you read the imported correlation, separate the two: the headline shock you trade around the open, and the regime shift you position for over weeks.

#### Worked example: the time-zone preview in dollars

A Vietnamese investor holds a **\$500,000** book (about 12.8 billion dong) and watches the US session the night before. US CPI prints hot; the S&P closes **−1.8%**, US 10-year yields jump **+12 basis points**, and the DXY rallies **+0.8%** overnight. Based on the imported channels, the investor estimates a VN-Index open down roughly **−1%** the next morning (a blend of the dollar and EM read, damped by the domestic counterweight). On the \$500,000 book that's an expected open-gap mark of about **−\$5,000**.

Knowing this *the night before*, the investor can choose to trim risk into the close-of-US-session signal rather than wait to be gapped against. The intuition: *the imported correlation plus the time zone gives a Vietnamese trader a free preview — the US close is tomorrow's VN-Index open, written a few hours early.* That preview is worth real money precisely because the lead/lag runs strictly from the US to Vietnam and not back.

## How it shows up in real markets

Two dated episodes and one mechanism make the imported correlation concrete. They are mirror images — one a risk-off that imported a crash, one a risk-on that imported a boom — and together they show the correlation working in both directions.

![Two regimes the 2020 dollar slump and the 2022 dollar surge against the dong](/imgs/blogs/vn-index-and-us-macro-the-imported-correlation-6.png)

This figure overlays the dollar (DXY) and USD/VND across 2019–2025, with the two regimes marked. Use it as the backdrop for the cases below.

### 2022: the global tightening drawdown

This is the canonical imported drawdown, and it's worth walking through link by link. Through 2022, US CPI ran red-hot, peaking at 9.1% in June. The Fed responded with the most aggressive hiking cycle in four decades, lifting the funds rate from near zero in March to **4.5%** by December. The DXY surged about **15%** off its 2020 low to that twenty-year peak of **114.8** in September. US 10-year yields rose from roughly 1.5% to over 4%.

Every imported wire lit up at once. Global funds slashed EM/frontier exposure; foreigners were persistent net sellers of Vietnamese blue chips; the dong came under pressure and the SBV hiked its policy rate from 4.0% to 6.0% to defend it; and the VN-Index fell from about 1,500 at the start of the year to under 1,000 — a drawdown of roughly **−33%**. (The domestic side made it worse: 2022 was also Vietnam's corporate-bond crisis, with a property-developer credit crunch and a margin unwind. The imported shock and the domestic shock compounded — a clean illustration that the imported correlation is *part* of the story, never all of it. The domestic leg gets its own post: [VN inflation, the SBV, and the domestic-asset correlation](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/vn-inflation-sbv-and-the-domestic-asset-correlation).)

For scale: a foreign investor who held a **\$1,000,000** Vietnamese equity book through 2022 lost about **−\$330,000** on the index move *and* a further slice on the dong's ~2% depreciation — call it roughly **−\$345,000** all-in. Nearly all of that pain was *imported*. The companies they owned didn't lose a third of their earning power; the global risk regime simply repriced them.

### 2023: the risk-on recovery

The mirror of 2022 came in 2023, and it is just as instructive. Through 2023, US inflation cooled steadily — headline CPI fell from over 6% at the start of the year toward 3% by mid-year — and the market began to price the *end* of the Fed's hiking cycle. The DXY eased back from its 2022 extreme toward 101 by year-end. US 10-year yields, after spiking near 5% in October 2023, fell sharply into year-end as the "Fed pivot" trade took hold. Global risk appetite improved, and the EM/frontier bucket got re-bid.

The VN-Index recovered through 2023, clawing back a large share of the 2022 drawdown, as foreign selling pressure eased and the SBV — no longer forced to defend the dong against a relentless dollar — *cut* its policy rate from 6.0% back to 4.5% to support domestic growth. Watch how cleanly the imported and domestic legs realigned in the *same* direction: a softer dollar relieved the imported pressure *and* gave the SBV room to ease domestically, so both wires pulled the index up at once. 2022 and 2023 together are the imported correlation's clearest demonstration: the same set of US-macro wires that crushed the index on the way up the dollar crushed it again, in reverse, on the way down. Nothing about Vietnamese companies' long-run earning power swung by 60 percentage points in two years; the global risk regime did.

### 2020: the global liquidity boom

Now run the tape in reverse. In March 2020 the COVID crash hit, and the Fed and global central banks responded with the largest liquidity injection in history — rates to zero, the Fed's balance sheet ballooning from about \$4.3 trillion to over \$7 trillion in months. The DXY *slumped* from its panic high back toward 90 by year-end. Global risk appetite came roaring back, and the EM/frontier bucket was a prime beneficiary.

The VN-Index, which had crashed with the rest of the world in March 2020, then rallied powerfully — roughly *doubling* from its March 2020 low into early 2022, one of the best-performing markets in the world over that stretch. Domestic factors helped (a flood of new local retail accounts, low domestic rates, a strong export recovery), but the *permission* for the rally — the cheap global dollar liquidity that lifted every risk asset at once — was imported. (For the global engine behind this, see [global liquidity and the everything correlation](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/global-liquidity-and-the-everything-correlation) and [crypto as a macro asset: the liquidity correlation](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/crypto-as-a-macro-asset-the-liquidity-correlation) — Vietnam rode the same tide as Bitcoin and the Nasdaq.)

### A US CPI shock's pass-through to Vietnam

The two big regimes are slow-moving, but the imported correlation also shows up at the speed of a single data release. Picture a US CPI report that comes in **+0.3 percentage points hotter** than expected on a Tuesday night, Vietnam time. The same-session reaction abroad is hawkish: the S&P falls, US 2-year and 10-year yields jump, the dollar rallies. (The full intraday choreography is covered in [the event-trading CPI post](/blog/trading/event-trading/cpi-the-report-that-moves-the-world).)

By the time HOSE opens on Wednesday morning, the global tape is already red. Foreign desks, reading a more hawkish Fed path, lean to the sell side on Vietnamese blue chips; the dong ticks weaker; domestic traders, watching the foreign net-sell number print negative, get defensive. The VN-Index gaps down at the open. None of this required a single piece of Vietnamese news. A US inflation surprise propagated through the dollar, US yields, and foreign flows, and arrived in Ho Chi Minh City overnight. *That* is why a Vietnamese investor watches the US CPI calendar.

#### Worked example: a risk-off move implied for the VN-Index

Let's combine the channels into one estimate. A hot US CPI print pushes the DXY up **+1.5%** in a session. The VN-Index's correlation with the dollar is about −0.40; suppose a −1.5% beta translation, so the dollar channel alone implies roughly **−0.6%** on the VN-Index. Layer on the EM channel: global EM falls about **−2%** on the same hawkish read, and with a VN beta of ~0.7 to EM, that's another **−1.4%**. The two channels are not independent (they're both the dollar/risk story), so we don't simply add them — but a combined imported drag of roughly **−1.5% to −2%** on the day is a reasonable estimate.

On a **\$1,000,000** Vietnamese book, that's an imported mark-to-market hit of about **−\$15,000 to −\$20,000** from a US data release, before any domestic news. The intuition: *the VN-Index has a real, sizable same-day beta to US macro surprises — small enough that domestic factors can override it, large enough that you cannot ignore the US calendar.*

## Common misconceptions

The imported correlation is easy to over- or under-state. Five myths, each corrected with a number.

**Myth 1: "The VN-Index just follows the S&P 500."** No. The S&P correlation is about +0.45 — meaningful, but that means *less than half* of the VN-Index's variance is shared with US large caps, and the relationship is stronger with the EM basket (+0.55) than with the S&P itself. Worse, treating Vietnam as an S&P clone will get you run over in the (frequent) stretches when domestic factors — SBV policy (−0.45 correlation), a credit crunch, a margin unwind — drive the tape *against* a rising US market. Vietnam follows global *risk*, filtered through a domestic amplifier. It is not a leveraged S&P.

**Myth 2: "Vietnam is a domestic, retail market, so global macro doesn't matter."** This is the opposite error, and it's just as wrong. Yes, local retail is most of the *volume*. But foreigners are the *marginal* price-setter of blue chips, and the foreign-flow correlation with the index is about +0.50. The marginal buyer makes the price. A market can be 90% domestic by volume and still import its biggest swings from the 10% at the margin.

**Myth 3: "A managed currency insulates Vietnam from the dollar."** Partly true, partly dangerous. The SBV does damp the dong's move — a 15% dollar surge in 2020–2022 produced only a ~2.3% dong depreciation. But the pressure doesn't vanish; it gets *converted* into tighter domestic policy (the SBV hiked to 6.0% in 2022 to defend the dong) and into foreign outflows. The managed currency changes *which pipe* the dollar's force flows through; it does not turn off the imported correlation. In some ways it routes the force straight into the rate and equity channels.

**Myth 4: "The correlation is stable, so I can hedge it with a fixed beta."** No — it is strongly regime-dependent. In calm, domestically-driven markets the VN-Index can decouple from global risk for months. In a global risk-off it *spikes*: the EM correlation jumps from ~0.55 toward ~0.8, and diversification between "my Vietnamese stocks" and "global risk" fails exactly when you need it. A fixed-beta hedge will be too small in the crisis and too big in the calm. (This is the general lesson of [correlation during crises: when diversification fails](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/correlation-during-crises-when-diversification-fails) applied to Vietnam.)

**Myth 5: "Higher US yields are good for Vietnam because they mean global growth."** Not for an emerging market. Rising US 10-year yields correlate about −0.25 with the VN-Index, because for a frontier market the dominant effect of higher US yields is *capital pulled home* and a *higher global discount rate*, not a growth tailwind. The growth read can dominate for US cyclicals; for Vietnam, the funding/flows read dominates. Sign matters, and for Vietnam the sign on US yields is negative.

## How to read it and use it

You don't need a Bloomberg terminal to use the imported correlation. You need a short watchlist and a regime check. Here is the playbook.

### The watchlist: the imported dashboard

A Vietnamese investor watching for the imported signal monitors, in roughly this order of importance:

- **Foreign net buy/sell on HOSE (daily).** The most direct, fastest read of the imported correlation transmitting. Sustained net selling is the belt running in reverse. This is your real-time tape.
- **The DXY (dollar index).** The master dial. A *rising* DXY is a global tightening and a VN-Index headwind; a *falling* DXY is a risk-on tailwind. Watch the *trend and rate of change*, not just the level — a high but stable dollar is far friendlier than a lower but fast-rising one.
- **US 10-year yield.** A proxy for the global discount rate and the pull-capital-home force. Rising fast is a headwind.
- **The US data calendar — CPI and FOMC above all.** These are the events that move the dollar and yields, and therefore the imported signal. A Vietnamese investor should know when US CPI prints. (See [the event-trading post on how the VN-Index reacts to US/SBV data](/blog/trading/event-trading/vietnam-events-sbv-vn-cpi-and-how-vn-index-reacts).)
- **MSCI EM / the broad EM ETF.** Your peer benchmark. If EM is being sold globally, Vietnam is probably being sold too.

### The regime check: which leg is driving?

The imported correlation is *conditional*, so before you act on it, ask which regime you're in.

![A matrix showing when the imported correlation dominates versus when domestic factors lead](/imgs/blogs/vn-index-and-us-macro-the-imported-correlation-7.png)

The matrix above is the decision tool:

- **Global risk-off (DXY rising sharply, heavy foreign selling, EM falling):** the imported correlation *dominates*. Do not fight it with a domestic thesis. This is when Vietnam is a high-beta member of the global EM trade, full stop. Reduce risk, or at least don't add leverage into it.
- **Global risk-on (DXY falling or stable, foreign net buying, EM rising):** the imported correlation *helps* — the tide is lifting Vietnam. This is the regime to lean into the domestic growth story, because the global backdrop is permissive.
- **Calm, domestically-led (DXY range-bound, light two-way flows):** the imported correlation *fades* and **domestic factors lead** — SBV policy, credit growth, the margin cycle, sector-specific stories. This is the regime where stock-picking and domestic analysis pay, and where watching the S&P every night will just add noise.

The regime check is the whole skill. The imported correlation is not a constant you can trade mechanically; it is a force that *switches on* in global stress and *recedes* in calm. Your job is to know which regime you're in before you decide whether the US calendar or the Vietnamese credit cycle is the thing to watch this week.

A practical way to operationalize the regime check is to ask three yes/no questions each morning: *Is the DXY trending up over the last few weeks? Are foreigners net sellers on a sustained basis? Is global EM falling?* Three yeses put you firmly in the imported-dominates regime — protect capital, don't add leverage, and expect Vietnamese fundamentals to be overridden. Three noes put you in the calm, domestic-led regime — lean on your stock-picking and the SBV/credit story, and treat the nightly US tape as background noise. A mixed answer is the in-between regime where you weight both legs. This is not precise enough to be a trading system on its own, but it is the right *first question* to ask before any Vietnamese position decision, because it tells you which book of analysis to open.

One more honest caveat on the regime check: the regime can flip *fast*. The 2022 risk-off didn't announce itself; it built over weeks as the Fed turned hawkish, then accelerated. By the time the imported correlation is obviously dominating — foreigners selling hard, the dong under visible pressure, the financial press writing about it — a large part of the move has already happened. The value of watching the dollar and US yields is that they often turn *before* the foreign-flow number does, giving you the regime change a little early. That is the practical payoff of understanding the chain link by link: you can read the upstream wires (US CPI, the Fed, the DXY) to anticipate the downstream one (foreign flows) before it shows up in your own market.

### Measuring the imported beta yourself

You don't have to take the −0.40 and +0.55 on faith; the imported correlation is something you can measure with a spreadsheet and free data. The recipe is the same one used throughout this series:

1. **Get the series.** Download weekly or monthly closing levels for the VN-Index and for a proxy of each driver: an MSCI EM ETF, the S&P 500, the DXY, the US 10-year yield. Vietnamese exchange data for the daily foreign net-buy number is published by HOSE and by every local broker.
2. **Convert levels to returns.** Correlation is computed on *changes*, not levels — take the percent change (or log return) of each series, period to period. Comparing raw levels is one of the classic traps that manufactures fake correlations out of two things that merely both trended up over time. (See [spurious correlation and the traps of macro data](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/spurious-correlation-and-the-traps-of-macro-data).)
3. **Compute the rolling correlation, not just the full-sample number.** A single full-history correlation hides the regime. Compute a *rolling* 26-week or 52-week correlation between VN-Index returns and each driver, and plot it through time. You will see the imported correlation *breathe*: near zero in calm domestic regimes, spiking toward +0.8 (with EM) and −0.6 (with the dollar) in global risk-offs like 2022. That breathing pattern is the single most important output, and it's why a fixed beta is the wrong tool. (See [rolling correlation and why the window matters](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/rolling-correlation-and-why-the-window-matters).)
4. **Estimate the beta with a regression.** Regress VN-Index returns on EM (or dollar) returns; the slope is your *beta* — the move-per-unit-move you actually size positions with — and the R-squared tells you how much of Vietnam's variance the global factor explains in that window. Re-run it per regime to see how the beta itself shifts.

The discipline here is to treat the imported correlation as a *measured, time-varying quantity*, not a folk belief. The numbers in this post are researched approximations; your own rolling estimate on current data is what you should actually trade against.

### What invalidates the signal

Be honest about when the imported read is wrong:

- **A purely domestic shock.** The 2022 corporate-bond crisis, a regulatory crackdown, or an SBV surprise can drive the VN-Index hard *against* the global tape. When the domestic news is the headline, the imported correlation takes a back seat.
- **A structural re-rating.** An MSCI/FTSE upgrade from frontier to emerging would pull a wave of passive foreign money in *regardless* of the short-term risk regime — a one-time structural inflow that overwhelms the cyclical signal. (Structural shifts are exactly the kind of thing that breaks a historical correlation — see [structural shifts: why today's correlations aren't yesterday's](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/structural-shifts-why-todays-correlations-arent-yesterdays).)
- **The correlation is lower than a developed EM peer's, and noisy.** Frontier status, capital controls, and the managed currency all loosen the link. Treat the imported beta as a *tendency*, sized conservatively, not a law.

The takeaway for a Vietnamese investor is the one in the TL;DR: you trade Vietnamese stocks, but you live in a dollar world. Keep one eye on the foreign net-buy number, the DXY, and the US data calendar — and know which regime you're in. The other eye stays on Vietnam.

It's worth ending on the deeper reason this matters beyond Vietnam, because the imported correlation is not a Vietnamese quirk — it is the defining feature of *every* small open market in a dollar-centric world. Brazil, Indonesia, South Africa, the Philippines: each is a domestic market that imports its biggest swings from the same upstream wires — US inflation, the Fed, the dollar, global risk appetite — through the same foreign-flow belt. What differs across them is only the *gauge* of the wire: how integrated the market is, how managed the currency is, how large the domestic counterweight. Vietnam happens to sit toward the muted-but-real end of that spectrum today, which is why its imported correlations cluster around 0.4–0.55 rather than 0.7+. But the *direction* of the relationship — long global risk, short the dollar, hostage to foreign flows in a crisis — is shared by the whole emerging-market complex.

And the gauge is not fixed. The single biggest structural change on Vietnam's horizon is a potential upgrade from frontier to emerging-market status in the major indices. If it comes, it would thicken the wire: more passive foreign money, a higher imported beta, larger and faster transmission of the global risk cycle. The very thing that makes Vietnam attractive as it matures — deeper integration with global capital — is the thing that would *raise* its imported correlation and make the US calendar matter even more. A Vietnamese investor who internalizes the imported correlation now is not just explaining 2022; they are preparing for a future in which Ho Chi Minh City trades a little more like the rest of the emerging world every year. Watch the dollar, watch the flows, and watch the wire get thicker.
