> **TL;DR** — Copper is the **growth metal** and gold is the **fear and real-rate metal**, so dividing one by the other cancels the shared "it is a metal" factor and leaves a clean, market-priced read on growth-versus-fear. That copper/gold ratio is **positively correlated with the 10-year Treasury yield** — both rise when markets price stronger growth — with a co-movement of roughly **+0.7** in a clean reflation regime.
>
> - **The number to remember:** in the 2020 to 2021 reflation, the copper/gold ratio rose from about 1.6 to 2.4 while the 10-year yield climbed from 0.93% to 1.52% — they rose together, which is the whole signal.
> - **A ratio, not a level.** Copper alone and gold alone both rise in a metals bull market, so neither isolates growth. The *ratio* divides the common factor out and leaves the growth-vs-fear spread.
> - **It is a confirmation tool.** When the ratio and the yield move the same way, the growth signal is clean. When they diverge, something other than growth is driving rates or metals — that is the warning.
> - **Be honest:** the relationship is a *regime*, not a law. In 2023 to 2025 the ratio fell while yields stayed high because gold surged on central-bank buying — the signal decoupled, and you have to know why.

In the autumn of 2020 the bond market and the metals market started telling the same story at the same time, in two completely different languages. A 10-year US Treasury note yielded 0.93% at the end of the year — barely above its all-time low — and to a casual observer that said the economy was still flat on its back. But under the surface the yield had quietly stopped falling and begun to climb, and so had something most people never look at: the price of a pound of copper *divided by* the price of an ounce of gold. That obscure ratio bottomed near 1.6 and started grinding higher. Both numbers were pricing the same thing — a recovery that the official data had not yet confirmed.

Over the next year the message got loud. By the end of 2021 copper had roughly doubled off its 2020 low, gold had gone sideways, and the copper/gold ratio had climbed to about 2.4. The 10-year yield had risen in lockstep, from 0.93% to about 1.52%. Two markets that share almost no traders, no exchange, and no obvious plumbing had moved together because they were both reading the same underlying variable: how much *growth* the world expected. The bond market priced it as a higher yield. The metals market priced it as copper beating gold. They agreed, and anyone watching either one had an early, market-tested read on the cyclical trade.

That is the entire thesis of this post. There is a single market-based thermometer for growth-versus-fear expectations, it is built from two metals you can buy on any commodity exchange, and it tracks the most important number in finance — the 10-year yield — closely enough to be useful as a confirmation and divergence tool. It is not magic, it is not always right, and it broke spectacularly in 2023 to 2025 for a reason we will dissect. But understood properly, the copper/gold ratio is one of the cleanest cross-asset windows into what markets are actually pricing about the real economy.

## Foundations: what each metal actually prices

Before we can divide one metal by another and call the result a signal, we have to understand *why each price moves at all*. Copper and gold sit at opposite ends of the commodity world. One is consumed; one is hoarded. One tracks the factory floor; one tracks fear and interest rates. The whole reason their ratio works is that they respond to almost opposite forces, so the ratio amplifies the difference between those forces — which turns out to be the growth-vs-fear spread.

### Why copper is the growth metal — "Dr. Copper"

Copper has a nickname on trading desks: **Dr. Copper**, "the metal with a PhD in economics," because its price seems to forecast the health of the economy. The nickname sounds like folklore, but the mechanism behind it is concrete and almost boring. Copper is used in *almost everything that gets built*. It wires houses and office towers. It is the windings inside electric motors. It carries current in the power grid, in transformers, in substations. A single internal-combustion car contains roughly 20 to 25 kilograms of copper; a battery-electric car contains two to four times as much, because the motor, the battery pack, and the charging infrastructure are all copper-hungry. Data centers, air conditioners, transmission lines, plumbing — copper is in the bill of materials for the physical economy.

That ubiquity is the point. Because copper is consumed in *construction and manufacturing* rather than hoarded, its demand rises and falls with the *building cycle*. When factories are humming, housing starts are climbing, and grids are being expanded, copper demand surges and the price rises. When growth stalls, projects are shelved, order books thin out, and copper demand — and price — sags. Copper is not a forecast in any mystical sense; it is a *real-time read on physical demand*, and physical demand is the cleanest expression of the growth cycle there is. That is why copper's correlation with a positive growth surprise is strongly positive, about **+0.60** in the cross-asset research, right alongside industrials and small caps.

There is a supply side too, and it matters for honesty. Copper mines take a decade to permit and build, ore grades are declining at the big deposits, and a strike in Chile or a permit fight in Peru can spike the price without any change in demand. So copper is a *growth gauge with noise* — most of its swing is the demand cycle, but a meaningful minority is supply shocks. We will come back to this, because supply shocks are one of the ways the copper/gold signal breaks.

One more nuance deserves a word, because it is where the *inflation* part of "the growth-vs-fear signal" sneaks in. Copper, like all industrial commodities, is partly an inflation asset: when the broad price level rises, the dollar price of a pound of copper tends to rise with it, because copper is a real thing whose extraction and processing costs go up. So a rising copper price can reflect *either* stronger real growth *or* higher inflation — and in a reflation, it usually reflects both at once, because the same demand surge that lifts growth also lifts prices. That is why the ratio's signal is best described as *growth-vs-fear* rather than purely *real growth*: copper carries a growth-plus-inflation impulse, gold carries a fear-and-real-rate impulse, and the spread between them is the market's net read on "is the economy running hot, or are people scared?" When the economy is genuinely accelerating, copper's growth-and-inflation impulse dominates and the ratio rises with the nominal yield; when fear takes over, gold's safe-haven impulse dominates and the ratio falls. The 10-year *nominal* yield — which also bundles growth and inflation expectations together — is the natural bond-market counterpart, which is part of why the ratio tracks the nominal yield rather than the real yield specifically.

### Copper's structural demand: the energy transition

It is worth pausing on copper's demand side, because a structural shift is quietly changing the metal's behavior and therefore the signal. Historically, copper demand was dominated by construction and the traditional industrial cycle, especially in China, which consumes roughly half the world's copper. That made the metal an almost pure read on the *global building cycle* — and on China's property sector in particular. When Chinese construction boomed, copper boomed; when Beijing tightened credit to cool a property bubble, copper sagged regardless of what the rest of the world was doing.

That single-driver story is fracturing. The *energy transition* — electric vehicles, grid expansion, renewable generation, and the data-center build-out for AI — is a vast new source of copper demand that is far less tied to the traditional Chinese property cycle. An electric vehicle uses two to four times the copper of a combustion car; a wind turbine can use several tonnes; expanding a national grid to carry more electrified load is one of the most copper-intensive activities there is. Forecasters expect this transition-driven demand to grow for decades. The practical consequence for the signal is twofold. First, copper now has a *secular* demand tailwind layered on top of its *cyclical* swing — so a flat copper price in a weak cycle (as in 2022-25) may be "stronger than it looks," because transition demand is offsetting cyclical weakness. Second, the metal's correlation with the *Chinese* construction cycle is loosening while its correlation with the *global* growth-and-electrification story is tightening. When you read the copper leg of the ratio, you are increasingly reading "is the world building *and* electrifying," not just "is China pouring concrete." This makes copper a richer growth gauge — but it also adds a slow-moving structural component that the ratio cannot distinguish from the cyclical signal, which is one more reason to cross-check against the cyclical survey data rather than treat the ratio as pure.

### Why gold is the fear and real-rate metal

Gold is the mirror image. Almost nobody *consumes* gold the way they consume copper — it is not wired into your house or wound into a motor. The vast majority of the gold ever mined is still sitting in vaults, jewelry, and central-bank reserves. Gold is *hoarded*, not used up. So its price is not set by industrial demand; it is set by how much people want to *hold* it relative to the alternatives.

What is the alternative to holding gold? Holding an interest-bearing asset — a Treasury bond, a bank deposit, anything that pays a yield. Gold pays *no cash flow*: no coupon, no dividend, no interest. So the cost of owning gold is the yield you give up by not owning the safe interest-bearing asset instead. Economists call that the **opportunity cost**, and the right measure of it is the **real yield** — the interest rate *after* subtracting expected inflation — because gold is itself an inflation store, so you only compare it to the inflation-adjusted return on bonds. When real yields are high, owning gold is expensive (you are forgoing a lot of real interest), so gold tends to fall. When real yields are low or negative, owning gold is cheap (you are forgoing little or nothing), so gold tends to rise. That is why gold is, first and foremost, a *real-yield play*, and why its correlation with real yields was roughly **−0.8** for the decade and a half through 2021.

Layered on top of the real-yield engine is the *fear* engine. Gold is the oldest safe-haven asset on earth. When investors are frightened — of a war, a banking crisis, a currency debasement — they want an asset with no counterparty, no default risk, and a five-thousand-year track record of holding value. They buy gold, and the price rises regardless of where real yields sit. So gold prices two things at once: the *opportunity cost* of holding it (real yields, the dominant driver most of the time) and *fear* (a spike-prone driver that takes over in a crisis). Critically, *neither* of those is growth. Gold's correlation with a positive growth surprise is roughly **−0.05** — essentially zero, and if anything slightly negative, because strong growth often means higher real yields, which hurt gold. Gold is the anti-growth metal.

There is a third gold driver that matters enormously for the *break* we will discuss later: **central-bank demand**. Central banks hold gold as a reserve asset, and when they decide to buy — to diversify away from the dollar, to hedge sanctions risk, to back a currency — they buy in size, steadily, and largely without regard to where real yields sit. This is *price-insensitive structural demand*, and when it turns on (as it did from 2022 onward), it can lift gold even into the teeth of high real yields, overriding the usual opportunity-cost mechanism. Central-bank buying is the single most important thing to understand about why gold decoupled from real yields in 2023-25, and therefore why the copper/gold ratio sent a false growth signal in that window. Keep it in mind; it is the villain of the break.

For the full mechanism behind gold and real yields — including why "gold hedges inflation" is the wrong way to say it — see [inflation and gold: the real-yield story](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/inflation-and-gold-the-real-yield-story) and the cross-asset treatment in [real yields: the variable that prices everything](/blog/trading/cross-asset/real-yields-the-variable-that-prices-everything).

### Why a ratio, and not just copper

Here is the clever part, and it is the reason this signal exists at all. Both copper and gold are *metals*. When the dollar weakens, when global liquidity floods in, when commodity indices get bid, *both* metals tend to rise together for reasons that have nothing to do with growth. Call that the **common metal factor** — the part of each metal's price that moves with "metals in general." If you only watch copper, you cannot tell whether a rally is a *growth* signal or just a weak-dollar, risk-on, everything-up melt-up. The growth message is tangled up with the metals-bull message.

Dividing copper by gold cuts that knot. The common metal factor — the weak dollar, the liquidity wave, the broad commodity bid — lifts the numerator and the denominator by roughly the same proportion, so it *cancels in the ratio*. What survives is the *difference* between the two metals' drivers: copper's growth pull minus gold's fear-and-real-rate pull. That residual is, almost by construction, a pure growth-versus-fear spread. When copper rises faster than gold, growth is winning; when gold rises faster than copper, fear is winning. The ratio is a long-copper, short-gold spread trade, and a spread between a growth asset and a fear asset *is* a growth signal.

This is the same logic that makes a *pairs trade* or a *relative-value spread* informative when the outright levels are not. You strip out the common factor (the market, the sector, the dollar) and isolate the difference you actually care about. Here the difference you care about is growth, and the ratio delivers it.

It helps to see the cancellation in a simple factor model. Imagine each metal's return is the sum of a *common metal factor* `M` (the weak dollar, the liquidity wave, the broad commodity bid) plus its own *idiosyncratic* driver: copper's return is roughly `M + G` where `G` is the growth pull, and gold's return is roughly `M + F` where `F` is the fear-and-real-rate pull. The percentage change in the ratio, for small moves, is approximately copper's return minus gold's return: `(M + G) − (M + F) = G − F`. The common factor `M` drops out exactly, and what remains is `G − F` — the growth pull minus the fear pull. That residual *is* the growth-vs-fear spread, with the "it is a metal" noise removed. This is why the ratio reads growth so much more cleanly than copper alone: copper alone is `M + G`, still contaminated by the metal factor, while the ratio is the pure `G − F`. The cancellation is approximate (the two metals do not have identical betas to the common factor, and ratios are not perfectly linear), but it is good enough that the ratio behaves like a long-growth, short-fear spread.

#### Worked example: how the ratio cancels a common metal rally

Suppose the dollar weakens sharply and *both* metals rally on the common factor alone: copper rises 8% and gold rises 8%, with no change in the growth-vs-fear spread. What does the ratio do? Approximately `+8% − 8% = 0%` — the ratio is *flat*, correctly telling you that nothing about growth-vs-fear has changed even though both metal prices jumped. Now suppose instead growth surprises to the upside: copper rises 8% (\$4.00 to \$4.32) while gold *falls* 2% (\$1,800 to \$1,764) because higher real yields hurt it. The ratio rises by roughly `+8% − (−2%) = +10%`, a strong growth signal — even though copper itself only moved 8%. Compare the two scenarios: copper rose 8% in *both*, but the ratio moved 0% in the first (a pure metal rally) and +10% in the second (a real growth signal). The intuition: watching copper alone, you could not tell a weak-dollar melt-up from a growth surge; the ratio tells them apart by subtracting gold's response, which is exactly the discrimination you want from a growth thermometer.

![Two metals, two demand engines: why the ratio isolates growth](/imgs/blogs/copper-gold-and-the-growth-inflation-signal-7.png)

#### Worked example: computing the copper/gold ratio

Make it concrete with real annual-average prices. In 2020 copper averaged about \$2.80 per pound and gold about \$1,770 per ounce. The copper/gold ratio is just one divided by the other: `2.80 / 1770 = 0.001582`. That number is awkwardly small, so practitioners almost always multiply by 1,000 to get a readable figure: **1.58**, which we round to 1.6. In 2021 copper averaged \$4.23 and gold \$1,799, so the ratio was `4.23 / 1799 × 1000 = 2.35`, call it 2.4. The ratio jumped from 1.6 to 2.4 — a **49% rise** — in a single year. Notice what drove it: copper went up 51% (from \$2.80 to \$4.23) while gold barely budged (up about 1.6%). The ratio rose because the *growth metal* outran the *fear metal*. The intuition: a 49% jump in the ratio is not a 49% jump in "metals" — gold was flat — it is a 49% jump in how much *more* the market favored growth over fear, which is exactly the signal you want.

## The signal: the copper/gold ratio tracks the 10-year yield

Now we connect the metals to the bond market. The claim is that the copper/gold ratio is *positively correlated with the 10-year Treasury yield*, and the reason is that **both price growth expectations**, just through different instruments.

Start with why the 10-year yield prices growth. A government bond yield is, very roughly, the sum of three things: expected average short-term real interest rates over the life of the bond, expected average inflation, and a term premium for bearing duration risk. The first piece — expected real rates — moves with expected *growth*, because when the economy is expected to grow faster, the central bank is expected to keep policy rates higher (to prevent overheating) and investors demand a higher real return to be talked out of equities and into bonds. So when growth expectations rise, the 10-year yield rises. When growth expectations fall, money flees to the safety of bonds, prices rise, and yields fall. The 10-year yield is, among other things, the bond market's vote on growth. (For the full mechanism, see [interest rates: the price of money](/blog/trading/macro-trading/interest-rates-the-price-of-money-master-variable) and [reading the yield curve](/blog/trading/macro-trading/reading-the-yield-curve-slope-inversion-recession).)

There is a subtlety worth making explicit, because it is what makes the *nominal* 10-year yield the right counterpart rather than the real yield. The nominal yield bundles together *both* growth expectations (via expected real rates) *and* inflation expectations (via expected average inflation). The copper/gold ratio bundles together the *same two things* from the other side: copper carries a growth-and-inflation impulse, gold carries a fear-and-real-rate impulse. So when growth and inflation both rise in a reflation — the typical pattern — the nominal yield rises (both of its components rise) and the ratio rises (copper's growth-and-inflation impulse beats gold's fear impulse). They track because they are bundling the *same* macro mix. If you tried to pair the ratio with the *real* yield instead, the fit would be worse, because the real yield strips out the inflation component that copper still carries. The match between the ratio and the *nominal* yield is therefore not a coincidence of two growth gauges — it is two instruments bundling the same growth-plus-inflation reflation impulse.

Now you have two independent growth votes. The metals market votes via the copper/gold ratio: copper beats gold when growth is being priced. The bond market votes via the 10-year yield: yields rise when growth is being priced. Because they are voting on the *same* thing, they should move together — and in a clean growth regime, they do, with a co-movement of roughly **+0.7**.

![Copper/gold ratio vs the 10-year yield](/imgs/blogs/copper-gold-and-the-growth-inflation-signal-2.png)

The figure plots the ratio (horizontal) against the 10-year yield (vertical), year by year. Follow the green reflation points: 2020 sits low-left (ratio 1.6, yield 0.93%), and 2021 jumps up-and-right (ratio 2.4, yield 1.52%). The fitted line through the reflation leg slopes clearly upward — higher ratio, higher yield. That is the positive correlation the thesis describes, and it is the regime in which the signal is most useful: when the world is recovering, both the metals and the bonds price the recovery, and they agree.

But look at the red diamonds — 2023, 2024, 2025. The ratio *falls* (from 2.0 to 1.6) while the yield stays *high* (near 4%). The signal decoupled. We will spend a whole section on why, because the break is as instructive as the relationship, and pretending the correlation is a constant would violate the first rule of this entire series: **correlation is a regime, not a law** (see [correlation is a regime, not a constant](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/correlation-is-a-regime-not-a-constant)).

#### Worked example: copper's beta to growth, and why the ratio amplifies it

Beta is the slope of one variable's response to another — how much asset A moves per unit move in driver B. Copper's correlation with a positive growth (ISM/PMI) surprise is about **+0.60**, while gold's is about **−0.05**. Suppose a growth surprise hits that, on average, lifts copper by 4% and is associated with a roughly flat-to-down 0.3% move in gold. The copper/gold ratio's response is approximately the copper move *minus* the gold move (for small percentage changes, the ratio's percentage change ≈ numerator's % change − denominator's % change): `+4.0% − (−0.3%) = +4.3%`. So the ratio moves *more* than copper alone — it adds copper's gain and gold's loss. If the same growth surprise moves the 10-year yield by, say, +8 basis points, then the ratio and the yield are both registering the same growth impulse, and the ratio's 4.3% move "confirms" the yield's 8bp move. The intuition: by subtracting gold's flat-to-negative response, the ratio *concentrates* the growth signal that is diluted in copper alone — it is a leveraged growth read.

![Copper is a growth metal, gold is not: loadings on a growth surprise](/imgs/blogs/copper-gold-and-the-growth-inflation-signal-4.png)

The bar chart makes the concentration visible. Copper loads **+0.60** on a growth surprise (green), sitting up with cyclical sectors, small caps, and the S&P 500. The 10-year yield loads **+0.45** (blue) — growth lifts yields. Gold loads **−0.05** (amber) — essentially nothing, and long Treasuries load **−0.45** (growth *hurts* the safe-haven bond). The copper/gold ratio is the spread between the top of this chart (copper) and the bottom-middle (gold), which is why it ends up reading growth so cleanly: you are buying the asset that loves growth and shorting the asset that ignores it.

## Measuring the relationship: window, lead/lag, and smoothing

The series' discipline is that a correlation is not a single number — it has a *window* over which you measure it, a *lead/lag* structure that tells you which series moves first, and a sensitivity to *how you smooth* the inputs. The copper/gold-vs-yield relationship is a good place to practice all three, because each one changes the answer.

**The window.** If you compute one correlation over the whole 2019-2025 sample of annual levels, you get a number close to zero — because the sample mixes the clean reflation regime (positive co-movement) with the COVID crash (a sharp risk-off spike) and the 2023-25 gold-driven break (negative co-movement). Those regimes have *different* signs, and averaging them washes the signal out. If instead you compute a *rolling* correlation over a moving 12- or 24-month window, you see the relationship breathe: strongly positive through the 2020-21 reflation, weakening through 2022, and going negative in the 2023-25 decoupling. The honest statement is therefore *regime-conditional*: "+0.7 in a growth-driven regime, near zero or negative when a non-growth force dominates one of the legs." This is the same lesson as the stock-bond correlation flip — see [rolling correlation and why the window matters](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/rolling-correlation-and-why-the-window-matters) for the general machinery, and never quote a full-sample number for a relationship you know is regime-dependent.

**The lead/lag.** Does the copper/gold ratio *lead* the yield, *lag* it, or move coincidentally? In practice the two are close to coincident — both react fast to the same growth news, often within the same week — but the metals can lead at cyclical turning points because physical demand sometimes shifts before the bond market fully reprices. This is the same reason the ISM new-orders survey leads earnings: the people placing orders know before the accountants do. You measure lead/lag by computing the cross-correlation at different lags (shift one series forward by k months and recompute the correlation; the lag where the correlation peaks is the lead time). For copper/gold versus the yield the peak is near zero lag, so treat them as *confirming each other in real time* rather than one forecasting the other. For the cross-correlation machinery, see [lead, lag, or coincident: the time axis of every correlation](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/lead-lag-leading-coincident-and-lagging-indicators).

**Smoothing.** Daily copper and gold prices are noisy, and the daily ratio whipsaws on every supply headline and futures-roll. Most practitioners smooth the ratio with a moving average — a 50-day or 200-day average is common — before reading it as a signal, exactly as a chartist smooths a price. The trade-off is the usual one: a shorter window is responsive but noisy and gives false signals; a longer window is stable but lags the turn. For a macro read, a monthly or weekly ratio smoothed over a quarter strikes a reasonable balance. The point is that the "signal" is not the raw tick-by-tick ratio — it is the *trend* in a smoothed ratio, read against the *trend* in the yield.

![Correlation of the copper/gold ratio with the 10Y yield, by regime](/imgs/blogs/copper-gold-and-the-growth-inflation-signal-8.png)

The figure shows the same two series measured three ways. Over the **reflation regime** (2020-2022) the annual-level correlation is **+0.45**, and on the *monthly changes* during the cleanest reflation stretch it runs closer to **+0.7** — strongly positive however you cut it. Over the **full annual sample** (2019-2025) it is **−0.05** — essentially nothing, because the sample mixes opposite-signed regimes. Over **the break** (2023-2025) it is deeply **negative**, as gold's surge drove the ratio down while yields stayed high. Same data, three different answers — and the only honest summary is the regime-conditional one.

#### Worked example: reading a rolling correlation honestly

Suppose you build a 24-month rolling correlation of the monthly change in the copper/gold ratio against the monthly change in the 10-year yield. Over the window centered on 2021 you read **+0.70**; over the window centered on 2024 you read **−0.30**. A naive analyst averages them and reports "+0.20, weakly positive" — and is wrong in every regime, because the true relationship is *strongly positive in one regime and negative in the other*, never +0.20. The correct report is: "the relationship is +0.7 when growth drives both legs, and turns negative when a non-growth force (here gold's central-bank bid) drives the ratio." The intuition: a single averaged correlation across two opposite-signed regimes is not a compromise estimate, it is a fiction — the regime label is part of the measurement, not an afterthought.

## The price paths: the reflation pop and the 2023-25 divergence

To understand both the signal and its break, look at what copper and gold each actually did.

![Copper and gold price paths](/imgs/blogs/copper-gold-and-the-growth-inflation-signal-3.png)

Two stories sit on top of each other. Copper (green, left axis) popped from \$2.80 in 2020 to \$4.23 in 2021 as the post-COVID reflation refilled order books worldwide, then went broadly sideways — \$4.00, \$3.85, \$4.20, \$4.30 — through 2022 to 2025, held back by a soft Chinese construction sector even as the energy transition supported demand elsewhere. Gold (amber, right axis) did the opposite shape: flat around \$1,800 from 2020 through 2022, then *surged* — \$1,943 in 2023, \$2,388 in 2024, roughly \$3,000 in 2025 — on a wave of central-bank buying and safe-haven demand.

The shaded band marks the 2023-25 divergence, and it is the key to the whole post. The copper/gold ratio fell over that window *not because copper crashed* — copper was roughly flat to up — but because *gold ran away from it*. The denominator of the ratio exploded. So the "growth signal" turned negative even though the growth metal itself was fine. This is the single most important caveat in the entire topic: the copper/gold ratio is a *spread*, and a spread can be moved by either leg. When gold moves for reasons that have nothing to do with growth — central banks diversifying out of dollars, a fear bid — the ratio sends a false growth-down signal. The yield, which was being held high by sticky inflation and a higher-for-longer Fed, did not follow. The two votes disagreed, and the disagreement *was the information*: the ratio was being driven by gold's special story, not by growth.

## Using the signal: confirmation and divergence

The practical value of the copper/gold ratio is almost never as a standalone forecast. It is a *cross-check* on the 10-year yield. You watch the two together, and you care most about whether they *agree*.

![The ratio as a signal](/imgs/blogs/copper-gold-and-the-growth-inflation-signal-5.png)

When the copper/gold ratio and the 10-year yield move the *same* way, the growth signal is *confirmed* — two independent markets are pricing the same thing, and you can lean into the cyclical trade (or the defensive trade) with more confidence. The green band on the left of the figure is 2020-21: both rise, reflation confirmed. When they *diverge* — one moves and the other does not, or they move in opposite directions — you have a *warning*. The red band on the right is 2023-25: the ratio falls but the yield stays high, telling you that *something other than growth* is driving at least one of the two markets, and you should find out what before you trade on either.

This confirmation/divergence logic maps cleanly onto a two-by-two grid.

![The confirmation-and-divergence grid: copper/gold versus the 10-year yield](/imgs/blogs/copper-gold-and-the-growth-inflation-signal-6.png)

Read the grid by combining the ratio's direction with the yield's direction. Top-left — *ratio rising, yield rising* — is **clean risk-on**: both agree that growth is being priced, so add cyclicals, copper miners, banks, and cut bond duration. Bottom-right — *ratio falling, yield falling* — is **clean risk-off**: both agree that growth fear is being priced, so own defensives and long Treasuries and trim cyclicals. The two off-diagonal cells are **divergences**, and they are where the analysis earns its keep. *Ratio rising but yield falling* often means a growth bid in metals that the bond market does not believe, or a flight-to-bonds for non-growth reasons (a risk-off scramble into Treasuries even as metals firm); *ratio falling but yield rising* — the 2023-25 case — often means gold-specific strength (central-bank buying, a fear bid) is dragging the ratio down while rates stay high on inflation. In both divergence cells, the right move is usually to *wait* and identify the non-growth driver, not to trade the signal at face value.

Notice the asymmetry between the two diagonal cells. The *clean risk-on* and *clean risk-off* cells are not just "trade the signal" instructions; they tell you *how aggressively* to position. When both gauges agree and the move is young (a low-but-rising ratio with a low-but-rising yield, as in late 2020), the agreement plus the early stage justifies maximum conviction — this is the highest-confidence cyclical entry the framework produces. When both agree but the move is old (a high-but-still-rising ratio with a high yield), the agreement is intact but the *reward-to-risk* has deteriorated, so you ride the trade with a tighter stop rather than adding to it. The grid gives you the *direction*; the *stage of the move* gives you the *size*. Combining them is how you avoid the classic error of piling into the cyclical trade right as both gauges peak.

### How the ratio compares to other growth proxies

It helps to place the copper/gold ratio next to its cousins so you know when to reach for it. The *yield curve slope* (2s10s) is a growth-and-policy gauge with a long lead time — it tends to invert a year or more before a recession, so it is a *strategic* signal, good for the multi-quarter view. The *ISM new-orders sub-index* is a survey-based leading gauge with a roughly six-month lead to earnings — a *tactical* signal, good for the next two or three quarters. *Credit spreads* are a risk-appetite gauge that tightens in good times and blows out in stress — a fast, sometimes coincident signal. The *copper/gold ratio* sits among these as the *commodity-market* growth vote: roughly coincident with the yield, priced by a different crowd, and uniquely able to register a *physical-economy* demand shift that the financial-market gauges might miss. No single one of these dominates; their value is in the panel. When the curve, the survey, the spreads, and the ratio all point the same way, you have a growth call built from four near-independent markets — and that is about as good as macro gets.

#### Worked example: a dollar-framed cyclical trade when the ratio confirms a yield move

Suppose it is late 2020. You are watching the 10-year yield tick up from 0.80% toward 1.00%, and you want to know whether it is a real growth signal or just noise. You check the copper/gold ratio: it has turned up off 1.6 and is rising. The two agree — growth is being priced — so you decide to express the cyclical trade. You allocate \$10,000 to a cyclical/industrials position with a beta of roughly 1.4 to the broad market and a strong positive loading on the growth surprise. Over 2021 the reflation plays out as both signals said it would: the cyclical basket returns about 30% as the ratio climbs to 2.4 and the yield rises to 1.52%. Your \$10,000 becomes about **\$13,000** — a \$3,000 gain — and the confirmation from two independent markets is what gave you the conviction to size the trade in the first place. The intuition: the ratio did not *cause* the gain, but its agreement with the yield turned a noisy 20bp yield move into a high-confidence growth call you were willing to put real money behind.

#### Worked example: the divergence signal and the trade you do NOT make

Now run the 2023-24 tape. The 10-year yield is sitting near 4%, the economy is growing, and a naive reading of "high yields = growth" might tempt you back into the cyclical trade. But you check the copper/gold ratio and it is *falling* — from 2.0 toward 1.8 and then 1.6. Divergence. Instead of blindly adding \$20,000 to cyclicals, you stop and ask *why* the ratio is falling. The answer is gold: it has surged from \$1,802 to \$2,388, a 33% jump, on central-bank buying — not because copper collapsed. So the ratio's "growth-down" message is an artifact of gold's special story, not a real growth warning. You correctly *do not* short cyclicals on a false signal. Had you shorted a \$20,000 cyclical basket that then rose 15% with the still-growing economy, you would have lost about **\$3,000** fighting a signal that was broken. The intuition: a divergence is not automatically a trade — it is a prompt to find the non-growth driver, and here finding it (gold's central-bank bid) told you to ignore the ratio, not to act on it.

## Common misconceptions

The copper/gold ratio is simple enough to be dangerous: it is easy to over-trust. Here are the myths that cost people money, each corrected with a number or a mechanism.

**Myth 1: "The copper/gold ratio is always positively correlated with yields, around +0.7."** No — that is a *regime* number, not a constant. The +0.7 holds in clean growth regimes like the 2020-21 reflation. Over the noisy full annual sample including the 2020 COVID crash and the 2023-25 gold surge, the simple correlation of the level of the ratio with the level of the yield is close to *zero* and can even be slightly negative. The signal lives in the *changes during growth-driven episodes*, and it breaks when a non-growth force (a gold bid, a copper supply shock) takes over. Quote the +0.7 as "the reflation-regime co-movement," never as a law.

**Myth 2: "If the ratio is falling, growth is slowing — sell cyclicals."** Not necessarily. The ratio is a *spread*, so it can fall because copper weakens (a real growth signal) *or* because gold strengthens (often not a growth signal at all). In 2023-25 the ratio fell entirely because gold soared on central-bank buying while copper was flat-to-up. Acting on the ratio's "growth-down" message would have had you short the cyclical trade into a growing economy. Always decompose: *which leg moved?*

**Myth 3: "Copper is a pure growth gauge."** Mostly, but not purely. Copper has a real *supply* side — mine strikes in Chile, falling ore grades, permit fights — that can move the price by 10-20% with no change in demand. A copper spike from a supply shock will lift the ratio and *look* like a growth signal when it is nothing of the kind. Cross-check the ratio against the actual growth data (the ISM new-orders sub-index, for instance) before treating a copper-driven move as a growth read. See [ISM and PMI: the leading correlation with cyclicals](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/ism-pmi-the-leading-correlation-with-cyclicals).

**Myth 4: "Gold rises with inflation, so the ratio reads inflation."** Gold's dominant driver is the *real* yield (the inflation-adjusted rate), not headline inflation. Gold can fall during high inflation if the central bank raises real rates fast enough — which is exactly what happened in 2022, when inflation was raging but real yields jumped from negative to +1.7% and gold went sideways. The copper/gold ratio reads *growth-vs-fear*, not inflation; for the inflation correlation use breakevens or oil, covered in [oil prices, CPI, and the energy-equity correlation](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/oil-prices-cpi-and-the-energy-equity-correlation) and [PCE, breakevens, and the forward inflation correlation](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/pce-breakevens-and-the-forward-inflation-correlation).

**Myth 5: "A single ratio can time the market."** No single cross-asset ratio times anything reliably. The copper/gold ratio is a *confirmation and divergence* tool — it makes a growth call from another source (the yield, the ISM, the cycle clock) more or less credible. Used as a standalone timing signal it whipsaws, because both of its legs have non-growth drivers that fire at random intervals. Its value is in *agreement*, not in solo forecasting.

## How it shows up in real markets

Theory is cheap. Here are the dated episodes where the copper/gold ratio earned its reputation, and the one where it broke.

### 2020: the COVID collapse and the V-shaped recovery

In March 2020 the world locked down. Copper crashed to about \$2.10 per pound as factories went dark, while gold held up as a safe haven, so the copper/gold ratio plunged — the textbook *risk-off, growth-fear* reading. The 10-year yield collapsed to 0.5%. Both signals agreed: fear was being priced. Then, from the spring low through year-end, copper roared back to \$3.50 and the ratio climbed off its bottom while the yield bottomed and turned up. The two markets called the V-shaped recovery in real time, before the GDP and earnings data confirmed it. Anyone who watched the ratio and the yield *together* had a market-tested signal that the recovery was real months before the hard data caught up.

The COVID episode is also a useful caution about the *level* of the signal versus its *change*. At the very bottom, both the ratio and the yield were extremely low — and a naive level-reading would have said "stay defensive, everything points down." But the *change* had already turned: the ratio had stopped falling and started rising, the yield had stopped collapsing and started climbing. The tradeable signal was the *turn*, not the depressed level, which is the same lesson the whole series keeps returning to. A low-but-rising ratio is a buy signal for cyclicals; a high-but-falling ratio is a sell signal. Read the derivative, not the level — and read it against the yield's derivative for confirmation.

### 2021: the reflation, the cleanest signal in years

2021 is the poster child. The copper/gold ratio rose from 1.6 to about 2.4 as copper doubled off its low and gold went flat. The 10-year yield rose from 0.93% to 1.52%. Both votes screamed *growth*, and the cyclical trade delivered: industrials, materials, small caps, and banks led the market, while long-duration tech and bonds lagged. This is the regime where the +0.7 co-movement is real and tradeable. If you want one episode that proves the signal works, it is this one — and the worked example above sizes the trade it justified.

### 2022: inflation, the Fed, and a partial break

2022 is more subtle. The Fed hiked aggressively, real yields exploded from negative to +1.7%, and the 10-year yield rocketed from 1.5% to nearly 4%. Copper *fell* from \$4.23 to \$4.00 (recession fears, a slowing China) while gold went sideways, so the ratio *dipped* from 2.4 to 2.2. Here the yield rose (on inflation and Fed hawkishness) while the ratio fell (on growth fear) — an early divergence. The bond market was pricing *inflation and policy*, not pure growth, while the metals were pricing the *growth scare*. The divergence correctly flagged that the yield's rise was not a clean growth signal — it was a rate-fear signal, which is a very different thing for the cyclical trade.

This is the moment to apply the decomposition discipline. A trader in mid-2022 staring at a 10-year yield ripping toward 4% might have been tempted to read "higher yields, growth must be strong, buy cyclicals." But the copper/gold ratio's *refusal* to rise — falling from 2.4 to 2.2 even as the yield doubled — was the tell that the yield's move was *not* about growth. Decompose: the yield's rise was driven by the *real-rate and inflation* components (the Fed crushing inflation with aggressive hikes), not by the *growth* component, which was actually deteriorating. The metals, which key off physical demand rather than policy, saw the growth deterioration and the ratio fell. The right read was therefore defensive, not cyclical, despite the rising yield — and indeed 2022 was a brutal year for the cyclical and equity trade. The ratio, by *diverging* from the yield, told you the yield's rise was the wrong kind of rise. That is the single most valuable thing a divergence can do: warn you that a headline number means something other than what it usually means.

### The China-demand drag on copper, 2022-24

It is worth isolating the China story, because it is the cleanest example of the *copper leg* sending a growth message that was partly real and partly local. China consumes roughly half the world's copper, the bulk of it historically through its property and infrastructure sector. From 2021 onward that sector entered a deep, policy-driven slump: a wave of developer defaults, a freeze in new construction starts, and a credit clampdown that Beijing was unwilling to fully reverse. Copper demand from Chinese construction fell hard, and that is a big part of why copper went sideways at \$3.85 to \$4.30 from 2022 to 2024 instead of riding the energy-transition tailwind to new highs. So the copper leg of the ratio was *weak*, and read naively it said "global growth is soft."

But the message was *half local*. The weakness was concentrated in Chinese property, not in the global growth-and-electrification story — copper demand from EVs, grids, and data centers was rising even as Chinese construction fell. A trader who read the soft copper price as a pure *global* growth signal would have missed that a structural demand source was offsetting a cyclical-and-local one. The lesson: when the copper leg moves, ask *which* copper demand moved — Chinese property is a different signal than the global cycle, and the ratio cannot tell them apart on its own. This is exactly why Step 4 of the playbook insists on decomposing the legs against the underlying drivers rather than reading the ratio at face value.

### 2023-25: the central-bank gold surge and the decoupling

The clean break. From 2023 the gold price went vertical — \$1,943, then \$2,388, then roughly \$3,000 — on sustained central-bank buying (notably from emerging-market reserve managers diversifying out of the dollar) and safe-haven demand. Copper stayed flat-to-up around \$3.85 to \$4.30. So the copper/gold ratio *fell* from 2.0 to 1.6 even though *copper was not signaling a growth slowdown at all*. Meanwhile the 10-year yield stayed elevated near 4% on sticky inflation and a higher-for-longer Fed. The result: the ratio said "growth fear," the yield said "no," and the divergence was driven entirely by gold's idiosyncratic story. This is the regime where the signal is *most* misleading if you read it naively, and *most* informative if you decompose it. The lesson is the series' core lesson: when a correlation breaks, the *reason it broke* is itself a tradeable insight — here, that gold had become a dollar-diversification trade, not a fear-or-real-rate trade.

## How to read it / use it

Put it all together into a repeatable process.

**Step 1 — compute the ratio.** Take copper (in dollars per pound) divided by gold (in dollars per ounce), and multiply by 1,000 for a readable number. Track it as a time series alongside the 10-year yield. You do not need tick data; weekly or even monthly is fine for a macro read.

**Step 2 — read the level for the regime, the change for the trade.** A *rising* ratio means growth is being priced (risk-on, cyclicals favored); a *falling* ratio means fear is being priced (risk-off, defensives favored). As always in this series, the *rate of change* carries more signal than the level — a ratio that is low but rising is more bullish for cyclicals than one that is high but falling.

**Step 3 — check agreement with the 10-year yield.** This is the heart of the method. If the ratio and the yield move the *same* way, the growth signal is confirmed: lean into the cyclical or defensive trade the agreement supports. If they *diverge*, treat it as a warning and proceed to Step 4.

**Step 4 — on a divergence, decompose the legs.** Ask *which leg moved and why*. Is the ratio falling because copper is weak (a real growth signal) or because gold is strong (often a non-growth signal)? Is the yield moving on growth, on inflation, or on the term premium? Cross-check copper against the ISM new-orders sub-index, and gold against the real yield. The divergence is only a growth signal if the *copper* leg is what is driving it.

**Step 5 — know what invalidates the signal.** The copper/gold growth read is *invalid* when (a) gold is being driven by a non-growth force — central-bank reserve buying, a geopolitical fear bid, a dollar-debasement trade; (b) copper is being driven by a supply shock — a major mine strike, a permit freeze; or (c) the bond market is being driven by inflation or policy rather than growth, so the yield is no longer a clean growth vote. In any of those cases, the ratio-vs-yield comparison is comparing apples to a non-growth orange, and you should down-weight it.

**Step 6 — combine it into a panel, do not trade it alone.** The copper/gold ratio is one vote, and its value comes from voting alongside others that read the same growth signal through different lenses. A robust growth panel might include: the *yield curve slope* (a steepening 2s10s says the bond market expects growth), the *ISM new-orders sub-index* (the survey that leads the cycle), *credit spreads* (tightening spreads say risk appetite is healthy), and the *copper/gold ratio* (the commodity vote). When all four agree, the growth call is as confident as a macro call ever gets. When they split, the *split* is the information: figure out which lens is seeing something the others are not. The copper/gold ratio is especially valuable in this panel because its participants — commodity traders, miners, physical hedgers, Chinese importers — overlap almost not at all with the bond traders setting the yield. An agreement across two near-disjoint crowds is much stronger evidence than two correlated signals agreeing. For where this panel sits in the cyclical rotation, see [the business-cycle correlation clock](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/the-business-cycle-correlation-clock).

**On sizing.** Because the ratio is a confirmation tool rather than a standalone forecast, the natural way to use it is to *scale conviction*, not to flip positions. A clean agreement between the ratio and the yield justifies sizing the cyclical (or defensive) trade toward the top of your range; a divergence justifies sizing toward the bottom or standing aside until you have decomposed the legs. Treating the ratio as a binary on/off switch — full cyclical when it rises, full defensive when it falls — is the fastest way to get whipsawed by its non-growth drivers. The signal is analog, and so should be your sizing.

The honest summary: the copper/gold ratio is a *cross-asset confirmation tool* for the growth call you make from the yield curve, the ISM, and the business-cycle clock. It is at its best confirming agreement and at its most valuable flagging divergence. It is at its worst when used as a solo timing signal, because both of its legs carry non-growth drivers that fire unpredictably. Treat it as one independent vote in a panel — a vote worth listening to precisely because it comes from a market (commodities) with a completely different set of participants than the bond market — and it will sharpen your read on whether the growth signal everyone is talking about is real.

### Why the cross-asset agreement matters so much

It is worth dwelling on the deepest reason the copper/gold ratio earns a place in the panel, because it generalizes to every cross-asset signal in this series. A correlation between two assets *within the same market* — two stock-market sectors, two points on the yield curve — is informative, but it is contaminated by shared participants, shared liquidity, and shared plumbing. When the bond market prices a growth slowdown, it is one crowd, reading one set of screens, sometimes herding on the same flawed model. The copper/gold ratio is priced by an almost entirely *different* crowd: physical traders hedging mine output, manufacturers locking in input costs, central banks managing reserves, importers in Shanghai. When that crowd's price (the ratio) agrees with the bond crowd's price (the yield), you have two independent observers reaching the same conclusion from different data. In statistics, two roughly independent signals agreeing is far stronger evidence than two correlated signals agreeing — it is closer to a genuine cross-validation. That independence is the structural reason a cross-asset confirmation is worth more than another within-market indicator, and it is why a humble ratio of two metals deserves a seat at the macro table.

This same principle — that the *most useful* correlations are the ones that bridge markets with disjoint participants — runs through the whole series. The stock-bond correlation matters because it spans the two largest, most distinct pools of capital. The dollar's cross-asset gravity matters because it touches every market through a single channel. The copper/gold ratio matters because it lets the *physical economy's* price-setters vote on growth alongside the *financial economy's* price-setters. When you build a macro view, weight the signals that come from genuinely different crowds most heavily, and treat agreement across them as your highest-confidence read. The copper/gold ratio, for all its quirks and breaks, is one of the few clean windows you have into what the people who buy and sell *real metal* think about growth — and that is a vote worth hearing.
