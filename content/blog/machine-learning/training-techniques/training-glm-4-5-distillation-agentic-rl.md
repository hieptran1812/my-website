The [previous article](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/glm-4-5-architecture) was about the *shape* of GLM-4.5 — the deep-narrow MoE, the heads, the routing. But a frontier agent is not made by its architecture; it's made by how it's *trained*. The architecture sets the ceiling on what is possible, and the training pipeline decides how much of that ceiling actually becomes a working model that can resolve a GitHub issue, browse the web to answer a hard question, and switch between deliberate, multi-step reasoning and instant direct response on demand.

This is the fifth stage of the [GLM lineage series](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/glm-lineage-frontier-llm-technique), and it covers the part of the [GLM-4.5 report](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.06471) that is genuinely new relative to everything before it: a multi-stage pipeline that trains *three separate expert models* and distills them into one, an agentic RL recipe built entirely around *verifiable* rewards, a difficulty curriculum that hunts the frontier of what the model can almost do, and an open-source RL infrastructure (slime) designed for the brutal economics of agentic rollouts. (For the full report read, the existing [GLM-4.5 paper-reading](/blog/paper-reading/large-language-model/glm-4-5-agentic-reasoning-and-coding-arc-foundation-models) is the companion; this article focuses on the training pipeline specifically.)

If the [GLM-130B article](/blog/machine-learning/mlops/engineering-glm-130b) was about not diverging and the [GLM-4 article](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/chatglm-to-glm-4-all-tools) was about alignment, this one is about *industrializing* alignment — turning the artisanal, largely hand-tuned SFT-then-RLHF of GLM-4 into a repeatable factory that reliably produces a frontier agent. Let's walk the factory floor — and pay attention to the machinery, because the machinery is the part that transfers, while the specific token counts and hyperparameters are tuned to GLM-4.5's scale and won't port unchanged to your run.

## Why "post-train with RLHF" doesn't describe this

The GLM-4 era could be summarized as "SFT then RLHF." GLM-4.5's training cannot, because the interesting moves are all in the details that summary erases. Here is the mismatch:

| Assumption | The naive view | The reality in the GLM-4.5 report |
| --- | --- | --- |
| "Train one model to do everything" | One SFT + RL pass | Train *three* expert models, then distill into one |
| "RL needs a reward model" | Reward model scores everything | Agentic RL uses *verifiable* rewards (unit tests, checkable answers) — no reward model to hack |
| "Longer RL context is better, ramp it up" | Curriculum from short to long | Progressive length *irreversibly unlearns* long context; single-stage at full length wins |
| "Harder problems are better RL data" | Train on hard problems | Train on problems where `pass@8=0` but `pass@512>0` — the *almost-solvable* frontier |
| "RL infra is just a training loop" | Standard trainer | Agentic rollouts are slow and variable; the infra (slime) must disaggregate rollout from training |

Every row is a section. The through-line is that GLM-4.5's training is a series of *non-obvious choices*, several of which (single-stage length, the difficulty filter, disaggregated infra) were arrived at by hitting the obvious approach's failure mode first.

This pattern — the obvious approach has a failure mode you discover by trying it — is worth flagging up front, because it's what makes the report valuable. A report that only described the *final* recipe would tell you what to do but not *why those specific choices*. GLM-4.5's report is more useful because it tells you what *didn't* work and why: progressive length curricula unlearn long context (so single-stage), reward models get hacked (so verifiable rewards), uniform-rollout infra stalls on agentic stragglers (so disaggregation). Each "do this" is paired with a "because the obvious alternative fails this way," and the failure is the transferable knowledge — it saves you from discovering it yourself at the cost of a failed run. As with the [GLM-130B](/blog/machine-learning/mlops/engineering-glm-130b) engineering report, the negative results are the expensive part, and the GLM team's willingness to publish them is what makes the whole lineage worth reading.

> The architecture is the engine; the training pipeline is the factory that builds and tunes it. GLM-4.5's factory has more interesting machinery than its engine — and the machinery is what most teams underestimate, because the architecture diagram is what gets the attention while the pipeline is where the model is actually made.

## The mental model: a 23-trillion-token journey

![A pipeline of the GLM-4.5 training journey from pretraining through mid-training, expert RL, distillation, and general RL](/imgs/blogs/training-glm-4-5-distillation-agentic-rl-1.png)

The pipeline above is the whole journey, and it's the mental model for this article. Read it as four phases:

1. **Pretraining (22T tokens).** 15T tokens of general data, then a 7T-token continual-pretraining phase that up-samples code and math/science. This two-phase structure — general first, then domain-concentrated — is itself a technique.
2. **Mid-training (~600B tokens).** Three stages that grow context and inject expensive capabilities: repo-level code (context 4K → 32K), ~500B tokens of synthetic reasoning, and long-context + agent data (32K → 128K).
3. **Post-training, stage 1: experts.** Train three separate domain expert models — Reasoning, Agent, General-chat — each with its own cold-start SFT and expert-specific RL.
4. **Post-training, stage 2: unify.** Distill the three experts into one hybrid model via an "Overall SFT" that mixes reasoning and non-reasoning data, then a final general RL pass.

The total is the "23T tokens" headline (22T pretraining + the mid-training stages, where the commonly-quoted 22T is the pretraining subtotal and the 23T includes the synthetic-reasoning and long-context mid-training data on top). The rest of this article walks each phase, spending the most time on the parts that are genuinely novel: the expert distillation, the agentic RL, and the infrastructure — the three pieces that most distinguish this pipeline from the earlier, simpler SFT-then-RLHF of GLM-4, and the three most worth studying if you're building your own post-training stack.

## 1. Pretraining and mid-training: building the raw material

> **Senior rule of thumb:** the cheapest place to add a capability is the place where data is cheapest, which is usually pretraining — but the *expensive* capabilities (long context, agentic trajectories, hard reasoning) belong in mid-training, where you can afford to be selective.

The pretraining is two-phase, and the structure is deliberate. The first 15T tokens are general — web (with Nemotron-CC-style quality bucketing, where the highest-quality bucket is seen 3+ times), multilingual, code, and math/science. Then a 7T-token **continual pretraining** phase reweights the corpus to up-sample GitHub code and coding/math/science web pages. The reason to concentrate code and reasoning data in a *second* phase rather than spreading it across the whole run is that it lets the model first build broad language competence on the general corpus, then specialize toward the capabilities (code, math) that the ARC mission demands — without diluting the entire 22T run with domain data that a general assistant doesn't need as much of.

**Mid-training** is where the expensive capabilities go, and it's three stages:

- **Repo-level code training** extends context from 4K to 32K by concatenating same-repo files and training on model-filtered issues, PRs, and commits in diff format. This is what builds *software-engineering* capability specifically — not "can write a function" but "can navigate a repository and make a coherent change across files."
- **Synthetic reasoning data** — ~500B tokens of synthesized math, science, and competition-coding reasoning traces, where chain-of-thought is generated by a reasoning model from Q&A drawn from web and books. This injects reasoning patterns the general corpus is too sparse in. The synthetic-reasoning stage is worth dwelling on because it's a now-standard technique with a subtle justification. Natural text rarely contains explicit step-by-step reasoning — people write conclusions, not their full chains of thought — so a model trained only on natural text sees relatively few examples of the *deliberate, multi-step* reasoning it needs to learn. Synthesizing reasoning traces (having a reasoning model produce detailed chains of thought for Q&A drawn from web and books) manufactures the data that the natural corpus lacks. The ~500B-token scale of this stage signals how important the team considered it — half a trillion tokens of synthetic reasoning is a major investment, reflecting a belief that reasoning capability is data-limited (the model can't learn what it rarely sees) and that the fix is to *manufacture* the missing data. This connects to the broader shift the [GLM-4 article](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/chatglm-to-glm-4-all-tools) noted toward thoughtful synthetic data: synthetic data is excellent for capabilities you can verify and patterns that are scarce in natural text, and reasoning traces are both. The lesson is to identify the *patterns your natural corpus is sparse in* and consider synthesizing them — for reasoning, that synthesis is a major and worthwhile investment.
- **Long-context and agent training** extends context from 32K to 128K, up-sampling long documents and large-scale synthetic agent trajectories (~100B tokens).

The reusable principle is a *data-cost gradient*: put the cheap, abundant capabilities in pretraining where you can afford trillions of tokens, and reserve mid-training for the expensive, scarce capabilities (repo-level code, synthetic reasoning, agent trajectories) where hundreds of billions of carefully-constructed tokens are worth more than trillions of generic ones. Mid-training is also where context grows, because extending context is cheaper on a partially-trained model than doing it from scratch.

### Why the two-phase pretraining split matters

The 15T-general-then-7T-code/reasoning structure deserves more than a passing mention, because it's a deliberate inversion of the naive approach (mix everything uniformly across the whole run). The reasoning is about *when* a capability is best learned. Code and math reasoning are easier to learn well once the model already has strong general language competence — they build *on* language understanding rather than replacing it. If you mix heavy code and math from token zero, you spend early capacity (when the model is still learning basic language) on domain data it can't yet fully exploit, and you dilute the general competence that the domain skills will later build on. By front-loading 15T tokens of general data, GLM-4.5 builds the foundation first; by concentrating code and reasoning in a 7T second phase, it specializes a model that's ready to specialize. This is a curriculum at the *pretraining* scale, and it reflects a maturing understanding that even pretraining data has an optimal *ordering*, not just an optimal *mixture*.

The quality-bucketing detail — highest-quality web seen 3+ epochs — is worth lifting too, because it inverts another naive instinct. The naive approach treats all web data equally and sees each document once. GLM-4.5 (following the Nemotron-CC line) *grades* web data by quality and *up-samples* the best of it, so the model sees the highest-quality documents multiple times and the lowest-quality ones rarely or never. The intuition is that a token of high-quality, information-dense text is worth more than a token of low-quality boilerplate, so your epoch budget should be spent disproportionately on the good stuff. Combined with aggressive deduplication (MinHash plus SemDedup, building on the [GLM-130B](/blog/machine-learning/mlops/engineering-glm-130b) dedup-first pipeline), this is how a 23T-token corpus delivers more learning signal than a larger but undifferentiated one. The principle: *up-sample quality, don't just filter* — filtering removes the worst, but up-sampling the best is what extracts maximum signal from a fixed epoch budget. For the data-pipeline lineage this builds on, see the [GLM-130B engineering article](/blog/machine-learning/mlops/engineering-glm-130b) and the [survey](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/glm-lineage-frontier-llm-technique).

### Repo-level code is a different skill than function-writing

The repo-level code mid-training stage targets a specific and underappreciated capability gap. Most code training data is function-level: a docstring and its implementation, a problem and its solution. That teaches "write a correct function," which is necessary but nowhere near sufficient for software engineering. Real software engineering is *repository-level*: understanding how files relate, making a change that spans several of them, respecting existing conventions, not breaking the build. GLM-4.5's repo-level stage — concatenating same-repo files into 32K-token contexts and training on issues, PRs, and commits in diff format — is what builds *that* skill, and it's why GLM-4.5 scores well on SWE-bench Verified (which measures resolving real GitHub issues) rather than just on function-level benchmarks like HumanEval. The lesson is that the *granularity* of your training data determines the granularity of the capability: if you want a model that can edit a repository, you have to train it on repositories, not on functions. The diff format specifically teaches the model to express changes as edits (what most coding agents actually need to produce) rather than as whole-file rewrites.

## 2. Expert distillation: train three specialists, ship one generalist

> **Senior rule of thumb:** training a specialist is easier than training a generalist, because each specialist's reward can be domain-pure. Distillation lets you have the specialists' quality in one deployable generalist.

This is the most novel structural idea in the pipeline, and it's worth understanding deeply.

![A graph showing three expert models — reasoning, agent, and general chat — distilled into one unified hybrid model](/imgs/blogs/training-glm-4-5-distillation-agentic-rl-2.png)

The graph above is the move. Rather than train one model to be good at everything simultaneously — which forces every reward signal to compete and every capability to be a compromise — GLM-4.5 trains **three separate expert models** from the mid-trained base:

- A **Reasoning expert**, cold-start SFT on extended chain-of-thought data, then RL with verifiable math/science/code rewards.
- An **Agent expert**, focused on agentic and coding tasks, with RL on verifiable agentic rewards (SWE unit tests, checkable search answers).
- A **General-chat expert**, aligned with RLHF and RLAIF for broad helpfulness and safety.

Each expert can be trained *purely* — the reasoning expert's RL doesn't have to worry about chat quality, the agent expert's RL doesn't have to worry about reasoning traces — which makes each one's training cleaner and its reward signal undiluted. Then the three are **distilled into one** model via an "Overall SFT" stage that samples millions of examples at up to 128K context, deliberately *mixing* data that contains full reasoning traces with data that has none. The output is a single **hybrid model** that has learned both to deliberate (thinking mode) and to answer immediately (direct mode) — and to choose between them.

The hybrid-reasoning property falls directly out of this construction. A hybrid model isn't a router in front of two checkpoints; it's one set of weights that, during the distillation SFT, saw both reasoning and non-reasoning behavior and internalized a *switch*. The mixing of CoT and no-CoT data in the Overall SFT is precisely what teaches that switch — the model learns "sometimes I produce a long thinking span before answering, sometimes I answer directly," conditioned on the request and a mode signal. Without the deliberate mixing, you'd get a model that always thinks (slow, over-thinking simple queries) or never thinks (fast, failing hard ones). The mix is the mechanism, and getting the *ratio* of CoT to no-CoT data right is its own tuning problem — too much CoT and the model over-thinks, too little and it can't reason when it needs to.

The economic trade is explicit: training three specialists and then distilling is *more total compute* than one generalist run. But it buys two things. First, each specialist's RL is cleaner — domain-specific rewards with no cross-objective interference, which is easier to get working and to debug. Second, you ship a single deployable model rather than three, so the inference cost is that of one model. For most teams the bottleneck is *getting RL to work at all*, and the expert approach makes that easier by removing the cross-talk between objectives. For the theory of why distillation transfers capability so efficiently, see [distillation in LLM](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/distillation-in-llm).

### Why training one generalist directly is so hard

To appreciate the expert approach you have to appreciate the failure it avoids: the *reward interference* of training one model on multiple objectives at once. When you RL a single model on reasoning, agentic, and chat rewards simultaneously, the gradients from these objectives don't just add — they can actively oppose each other. Optimizing for terse, correct agentic tool-calls can push the model toward clipped, mechanical responses that hurt chat quality; optimizing for elaborate reasoning traces can make the model over-think simple chat queries; optimizing for chat helpfulness can make it too verbose for efficient tool use. Each reward is pulling the shared weights in a different direction, and the result is a model that's a *compromise* across all three rather than excellent at any. Worse, this interference makes the RL itself harder to get working — when your loss is a tug-of-war between conflicting objectives, it's hard to tell whether a plateau means "converged" or "stuck in a bad equilibrium between competing rewards."

The expert approach dissolves this. Each expert's RL optimizes *one* reward family, so there's no tug-of-war — the reasoning expert can push reasoning as hard as it wants without worrying about chat, and its training is correspondingly cleaner and easier to debug. The interference only reappears at the *distillation* step, but distillation is supervised learning (imitate the experts' outputs), which is far more stable than RL — you're not optimizing conflicting rewards, you're fitting a mixture of demonstrations. So the expert structure moves the hard multi-objective problem out of the unstable RL phase and into the stable SFT phase, which is the real reason it works. The lesson generalizes: when multiple RL objectives conflict, *separate the optimization* (train specialists) and *combine via supervision* (distill), rather than fighting the conflict inside a single RL run.

### The distillation mixing is the hybrid-reasoning mechanism

It's worth being very precise about how the "Overall SFT" produces a hybrid model, because it's subtle and it's the key to the whole thing. The distillation SFT trains the unified model to imitate the three experts. But the reasoning expert produces *long chain-of-thought* outputs, while the chat expert produces *direct* outputs. If you distilled only the reasoning expert, you'd get a model that always thinks (slow, over-thinking trivial queries); if you distilled only the chat expert, you'd get a model that never thinks (fast, failing hard problems). By *mixing* CoT data (from the reasoning and agent experts) with no-CoT data (from the chat expert) in the same SFT, conditioned on a mode signal, the unified model learns *both* behaviors and a *switch* between them. The mode signal (a flag in the prompt, exposed to users as a thinking/non-thinking toggle) tells the model which behavior to produce. So the hybrid capability is not a separate training stage or a router — it's an emergent property of distilling experts with different output styles into one model, with the mode signal as the conditioning variable. This is genuinely clever: it gets a thinking/non-thinking model *for free* as a side effect of the expert-distillation structure, rather than requiring a separate mechanism. The lesson is that distilling *stylistically different* experts into one model, conditioned on a style signal, is a general recipe for a model that can switch styles on command.

## 3. Agentic RL: only train on what you can check

> **Senior rule of thumb:** RL is only as trustworthy as its reward. The safest reward is one you can *verify* mechanically — a unit test, a checkable answer — because a verifiable reward can't be hacked the way a learned reward model can.

This is the heart of the modern pipeline, and its defining discipline is restraint about *which* tasks get RL.

![A graph of GLM-4.5 agentic RL: web-search and SWE agents produce verifiable rewards that drive a GRPO-without-KL update](/imgs/blogs/training-glm-4-5-distillation-agentic-rl-3.png)

The graph above is the agentic RL loop. The deliberate choice is to focus agentic RL on **web-search and code-generation agents — domains where every action or answer can be automatically checked.** A web-search answer is graded against a known target; a code patch is graded by running unit tests in a hardened sandbox. That gives *dense, verifiable* rewards with no reward model in the loop to hack. The mechanics carry several stealable details:

- **GRPO without the KL term.** Sample K trajectories per prompt, set each trajectory's advantage to its reward minus the group mean, and drop the KL regularizer. Removing the KL term is a notable choice — it lets the policy move more freely, which the verifiable reward makes safe (you can't drift into reward-hacking territory when the reward is a unit test).
- **Mask the environment tokens.** Only model-generated tokens contribute to the loss; tool outputs and environment feedback are masked out. This is a subtle correctness detail — you don't want to train the model to "predict" search results or test output, only to generate good actions.
- **Format penalty as a hard gate.** If the model emits a malformed tool call, the trajectory is halted with *zero* reward. Reliability is trained, not hoped for.

Here is the loss masking — the part that's easy to get wrong:

```python
import torch

def grpo_advantages(rewards):
    """GRPO: advantage = reward - group mean (no value network, no KL)."""
    return rewards - rewards.mean()

def masked_policy_loss(logp, ref_logp, advantages, model_token_mask):
    """Only model-generated tokens contribute. Environment/tool-output tokens
    (model_token_mask == 0) are excluded from the objective entirely."""
    ratio = torch.exp(logp - ref_logp)
    per_token = ratio * advantages.unsqueeze(-1)
    per_token = per_token * model_token_mask          # <-- the load-bearing line
    # token-weighted mean (GLM-4.5's choice for code RL: avoids length bias)
    return -(per_token.sum() / model_token_mask.sum())
```

The data behind the rewards is itself engineered. Web-search Q&A is synthesized two ways: multi-hop reasoning over knowledge graphs (which produces questions with verifiable answers and a known reasoning path), and human-in-the-loop obfuscation of web content (making questions that require actual searching rather than memorized recall). SWE tasks come from real GitHub PRs and issues with executable unit tests in a hardened sandbox. And there's a loop within the loop: **iterative self-distillation** — once the RL model plateaus, its own best outputs replace the cold-start SFT data, a stronger SFT model is trained on them, and RL resumes from there at higher difficulty. Each lap ratchets the floor up, which is why the figure routes "stronger policy" into "new SFT data." For the GRPO base algorithm, see the blog's [GRPO finetuning walkthrough](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/fine-tuning-llm-with-grpo) and [the GRPO/DPO/PPO decision guide](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/grpo-vs-dpo-vs-ppo-decision-guide).

### Why dropping the KL term is safe here

Removing the KL regularizer from GRPO is a choice that would be reckless in a normal RLHF setup and is reasonable here, and the difference is the *reward*. In standard RLHF, the KL-to-reference penalty exists to keep the policy from drifting too far from the SFT model — because the reward model is a flawed proxy, and if you optimize it too hard (drift too far), the policy finds the proxy's cracks and reward-hacks. The KL term is a leash that keeps you in the region where the proxy is still trustworthy. But with a *verifiable* reward — a unit test, a checkable answer — there are no cracks to find. The reward is the actual thing you want, not a proxy for it, so optimizing it hard is *good*, and the leash that the KL term provides is unnecessary and even counterproductive (it slows the policy's movement toward genuinely better behavior). Dropping the KL term lets the policy move freely toward higher verifiable reward, which is exactly what you want when the reward can't be hacked. The lesson is a precise one: the KL term's value is inversely related to how trustworthy your reward is. A perfect (verifiable) reward needs no KL; a noisy (learned-reward-model) reward needs it badly. Knowing which regime you're in tells you whether to keep the leash.

### The verifiable-reward data engineering

The phrase "verifiable rewards" makes it sound like the rewards come for free, but the *data engineering* to produce verifiable tasks is substantial and is itself a contribution. For web search, you need questions that (a) have a verifiable answer and (b) genuinely require searching rather than recall. GLM-4.5 produces these two ways. Multi-hop reasoning over knowledge graphs generates questions whose answer is derivable by traversing the graph — so you know the answer (verifiable) and you know the question requires connecting multiple facts (non-trivial). Human-in-the-loop obfuscation takes web content and rewrites questions so the answer can't be recalled directly, forcing the model to actually search. For SWE tasks, you need real bugs with executable tests, which means mining GitHub for issues that have associated PRs with tests, and building a *hardened sandbox* where the model's patch can be run against those tests safely (untrusted code execution at scale is its own security problem). None of this is the RL algorithm — it's the *infrastructure of verifiability* — and it's often the larger share of the work. The lesson for anyone planning verifiable-reward RL: budget more for building the verifiers and the verifiable-task data than for the RL algorithm itself, because the algorithm is the easy part and the verification is the hard part.

The **token-weighted mean loss** (the last line of the code above) is a small but real choice for code RL specifically. The naive sequence-mean loss weights every trajectory equally regardless of length, which biases the model toward shorter solutions (each token in a short solution carries more weight). Token-weighting normalizes by total tokens instead, which mitigates that length bias and, the report notes, converges faster for code RL. It's the same family of "re-derive what your loss is averaging over" lesson that [LongAlign](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/chatglm-to-glm-4-all-tools) taught for packing — the default averaging is rarely exactly what you want once lengths vary.

## 4. The reasoning RL curriculum and the single-stage finding

Two findings from the reasoning-RL chapter are the kind that only show up after expensive mistakes, and both are worth stealing.

![A matrix of the two-stage reasoning RL difficulty curriculum](/imgs/blogs/training-glm-4-5-distillation-agentic-rl-8.png)

The matrix above is the **difficulty curriculum**. Reasoning RL runs in two stages from a verified-answer pool. Stage one trains on *moderate* problems with 16 samples per prompt — building broad competence. Stage two trains on *extremely difficult* problems, specifically those where `pass@8 = 0` but `pass@512 > 0`, sampled 512 times each. That filter is the clever part: it isolates problems the model *can* eventually solve (pass@512 > 0 means at least one of 512 attempts succeeds) but *usually* fails (pass@8 = 0 means it almost never gets it in 8 tries). That's exactly the frontier where the learning signal is densest — too-easy problems teach nothing (the model already solves them), too-hard problems teach nothing (the model never gets a correct trajectory to reinforce), and the sweet spot is "can occasionally do it, usually can't." Targeting that band is what lifts AIME'24 to ~83% in the ablations, before the rest of the stack.

```python
def select_stage2_problems(model, problems, pass_at_8_n=8, pass_at_512_n=512):
    """Keep only problems on the learnable frontier: the model almost never
    solves them in 8 tries, but can occasionally in 512. Too-easy and too-hard
    problems teach nothing; this band is where the gradient signal lives."""
    frontier = []
    for p in problems:
        if pass_at_k(model, p, k=pass_at_8_n) == 0 and pass_at_k(model, p, k=pass_at_512_n) > 0:
            frontier.append(p)
    return frontier
```

![A before-after showing that single-stage RL at full 64K length beats a progressive length curriculum](/imgs/blogs/training-glm-4-5-distillation-agentic-rl-4.png)

The before-after above is the **single-stage length** finding, and it's counterintuitive enough to be valuable. The natural way to RL a long-context reasoner is to start with short outputs and lengthen gradually — a curriculum. GLM-4.5 tried it and found that progressive length curricula caused the model to **"unlearn" its long-context capabilities — a significant and irreversible drop.** Each stage that lengthened the output budget seemed to overwrite the long-context skill from the previous regime, and once lost it never came back. Their fix was to run single-stage RL directly at the full 64K output length, which "continually pushes the model's limits" without the unlearning. The transferable warning: **a curriculum that removes a capability between stages can cause irreversible forgetting.** Sometimes the harder, full-difficulty regime from step one is safer than the gentle ramp, because the ramp's intermediate stages can teach the model to abandon a skill it will need later.

A third reasoning-RL technique is **dynamic sampling temperature**: raise the temperature when average reward stabilizes (to restore exploration just as the policy starts to converge and stagnate), guarded by a validation check that forbids any temperature causing a >1% performance drop from the current optimum. It's a small control loop that treats exploration-vs-exploitation as a *schedule* rather than a one-time choice — and the validation guard is what keeps the exploration from degrading the model. The problem it solves is real and common in RL: as the policy converges, its sampled trajectories become more similar (lower temperature behavior, the policy is confident), which means less exploration and a tendency to get stuck in whatever local optimum it found. Raising the temperature re-injects diversity into the rollouts, giving the policy fresh trajectories to learn from and a chance to escape the local optimum. But raising temperature too far produces garbage rollouts that hurt the model, which is what the validation guard prevents — it caps the temperature at the highest value that doesn't drop validation performance by more than 1%. So it's exploration *as much as you can get away with*, measured against a held-out check. The general technique — adapt an exploration hyperparameter online, guarded by a held-out validation metric — applies beyond temperature to any knob that trades exploration for exploitation, and the guardrail is what makes online adaptation safe rather than reckless. The blog's [tricks or traps RL deep-dive](/blog/paper-reading/large-language-model/part-i-tricks-or-traps-a-deep-dive-into-rl-for-llm-reasoning) goes deeper on these dynamics.

### Why the frontier band is where learning happens

The `pass@8=0, pass@512>0` filter is worth understanding at the level of *what gradient the RL actually receives*, because it explains why the band is so special. RL from verifiable rewards learns by reinforcing trajectories that succeeded and discouraging those that failed. For a problem the model *always* solves (high pass@8), almost every sampled trajectory succeeds, so the advantage (reward minus group mean) is near zero for all of them — there's nothing to learn, because every attempt is already good. For a problem the model *never* solves (pass@512 = 0), every sampled trajectory fails, so again the advantage is near zero across the group — there's no successful trajectory to reinforce, so the model gets no signal about what *would* have worked. Only in the middle band — where some attempts succeed and some fail — is there a *spread* of rewards within the group, and that spread is exactly the advantage signal GRPO uses. The model sees "these trajectories worked, those didn't" and learns the difference. So the filter isn't a heuristic about difficulty; it's a precise targeting of the problems where the GRPO advantage is non-degenerate. The `pass@8=0` condition ensures the problem is hard enough to still be unsolved (so there's room to improve), and `pass@512>0` ensures it's solvable enough that 512 samples will find *some* successful trajectories to provide the positive signal. It's a beautiful piece of reasoning about where the gradient lives.

This also explains why you need the *large* sample budget (512) for stage 2. At pass@8=0, eight samples will all fail, giving you no positive trajectory — you'd see zero advantage spread and learn nothing. You need to sample enough (512) that even a problem the model solves only occasionally produces a few successes, which then provide the signal. The large sample budget is the cost of mining gradient from the hardest learnable problems, and it's expensive — 512 samples per prompt is a lot of generation — which is why it's reserved for stage 2 and the carefully-filtered frontier problems rather than applied to everything. The two-stage structure (cheap 16-sample broad training, then expensive 512-sample frontier training) is a compute-allocation strategy: spend the cheap budget building broad competence, then spend the expensive budget only on the problems where it'll pay off. It's worth appreciating that this ordering also *changes which problems are on the frontier*. After stage 1 lifts broad competence, problems that were too-hard (pass@512=0) for the stage-1 model may become learnable (pass@512>0) for the stage-2 model — the frontier *moves* as the model improves. So the curriculum isn't a fixed difficulty ramp; it's an adaptive one where stage 1 expands what's learnable so stage 2 can target a frontier that didn't exist before training started. This is why you re-measure the pass rates *after* stage 1 to select stage-2 problems, rather than selecting them all up front: the selection has to track the model's evolving ability, because the whole point is to always be training on the current edge of competence, and that edge keeps moving outward as the model learns.

## 5. General RL: decomposing "good behavior" into failure modes

Verifiable rewards are the glamorous part, but most of "being a good assistant" is not automatically checkable. GLM-4.5's General RL handles that, and its structure is the lesson.

![A tree of the four General RL sub-stages: holistic, instruction-following, function-calling, and pathology](/imgs/blogs/training-glm-4-5-distillation-agentic-rl-5.png)

The tree above shows the decomposition. Rather than one undifferentiated RLHF pass, General RL splits into four targeted sub-stages, each addressing a named failure mode with the cheapest sufficient reward:

- **Holistic RL** over ~5,000 prompts spanning a tiered category hierarchy, to lift broad helpfulness.
- **Instruction-Following RL** against 7 major and 151 minor constraint types — the "did it do all five things I asked, in the format I specified" axis.
- **Function-Calling RL**, split into a *step-wise* rule-based reward (exact match on tool name and arguments) and an *end-to-end* multi-turn reward over a whole trajectory, with an LLM-simulated user driving the conversation.
- **Pathology RL**, a dataset built specifically to kill language-mixing, repetition, and format errors — the failures that don't show up in benchmarks but instantly break user trust.

The reward sources are *multi-source*: rule-based where possible (exact match, format checks), a human-preference reward model (RLHF), and a model-based judge (RLAIF). The structural lesson is the one worth stealing: **don't run one undifferentiated RLHF pass and hope.** Decompose "good behavior" into named failure modes and target each with the cheapest sufficient reward — exact-match rules where you can, preference models where you must. This is the [GLM-4 alignment](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/chatglm-to-glm-4-all-tools) philosophy ("name the failure mode, fix it specifically") industrialized into a four-stage RL pipeline.

### The Pathology RL sub-stage is the unsung hero

Of the four sub-stages, Pathology RL is the one most worth lifting, because it targets the failures that benchmarks don't catch but users feel instantly. Language-mixing (switching between languages mid-response), repetition (looping on a phrase), and format errors (broken markdown, malformed lists) don't show up in MMLU or AIME scores — a model can ace every academic benchmark and still produce these pathologies, which immediately destroy user trust. The reason they persist is that they're *rare* in aggregate (so they barely move any average metric) but *catastrophic* when they happen (a single language-mixed response makes the model feel broken). Standard RLHF, optimizing average preference, under-weights rare-but-catastrophic failures because they're rare. Pathology RL fixes this by building a *targeted dataset* of exactly these failures and training specifically against them — essentially oversampling the rare failures so the model learns to avoid them. The lesson generalizes: rare-but-catastrophic failures need *dedicated* training, because they're invisible to aggregate metrics. If you have a failure mode that's rare but trust-destroying, don't expect general RLHF to fix it — build a targeted dataset and train against it specifically.

### Function-Calling RL: two granularities of reward

The Function-Calling sub-stage's split into *step-wise* and *end-to-end* rewards is a nice example of using two reward granularities for one capability. The step-wise reward is rule-based: did the model emit the correct tool name with the correct arguments at this step? That's exact-match and gives dense, immediate signal — every tool call is graded. The end-to-end reward is trajectory-level: did the whole multi-turn interaction achieve the goal? That's sparser but captures things the step-wise reward can't, like whether the *sequence* of tool calls made sense and whether the model recovered from a tool error. Using both gives you dense local signal (step-wise) *and* the global correctness signal (end-to-end) that local signal can miss — a model could make individually-correct tool calls that don't add up to solving the task, and only the end-to-end reward catches that. The LLM-simulated user driving the multi-turn conversation is the infrastructure that makes the end-to-end reward possible at scale (you can't have a human in every training conversation). The lesson: for multi-step capabilities, combine a dense step-level reward (for sample efficiency) with a sparse outcome-level reward (for global correctness), because each catches what the other misses.

## 6. slime: the infrastructure that makes agentic RL affordable

> **Senior rule of thumb:** agentic RL is rollout-dominated and the rollouts have wildly variable length, so the infrastructure problem (keep the GPUs busy despite a long trajectory stalling the batch) is as hard as the algorithm problem.

The agentic RL is only affordable because of the infrastructure underneath it. GLM-4.5's open-source **slime** stack is the part of the report most directly useful to anyone building their own RL system.

![A grid of the slime RL infrastructure: a data buffer bridging Megatron training and SGLang rollout](/imgs/blogs/training-glm-4-5-distillation-agentic-rl-6.png)

The grid above is the architecture: three modules. **Training** (Megatron) reads from a **Data Buffer**; **Rollout** (SGLang + a router) writes generated data and rewards to it. The Data Buffer is the bridge that decouples the two, and that decoupling is what enables the two execution modes.

![A before-after of slime's two modes: synchronous colocated versus asynchronous disaggregated](/imgs/blogs/training-glm-4-5-distillation-agentic-rl-7.png)

The before-after above shows the two modes, and choosing between them is the key infrastructure decision:

- **Synchronous (colocated)** mode runs training and inference on the *same* GPUs. This is best for reasoning, math, and code RL, where rollouts are *fast and uniform-length* — you generate a batch of similar-length completions, score them, and update, and colocation keeps everything busy because there's no straggler.
- **Asynchronous (disaggregated)** mode splits the rollout GPUs from the training GPUs. This is best for *slow, variable-length* agentic and SWE rollouts. A single agentic trajectory might involve dozens of tool calls and minutes of sandboxed test execution; if you ran that synchronously, the slowest trajectory in a batch would stall every training GPU behind it. Disaggregation lets the training GPUs keep working on already-collected data while the slow rollouts finish in their own pool.

The principle — **match your training topology to the *variance* of your rollout, not just its average** — is the kind of thing you only learn after a fleet of expensive GPUs spends a week mostly idle waiting on stragglers. There's also a precision trick: slime runs **BF16 for training but FP8 for the inference rollout**, with online block-wise FP8 quantization of the policy parameters each iteration. Cheap rollouts make RL affordable, and the asymmetry is the same one [GLM-130B](/blog/machine-learning/mlops/engineering-glm-130b) and the [architecture article](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/glm-4-5-architecture) showed — spend precision only where the gradient needs it, and a rollout is a sampled trajectory you're going to grade anyway, so FP8 noise there costs you a slightly noisier reward, not a corrupted gradient. For the serving engine underneath, see the blog's [SGLang deep-dive](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/sglang-inference) and [verl RLHF library](/blog/machine-learning/open-source-library/verl-rlhf-library-deep-dive).

### Why agentic rollouts break naive RL infrastructure

To understand why slime needs two modes, you have to understand how badly agentic rollouts break the assumptions of a normal RL trainer. A normal RL trainer assumes rollouts are roughly *uniform* in cost — you generate a batch of completions, they all take about the same time, you collect them, score them, and update. That assumption holds for reasoning RL (every completion is a few thousand tokens, generated in roughly the same time) and it's why colocated synchronous mode works there: the GPUs generate, then train, then generate, in lockstep, with little idle time. Agentic rollouts shatter that assumption. One agentic trajectory might resolve in three tool calls and ten seconds; another might take thirty tool calls, several minutes of sandboxed test execution, and a few hundred thousand tokens of context. In a synchronous setup, the *entire batch* waits for the slowest trajectory — so if one trajectory takes 10× the median, your training GPUs sit idle 90% of the time waiting for it. On a fleet of expensive GPUs, that idle time is the dominant cost, and it's pure waste.

The disaggregated asynchronous mode is the structural fix. By separating the rollout GPUs (which generate trajectories) from the training GPUs (which do gradient steps), and bridging them with the Data Buffer, the training GPUs can keep working on *already-collected* trajectories while the slow rollouts finish in their own pool. The rollout pool produces a steady stream of completed trajectories into the buffer (fast ones finish quickly, slow ones eventually), and the training pool consumes from the buffer at its own pace, never blocked on a straggler. This decoupling is what makes agentic RL economically viable — without it, the GPU utilization would be so low that the cost would be prohibitive. The orchestration uses Ray for placement, and the system is designed so high-concurrency Docker-isolated environments (one per agentic task) can run in parallel. The general principle, worth stating once more because it's the core infra lesson: **when your rollout-time distribution has a heavy tail, you must decouple rollout from training, because any synchronous design pays the cost of the tail on every step.** Profile the distribution, not the mean; the mean tells you the average cost, but the tail tells you the *waste*. This is a lesson that generalizes well beyond RL to any batch-parallel system: whenever you process a batch and wait for all items to finish before proceeding, your throughput is set by the *slowest* item, not the average, so high variance in item time is catastrophic for utilization. Distributed systems engineers know this as the "tail latency" problem, and the fix is always some form of decoupling — let fast items proceed without waiting for slow ones. slime's disaggregated mode is the RL-training instance of that general fix. The reason it's easy to miss when you first build an RL system is that the small-scale prototype (reasoning RL with uniform rollouts) works fine synchronously, and the variance problem only appears when you scale to agentic tasks — so you build the synchronous system, it works, and then it falls over when you add agents, exactly when the stakes are highest.

### The async-vs-sync decision in practice

The practical decision between slime's modes comes down to a single question: is your rollout-time distribution tight or heavy-tailed? Reasoning, math, and code RL have tight distributions (completions of similar length, no external execution), so synchronous colocated mode is right — it's simpler, has no cross-pool communication overhead, and keeps the GPUs busy. Agentic and SWE RL have heavy-tailed distributions (variable tool calls, variable sandbox execution time), so asynchronous disaggregated mode is right despite its added complexity. The fact that slime ships *both* and lets you choose per task is the real design insight — there's no universal answer, the right topology depends on the workload, and a mature RL system supports both rather than forcing one. This mirrors the broader lesson of the GLM lineage that the team consistently refuses one-size-fits-all answers in favor of matching the tool to the specific problem. For the disaggregation idea in serving (as opposed to training), the blog's [serving at scale](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/serving-llms-at-scale-production-systems) deep-dive covers the inference-side analog.

## 7. Results: what the factory produced

The matrix above is the output of the whole pipeline. GLM-4.5 posts **AIME 24 91.0** (reasoning, comparable to o3 and DeepSeek-R1), **TAU-bench 70.1** (agentic, ranking 2nd on the agentic axis among all evaluated models), and **SWE-bench Verified 64.2** (agentic coding, beating GPT-4.1). The training pipeline is the reason a smaller model reaches frontier agentic and reasoning performance: the expert distillation gives it clean specialist capabilities unified into one model, and the verifiable-reward agentic RL gives it the agentic and coding skill that the ARC mission targets. The architecture set the ceiling; this pipeline is what delivered it.

### Attributing the results to the pipeline

It's worth asking *which* parts of the pipeline drove *which* results, because the report's ablations let you partially attribute. The reasoning scores (AIME 91.0) trace primarily to the reasoning expert's RL with the difficulty curriculum — the `pass@8=0/pass@512>0` filter is credited with lifting AIME to ~83% before the rest of the stack, and the single-stage 64K length avoided the unlearning that would have capped it. The agentic and coding scores (TAU-bench 70.1, SWE-bench 64.2) trace to the agent expert's verifiable-reward RL on web-search and SWE tasks, which is only affordable because of slime's disaggregated infrastructure. The parameter-efficiency angle (third overall at half DeepSeek-R1's parameters) is partly architecture (the deep-narrow MoE from the [previous article](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/glm-4-5-architecture)) and partly training (clean specialist RL that extracts more capability per parameter). The two compound: a more parameter-efficient architecture trained with a more sample-efficient pipeline produces a model that punches above its weight on both axes. That compounding is the GLM-4.5 story in one sentence — efficient architecture times efficient training equals frontier capability at sub-frontier scale. ## War stories: ten training decisions worth stealing

### 1. Three experts, one model

The expert-distillation structure is the pipeline's signature move, and the reason it works is that it *decouples* the hard problem (getting each capability's RL to converge) from the deployment constraint (ship one model). Training a single model to be simultaneously a great reasoner, a great agent, and a great chatbot forces every reward to compete; training three specialists lets each reward be pure. The lesson generalizes to any multi-capability RL: if your rewards conflict (and reasoning, agentic, and chat rewards do — optimizing one can hurt another), consider training specialists and distilling rather than fighting the cross-talk in a single run. The cost-benefit is concrete enough to reason about: the expert approach costs roughly 3× the post-training compute of a single run (three RL runs plus a distillation), but it converts an *unstable* multi-objective RL problem into three *stable* single-objective RL problems plus a stable supervised distillation. If your single-run multi-objective RL is failing to converge — thrashing between objectives, plateauing in a bad equilibrium — the 3× compute of the expert approach can be cheaper than the engineering time spent trying to make the single run work. Stability has a price, and sometimes paying 3× compute for it is the right trade.

### 2. Verifiable rewards over reward models

The decision to focus agentic RL on web-search and code — domains with *checkable* outcomes — is the deepest safety choice in the pipeline. A learned reward model is a proxy that can be hacked; a unit test cannot. By restricting agentic RL to verifiable domains, GLM-4.5 gets dense reward signal with no reward-hacking surface. The lesson: when you can make your reward *verifiable* (a test passes, an answer matches), do it, because verifiable rewards are the only ones you can optimize hard without fear. The corollary is that the *engineering of the verification* (the sandbox, the test harness, the answer-checking) is part of the RL, not separate from it. This reframes where the difficulty of agentic RL actually lives. Teams often assume the hard part is the RL algorithm, and pour effort into the optimizer, the advantage estimation, the KL handling. But with verifiable rewards, the algorithm is relatively settled (GRPO works); the hard part is *building reliable, fast, secure verification at scale* — a sandbox that can run untrusted model-generated code against unit tests, thousands of times in parallel, without security holes or flaky failures. A flaky test that sometimes fails for infrastructure reasons (not because the model's code is wrong) injects noise into the reward and degrades training. A security hole in the sandbox is a disaster when you're executing untrusted code at scale. So the verification infrastructure is both load-bearing and genuinely hard, and underinvesting in it caps the quality of everything downstream. The lesson: in verifiable-reward RL, allocate engineering effort proportional to where the difficulty actually is — and that's usually the verification infrastructure, not the RL algorithm.

### 3. Masking the environment tokens

The one-line detail of masking tool-output and environment tokens out of the loss is a correctness requirement that's easy to get wrong, and getting it wrong is subtle: if you include environment tokens in the loss, you train the model to *predict* the search results or test output, which is both useless (it can't control them) and harmful (it distorts the policy). The lesson is that in any RL-over-trajectories setup, you must be precise about *which* tokens the policy is responsible for and mask the rest. The model is graded on its actions, not on the environment's responses, and getting this masking right is one of the few places where a one-line bug silently degrades the whole run. There's a concrete bug this prevents: without masking, the policy gradient would push the model to assign high probability to the *tool output tokens* it observed — but those tokens were produced by the environment (a search engine, a Python interpreter), not by the model, and the model has no control over them. Training the model to predict them is at best wasted capacity and at worst actively harmful, because it teaches the model to expect specific environment responses, making it brittle when the environment responds differently. The masking ensures the gradient only flows through the *decisions the model actually made* — which action to take, which tool to call, what arguments to pass — and ignores the environment's deterministic responses. It's a one-line `* model_token_mask` in the loss, but getting it wrong silently corrupts the policy in a way that's hard to diagnose, because the model still trains and the reward still goes up; it's just learning the wrong thing about what it controls.

### 4. The almost-solvable frontier

The `pass@8=0, pass@512>0` difficulty filter is the most elegant data-selection idea in the report. It formalizes the intuition that the best training problems are the ones at the edge of the model's ability — hard enough that it usually fails, easy enough that it occasionally succeeds. Too-easy problems give no gradient (already solved); too-hard problems give no gradient (never solved, no correct trajectory to reinforce). The filter is a cheap, automatic way to find the frontier, and it generalizes to any RL-from-verifiable-rewards setup: measure pass rates at two sample budgets and keep the problems where the low budget fails but the high one occasionally succeeds. That band is where your model learns the most per step. There's a nice connection to human learning here, the "zone of proximal development" — you learn most from problems just beyond your current ability, neither trivial nor impossible. The `pass@8=0/pass@512>0` filter is a precise, automatic operationalization of that zone for an RL model, and the fact that it can be computed cheaply (just measure pass rates at two budgets) makes it practical to apply at scale. The broader lesson is that *data selection is as important as the RL algorithm* — the same GRPO update on frontier-band problems learns far more than on a random mix, so investing in the selection (the verified-answer pool, the pass-rate filtering) pays off more than tweaking the optimizer.

### 5. Single-stage length, against intuition

The finding that progressive length curricula *irreversibly* unlearn long-context capability is a genuinely surprising result that overturns the obvious approach. The intuition that you should ramp difficulty (including length) gradually is usually right — but here it caused permanent damage, because the intermediate short-length stages taught the model to abandon long-context behavior it would need later. The lesson is a warning about curricula in general: a curriculum that *removes* a capability between stages risks irreversible forgetting, and sometimes training at full difficulty from the start is safer. When you design a curriculum, ask whether any stage *takes away* something a later stage needs. The mechanism, as best one can reconstruct it, is that RL at a short output length actively trains the model to *stop early* — to produce concise completions — which is the opposite of the long-context behavior you want later. So the short-length stage isn't neutral with respect to long context; it's actively teaching a contradictory behavior, and RL's strong optimization burns that behavior in deeply enough that the later long-length stage can't undo it. This is more dangerous in RL than in supervised learning because RL optimizes harder against its current objective — it doesn't just expose the model to short completions, it *rewards* shortness, which is a stronger signal than mere exposure. The general principle for RL curricula specifically: be wary of any early stage whose objective is in *tension* with the final objective, because RL will optimize that early objective hard enough to cause lasting damage. Supervised curricula are more forgiving (exposure is gentler than reward); RL curricula need the stages to be *aligned* in direction, not just increasing in difficulty.

### 6. Iterative self-distillation

The loop where the RL model's own outputs become the next round's SFT data is a ratchet that keeps lifting the floor. Once RL plateaus, you've extracted what this SFT starting point allows; replacing the SFT data with the RL model's best outputs gives a stronger starting point, and RL from there can climb higher. The lesson is that SFT and RL aren't a one-shot sequence; they're a *loop*, and alternating them (RL improves the policy, the improved policy generates better SFT data, better SFT gives a higher RL starting point) compounds in a way that a single SFT-then-RL pass doesn't. There's a subtle reason this loop helps that's worth naming: RL is good at *finding* better behaviors (through exploration and reward) but the policy gradient is a relatively weak way to *consolidate* them, whereas SFT is a strong, stable way to consolidate behaviors but can't *discover* anything beyond its data. By alternating, you use RL to discover better trajectories and then SFT to consolidate them firmly into the weights, getting both the discovery (RL) and the consolidation (SFT) that neither does well alone. Re-distilling the RL model's outputs into a fresh SFT is essentially "save the progress RL made into a stable checkpoint before pushing further." The pattern — explore with RL, consolidate with SFT, repeat — is increasingly the standard recipe at the frontier, and GLM-4.5's iterative self-distillation is a clean instance of it.

### 7. Dynamic temperature with a guard

The dynamic sampling temperature — raise it when reward plateaus to restore exploration, but guarded by a 1%-validation check — is a small control loop with a big idea: exploration and exploitation aren't a one-time tradeoff but a *schedule* you manage online. The validation guard is what makes it safe (you only raise temperature as far as you can without degrading the model). The lesson is that several RL hyperparameters you might set once (temperature, KL weight, difficulty) are better managed as *adaptive schedules* with a held-out guardrail, because the right value changes as the policy evolves.

### 8. Match infra topology to rollout variance

slime's two modes — colocated for fast uniform rollouts, disaggregated for slow variable ones — encode the insight that the *variance* of your rollout length, not just its mean, determines your infrastructure. Fast uniform rollouts (reasoning) keep colocated GPUs busy; slow variable rollouts (agentic) need disaggregation so a straggler doesn't stall training. The lesson for anyone building RL infra: profile the *distribution* of your rollout times, and if it's high-variance (some rollouts 10× longer than others), you need a disaggregated architecture or you'll waste most of your GPU time waiting on the long tail.

### 9. FP8 rollouts, BF16 training

The mixed-precision split — BF16 for the gradient-bearing training, FP8 for the throwaway rollout — is the same "precision per role" discipline the whole [GLM lineage](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/glm-lineage-frontier-llm-technique) shows, applied to RL. A rollout is a sampled trajectory you're going to grade; a small numerical error there costs you a slightly noisier reward estimate, not a corrupted gradient, so you can be aggressive with precision. The lesson: in any pipeline with a "generate then grade" structure, the generate step tolerates lower precision than the gradient step, and exploiting that asymmetry is free throughput. The online block-wise quantization detail matters too: because the policy changes every iteration, the FP8 version of the policy used for rollout has to be re-quantized each step from the current BF16 weights. Doing this *block-wise* (quantizing in blocks with per-block scales, rather than one scale for the whole tensor) preserves enough fidelity that the FP8 rollout stays close to what the BF16 policy would produce — which keeps the rollout distribution aligned with the policy you're actually training, avoiding a train/rollout mismatch that would bias the gradient. So the FP8 rollout isn't a crude approximation; it's a carefully-quantized one that stays faithful to the policy. The general pattern — quantize the generation pass but keep it faithful enough that it doesn't drift from the training policy — is the subtle part that makes the precision asymmetry actually safe rather than just cheap.

### 10. The XML tool-call template

A tiny but intensely practical detail from the SFT engineering: GLM-4.5 uses an XML-like function-call template (`<tool_call>`, `<arg_key>`/`<arg_value>` tags) instead of JSON, specifically to reduce character-escaping headaches when function arguments contain code. For an agentic foundation model whose arguments are *frequently* code, JSON's quote-and-backslash escaping is a constant source of malformed calls; an XML-style template sidesteps it. The lesson is that for agentic models, the serialization format of tool calls is a *reliability* decision, not a cosmetic one — and it's the most copy-pasteable idea in the report. The deeper point is that the model's *output format* is part of the training, not just the inference harness — if you train the model to emit XML-style tool calls, it learns to produce them reliably; if you bolt a format onto a model that wasn't trained for it, you get more malformed outputs. Format reliability is trained, like everything else.

### 11. The data-cost gradient

A pipeline-level lesson that's easy to miss in the stage-by-stage detail: GLM-4.5 places each capability at the training stage where its data is cheapest *relative to its value*. Abundant, cheap capabilities (general language) go in pretraining where trillions of tokens are affordable. Scarce, expensive capabilities (long context, agent trajectories, frontier reasoning) go in mid-training and post-training where hundreds of billions of carefully-constructed tokens are worth more than trillions of generic ones. This "data-cost gradient" — cheap capabilities early and in bulk, expensive capabilities late and selectively — is an allocation strategy that maximizes capability per dollar of data. The lesson for anyone planning a training run is to map your target capabilities against where their data is cheapest, and structure your stages accordingly, rather than trying to teach everything everywhere.

### 12. Publishing the infrastructure

slime being *open-source* is itself a strategic decision worth noting. The hardest part of reproducing GLM-4.5's training isn't the algorithm (GRPO is published) but the *infrastructure* (the disaggregated rollout system, the data buffer, the FP8-rollout machinery). By open-sourcing slime, the GLM team made the agentic-RL recipe actually reproducible, which propagates their techniques into the ecosystem the same way their open model weights do. The lesson is that for RL specifically, the infrastructure is often the real moat, and open-sourcing it is how a lab's training methods become the field's defaults. It's the same open-ecosystem strategy the [whole lineage](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/glm-lineage-frontier-llm-technique) pursues, applied to training infrastructure rather than model weights.

## When to reach for the GLM-4.5 training playbook

**Reach for these techniques when:**

- **You're training a multi-capability model with conflicting rewards.** The expert-distillation structure (train specialists, distill into one) is the cleanest way to avoid reward cross-talk. If your reasoning RL and your chat RL fight each other, separate them and distill. There's a team-scaling benefit too: separate experts can be developed by separate teams in parallel, each owning one capability's RL, with the distillation as the integration point — a far more scalable way to build a multi-capability model than one team wrestling a single monolithic run that's sensitive to every change. The expert structure is as much a team-organization pattern as a model-quality one.
- **Your task has verifiable outcomes.** Verifiable-reward agentic RL (with environment-token masking and format-penalty gating) is the highest-leverage post-training technique here — but *only* where you can automatically grade the result. Build the verifier; it's part of the RL.
- **You're doing RL with variable-length rollouts.** The slime sync/async distinction and the FP8-rollout trick are directly applicable. Profile your rollout-time distribution and disaggregate if it's high-variance.
- **You want a hybrid (thinking + direct) model.** The Overall-SFT-mixes-CoT-and-no-CoT recipe is how you get one model that toggles reasoning, rather than shipping two models. The cost is collecting or generating both reasoning and non-reasoning demonstrations and conditioning on a mode signal; the benefit is one deployable model that serves both fast and deliberate requests, which is operationally far simpler than maintaining two.
- **You're alternating SFT and RL.** The iterative self-distillation loop (RL to discover, SFT to consolidate, repeat) compounds in a way a single pass doesn't. If your single SFT-then-RL has plateaued, try re-distilling the RL model's outputs into a fresh SFT and resuming RL.

**Skip or adapt when:**

- **Your tasks aren't verifiable.** Agentic RL on un-checkable tasks reintroduces exactly the reward-hacking that verifiable rewards avoid. Use RLHF/RLAIF there (as GLM-4.5 does for General RL), not verifiable-reward RL.
- **You can't afford three expert training runs.** Expert distillation assumes you can train specialists *and* distill. On a single budget, a single well-mixed SFT-then-RL may be the pragmatic call — you lose the clean separation but save the compute.
- **Your rollouts are fast and uniform.** The disaggregated infra solves a variance problem you don't have. Colocated synchronous RL is simpler; only reach for disaggregation when your rollout times are genuinely high-variance.
- **You're below frontier scale.** Much of this (the 23T pretraining, the slime infra, the three-expert pipeline) is frontier machinery. The *principles* (verifiable rewards, the difficulty frontier, mask the environment, precision-per-role) transfer to smaller RL; the full apparatus doesn't. A small team doing RL on a 7B model should absolutely steal the verifiable-reward discipline, the environment-token masking, and the frontier-band difficulty filter — those are scale-free and high-leverage — while skipping the three-expert pipeline and the disaggregated infra, which solve problems that only appear at frontier scale and budget.

A closing note on what makes this pipeline *teachable* despite its complexity: every stage has a one-sentence justification tied to a specific failure mode, so you can hold the whole thing in your head as a list of problems-and-fixes rather than an opaque recipe. Three experts (avoid reward interference), verifiable rewards (avoid reward hacking), mask environment tokens (don't train on what you can't control), frontier-band difficulty (target the learnable gradient), single-stage length (avoid irreversible unlearning), four General-RL sub-stages (target each failure mode), disaggregated infra (don't stall on stragglers), FP8 rollouts (precision per role). Eight problems, eight fixes. That structure — a pipeline as a list of named problems with named solutions — is itself worth emulating, because it makes a complex system legible and debuggable: when something goes wrong, you can ask which of the eight problems your fix stopped addressing.

The unifying thread is that GLM-4.5's training pipeline is a series of choices arrived at by hitting the obvious approach's failure mode first — the progressive-length unlearning, the reward-model hacking, the rollout-straggler stall — and engineering around it. That's the same empirical, failure-mode-driven discipline the whole [GLM lineage](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/glm-lineage-frontier-llm-technique) shows, applied to the hardest part of the modern stack: turning a capable base model into a frontier agent. The next articles cover how the GLM team *measures* all this (the benchmark-design discipline) and how the same instincts extend to vision.

If there's a single idea to carry out of this article, it's that **modern frontier training is a composition of failure-mode-driven choices, not a recipe.** Every interesting decision here — three experts instead of one, verifiable rewards instead of a reward model, single-stage length instead of a curriculum, the frontier-band difficulty filter, disaggregated infrastructure — exists because the obvious alternative was tried and failed in a specific, instructive way. You cannot get to this pipeline by following a recipe; you get to it by running into each failure mode and engineering around it, and the value of the GLM-4.5 report is that it hands you the failure modes so you don't have to rediscover them at the cost of failed frontier-scale runs. The pipeline will keep evolving — GLM-4.6 and beyond will hit new failure modes and engineer new fixes — but the *method* (try the obvious thing, find where it breaks, fix that specifically, write it down) is the durable part, and it's the same method that has run through the entire [GLM lineage](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/glm-lineage-frontier-llm-technique) from the 2021 objective to the 2025 agent.
