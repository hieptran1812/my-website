There is a quiet pattern in the GLM reports that's easy to miss until you go looking for it: a striking fraction of the benchmarks the team evaluates on are benchmarks the team *built*. AgentBench, AlignBench, LongBench, NaturalCodeBench, HumanEval-X — these aren't borrowed rulers, they're rulers Zhipu and the THUDM group designed and published. That's not an accident or a vanity move. It's a deliberate methodology, and it's the subject of this article.

This is the sixth article in the [GLM lineage series](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/glm-lineage-frontier-llm-technique). The previous articles covered how GLM models are *built* — the [objective](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/autoregressive-blank-infilling-glm), the [engineering](/blog/machine-learning/mlops/engineering-glm-130b), the [alignment](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/chatglm-to-glm-4-all-tools), the [architecture](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/glm-4-5-architecture), and the [training pipeline](/blog/machine-learning/training-techniques/training-glm-4-5-distillation-agentic-rl). This one is about how they're *measured*, because measurement turns out to be a first-class part of the methodology rather than an afterthought you run once at the end.

The thesis is simple and worth stating plainly up front: **you cannot optimize a capability you cannot measure, and for genuinely new capabilities the measurement often doesn't exist yet — so building it becomes part of the work.** Every benchmark the GLM team built exists because an existing benchmark was measuring the wrong thing, and understanding *what* was wrong with the old rulers is the fastest way to understand what good evaluation looks like.

## Why you sometimes have to build your own ruler

The instinct, when you want to know if your model is good, is to reach for the standard benchmarks: MMLU for knowledge, HumanEval for code, GSM8K for math. These are useful and you should run them. But they share a weakness that becomes fatal as you push into new capabilities: they measure what was easy to measure when they were built, which is not always what you actually care about.

![A tree of the benchmarks Zhipu and THUDM built — AgentBench, AlignBench, LongBench, NaturalCodeBench — each targeting a capability public benchmarks missed](/imgs/blogs/how-zhipu-measures-glm-benchmarks-1.png)

The tree above is the map of what the GLM team built and why. Each branch is a capability the team was optimizing where the public rulers fell short:

- **AgentBench** — for *agency*, because no benchmark measured an LLM's ability to act across realistic interactive environments.
- **AlignBench** — for *Chinese alignment*, because alignment benchmarks were English-centric and didn't capture Chinese-language quality.
- **LongBench** — for *long-context use*, because there was no good bilingual, multi-task measure of whether a model actually uses a long context.
- **NaturalCodeBench** — for *real-world coding*, because HumanEval-style problems don't predict performance on messy real user requests.

Notice the shape of the argument in each case: it's not "the old benchmark is wrong," it's "the old benchmark measures a *proxy* for what I care about, and the proxy has diverged from the target." HumanEval measures "can write a self-contained function," which used to correlate with "is a good coder" but stopped correlating as models got good at the toy version. The job of a new benchmark is to *re-establish the correlation* — to measure the thing you actually care about, directly, when the old proxy has been saturated or gamed.

> A benchmark is a proxy for a capability. Proxies decay: as models optimize them, the gap between "high score" and "actually good" widens. Building a new benchmark is how you reset the proxy to track the capability again.

This decay happens through a mechanism worth naming: **Goodhart's law**, the observation that "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." The moment a benchmark becomes the thing labs compete on, every lab optimizes for it — directly (training on similar data) and indirectly (architecture and data choices that happen to help that benchmark). The score goes up, but the *correlation* between the score and the underlying capability goes down, because some of the gains are benchmark-specific rather than capability-general. HumanEval is the textbook case: years of optimization pushed scores into the 90s while real-world coding remained much harder, so a 95% HumanEval score in 2024 told you far less about coding ability than a 70% did in 2021. The benchmark didn't get easier; the models got specifically good at *it*. The only durable defense is to keep building fresh measurements that target the capability directly, faster than the optimization can saturate them — which is exactly what the GLM team did, repeatedly, across agency, alignment, long context, and coding.

There's a second, subtler reason public benchmarks fall short for a frontier lab: they measure what was *common* when they were built, not what *you specifically* are pushing on. A lab betting heavily on Chinese-language quality, or on agentic tool use, or on 128K-context reasoning, is optimizing capabilities that the English-centric, single-turn, short-context benchmarks of a few years earlier simply don't capture. You can't see your own progress on the axis you care about most if no benchmark measures that axis. Building the benchmark isn't just about resetting a decayed proxy — it's about creating *visibility* into the specific capability you're betting your model on. Without that visibility, you're optimizing blind, trusting that gains on generic benchmarks transfer to your specific bet, which they often don't.

## The glossary: five rulers and what each resists

Before the deep dives, here's the whole set in one view, because each benchmark pairs a *capability* with a *measurement method designed to resist gaming*.

![A matrix of the GLM benchmark glossary: each benchmark with what it measures and how it resists gaming](/imgs/blogs/how-zhipu-measures-glm-benchmarks-2.png)

The matrix above is the glossary. Read the second column — "how it resists gaming" — because that's the design insight in each:

- **AgentBench** uses *live environments*. You can't memorize the answer to "navigate this OS" the way you can memorize a multiple-choice question; the environment responds to your actions, so the score reflects real interactive capability.
- **AlignBench** uses a *CoT LLM-judge calibrated by a reference*. A single "rate this 1-10" judge is noisy and gameable; reasoning before rating, against a human-verified reference, makes the judgment more reliable.
- **LongBench** uses *21 diverse task types*. A single long-context task (like needle-in-a-haystack retrieval) can be gamed by a model that's good at retrieval but bad at using what it retrieves; 21 task types force genuine long-context understanding.
- **NaturalCodeBench** uses *Docker execution*. You can't fake passing a unit test; the code either runs and passes or it doesn't, which is the least gameable signal in coding evaluation.
- **HumanEval-X** (from [CodeGeeX](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.17568)) is *execution-based* and *multilingual*, extending the execution-grading idea across programming languages — because a model good at Python isn't necessarily good at the other languages real codebases use.

Notice that these five span the full range of the trustworthiness spectrum. NaturalCodeBench and HumanEval-X sit at the top (executed outcomes — the code runs or it doesn't). AgentBench is just below (live interaction — a real trajectory through a stateful environment). LongBench is a diverse-task aggregate (hard to game with one trick). AlignBench, judging inherently subjective quality, sits lowest but is calibrated as hard as a judge can be. The GLM team didn't pick the easiest measurement for each capability; it picked the *most checkable measurement the capability admits*, which is a different and harder choice. For code, the capability admits execution, so they execute. For agency, it admits interaction, so they interact. For Chinese alignment, it admits only judgment, so they build the best-calibrated judge they can. The discipline isn't "use execution" — execution isn't always available — it's "use the most checkable method this particular capability allows," and that discipline is what makes the whole suite trustworthy rather than just the parts that happen to be executable.

The common thread is that the best evaluation signal is one that's *hard to fake*: a live environment, an executed test, a diverse task suite. The further your measurement is from "match this string" and the closer it is to "actually do the thing and we'll check the outcome," the more it resists the gaming that saturates static benchmarks. This is the same instinct that drives GLM-4.5's *verifiable-reward* RL (covered in the [training article](/blog/machine-learning/training-techniques/training-glm-4-5-distillation-agentic-rl)) — a checkable outcome is the most trustworthy signal, whether you're training on it or evaluating with it.

It's worth making the connection between evaluation and training explicit, because it's the deepest reason the GLM team invests in benchmarks. A benchmark that resists gaming is, almost by definition, also a good *reward signal* for RL. AgentBench's interactive environments are the kind of verifiable, outcome-based setting that GLM-4.5's agentic RL trains on. NaturalCodeBench's Docker-executed tests are exactly the verifiable rewards the coding RL uses. So building good benchmarks isn't a separate activity from building good training signals — they're the same activity, and a team that has invested in hard-to-fake evaluation has also built the infrastructure for hard-to-fake rewards. The line between "how we measure the model" and "how we train the model" is much thinner than the textbook pipeline suggests, and the GLM lineage treats them as two uses of the same underlying asset: a reliable, automatic way to check whether an output is good.

## AgentBench: measuring agency across eight worlds

[AgentBench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.03688) (ICLR 2024) was built to answer a question no existing benchmark could: how good is an LLM as an *agent* — at perceiving a situation, planning, taking actions, and adjusting based on feedback?

![A grid of AgentBench's eight interactive environments, from operating systems to web browsing to lateral thinking](/imgs/blogs/how-zhipu-measures-glm-benchmarks-4.png)

The grid above shows the answer: agency is measured across **eight deliberately diverse interactive environments** — an operating system (run shell commands to accomplish a task), a database (write SQL to answer questions), a knowledge graph, a digital card game, ALFWorld (embodied household tasks), web shopping, web browsing, and lateral-thinking puzzles. AgentBench evaluated 27 different LLMs across these.

The diversity is the design insight, and it's the same one [AgentTuning](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/chatglm-to-glm-4-all-tools) used on the training side. Agency is a *transferable* competence — plan, act, read feedback, recover from errors — and you can only measure the transferable skill by testing across environments that share *no surface features*. A benchmark with one environment measures "good at that environment"; a benchmark with eight diverse ones measures "good at being an agent," because the only way to score well across all eight is to have the general skill rather than environment-specific tricks. The headline finding from AgentBench's 27-model evaluation was diagnostic: the main bottlenecks for agents were poor long-horizon reasoning, weak decision-making, and instruction-following failures — and notably, that training on code and high-quality multi-turn alignment data improved agent ability, a finding that directly informed how the GLM models were later trained.

What makes AgentBench resist gaming is that the environments are *interactive*: the model takes an action, the environment responds, and the next action depends on that response. You cannot precompute the answer, because the answer is a *trajectory* through a stateful environment, and the right trajectory depends on the environment's responses, which depend on your actions. This is fundamentally different from a static benchmark where the question and answer are fixed. For the modern agentic benchmarks that succeeded AgentBench (TAU-bench, SWE-bench, BrowseComp), the same interactive principle holds — see the [training article](/blog/machine-learning/training-techniques/training-glm-4-5-distillation-agentic-rl) for how GLM-4.5 is evaluated on them.

The scoring in an interactive benchmark is harder than it looks, and worth understanding because it's where most agentic evaluation goes wrong. In a static benchmark, scoring is trivial: compare the model's answer to the gold answer. In an interactive one, the model produces a *trajectory* — a sequence of actions and environment responses — and you have to decide whether that trajectory *succeeded*. For some environments this is clean (did the database query return the right rows? did the shell commands achieve the goal state?), but for others it requires defining success carefully (did the web-shopping agent buy the right item within budget?). AgentBench's contribution is partly the *infrastructure* for running 27 models through 8 stateful environments and scoring their trajectories consistently — which is a substantial engineering effort, not just a dataset. This is a general truth about agentic evaluation: the dataset is the easy part; the harness that runs agents in environments and scores their trajectories reliably is the hard part, and it's where the real cost lives. A team that has built that harness for evaluation has also built most of what it needs to *train* agents with RL, which is why AgentBench and the later agentic RL share so much DNA.

The diagnostic findings from AgentBench's broad evaluation also shaped the field's understanding of *why* early agents failed. It wasn't that models couldn't take individual actions — they could write a SQL query or a shell command — it was that they couldn't *sustain* coherent behavior over a long horizon: they'd lose track of the goal, fail to recover from an error, or stop following the task's constraints after a few turns. Naming these specific failure modes (long-horizon reasoning, error recovery, instruction-following under multi-turn pressure) gave the field concrete targets, and the GLM team's later training — the multi-turn function-calling RL, the agentic trajectories in mid-training — reads as a direct response to exactly the weaknesses AgentBench surfaced. This is the benchmark-shapes-training loop in action: the measurement revealed the failure modes, and the training targeted them.

## AlignBench: judging alignment without a unit test

Some capabilities can't be graded by a unit test. "Is this response helpful, safe, and well-aligned with what a Chinese user wanted?" has no executable answer — it's a judgment call. [AlignBench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.18743) (ACL 2024) is the GLM team's answer to grading that judgment reliably and at scale.

![A graph of AlignBench's rule-calibrated LLM-as-judge process: a query, the model response, a reference answer, and a rubric all feed a judge that reasons before rating](/imgs/blogs/how-zhipu-measures-glm-benchmarks-5.png)

The graph above is the grading process, and every element is there to make a subjective judgment more reliable. AlignBench has **683 real-scenario queries across 8 categories** (Chinese reasoning, Chinese language, professional knowledge, and so on). For each query, a model produces a response, and that response is graded by an **LLM judge** — but not a naive one. The judge is given two crucial pieces of calibration: a **human-verified reference answer** (so it knows what a good answer looks like) and a **per-category rubric** (so it knows what to weight for this kind of query). And critically, the judge produces a **chain-of-thought before its rating** — it reasons about the response's strengths and weaknesses first, then assigns a multi-dimensional score with an explanation.

Each of those design choices counters a known failure of LLM-as-judge:

- **The reference answer** counters the judge's own ignorance. Without a reference, the judge might not know the correct answer to a hard professional question and could rate a wrong response highly. With a human-verified reference, the judge has ground truth to compare against.
- **The per-category rubric** counters one-size-fits-all grading. What makes a good *creative writing* response is different from what makes a good *math* response; the rubric tells the judge what to weight for each category.
- **The chain-of-thought before rating** counters snap judgments. An LLM asked to immediately output a number anchors on superficial features (length, confidence); an LLM asked to reason first produces more considered, more consistent scores. The explanation also makes the judgment *auditable* — you can read why it scored what it did.

The lesson generalizes well beyond Chinese alignment: when you must use an LLM as a judge (because the capability isn't executable), calibrate it with a reference and a rubric, and make it reason before rating. A bare LLM-judge is noisy and gameable; a calibrated, reasoning one is a usable measurement instrument. For more on the pitfalls of LLM-as-judge, the blog's [evaluating conversational LLMs](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/evaluating-conversational-llms-beyond-perplexity) deep-dive covers the broader space.

It's worth being honest about the limitation that LLM-as-judge can never fully escape: the judge is itself a model, with its own biases. Judges are known to favor longer responses, to prefer the style of the model family they come from, and to be swayed by confident-sounding wrong answers. AlignBench's calibration machinery (reference, rubric, reasoning) *reduces* these biases but doesn't eliminate them, and the honest framing is that a calibrated LLM-judge is a *better-than-nothing, worse-than-execution* instrument for capabilities that have no executable answer. The reason it's still valuable is that for genuinely subjective qualities — is this Chinese response natural, helpful, appropriately formal — there is no executable ground truth, and a calibrated judge that correlates reasonably well with human preference is far cheaper and faster than human evaluation at scale. The art is knowing where on the trustworthiness spectrum your judge sits and not over-trusting it: AlignBench scores are a useful signal, not a verdict, and the GLM team treats them as one input among several rather than the single arbiter. The lesson for anyone building subjective-quality evaluation is to calibrate the judge as hard as you can *and* to remain appropriately skeptical of its absolute numbers, trusting relative comparisons (model A vs model B on the same judge) more than absolute scores.

The **8-category** structure is itself a measurement-design choice worth noting. Rather than a single "alignment" score, AlignBench reports per-category scores (Chinese reasoning, language, professional knowledge, and so on), which gives *resolution*: a model can be well-aligned on everyday chat but poorly aligned on professional knowledge, and a single aggregate score would hide that. Per-category scoring turns "the model is misaligned" into "the model is weak on professional-knowledge alignment specifically," which is actionable in a way the aggregate isn't. This is the same multi-dimensional principle that the [GLM-4 SFT rubric](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/chatglm-to-glm-4-all-tools) used (scoring safety, factuality, relevance, helpfulness separately) — resolution in your measurement gives resolution in your diagnosis.

## LongBench: measuring whether the model *uses* the long context

Having a long context window and *using* it are different things, and the difference is exactly what most long-context evaluation misses. The popular "needle in a haystack" test — hide a fact in a long document and ask the model to retrieve it — measures *retrieval*, which a model can do well while still being bad at *reasoning over* the long context.

[LongBench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.14508) was built to measure the harder thing. It's the first **bilingual, multi-task** long-context benchmark: **21 datasets across 6 task categories** (single-document QA, multi-document QA, summarization, few-shot learning, synthetic tasks, and code completion), in both English and Chinese, with average lengths around 6,700 words (English) and 13,400 characters (Chinese). The companion **LongBench-Chat** targets the chat setting specifically, with 10K–100K-token queries.

The design insight is the *multi-task* structure. A single long-context task can be gamed: a model good at retrieval aces needle-in-a-haystack while failing to summarize or reason over the document. Six task categories force the model to demonstrate genuine long-context *understanding* — to summarize (which requires reading the whole thing), to answer multi-document questions (which requires synthesizing across sources), to do few-shot learning over a long prompt (which requires using examples scattered through the context). You cannot ace all six with a single trick, which is the point. This is the evaluation-side counterpart to the [LongAlign](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/chatglm-to-glm-4-all-tools) training recipe: LongAlign teaches the model to use long context, and LongBench measures whether it actually does, and the two co-evolved (a sharper benchmark guides sharper training).

The bilingual design is also deliberate and reflects the GLM team's priorities. Long-context behavior can differ by language — tokenization, document structure, and the available training data all vary — so a benchmark that only measures English long-context tells you nothing about Chinese long-context. For a bilingual model, you need a bilingual ruler.

The "lost in the middle" pathology is the specific failure that multi-task long-context evaluation catches and needle-in-a-haystack misses. Models with long context windows frequently attend strongly to the *beginning* and *end* of a long input while under-weighting the *middle* — so a fact placed at position 60,000 of a 128,000-token context gets ignored even though the window technically reaches it. A needle test that always places the needle in retrievable positions won't catch this; a summarization task (which requires integrating the *whole* document, middle included) will, because a model that ignores the middle produces a summary that omits it. So LongBench's task diversity isn't just about resisting gaming — it's about probing the *specific ways* long-context understanding fails, which a single task can't do. The code-completion task in particular tests something distinctive: using a long context of surrounding code to complete a function correctly, which is the long-context capability that matters most for coding agents working in large repositories. The lesson is that "long context" is not one capability but several (retrieval, integration, reasoning, in-context learning over long inputs), and a benchmark that measures only one of them will mislead you about the others.

There's also a practical scoring subtlety in long-context evaluation: the tasks have to be ones where the long context is *necessary*, not just present. If you can answer a question from general knowledge without reading the document, the benchmark measures knowledge, not long-context use. LongBench's tasks are constructed so the answer genuinely depends on the provided context — multi-document QA requires synthesizing the specific documents given, summarization requires the specific document, and so on. This "the context must be load-bearing" property is what makes it a *long-context* benchmark rather than a knowledge benchmark that happens to have long inputs, and it's a subtle design requirement that's easy to get wrong.

## NaturalCodeBench: when the toy benchmark lies

The clearest case of a decayed proxy is in code. HumanEval — write a function from its docstring — was a reasonable measure of coding ability in 2021. By 2024 it had two problems: models had largely saturated it, and (more importantly) high HumanEval scores stopped predicting real-world coding performance, because real coding is nothing like writing a clean self-contained function from a clean docstring.

![A before-after contrasting toy HumanEval-style benchmarks with NaturalCodeBench's real-world queries graded by Docker execution](/imgs/blogs/how-zhipu-measures-glm-benchmarks-3.png)

The before-after above is the contrast. HumanEval-style problems are *clean and self-contained* — a single function, a precise docstring, a tidy test. Real user queries are *messy* — ambiguous requirements, multiple files, domain context, the need to ask clarifying questions. [NaturalCodeBench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.04520) (Findings of ACL 2024) closes this gap by drawing its **402 problems from natural user queries** (not invented toy problems), in **both Python and Java**, across 6 domains, and grading them by **executing the code in Docker** against test cases.

Three design choices make NaturalCodeBench a better predictor of real coding ability:

- **Natural queries.** The problems come from how people actually ask for code — with the ambiguity, context, and messiness of real requests — rather than from clean invented specs. A model that aces clean specs but flounders on ambiguous real requests will score lower here, which is correct, because that model *is* worse at real coding.
- **Two languages.** Python-only benchmarks reward Python-specialized models; including Java tests whether the coding ability generalizes. For a general assistant, multilingual coding is the relevant capability.
- **Docker execution.** The code is *run* against test cases, not pattern-matched against a reference solution. Execution is the least gameable grading method in code: the code either passes the tests or it doesn't, and there's no partial credit for code that looks right but doesn't work.

NaturalCodeBench also handles contamination deliberately: it's split into **140 open (dev) problems and 262 closed (test) problems**, so the test set can stay uncontaminated even after the dev set is public. This is the contamination-aware design that the next section is about.

The "natural queries" sourcing deserves emphasis because it's the part most benchmarks skip and the part that most determines real-world predictiveness. Inventing coding problems is easy, and invented problems tend to be clean, unambiguous, and solvable in one shot — which is exactly *unlike* how real users ask for code. Real requests are underspecified ("make this faster"), contextual ("add a column to the users table and update the API"), and often require judgment about what the user actually wants. By sourcing from natural queries, NaturalCodeBench measures the model's ability to handle that messiness, which is the capability that actually matters when the model is deployed. The cost is that natural queries are harder to turn into clean test cases — you have to construct tests for an ambiguous request, which requires deciding what "correct" means — and that construction effort is the price of a benchmark that predicts real performance. The lesson generalizes to any capability: if you want your benchmark to predict real-world performance, source your tasks from real-world usage, even though invented tasks are easier to construct. The gap between "scores well on invented tasks" and "works for real users" is exactly the gap that natural sourcing closes.

## The meta-lessons: what good evaluation discipline looks like

Stepping back from the individual benchmarks, the GLM team's evaluation methodology embodies a few principles worth lifting wholesale.

**Build the ruler when the capability is new.** This is the headline meta-technique. If you're optimizing a capability that no public benchmark measures well — because it's new, or because the existing benchmarks have decayed — then building the benchmark is part of the deliverable, not an afterthought. You cannot improve what you cannot measure, and for genuinely novel capabilities the measurement often doesn't exist. The GLM team built AgentBench *before* agentic models were common precisely because they were going to optimize agency and needed a way to track it. The benchmark and the model co-evolve: a sharper benchmark produces sharper training, which is why the measurement is upstream of the model, not downstream.

**Prefer hard-to-fake signals.** Across all five benchmarks, the grading method is chosen to resist gaming: live environments (AgentBench), executed tests (NaturalCodeBench, HumanEval-X), diverse task suites (LongBench), calibrated reasoning judges (AlignBench). The ordering of trustworthiness is roughly: executed outcome > live interaction > diverse-task aggregate > calibrated LLM-judge > naive LLM-judge > string match. Push your evaluation as far up that ordering as the capability allows. For code and math, you can execute; for agency, you can interact; for subjective quality, you're stuck with judges, so calibrate them as hard as you can. A useful way to operationalize this when designing an evaluation is to ask, for your capability: what is the *most checkable* form of evidence I can extract? If the model writes code, run it. If the model takes actions, put it in an environment and check the outcome. If the model answers a factual question, check against a verified reference. Only when none of those is available should you fall back to a judge, and even then, calibrate the judge with whatever ground truth you do have. The discipline of always reaching for the most checkable signal available is what separates evaluation you can trust from evaluation that flatters.

**Decontaminate, and design for it.** The NaturalCodeBench dev/test split is a deliberate contamination defense: by keeping a test set closed, you preserve a clean measurement even after the rest is public and potentially scraped into training data. The broader GLM data pipeline does explicit benchmark *decontamination* — removing benchmark data from training so the scores reflect generalization, not memorization. Contamination is the silent killer of benchmark validity; a model that memorized the test set scores high and means nothing. Designing benchmarks (closed test sets) and pipelines (decontamination) against it is what keeps the numbers honest.

Contamination is worth dwelling on because it's both rampant and under-discussed. Modern models train on trillions of tokens scraped from the web, and public benchmarks *are* on the web — so unless you actively remove them, your training set contains your test set, and your scores measure memorization rather than capability. The defenses operate at two levels. At the *benchmark* level, keeping a test set closed (like NaturalCodeBench's 262 closed problems) means it was never public to be scraped, so even a model trained on everything public hasn't seen it. At the *pipeline* level, explicit decontamination scans the training corpus for benchmark data (by n-gram overlap or embedding similarity) and removes it before training. The GLM data pipeline does the latter, which is why its reported scores can be trusted as generalization rather than recall. The uncomfortable truth is that many headline benchmark numbers across the field are contaminated to some unknown degree, which is part of why execution-based and closed-test-set benchmarks are so valuable — they're much harder to contaminate, because you can't memorize the *output* of running code you've never seen, and you can't memorize a test set that was never released. Be more skeptical of high scores on old, fully-public, string-matched benchmarks, and more trusting of scores on executed or interactive ones.

**Measure the capability you ship, not the one that markets.** A revealing signal in any model report is *which* benchmarks the team foregrounds. The GLM reports lean on agency (AgentBench), alignment (AlignBench), long context (LongBench), and real-world code (NaturalCodeBench) — the capabilities a deployed assistant lives or dies on — rather than over-indexing on the trivia and contest-math benchmarks that dominate leaderboard marketing. The benchmarks a team builds and foregrounds tell you what they actually optimized for, and a team that builds rulers for agency and real-world code is telling you it's building a product, not a leaderboard entry.

## When to build your own benchmark (and when not to)

**Build your own benchmark when:**

- **You're optimizing a capability no public benchmark captures.** New capabilities (agency in 2023, long-context use, real-world coding) often have no good ruler. If you can't measure what you're improving, you can't improve it reliably — build the ruler first.
- **The standard benchmark has decayed.** If everyone scores 95%+ on a benchmark, or if high scores no longer predict the real capability, the proxy has saturated or diverged. A new benchmark resets it.
- **Your capability has a hard-to-fake grading method available.** If you can execute, interact, or check an outcome, a custom benchmark using that method will be far more trustworthy than a string-match proxy.

**Don't build your own when:**

- **A good public benchmark already measures your capability.** Don't reinvent MMLU. Custom benchmarks are expensive to build and validate, and a well-established public one has the advantage of comparability across models.
- **You'd be tempted to grade your own homework.** A benchmark you build and report on yourself invites the suspicion that it's tuned to flatter your model. The GLM benchmarks largely escape this because they're *published* (others can run them) and *execution-based* (hard to tune in your favor) — but a private, subjective, self-reported benchmark is a red flag, and you should hold your own to the same standard.
- **You can't maintain it.** A benchmark needs a clean test set, contamination defenses, and updates as models improve. If you can't commit to maintaining it, a one-off custom benchmark will decay faster than a maintained public one.

The unifying lesson is that evaluation is not a thing you do *to* a finished model; it's a thing you build *alongside* the model, because the measurement shapes the training. The GLM team's willingness to build AgentBench, AlignBench, LongBench, and NaturalCodeBench — and to publish them so others could use and scrutinize them — is the same open, empirical discipline that runs through the whole [GLM lineage](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/glm-lineage-frontier-llm-technique). You measure what you mean to improve, you measure it in a way that's hard to fake, and you keep the ruler honest. There's a final, almost cultural point worth making. A lab that builds its own benchmarks is making a statement about what it values: it's saying "we care enough about this capability to invest in measuring it properly, rather than borrowing a proxy and hoping." That investment — the dataset construction, the execution harness, the contamination defenses, the maintenance — is real and unglamorous, and most labs would rather spend the effort on the model. The GLM team's repeated willingness to spend it on benchmarks is part of why the lineage's reported numbers are credible and why its training (which uses these same measurement assets as reward signals) is effective. Evaluation and training are not separate disciplines with a handoff between them; they're two faces of the same underlying capability — knowing, reliably and automatically, whether an output is good. Build that capability well and you get both a trustworthy benchmark and a trustworthy reward; build it poorly and you get neither. The final article in this series turns to the vision branch, where the same evaluation instincts extend to multimodal reasoning, and where building the right verifiable rewards across many visual domains is again the crux.
