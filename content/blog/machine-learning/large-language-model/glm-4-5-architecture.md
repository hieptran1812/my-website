By 2025 the open Mixture-of-Experts recipe had a default shape, and DeepSeek-V3 was its reference implementation: very wide, hundreds of experts, lots of attention heads, sparse activation. The easy thing for GLM-4.5 to do would have been to copy it. Instead, the [GLM-4.5 technical report](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.06471) describes a model that goes almost deliberately *against* that template — deeper and narrower, with *fewer* experts but *2.5× more attention heads per unit of width* — and backs the choice with one of the most quietly subversive empirical findings in recent model reports: the extra heads didn't lower training loss at all, but they consistently raised reasoning benchmarks.

This is the fourth stage of the [GLM lineage series](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/glm-lineage-frontier-llm-technique), and the first to reach the frontier MoE era. The [previous article](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/chatglm-to-glm-4-all-tools) covered the alignment story of GLM-4; this one is purely about the GLM-4.5 *architecture* — the shape of the network and the optimizer that trains it. (The training *pipeline* — expert distillation, agentic RL, the slime infrastructure — is the subject of the next article. There is also an existing [paper-reading of the full GLM-4.5 report](/blog/paper-reading/large-language-model/glm-4-5-agentic-reasoning-and-coding-arc-foundation-models) on this blog; this article is the architecture-focused companion to it.)

The reason the architecture deserves its own deep dive is that almost every choice in it is a *bet against the consensus*, made for a stated reason, and the reasons are the transferable part. A model's specific numbers — 355B parameters, 89 layers, 160 experts — are tuned to its scale and won't port to your model, but the *reasoning* behind each choice (depth for compositional reasoning, a controller instead of a balance loss, a draft head for cheap speculative decoding) is method that applies at any scale. Read this article for the reasons, not the numbers — the numbers are a snapshot of one team's instantiation, but the reasons are the durable engineering knowledge. Let's take them one at a time.

## Why "just copy DeepSeek-V3" was the wrong move

The consensus MoE recipe in 2025 was well-established, and copying it would have been defensible. GLM-4.5's report is a sustained argument for departing from it on specific axes. Here is the mismatch:

| Assumption | The consensus (DeepSeek-V3-style) | GLM-4.5's departure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| "Wider is better" | Large hidden dim, hundreds of experts | Narrower hidden dim (5120 vs 7168), fewer experts (160 vs 256) |
| "Depth is expensive, keep it moderate" | ~58 MoE layers | 89 MoE layers — deeper, because depth buys reasoning |
| "Match heads to width" | 128 heads at 7168 hidden | 96 heads at 5120 hidden — 2.5× more heads per width |
| "Balance experts with an auxiliary loss" | Aux-loss load balancing | Loss-free, bias-based balancing (no gradient fight) |
| "AdamW is the optimizer" | AdamW | Muon — faster convergence, larger batches |
| "WSD is the modern schedule" | Warmup-stable-decay | Cosine decay — WSD underfit on SimpleQA/MMLU |

Every row is a section. The unifying thesis is that GLM-4.5 is a *reasoning-first* architecture, optimized for downstream reasoning rather than for the training-loss curve — and several of its choices look wrong if you only watch the loss.

It's worth pausing on why "reasoning-first" is even a meaningful design stance, because it wasn't always. For the first few generations of large models, the objective was broad capability — be good at everything — and the architecture choices followed the scaling laws, which are stated in terms of loss. But by 2025 the frontier had shifted: the models that mattered were the ones that could *reason* (multi-step math, code, agentic planning), and reasoning turned out to be a capability that the loss-based scaling laws don't directly predict. A model can be loss-optimal and a mediocre reasoner, or slightly loss-suboptimal and a strong one. Once you internalize that, "reasoning-first" becomes a real design philosophy: it means making architecture choices that serve reasoning *even when they're neutral or negative on loss*, which is precisely the inversion of the loss-driven scaling-law approach. GLM-4.5 is one of the clearest examples of a model designed under this philosophy, and reading its architecture is partly an education in what "designing for reasoning" concretely means at the level of layers, heads, and routing.

> The most dangerous thing you can optimize is the metric you can see. GLM-4.5's architecture is a series of decisions that the visible metric (training loss) would have rejected, made because the team trusted the invisible one (reasoning) more.

## The mental model: one block, three deliberate choices

![A layered stack of one GLM-4.5 transformer block, from the input through GQA attention with QK-Norm to the MoE FFN and the MTP head](/imgs/blogs/glm-4-5-architecture-1.png)

The stack above is one GLM-4.5 transformer block, and it's the mental model for the whole architecture. Most of it is the modern-transformer consensus — RMSNorm, residual connections, rotary positions — and that conventionality is deliberate, so the novelty budget concentrates on three choices:

1. **GQA attention with QK-Norm** — grouped-query attention (96 query heads, 8 KV heads) for a small KV cache, plus a normalization on the query and key projections to keep attention logits in range. This is where the "more heads" bet lives.
2. **A sparse MoE FFN** — instead of a dense feed-forward network, each token is routed to its top-8 of 160 experts, plus one shared expert that always runs. This is where the "deep-narrow, fewer-experts" bet lives.
3. **An MTP head** — a multi-token-prediction layer added for speculative decoding at inference. This is an architecture choice made for *serving*, baked in at training.

The rest of this article is a tour of those three choices plus the optimizer, with the empirical justifications the report gives for each.

Notice what's *not* in the novelty list: the normalization (RMSNorm), the activation (SwiGLU), the positional scheme (rotary), the residual structure. These are the modern consensus, unchanged, and that's intentional. A model that innovates on everything at once produces results you can't attribute — if the model is good, was it the new normalization, the new activation, the new attention, or the new objective? By holding the boring parts fixed at the field's best-known defaults, GLM-4.5 ensures that any gain or regression traces to one of its few deliberate departures. This is the same "concentrate the novelty" discipline that makes the whole [GLM lineage](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/glm-lineage-frontier-llm-technique) legible, and it's worth internalizing as a research method: the value of a model report is proportional to how *attributable* its results are, and attribution requires keeping most things constant.

## 1. The deep-narrow bet

> **Senior rule of thumb:** at a fixed parameter budget, *how* you spend the budget — on width, depth, experts, or heads — is a set of independent bets, and the loss curve is a misleading guide to several of them.

![A before-after comparing DeepSeek-V3's wide design with GLM-4.5's deep-narrow design](/imgs/blogs/glm-4-5-architecture-2.png)

The before-after above contrasts the two philosophies. DeepSeek-V3 is *wide*: hidden dimension 7168, 256 routed experts, 58 MoE layers, 128 attention heads (with 128 KV heads). GLM-4.5 is *deep-narrow*: hidden dimension 5120, 160 experts, but **89 MoE layers** and 96 attention heads with only 8 KV heads. The report's stated reasoning is direct:

> We reduce the width (hidden dimension and number of routed experts) of the model and increase its height (number of layers), as we found that deeper models exhibited better reasoning capacity.

This is a real, falsifiable claim about the relationship between depth and reasoning, and it cuts against the grain of a field that had been scaling width because width parallelizes more cleanly. Depth is *harder* to train (more layers means more chances for the gradient to vanish or explode, more sequential dependency in the pipeline) and *harder* to serve (more sequential layers means higher latency per token). GLM-4.5 paid both costs because the team's experiments said depth bought reasoning. The intuition, though the report doesn't fully formalize it, is that reasoning is a *sequential composition* problem — multi-step inference is literally a chain of transformations — and a deeper network has more sequential transformation steps to compose, while a wider network has more parallel capacity that doesn't directly add compositional depth.

![A matrix of the GLM-4.5 architecture spec versus GLM-4.5-Air and DeepSeek-V3](/imgs/blogs/glm-4-5-architecture-3.png)

The matrix above is the full spec, with the Air variant for contrast. **GLM-4.5** is 355B total parameters, 32B activated per token; **GLM-4.5-Air** is 106B total, 12B activated — same recipe, roughly a third the scale. Both use top-8 routing over their experts plus one shared expert. Note the activation ratio: GLM-4.5 activates 32B of 355B, about 9% — that sparsity is what makes a 355B model affordable to serve, because you only pay the FLOPs for the 32B you activate. This is the fundamental bargain of MoE: you get the *knowledge capacity* of a 355B model (all those experts store distinct knowledge and skills) at the *compute cost* of a 32B model (you only run 8 of 160 experts per token, plus the shared one). The catch — and it's a real one — is that you pay the *memory* cost of all 355B, because every expert must be resident in GPU memory even though most are idle for any given token. So MoE trades memory for compute: you spend more memory (store everything) to spend less compute (run little). Whether that's a good trade depends on your bottleneck. If you're compute-bound (lots of tokens, limited FLOPs), MoE is a huge win. If you're memory-bound (limited GPU memory), MoE's large total-parameter footprint is a liability, which is exactly why GLM-4.5's *lower* total parameter count (355B vs DeepSeek's 671B) matters — it eases the memory side of the MoE bargain. The comparison column shows the departure from DeepSeek-V3 clearly: GLM-4.5 has *fewer* total parameters (355B vs 671B), *fewer* active parameters (32B vs 37B), *fewer* experts (160 vs 256), and a *narrower* hidden dim (5120 vs 7168) — yet it's competitive with or better than DeepSeek-R1 on reasoning. Doing more with a smaller, deeper, head-richer model is the whole bet, and the results section shows it paid off.

One more spec worth flagging: GLM-4.5 uses **QK-Norm** and GLM-4.5-Air does *not*. This is a reminder that these are *configuration* choices, not laws — the same team, building two models in the same family, made different normalization choices, presumably because the larger model's attention logits needed the extra stabilization more. When you read a spec table, the *differences* between sibling models tell you which choices are scale-dependent.

### The parameter budget as a portfolio

The deepest way to understand the deep-narrow bet is to think of a fixed parameter budget as a *portfolio* you allocate across four assets: width (hidden dimension), depth (number of layers), expert count, and attention heads. Every model with the same total parameters has made an implicit allocation across these four, and the allocations are *not* interchangeable — each asset buys a different capability with a different cost. Width buys raw representational capacity per layer and parallelizes cleanly (a wide matmul is one big efficient GPU operation). Depth buys *compositional* capacity — more sequential transformation steps — but is harder to train (more layers for gradients to traverse) and slower to serve (more sequential dependency). Expert count buys *specialization* capacity in the MoE FFN but adds routing complexity and load-balancing risk. Attention heads buy *parallel attention patterns* per layer.

DeepSeek-V3's portfolio is width-and-expert-heavy; GLM-4.5's is depth-and-head-heavy. Neither is universally correct — they're different bets about which capability matters for the target. GLM-4.5's bet is that *reasoning* is the binding capability for a frontier agent, and that reasoning is served by depth (compositional steps) and heads (parallel attention patterns) more than by width (per-layer capacity) or sheer expert count. The report's evidence is the more-heads finding (heads help reasoning, not loss) and the stated depth-reasoning relationship. The reason this framing matters for a practitioner is that it tells you *how to think* about your own architecture: don't ask "should I make it bigger," ask "given my fixed budget, which of the four assets does my target capability most reward, and am I over-allocated to the easy-to-scale ones (width) at the expense of the capability-relevant ones (depth, heads)?" Most teams over-allocate to width because it's the easiest to scale and the friendliest to the loss curve — which is exactly the trap GLM-4.5 avoided.

### What deep-narrow costs you

It would be dishonest to present the deep-narrow bet as free. Depth has two real costs the report accepts. The first is **training stability**: a 89-layer network has 89 layers for gradients to vanish or explode through, which is why the careful normalization (RMSNorm everywhere, QK-Norm on the flagship's attention) and the Muon optimizer matter more here than they would for a shallower model — the stability machinery is partly *paying* for the depth. The second is **inference latency**: generating one token requires a forward pass through all 89 layers *sequentially* — you can't parallelize across layers within a single token's generation — so a deeper model has higher per-token latency than a wider-shallower one with the same parameter count. The MTP head (speculative decoding) is partly a *response* to this latency cost: by drafting multiple tokens per main-model pass, it amortizes the deep sequential cost over several tokens. So the architecture is internally coherent in a way that's easy to miss: the depth bet *creates* a latency problem, and the MTP head *solves* it, and the QK-Norm and Muon choices *pay for* the stability the depth demands. The choices aren't independent; they're a system designed around the central bet on depth. This systemic coherence is the mark of a well-designed architecture as opposed to a checklist of individually-fashionable features. A weaker model report reads like "we used GQA (everyone does), and MoE (everyone does), and MTP (it's trendy), and Muon (it's new)" — a pile of features with no through-line. GLM-4.5's choices, read carefully, are *causally connected*: the depth bet drives the latency problem that MTP solves and the stability demand that QK-Norm and Muon meet, while the narrowness and fewer-experts drive the memory efficiency that the lower total-parameter count delivers. When you evaluate an architecture — your own or someone else's — the question isn't "does it have the fashionable features" but "do the choices form a coherent system around a central thesis." GLM-4.5's thesis is *depth for reasoning*, and every other choice either serves it or pays for it. For how GLM-4.5's MoE compares to the broader landscape, see [modern LLM architectures](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/modern-llm-architectures-qwen-llama-gemma-deepseek) and the blog's [MoE architecture deep-dive](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/moe-llm-architecture-training-finetuning-case-studies).

## 2. MoE routing and loss-free load balancing

> **Senior rule of thumb:** in a sparse MoE, the router is the whole game. Get load balancing wrong and either your experts collapse (a few do all the work) or your balancing loss degrades quality. The art is balancing load *without* a gradient that fights your objective.

![A graph of GLM-4.5 MoE routing: a sigmoid-gated router sends each token to its top-8 of 160 experts plus a shared expert](/imgs/blogs/glm-4-5-architecture-4.png)

The graph above is the routing mechanism. Each token's hidden state goes two places: to the **router**, which computes a sigmoid gate score for each of the 160 experts and selects the **top-8**, and to the **shared expert**, which *always* runs. The outputs of the 8 routed experts and the shared expert are combined into the block's output; the other 152 experts are skipped for that token. The shared expert is a cheap, important idea: it absorbs the *common* computation that every token needs, which frees the routed experts to *specialize* rather than each having to re-learn the basics. Without a shared expert, every routed expert wastes capacity on common patterns; with one, the routed experts can be genuinely specialized.

The hard problem is **load balancing**. If the router is left to its own devices, it tends to collapse: a few popular experts get most of the tokens (and most of the gradient, reinforcing their popularity) while others starve and never learn. The classic fix is an *auxiliary loss* that penalizes imbalance — but that auxiliary loss is a second objective pulling against the language-modeling objective, and it measurably degrades quality. GLM-4.5 uses the **loss-free** alternative (the aux-loss-free approach popularized by DeepSeek).

![A before-after contrasting auxiliary-loss balancing with GLM-4.5's loss-free bias controller](/imgs/blogs/glm-4-5-architecture-5.png)

The before-after above shows the difference, and it's the cleanest example in the architecture of a *control-theory* mindset replacing a *loss* mindset. Instead of adding a balance term to the objective, you maintain a per-expert **bias** that's added to the gate scores *before* the top-8 selection — but is *not* used to weight the expert's output. Under-loaded experts get a positive bias nudge (making them more likely to be selected); over-loaded experts get a negative nudge. The bias is a thermostat: it steers routing toward balance without ever appearing in the backward pass of the main loss. Here it is in code:

```python
def loss_free_route(hidden, gate_w, bias, k=8, lr=1e-3):
    """Loss-free MoE routing. `bias` steers SELECTION but not the OUTPUT weight,
    so it never enters the language-modeling gradient."""
    scores = torch.sigmoid(hidden @ gate_w)         # per-expert gate in [0,1]
    topk = torch.topk(scores + bias, k, dim=-1)     # bias shifts SELECTION only
    idx = topk.indices                              # which experts run
    weights = scores.gather(-1, idx)                # OUTPUT weight uses raw score
    weights = weights / weights.sum(-1, keepdim=True)
    return idx, weights

def update_bias(load_counts, bias, target, lr=1e-3):
    """Thermostat: nudge under-loaded experts up, over-loaded down. Sign-based,
    so it's a controller, not a loss term. lr=1e-3 for first 15T tokens, then 0."""
    return bias + lr * torch.sign(target - load_counts)
```

The two-line distinction is everything: `topk(scores + bias)` uses the bias to *choose* experts, but `weights = scores.gather(...)` weights their outputs by the *raw* score, not the biased one. So the bias affects *which* experts run (the discrete routing decision, which has no gradient anyway) but not *how much* their outputs count (which does have a gradient). The result is balanced load with zero interference to the language objective. GLM-4.5 runs the bias update at `lr = 0.001` for the first 15T tokens, then freezes it to 0 (by then the experts have specialized and the router is stable), and keeps a tiny **sequence-level auxiliary balance loss weighted 1e-4** as a safety net against per-sequence imbalance. The 1e-4 weight is small enough not to meaningfully fight the objective, but present enough to catch the pathological case the bias controller alone might miss.

### Why expert collapse happens, and why the bias fixes it

To appreciate the bias controller you have to understand the failure it prevents, which is a *positive feedback loop*. Early in training the router's gate scores are nearly random, so by chance a few experts get slightly more tokens. Those experts receive more gradient (they're processing more tokens), so they improve faster, so the router learns to prefer them, so they get even more tokens — a rich-get-richer spiral that ends with a handful of experts doing all the work and the rest dead. This is **expert collapse**, and it's catastrophic because a 160-expert model that effectively uses 12 experts has thrown away most of its capacity. The auxiliary-loss fix breaks the loop by adding a penalty for imbalance, but at the cost of a gradient that fights the language objective on every step. The bias controller breaks the same loop *without* that cost: when an expert gets over-loaded, its bias is nudged *down*, making it less likely to be selected next step, which redistributes tokens to the starving experts and lets them catch up. It's negative feedback (a thermostat) deliberately introduced to cancel the positive feedback (the collapse spiral). Control theory has a name for this — it's exactly what a feedback controller does, sensing the state (expert load), comparing to a setpoint (balanced load), and applying a correction (the bias nudge) proportional to the error's sign. Framing MoE load balancing as a control problem rather than a loss problem is the conceptual move that unlocks the loss-free approach, and it's a reminder that not every constraint in a deep-learning system has to be expressed as a differentiable loss. Some constraints are better handled by classical control: a sensor, a setpoint, and an actuator that operates outside the gradient. The teams that can move fluidly between "express it as a loss" and "express it as a controller" have a strictly larger toolbox than those who reflexively reach for a loss term every time.

The elegance is in *where* the bias acts. Routing — the discrete choice of which top-8 experts to run — has no gradient anyway (you can't backpropagate through a `topk`), so adding a bias to the *selection* is free from the optimizer's perspective. The output weights, which *do* have a gradient (they scale the experts' contributions to the loss), use the *raw* gate scores, untouched by the bias. So the bias influences exactly the part of routing that the gradient can't see, and leaves untouched the part the gradient does see. That's why it's "loss-free": it operates entirely in the gradient's blind spot. The schedule — `lr=1e-3` for the first 15T tokens, then 0 — reflects that balancing is an *early-training* problem: once the experts have specialized and the router has learned a stable assignment, you can freeze the bias and let the now-balanced system run. Freezing it also removes a tiny source of training noise late in the run. For the broader MoE training picture, the blog's [optimizing MoE training and inference](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/optimizing-moe-training-and-inference) deep-dive is the companion.

### Sigmoid gates, and why not softmax

A subtle choice in the router is the **sigmoid** gate rather than the more common softmax. With softmax routing, the gate scores for all experts are coupled — they must sum to 1, so raising one lowers the others, which creates a competition that can amplify the collapse dynamics. With sigmoid gates, each expert's score is *independent* — `sigmoid(score)` for each expert separately — so the router can express "this token strongly needs experts 3, 17, and 92" without those scores having to trade off against each other. The independence makes the gate scores cleaner signals of how much each expert is actually needed, and it composes naturally with the additive bias (you add the bias to an independent score, not to a coupled distribution). It's a small change with a real effect on routing stability, and it's part of why the loss-free approach works as cleanly as it does.

## 3. The more-heads finding

> **Senior rule of thumb:** when a change helps a downstream metric but not the loss, the loss is lying to you about what matters. Trust the downstream metric — but only if you measured it.

This is the single most quotable result in the architecture, and it's a direct warning against optimizing the proxy instead of the goal.

![A matrix of the more-heads finding: adding attention heads left training loss flat but raised MMLU and BBH](/imgs/blogs/glm-4-5-architecture-6.png)

The matrix above is the finding. GLM-4.5 uses 96 attention heads on a 5120-wide model — about 2.5× the heads-per-width of comparable models. The report is blunt about what the extra heads did and didn't do:

> While this increased head count does not improve training loss compared to models with fewer heads, it consistently improves performance on reasoning benchmarks such as MMLU and BBH.

Read the matrix column by column. The "fewer heads" column is the baseline. The "2.5× heads" column shows training loss **flat** (no change) but MMLU and BBH both **higher**. The decision row is the punchline: if you ranked architecture variants by *training loss*, you would *drop* the extra heads — they cost parameters and FLOPs for no loss improvement. If you rank by *reasoning*, you *keep* them. The team kept them, and the matter-of-fact way the report states this is itself instructive — there's no agonizing, no lengthy justification, just "loss flat, reasoning up, we kept the heads." That brevity signals a team for whom "optimize the capability, not the metric" is settled doctrine rather than a debate.

This is worth sitting with because it indicts a standard practice. Most architecture search is ranked by validation loss (or perplexity), because loss is cheap to measure and you can sweep many variants. But loss is *blind* to some of the things you actually care about — and the extra attention heads are exactly such a thing. Why would more heads help reasoning without helping loss? The likely mechanism: more heads means more distinct attention patterns the model can use *in parallel* at each layer, which gives it more ways to route information for multi-step reasoning, even though the average next-token prediction (what loss measures) doesn't improve. Loss measures the *average* token; reasoning depends on the *hard* tokens where having an extra attention pattern available is the difference between getting the step right or wrong, and those hard tokens are rare enough not to move the average loss. The transferable practice: **when you can afford it, evaluate architecture variants on downstream tasks, not just loss, because the loss curve systematically discards changes like this one.** The cost is that downstream eval is more expensive than loss; the benefit is that you don't throw away your reasoning gains.

### The general failure of loss as a proxy

The more-heads finding is a specific instance of a general and under-appreciated problem: **loss is a proxy, and proxies fail in predictable directions.** Training loss measures average next-token prediction quality over your training distribution. But the things you actually want — reasoning, instruction-following, factuality, agentic competence — are *functions* of next-token prediction, not next-token prediction itself, and the mapping is non-linear. A change can improve the average token (lower loss) while hurting the hard tokens that reasoning depends on, or — as with the extra heads — leave the average untouched while improving the hard tokens. Either way, ranking by loss gives you the wrong answer about reasoning. This is why the most capable labs have largely moved to *downstream-eval-in-the-loop* for their important architecture and data decisions, accepting the higher cost of running benchmarks because the loss-based shortcut systematically misleads. The discipline GLM-4.5 demonstrates is to *know which decisions loss can be trusted for and which it can't*: loss is fine for "is this run healthy, is it converging," but for "does this architectural change help the capability I care about," you need the downstream measurement. The teams that conflate these — using loss as the sole arbiter of everything — ship models that are loss-optimal and capability-suboptimal, and they never know what they left on the table because the metric they trusted never showed it to them.

There's a deeper, almost philosophical point here that the report gestures at: the more-heads result implies that *some capabilities are not visible in the training objective at all*. If extra heads help reasoning without touching loss, then the model is using those heads for something the loss doesn't reward — which means the loss is not a complete description of what the model is learning. This is humbling, because it means you cannot fully understand a model's capabilities by watching it train; you have to *test* it on the capabilities you care about, because some of them are invisible to the thing you're optimizing. It's a strong argument for rich, capability-specific evaluation as a first-class part of model development, not an afterthought run once at the end.

## 4. QK-Norm, partial RoPE, and the context extension

The attention block has two more choices worth understanding, both about keeping the attention numerically well-behaved and extensible.

**QK-Norm** applies a normalization (RMSNorm) to the query and key projections before the attention dot product. The problem it solves is the same one [GLM-130B](/blog/machine-learning/mlops/engineering-glm-130b) fought with FP32 softmax: as the model scales, the pre-softmax attention logits can grow large enough to cause numerical instability (and, in extreme cases, attention collapse where one token dominates). Normalizing the queries and keys bounds the magnitude of their dot product, keeping the logits in a sane range. It's the modern, cleaner descendant of GLM-130B's FP32-softmax hack — same disease (attention logit range), better-engineered cure (normalize the inputs to the dot product rather than upcast the output). The fact that GLM-4.5 uses it and GLM-4.5-Air doesn't tells you it's a stabilization that the larger model needs more, where the logit magnitudes are larger. (Compare Kimi K2's QK-Clip, which *clips* the logits rather than normalizing the inputs — same problem, different prescription, as the [survey](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/glm-lineage-frontier-llm-technique) notes.)

**Partial RoPE** applies rotary position embeddings to only a *fraction* of each head's dimensions, leaving the rest position-independent. The intuition is that not every dimension needs to encode position — some should carry position-invariant semantic content — and applying RoPE to only part of the head gives the model both. It also interacts cleanly with context extension: when GLM-4.5 extends context (to 32K, then 128K in mid-training), it raises the **RoPE base frequency from 10,000 to 1,000,000**. The base frequency controls how fast the rotary phase rotates with position; a larger base means slower rotation, which means the model can distinguish positions that are far apart without the phases wrapping around and aliasing. Raising the base is the standard knob for context extension, and GLM-4.5 turns it up by 100× to reach long context. The combination — GQA for a small KV cache, partial RoPE for extensible positions, QK-Norm for stable logits — is the attention design that makes GLM-4.5's long context affordable and stable.

To make QK-Norm concrete, here is the operation it inserts into the attention computation:

```python
def attention_with_qk_norm(x, wq, wk, wv, q_norm, k_norm, n_heads, n_kv_heads):
    q = (x @ wq).view(*x.shape[:-1], n_heads, -1)
    k = (x @ wk).view(*x.shape[:-1], n_kv_heads, -1)
    v = (x @ wv).view(*x.shape[:-1], n_kv_heads, -1)
    q = q_norm(q)                       # RMSNorm on the query projection
    k = k_norm(k)                       # RMSNorm on the key projection  <-- QK-Norm
    q, k = apply_partial_rope(q, k)     # rotary on a fraction of dims
    k, v = repeat_kv_for_gqa(k, v, n_heads // n_kv_heads)  # 8 KV -> 96 query
    scores = (q @ k.transpose(-1, -2)) * (q.shape[-1] ** -0.5)
    return torch.softmax(scores, dim=-1) @ v
```

The two `q_norm`/`k_norm` lines are the entire intervention, and their placement matters: they normalize *before* the RoPE rotation and the dot product, so the magnitudes entering the dot product are bounded regardless of how large the raw projections grew. The reason this works where simply scaling the logits wouldn't is that it controls the *inputs* to the dot product rather than patching its *output* — a bounded query dotted with a bounded key produces a bounded logit by construction, so there's no large value to overflow or to dominate the softmax in the first place. It's prevention rather than treatment, which is why it's cleaner than GLM-130B's FP32-softmax (treatment) even though both target the same disease. There's also a cost difference worth noting: FP32 softmax upcasts a (potentially large) attention matrix to FP32 for the reduction, which costs memory bandwidth on every attention operation; QK-Norm adds two small RMSNorm operations on the (much smaller) query and key projections, which is cheaper. So QK-Norm is not only cleaner conceptually but also lighter computationally — prevention at the input is both more robust and less expensive than treatment at the output. This is a general pattern in numerical-stability engineering: it's almost always cheaper to keep values in range than to handle out-of-range values after the fact, just as it's cheaper to prevent a bug than to debug a crash. The progression from FP32-softmax to QK-Norm across the GLM lineage is the team internalizing that lesson and moving the fix upstream.

The context-extension mechanism deserves one more note because it's a common source of confusion. Raising the RoPE base from 10,000 to 1,000,000 doesn't *change* what the model learned at short context — it changes how positions are *encoded* so that long-range positions remain distinguishable. With the original base of 10,000, positions tens of thousands of tokens apart would have rotary phases that wrap around and alias (two distant positions looking similar to the model). Raising the base slows the rotation so that even positions 128K apart have distinct phases. The reason you do this *during* a continued-pretraining stage (rather than just at inference) is that the model has to *learn* to use the re-scaled positions — flipping the base at inference time without continued training degrades quality, because the model's attention patterns were tuned for the old encoding. GLM-4.5 extends context in mid-training precisely so the model adapts to the new positional encoding with real long-document data. For the KV-cache economics that make all of this matter, see the blog's [KV-cache deep-dive](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/kv-cache) and [KV-cache optimization](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/kv-cache-optimization-and-management).

## 5. The MTP head: architecture for serving

> **Senior rule of thumb:** the cheapest inference optimization is one you bake into the architecture at training time, because you can't bolt a good draft model on afterward.

![A pipeline of the MTP head: train it to predict future tokens, then use it to draft speculative tokens the main model verifies in one pass](/imgs/blogs/glm-4-5-architecture-7.png)

The pipeline above shows how one extra MoE layer becomes an inference speedup. **Multi-Token Prediction (MTP)** adds a head that, during training, predicts not just the next token but the next *k* tokens. The MTP loss is annealed — weighted **0.3 for the first 15T tokens, then 0.1** — so the model learns multi-token prediction strongly early (when it helps representation learning) and then de-emphasizes it (so it doesn't distort the primary next-token objective). At inference, the MTP head *drafts* k speculative tokens, and the main model *verifies* all k in a single forward pass, accepting the longest correct prefix. This is **speculative decoding**, and the key insight is that GLM-4.5 doesn't need a *separate* draft model — the MTP head, trained jointly, is the draft model, and it's already aligned with the main model's distribution (because they were trained together), so its acceptance rate is high.

The reason this is an *architecture* decision and not a deployment one is that you cannot add a good MTP head after training — it has to be trained jointly with the main model to share its representations and predict in its distribution. Baking speculative decoding into the architecture at pretraining time is the cheapest way to get it, because the alternative (training a separate draft model afterward) is both more work and produces a worse draft. The MTP layer is repurposed from one of the MoE layers, so it costs little — a single extra layer, trained jointly, buys you a draft model that would otherwise be a separate training effort. For the broader speculative-decoding picture, the blog's [speculative decoding deep-dive](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/speculative-decoding) is the companion. The lesson generalizes: if you know an inference optimization needs model support (speculative decoding, certain quantization schemes, certain caching strategies), design for it at training time, because retrofitting it is always worse.

### Why the acceptance rate is the whole game

Speculative decoding only speeds you up if the draft tokens are *accepted*, so the acceptance rate is the metric that decides whether the MTP head is worth its cost. The mechanism: the MTP head proposes k tokens; the main model, in a single forward pass, computes what *it* would have generated at each of those k positions; you accept the longest prefix where the draft matches the main model, and discard the rest. If the draft is good (high acceptance), you got several tokens for the price of one main-model pass — a real speedup. If the draft is bad (low acceptance), you wasted the draft computation and fell back to one token per pass — no speedup, slight overhead. So the value of MTP is entirely a function of how well the draft head predicts what the main model would do.

This is exactly why training the MTP head *jointly* with the main model matters so much. A separately-trained draft model (the classic speculative-decoding setup) predicts in *its own* distribution, which differs from the main model's, so its acceptance rate is limited by that distribution gap. The MTP head, trained on the same data with shared lower layers, predicts in *nearly the same* distribution as the main model, so its proposals match more often and the acceptance rate is higher. The annealing schedule (MTP loss 0.3 → 0.1) is tuned to this: early in training the strong MTP signal helps the shared representations learn to predict multiple tokens ahead (which incidentally improves the main model's representations too — predicting further ahead is a richer objective), and later the reduced weight keeps the MTP head sharp without letting it distort the primary objective. The net result is a draft head with a high acceptance rate that cost almost nothing extra to train, which is the cheapest possible speculative decoding. For the broader speculative-decoding picture, the blog's [speculative decoding deep-dive](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/speculative-decoding) and [efficient LLM inference](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/efficient-llm-inference-techniques) are the companions.

## 6. Muon and the schedule: optimizer choices that the loss-watchers would miss

> **Senior rule of thumb:** the optimizer and the learning-rate schedule are not neutral infrastructure; they're modeling choices that interact with everything else, and the fashionable default is not always right for your run.

![A before-after of GLM-4.5's optimizer choices: Muon over AdamW, cosine decay over WSD](/imgs/blogs/glm-4-5-architecture-8.png)

The before-after above captures two optimizer-level departures from the consensus. The first is **Muon** instead of AdamW. Muon is a newer optimizer that, in the GLM team's words, "can accelerate convergence and tolerate larger batch sizes." It works by orthogonalizing the gradient update (via a few Newton-Schulz iterations) before applying it, which produces better-conditioned steps. GLM-4.5's hyperparameters: **Newton-Schulz iteration steps N = 5, momentum µ = 0.95, update RMS scaled to 0.2**, applied to all parameters *except* word embeddings, biases, and RMSNorm weights (which keep a standard optimizer). The reason Muon's "tolerate larger batches" property matters is throughput: a larger batch means more data-parallel scaling and fewer optimizer steps for the same tokens, which at frontier scale is real wall-clock savings. (This is the plain Muon; compare Kimi K2's MuonClip, which adds the QK-Clip stabilizer on top — two teams, same optimizer base, different stabilization.)

The second departure is **cosine decay instead of WSD** (warmup-stable-decay), the schedule that had become fashionable. The GLM team rejected WSD for a measured reason:

> Models trained with the WSD schedule perform worse on general benchmarks (SimpleQA, MMLU), indicating underfitting in the stable stage.

This is a useful counter-data-point to a popular technique. WSD's appeal is that the long "stable" phase at a constant high learning rate lets you checkpoint and branch flexibly. But the GLM team measured WSD-trained models *underfitting* — the stable phase apparently doesn't push the loss down as hard as a continuously-decaying cosine, leaving capability on the table on SimpleQA and MMLU. So they used plain cosine decay, warming up to 2.5e-4 and decaying to 2.5e-5. The lesson is that learning-rate schedules are not universal goods; WSD is great for some things and underfit for GLM-4.5's configuration, and the only way to know is to measure. The team also used a **batch-size warmup from 16M to 64M tokens over the first 500B tokens** — starting with a smaller batch (which gives noisier, more exploratory gradients early when the model is far from any optimum) and growing it (which gives smoother, more precise gradients later when you're fine-tuning the optimum). Batch-size warmup is itself a tunable schedule that interacts with the optimizer, and Muon's large-batch tolerance is part of what makes the 64M target reachable. The batch-size warmup deserves a moment because the intuition is genuinely useful. A small batch gives a noisy gradient estimate; a large batch gives a precise one. Early in training, when the model is far from any good solution, you *want* some noise — it helps the optimizer explore and escape bad regions rather than committing hard to a direction that's only locally good. Later, when the model is near a good solution and you're refining, you want precision — a clean gradient that points reliably downhill. Growing the batch from 16M to 64M tokens over the first 500B tokens encodes exactly this: explore early with noisy small-batch gradients, refine late with precise large-batch ones. It's the same logic as a learning-rate schedule (high then low) applied to the batch size, and the two schedules interact — which is part of why getting the recipe right requires tuning them together rather than independently.

### What Muon actually does differently

It's worth understanding *why* Muon tolerates larger batches and converges faster, because the mechanism explains the choice. AdamW scales each parameter's update by a per-coordinate estimate of the gradient's second moment — it's an element-wise adaptive method, treating every weight independently. Muon, by contrast, operates on the weight *matrices*: it takes the momentum-accumulated gradient for a matrix and *orthogonalizes* it (approximately, via a few Newton-Schulz iterations) before applying it. Orthogonalizing means the update directions across the matrix are made mutually independent and uniformly scaled, which produces a better-conditioned step — no single direction dominates, and the update explores the parameter space more evenly. The practical consequences are the two properties GLM-4.5 cites: better-conditioned steps *converge faster* (you make more progress per step), and they're more *robust to large batches* (large batches reduce gradient noise, and well-conditioned methods exploit that low noise better than element-wise methods, which can stall when the per-coordinate variance estimates become unreliable at large batch).

The hyperparameters tell a story too. `N = 5` Newton-Schulz iterations is a cheap approximation of true orthogonalization — five iterations is enough to get most of the conditioning benefit without the cost of an exact orthogonalization, which would be prohibitively expensive on large matrices. `µ = 0.95` momentum is high, smoothing the gradient over many steps (appropriate for a large, stable run). The update RMS scaled to `0.2` controls the step size in a way that's comparable across matrices of different shapes. And the choice to apply Muon to *all parameters except* embeddings, biases, and RMSNorm weights is important: those excluded parameters are either vectors (biases, norm weights) where matrix orthogonalization doesn't apply, or the embedding table (which has its own pathologies, as [GLM-130B's embedding-gradient-shrink](/blog/machine-learning/mlops/engineering-glm-130b) showed) and is better left to a standard optimizer. The lesson is that "use Muon" isn't a blanket swap — it's "use Muon for the weight matrices where its matrix-level conditioning helps, and keep a standard optimizer for the vector parameters where it doesn't apply." Mixed-optimizer setups like this are increasingly common at the frontier and reflect the same per-role discipline that the [GLM-130B precision choices](/blog/machine-learning/mlops/engineering-glm-130b) showed — the right tool for each part, not one tool for everything.

## 7. Results: the deep-narrow bet, scored

The architecture's job is to produce a good model, and on the ARC (agentic, reasoning, coding) axes GLM-4.5 delivered at a fraction of its rivals' parameters.

![A matrix of GLM-4.5 and GLM-4.5-Air across agentic, reasoning, and coding benchmarks](/imgs/blogs/glm-4-5-architecture-9.png)

The matrix above is the scorecard across the three ARC axes. GLM-4.5 posts **TAU-bench 70.1** (agentic), **BFCL v3 77.8** (best-in-class function calling), **AIME 24 91.0** (reasoning), and **SWE-bench Verified 64.2** (agentic coding). The report places GLM-4.5 **third overall and second on agentic** across all evaluated models — and the headline is that it does this at roughly *half* DeepSeek-R1's parameters and *a third* of Kimi K2's. The deep-narrow, fewer-experts, more-heads architecture is the reason a smaller model competes with larger ones on exactly the axis (reasoning) the architecture was tuned for. GLM-4.5-Air, at 106B/12B, trails the flagship but holds up remarkably well (BFCL 76.4, AIME 89.4), which validates that the recipe *scales down* cleanly — the same architectural choices work at a third the size. For the full evaluation across every benchmark and competitor, the [GLM-4.5 paper-reading](/blog/paper-reading/large-language-model/glm-4-5-agentic-reasoning-and-coding-arc-foundation-models) has the complete tables.

### Reading the parameter efficiency

The "third overall at half the parameters" headline deserves a careful read, because parameter efficiency is the architecture's real claim and it's easy to over- or under-state. The honest framing: GLM-4.5 activates 32B parameters per token, which is *comparable* to DeepSeek-R1 and Kimi K2 — the sparsity ratios are similar across these models. Where GLM-4.5 is more efficient is in *total* parameters (355B vs 671B vs 1T), which matters for *memory* (you need to store all the experts even if you only activate some) but less for *compute* (you only pay FLOPs for the active 32B). So "half the parameters" is primarily a *memory and storage* efficiency claim, not a compute one — GLM-4.5 fits in less GPU memory than its rivals while computing roughly the same per token. That's a genuine and valuable efficiency (it lowers the serving hardware requirement), but it's specifically a *total-parameter* efficiency, and the architecture earns it by getting more capability out of fewer, deeper, head-richer experts rather than by activating fewer parameters.

The Air variant is the more impressive efficiency story. At 106B total / 12B active, GLM-4.5-Air is genuinely *small* by frontier standards, yet it posts BFCL 76.4 and AIME 89.4 — within a few points of the flagship and competitive with much larger models. That the same architectural recipe (deep-narrow, loss-free routing, more heads) works at a third the scale is the validation that the choices are *fundamental* rather than scale-specific tuning. An architecture that only works at one scale is a fluke; one that works across a 3× scale range is a principle. GLM-4.5-Air is the evidence that deep-narrow is a principle. It also makes the recipe *accessible*: a 106B/12B model is something a much wider set of teams can actually serve and finetune than a 355B flagship, so releasing Air with the same architecture is how the deep-narrow lessons propagate into the open ecosystem — the same open-release strategy the [whole lineage](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/glm-lineage-frontier-llm-technique) has pursued since GLM-130B. A principle that ships only in an un-runnable flagship influences few; one that ships in a finetunable mid-size model becomes a default.

## War stories: ten architecture decisions worth stealing

### 1. Trusting reasoning over loss

The more-heads finding is the report's most important methodological lesson: the team kept a change that *hurt* their visible metric (it cost parameters for no loss improvement) because it *helped* their real goal (reasoning). This requires both the discipline to evaluate on downstream tasks and the conviction to act on a result that the loss curve contradicts. Most teams, ranking by loss, would have removed the extra heads in an ablation and never known what they gave up. The transferable practice is to maintain a downstream-reasoning eval in your architecture search and to *trust it over loss* when they disagree — because loss is an average and reasoning lives in the tail. The deeper point is that this finding is a *gift* to the field: by publishing "more heads help reasoning but not loss," the GLM team handed everyone else a free architecture improvement *and* a methodological warning. Anyone now scaling a reasoning model knows to (a) consider more heads and (b) not rank that choice by loss. That's the value of a report that publishes a counterintuitive result rather than just the final recipe — it transfers not just the *what* but the *how you'd discover it*. The organizational courage this requires is underrated: when your loss curve says "this change is neutral or slightly negative" and your reasoning eval says "this change is good," someone has to overrule the loss, and loss is the metric everyone has been trained to trust. The teams that get this right have usually been burned before — they shipped a loss-optimal model that underperformed on reasoning and learned the hard way that loss isn't the goal. The report's matter-of-fact presentation of the more-heads finding suggests the GLM team had internalized this; they didn't agonize over keeping a change the loss disliked, because they'd already decided reasoning was the arbiter.

### 2. Depth for reasoning, despite the costs

Going to 89 MoE layers — deeper than DeepSeek-V3's 58 — was a bet that depth buys reasoning, paid for in training difficulty (deeper networks are harder to keep stable) and inference latency (more sequential layers per token). The team accepted both costs because their experiments said reasoning is a compositional, sequential problem that depth serves. The lesson is to think of your parameter budget as a *portfolio* of independent bets — width, depth, experts, heads — each with its own risk-return, rather than scaling everything uniformly. Depth and width are not interchangeable; they buy different capabilities, and a reasoning-focused model should over-weight depth.

### 3. Routing as control, not loss

The loss-free balancing is a beautiful example of replacing a *loss* solution with a *control* solution. The old way (auxiliary balance loss) adds a second objective that fights the first; the new way (bias thermostat) steers routing with a controller that never touches the gradient. The general principle — *when you need to enforce a constraint, ask whether a controller can do it instead of a loss term* — applies far beyond MoE. Any time you're tempted to add a regularization loss to enforce some property, consider whether you can instead adjust a non-differentiable knob (like a routing bias) that achieves the property without distorting the objective. The general framing is "constraint satisfaction via control vs. via penalty." A penalty (loss term) is differentiable and integrates cleanly into gradient descent, but it competes with your main objective for the gradient's attention and biases the solution. A controller (a non-differentiable knob updated by a rule) sits outside the gradient entirely, so it enforces the constraint without distorting the objective — but it only works for constraints you can enforce through a non-differentiable lever. Load balancing is perfect for the controller approach because the lever (the routing bias) is naturally non-differentiable (it affects a `topk`, which has no gradient). Recognizing when a constraint has a non-differentiable lever available is the skill; when it does, the controller is almost always cleaner than the penalty.

### 4. The shared expert that frees the others

The single always-on shared expert is a small idea with a large effect: it absorbs the common computation so the 160 routed experts can specialize instead of each re-learning the basics. Without it, every expert wastes capacity on common patterns, and the routing has to work harder because the common case is competing for the same expert slots as the specialized cases. The lesson generalizes to any mixture or ensemble: a shared component that handles the common case lets the specialized components be genuinely specialized, which is a more efficient use of capacity than making every component a generalist. There's a nice analogy to organizational design: a company where every team re-implements its own logging, auth, and deployment is wasting effort; a shared platform team that handles the common infrastructure lets the product teams focus on what's actually differentiated. The shared expert is the platform team of the MoE. The cost is that the shared expert runs for *every* token (it's not sparse), so it adds to the active parameter count — which is why you have exactly *one* shared expert, not several: you want enough shared capacity to absorb the common case without eroding the FLOP savings that make MoE worthwhile in the first place. The single-shared-expert choice is the balance point between "specialize the routed experts" and "keep the activation sparse."

### 5. QK-Norm as the descendant of FP32 softmax

QK-Norm solving the same attention-logit-range problem that GLM-130B solved with FP32 softmax is a clean illustration of how techniques evolve while the problem stays constant. The problem (attention logits growing until they're numerically dangerous) is intrinsic to scaling attention; the solutions improve over time (FP32 softmax → QK-Norm → QK-Clip). The lesson is to recognize the *invariant problem* behind the changing solutions — if you understand that attention-logit range is a perennial scaling issue, you'll recognize each generation's fix as another answer to the same question, and you'll know to check for it in your own scaling. Practically, this means that when you scale up and your attention starts misbehaving (NaNs, one token dominating the softmax, training instability that correlates with attention layers), you should reach immediately for the QK-Norm/QK-Clip family rather than rediscovering the problem from scratch. The GLM lineage has effectively pre-diagnosed this failure for you across three generations; the only open question for your model is *which* of the known fixes fits your precision and scale. This pattern — an invariant problem with an evolving series of solutions — recurs throughout the GLM lineage and is worth training your eye to spot. Training stability (Pre-LN to DeepNorm to QK-Norm), low precision (INT4 to FP8), variable-length generation (the `[END]` token to `[gMASK]` to hybrid reasoning): in each case the *problem* is constant across the lineage and the *mechanism* improves. When you read a series of model reports, tracking these invariant problems is more illuminating than tracking the headline features, because the invariant problems are what the field is actually fighting, and each report's solution tells you the current state of the art on a fundamental issue rather than a fashionable one.

### 6. MTP: design for the inference you want

Baking the MTP head in at training time, because you can't add a good draft model afterward, is the architecture-for-serving lesson. Speculative decoding needs a draft model aligned with the main model's distribution, and the best way to get that alignment is to train them jointly. The lesson: inventory the inference optimizations you'll want (speculative decoding, KV-cache tricks, quantization) and check which ones need *model support* — those have to be designed in at training time, because retrofitting them produces a worse result. A useful exercise before you start a pretraining run is to write down the serving requirements as if they were product requirements: what latency, what throughput, what hardware, and therefore which optimizations you'll need — then check which of those optimizations require architectural support. Speculative decoding wants a draft head (MTP). Aggressive quantization wants a narrow weight distribution (an architecture-and-training property, as [GLM-130B](/blog/machine-learning/mlops/engineering-glm-130b) showed). Long context wants a small KV cache (GQA). Each of these is cheaper to grant at design time than to bolt on later. The cheapest fast-inference model is the one that was architected to be fast. This is a place where the traditional separation of "research builds the model, infra serves it" actively hurts you: if the research team designs the architecture without thinking about serving, you end up retrofitting inference optimizations onto a model that wasn't built for them, and you leave speedups on the table. GLM-4.5's MTP head is what it looks like when the serving requirements feed *back* into the architecture — the team knew they'd want speculative decoding, so they built the draft head into the model. The lesson for an organization is that inference and training shouldn't be siloed; the inference team's wishlist (speculative decoding, quantization-friendliness, KV-cache efficiency) should be an input to the architecture, because several items on that wishlist can only be granted at training time.

### 7. Muon, and not being precious about AdamW

Swapping AdamW for Muon is a reminder that the optimizer is a *choice*, not a fixed piece of infrastructure. AdamW is the default not because it's optimal but because it's reliable and familiar. Muon's faster convergence and large-batch tolerance were worth the risk of using a newer, less-battle-tested optimizer, because at frontier scale those properties translate to real wall-clock and cost savings. The lesson is to periodically re-examine your "neutral infrastructure" choices — the optimizer, the schedule, the initialization — because they're modeling decisions in disguise, and the field's defaults lag the frontier.

### 8. WSD underfit: measure your schedule

The rejection of WSD is the counterweight to war story 7: just as you shouldn't be precious about AdamW, you shouldn't adopt a new technique (WSD) just because it's fashionable. The GLM team *measured* WSD underfitting on SimpleQA and MMLU and used cosine instead. The discipline is symmetric: be willing to adopt the new (Muon) and willing to reject the new (WSD), and let *measurement* — not fashion — decide. A schedule that's great for one configuration can underfit another; the only way to know is to run both and look at the downstream benchmarks, not just the loss. There's a broader cultural point in the Muon-yes-WSD-no pairing: a healthy research culture is *empirical*, not *ideological*, about its tools. It adopts the new thing when measurement says it helps (Muon) and rejects the new thing when measurement says it doesn't (WSD), without attachment to either novelty or tradition. The failure modes on both sides are common — teams that reflexively use whatever is fashionable (adopting WSD because everyone is), and teams that reflexively use whatever they've always used (sticking with AdamW out of habit). GLM-4.5's optimizer choices are a small demonstration of getting both calls right by deferring to measurement, and the meta-lesson is worth more than either specific choice: build a culture where the answer to "should we use X?" is "let's measure it on the benchmarks we care about," not "X is the standard" or "X is the hot new thing."

### 9. Different choices for different scales

GLM-4.5 uses QK-Norm; GLM-4.5-Air doesn't. The two models, built by the same team in the same family, made different normalization choices because the larger model's logit magnitudes needed more stabilization. The lesson is that architecture choices are *scale-dependent*, and you shouldn't assume the flagship's recipe transfers unchanged to a smaller model (or vice versa). When you scale a model up or down, re-examine the stabilization choices — the ones that were unnecessary at small scale may become essential, and the ones that were essential may become overhead. This is the same lesson [GLM-130B](/blog/machine-learning/mlops/engineering-glm-130b) taught with its LayerNorm bake-off: stability choices don't extrapolate across scale, and a choice tuned on a small proxy can be wrong at the target. The QK-Norm difference between the flagship and Air is the smaller, cleaner version of that lesson — same family, same team, same recipe, but a stabilization that the 355B model needs and the 106B model doesn't. The practical takeaway: keep a short list of "scale-dependent stabilizers" (normalization choices, gradient clipping, attention-logit control) and re-evaluate every item on it whenever you change scale by more than ~2×, rather than assuming the recipe ports.

### 10. Conventional everywhere it doesn't matter

The deepest pattern is what GLM-4.5 *didn't* change: RMSNorm, SwiGLU, rotary positions, the basic transformer block. The novelty is concentrated in a few high-leverage choices (deep-narrow, more heads, loss-free routing, QK-Norm, MTP, Muon), and everything else is deliberately the consensus. This concentration is what makes the report legible — you can attribute the results to the few things that changed rather than to a confounded pile of novelty. The lesson, which recurs across the whole [GLM lineage](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/glm-lineage-frontier-llm-technique), is to spend your novelty budget narrowly and keep everything else boring, so that your wins are attributable and your model is debuggable. There's a discipline cost to this — it's tempting to upgrade every component to its latest-and-greatest version — but the payoff is a model whose behavior you can actually reason about when something goes wrong, and a report whose claims the field can actually trust. This is the same discipline [GLM-130B](/blog/machine-learning/mlops/engineering-glm-130b) showed three years earlier with its deliberately conventional architecture around a novel objective, and it's arguably the most consistent meta-principle across the entire lineage: be radical in a few well-chosen places and conservative everywhere else. The opposite — being mildly novel everywhere — produces a model whose results you can't attribute to anything, because every part changed at once. Concentrated novelty is not just good engineering hygiene; it's what makes a model report *scientifically useful*, because the reader can isolate which specific change bought which specific gain. GLM-4.5's report is readable precisely because it changed few things and explained each one.

## When to reach for the GLM-4.5 architecture playbook

**Reach for these techniques when:**

- **You're building a reasoning-focused model.** The deep-narrow shape and the extra attention heads are specifically tuned for reasoning, even at the cost of training loss and latency. If reasoning is your priority, this is the architecture to study.
- **You're training a sparse MoE.** Loss-free balance routing (bias thermostat + tiny safety loss) is the current best practice for load balancing without degrading quality. The shared expert is a cheap capacity win.
- **You care about inference cost.** Bake in the MTP head for speculative decoding at training time; it's the cheapest way to get a good draft model. QK-Norm and GQA keep attention cheap and stable.
- **You're at a scale where the optimizer matters.** Muon's large-batch tolerance and faster convergence are worth evaluating against AdamW at frontier scale — but evaluate it, don't just adopt it, and keep a standard optimizer for the embedding and vector parameters.
- **You're designing a model and a serving stack together.** The MTP-for-speculative-decoding pattern, and the QK-Norm/GQA attention design, are what it looks like when serving requirements feed back into the architecture. If you control both training and inference, exploit that.

**Skip or adapt when:**

- **You're optimizing for loss/perplexity benchmarks specifically.** The more-heads bet *costs* you on loss; if your target metric is perplexity, don't add the heads. The architecture is reasoning-first, which is the wrong objective if your product is, say, a perplexity-sensitive autocomplete where the average token quality is exactly what you're selling and the rare hard reasoning token doesn't matter.
- **You're below MoE scale.** Loss-free routing, the shared expert, and MTP are MoE-specific. A dense model below ~10B doesn't have these problems; the QK-Norm and optimizer lessons still apply.
- **Latency is your hard constraint.** The deep-narrow shape *increases* per-token latency (more sequential layers). If you're latency-bound, a wider-shallower model may serve better, and you should weigh the reasoning gain against the latency cost.
- **You can't afford a downstream eval in your architecture loop.** The more-heads finding only helps if you *measure* reasoning during architecture search. If you can only afford loss-based search, you'll systematically miss changes like this — and you should at least know that you're missing them. The pragmatic middle ground is to do most of your search on loss (cheap) but periodically validate the top candidates on a small reasoning eval (expensive but rare), so you catch the loss-invisible wins without paying for a full downstream eval on every variant.
- **Your model isn't deep enough for the depth bet to pay off.** The deep-narrow advantage is about *reasoning*, which depth serves through compositional capacity. Below a certain scale, the depth-vs-width tradeoff shifts — a very small model may need its parameters in width just to have enough per-layer capacity to be useful at all. The deep-narrow bet is a frontier-scale bet; don't assume it transfers to a 1B model.

The honest summary of when *not* to copy GLM-4.5's architecture is: when your objective genuinely is loss/perplexity, when you're latency-bound rather than reasoning-bound, when you're below MoE scale, or when you can't measure the reasoning that justifies the loss-negative choices. Outside those cases, the architecture is a strong template for a reasoning-focused frontier model, and even inside them, the *methodological* lessons (trust downstream over loss, concentrate novelty, design for serving, be empirical about optimizers) transfer regardless of scale or objective.

The unifying thread is that GLM-4.5's architecture is a series of bets *against the visible metric* — choices the loss curve would reject, made because the team trusted reasoning more and measured it directly. The next article covers how this architecture is *trained*: the expert-distillation pipeline and agentic RL that turn this network into a frontier agent. But the architectural lesson stands on its own: spend your novelty narrowly, bet on depth for reasoning, route with a controller not a loss, and never let the metric you can see override the one you actually care about.

If there's a single sentence to carry out of this article, it's that **GLM-4.5's architecture is what you get when a team optimizes for the capability it wants instead of the metric it can measure.** Every departure from the consensus — deep-narrow, more heads, loss-free routing, Muon, cosine-over-WSD — is a case where the team had a hypothesis about what serves reasoning, tested it on reasoning benchmarks, and acted on the result even when the loss curve was silent or disagreed. That discipline is harder than it sounds, because the loss is right in front of you and the reasoning eval is expensive and noisy and easy to rationalize away. The labs that will define the next few years of frontier models are the ones that have built the muscle to trust the right metric, and GLM-4.5's report is a clinic in what that muscle produces at the level of layers, heads, experts, and optimizers. The architecture is the visible artifact; the judgment behind it is the thing worth stealing. And that judgment — measure the capability you care about, concentrate your novelty, design the model and its serving stack together, and stay empirical about your tools — is exactly what the next article will show being applied to the *training* of this architecture, where the same instincts produce the expert-distillation and agentic-RL pipeline that turns these 355 billion parameters into a frontier agent.
