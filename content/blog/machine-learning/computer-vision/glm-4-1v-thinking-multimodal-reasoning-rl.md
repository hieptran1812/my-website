> [!tldr]
> - **What it claims:** the text-reasoning recipe that built GLM-4.5 — cold-start SFT for a thinking format, then reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards and a difficulty curriculum — ports almost unchanged to *vision*, producing a multimodal model that reasons explicitly before answering.
> - **Why it matters:** a 9B GLM-4.1V-Thinking surpasses the eight-times-larger Qwen2.5-VL-72B on most hard multimodal reasoning benchmarks, which is strong evidence that *reasoning-focused RL*, not raw scale, is what's been missing from vision-language models.
> - **Most surprising finding:** applying RL with verifiable rewards *jointly across eight visual domains at once* works — and a single weak per-domain reward can derail the whole run, so the verifier engineering is the crux.
> - **Where it fails:** the verifiable-reward approach is hardest exactly where vision is most subjective (aesthetic judgment, open-ended description), so the gains concentrate in the checkable domains.

This is the final article in the [GLM lineage series](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/glm-lineage-frontier-llm-technique), and it closes the loop on a thesis the whole series has been building: that GLM is a *methodology*, not a bag of text tricks. The strongest evidence for that thesis is what happens when the methodology crosses modalities. The report — [GLM-4.1V-Thinking and GLM-4.5V](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.01006) — takes the text-reasoning playbook from [GLM-4.5](/blog/machine-learning/training-techniques/training-glm-4-5-distillation-agentic-rl) and applies it to images, charts, documents, screenshots, and video, almost unchanged. That it works as well as it does is the point — and it's a fitting place to end a series that has argued, article by article, that the GLM lineage's real asset is a reusable way of building capable models, not any single model.

Reading this report after the previous six articles, the sense of recognition is strong: the cold-start SFT for a thinking format, the verifiable rewards, the difficulty curriculum, the inherited reasoning backbone, the concentrated novelty, the honest reporting of the failure mode — every one of these is a move we've watched the GLM team make before, in text. Seeing them all reappear, almost unchanged, in a vision-language model is the clearest demonstration the series offers that we've been studying a *method* this whole time, not a sequence of unrelated models that happened to share a name.

## Context: vision-language models that see but don't reason

By 2025, vision-language models (VLMs) were good at *perception* — describe this image, read this text, answer a question about what's shown — and weak at *reasoning* over what they perceived. A VLM could tell you a chart showed sales by quarter; it struggled to reason about the trend, compute a derived quantity, or solve a multi-step problem grounded in the image. The text world had largely solved its version of this with reasoning models (chain-of-thought, RL on verifiable rewards) — GLM-4.5, DeepSeek-R1, o3. The vision world hadn't caught up — VLMs were, in a sense, all eyes and no deliberation, able to describe a scene in fluent detail while fumbling any question that required thinking a few steps past what was directly visible.

The gap wasn't architecture; VLMs had perfectly good ViT-plus-LLM stacks. The gap was *post-training*. Text reasoning models are made by RL on verifiable rewards — math problems with checkable answers, code with unit tests — that teaches the model to reason carefully because careful reasoning gets rewarded. VLMs were mostly trained with supervised finetuning on image-text pairs, which teaches perception and description but not deliberate reasoning. The question GLM-4.1V-Thinking asks is direct: *can you apply the text-reasoning RL recipe to vision, and does it produce the same reasoning gains?* The answer, as we'll see, is yes — with the crucial caveat that you need verifiable rewards across every visual domain, which is a substantial data-and-verifier engineering problem.

It's worth being precise about *why* SFT alone doesn't produce reasoning, because it's the crux of the whole motivation. Supervised finetuning on image-text pairs teaches the model to imitate the *outputs* in its training data — captions, answers, descriptions. If those outputs don't contain explicit step-by-step reasoning (and most image-text pairs don't — they're "image: caption," not "image: here's how I'd reason about this image"), the model never learns to reason; it learns to produce plausible-looking answers directly. That works for perception (the answer is right there in the image) and fails for reasoning (the answer requires multiple inference steps the model was never trained to take). RL on verifiable rewards fixes this because it rewards the *outcome* (a correct answer) regardless of *how* the model got there — so the model is free to discover that producing a reasoning trace before answering gets more rewards, and it learns to reason because reasoning *works*. This is exactly the mechanism that produced text reasoning models, and the report's bet is that the same mechanism, applied to vision, produces visual reasoning. The bet is well-founded: there's nothing text-specific about "reward correct outcomes and let the model discover that reasoning helps." The only thing that's modality-specific is the *verifier* — what counts as a correct outcome, and how you check it — which is why so much of the engineering is in the verifiers. This is a clarifying way to think about what does and doesn't transfer across modalities: the *learning principle* (reward verifiable outcomes, let reasoning emerge) is modality-agnostic, while the *verification mechanism* (how you check an outcome) is modality-specific. Text verification is relatively easy (string match, code execution, answer checking); vision verification is harder and more varied (bounding-box IoU for grounding, string match for OCR, rendered-output comparison for screenshot-to-code, judge models for description). So porting the recipe to a new modality is mostly a matter of *building the right verifiers for that modality* — the rest of the recipe comes along unchanged. That's a useful mental model for anyone wanting to bring reasoning RL to audio, robotics, or any other modality: the principle ports, and your job is to figure out how to verify outcomes in your modality.

There's a useful historical parallel here. The text world went through exactly this transition: early LLMs trained on next-token prediction were good at fluent generation and weak at reasoning, until RL on verifiable rewards (the DeepSeek-R1 and GLM-4.5 generation) taught them to reason. The vision world is a couple of years behind on the same curve, and GLM-4.1V-Thinking is one of the models pulling it through the transition. Reading it, you're watching the vision field repeat, compressed, the reasoning revolution that text already went through — which is part of why the recipe ports so cleanly: it's the same transition, just in a new modality.

## Contributions

The report's contributions, tightened:

1. **A think-then-answer multimodal model.** GLM-4.1V-Thinking and GLM-4.5V produce explicit `<think>…</think><answer>…</answer>` traces, bringing chain-of-thought reasoning to vision-language tasks.
2. **RLVR across eight domains jointly.** Reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards is applied *simultaneously* across STEM, OCR, charts, grounding, spatial reasoning, GUI agents, video, and general VQA — not domain-by-domain.
3. **RLCS — Reinforcement Learning with Curriculum Sampling.** Offline difficulty scoring plus online adaptive reweighting keeps each training sample's difficulty matched to the model's evolving ability.
4. **An architecture for extreme inputs.** A ViT with 2D-RoPE handles aspect ratios over 200:1, and 3D convolutions plus 3D-RoPE handle video.
5. **A parameter-efficiency result.** GLM-4.1V-9B-Thinking surpasses the 72B Qwen2.5-VL on 29 of 42 benchmarks, and GLM-4.5V matches closed models like Gemini-2.5-Flash on many.

These contributions interlock in a way worth noticing. The think-then-answer format (1) is the *vehicle* for reasoning; the boxed answers inside it are what make the format *verifiable*, which enables the RLVR (2); the RLVR across many domains creates a heterogeneity problem that RLCS (3) solves; the architecture (4) is what lets all eight domains' inputs (extreme aspect ratios, video) flow through one model; and the parameter-efficiency result (5) is the payoff that validates the whole stack. Pull any piece and the others weaken — without the boxed format you can't verify, without verifiers you can't do RLVR, without RLCS the heterogeneous RL is unstable. This tight coupling is the signature of a well-designed system, and it's the same interlocking-contributions structure that the [original GLM paper](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/autoregressive-blank-infilling-glm) showed back in 2021 — another instance of the lineage's consistent design discipline.

## Method: the architecture and the recipe

![A layered stack of the GLM-4.1V architecture, from the ViT encoder through the MLP projector to the GLM language decoder](/imgs/blogs/glm-4-1v-thinking-multimodal-reasoning-rl-1.png)

The stack above is the architecture, and it's deliberately conventional in shape — the now-standard VLM design of a vision encoder feeding a language model through a projector. The pieces:

- **A ViT encoder**, initialized from AIMv2-Huge, which turns the image (or video) into a sequence of visual feature tokens. Two modifications matter: its 2D convolutions are replaced with **3D convolutions** to downsample video temporally, and **2D-RoPE** is added to its self-attention so it can handle extreme aspect ratios — over 200:1, which is what you need for tall screenshots or wide panoramas.
- **An MLP projector** that maps the ViT's visual features into the language model's token space, so the LLM can attend to them as if they were text tokens.
- **A GLM language decoder** with **3D-RoPE**, which does the actual reasoning and generation. The backbone is **GLM-4-9B-0414** (a 9B dense model) for GLM-4.1V-Thinking, and **GLM-4.5-Air** (the 106B/12B MoE) for GLM-4.5V — so the multimodal models inherit the text models' reasoning backbones directly.

The architectural cleverness is concentrated in the positional encodings. **2D-RoPE in the vision tower** is what handles the extreme aspect ratios: a standard positional scheme assumes roughly square-ish images, and breaks down when an image is 200× wider than it is tall, but a 2D rotary scheme encodes the row and column positions independently so the model can localize content in a very non-square image. **3D-RoPE in the language decoder** extends this to the temporal dimension for video — position now has a time axis. The 3D convolutions in the ViT downsample video frames temporally so a video doesn't explode into an unmanageable number of tokens. These are the modifications that let one architecture handle images, tall documents, and video without separate pathways. The rest — the ViT, the projector, the LLM — is the consensus VLM design, which is the same "concentrate the novelty" discipline the whole [GLM lineage](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/glm-lineage-frontier-llm-technique) shows.

The choice to **inherit the text reasoning backbone** is more consequential than it looks, and it's worth dwelling on. GLM-4.1V-Thinking is built on GLM-4-9B-0414 and GLM-4.5V on GLM-4.5-Air — both of which are *already* strong text reasoners. This is deliberate: the multimodal model doesn't have to learn reasoning from scratch, it has to learn to *apply* reasoning it already has to visual inputs. The vision encoder and projector teach the model to *see*; the inherited LLM already knows how to *think*; the post-training teaches it to connect the two — to reason about what it sees. Starting from a strong reasoning LLM is what makes the recipe efficient, because the hard, expensive part (learning to reason) is already done in the text pretraining and RL. The implication for anyone reproducing this is important: the quality of your text reasoning backbone caps your multimodal reasoning, so you should start from the best reasoning LLM you have, not from a generic base model. The multimodal reasoning is largely the text reasoning, redirected at pixels.

The **2D-RoPE for extreme aspect ratios** deserves a concrete example of why it matters. Consider a tall screenshot of a web page — it might be 1000 pixels wide and 20,000 pixels tall, a 1:20 aspect ratio, or worse for a long document. A standard positional encoding, tuned for roughly square images, has no good way to represent a position that's at row 18,000 of a 20,000-row image — the position values go far outside the range the encoding was designed for, and the model loses track of where things are. 2D-RoPE encodes row and column positions *independently* with rotary embeddings, so an extreme position in one dimension doesn't break the encoding — the row position rotates through its phase regardless of how the column position behaves. This is what lets GLM-4.1V handle GUI screenshots and long documents, which are exactly the inputs where aspect ratios get extreme and where a lot of practically-valuable multimodal tasks live (reading a long form, navigating a tall app screen). The architecture choice is in direct service of the *use cases* — GUI agents and document understanding — that the eight-domain training targets.

### One RL run, eight domains

![A grid of the eight visual domains RLVR is applied to jointly: STEM, OCR, charts, grounding, spatial reasoning, GUI agents, video, and VQA](/imgs/blogs/glm-4-1v-thinking-multimodal-reasoning-rl-2.png)

The grid above is the most distinctive choice in the post-training: RLVR is applied **jointly across all eight domains at once**, not one domain at a time. The domains span STEM (math and physics problems grounded in images), OCR (reading text from images), charts and documents, grounding (localizing objects), spatial reasoning, GUI agents (acting on screenshots), video understanding, and general VQA. Training on all of them together — rather than sequentially or in isolation — is what makes the reasoning skill *shared* rather than siloed: the model learns a general "look carefully, reason step by step, then answer" competence that transfers across visual domains, rather than eight separate domain-specific skills.

The risk of joint training, and the report's key warning, is that the domains are coupled through the shared weights. A weak reward in one domain doesn't just fail to help that domain — it can *derail* the others, because the model can learn to game the weak reward in ways that corrupt its shared reasoning. We'll return to this in the critique; it's the central engineering challenge of the whole approach.

The *why* of joint training is worth understanding, because it would have been safer to train each domain separately. The argument for joint training is that reasoning is a *shared* skill — the ability to look carefully, decompose a problem, reason step by step, and check your answer is the same competence whether you're solving a physics problem grounded in a diagram or navigating a GUI screenshot. If you train each domain separately, you risk the model learning eight narrow, domain-specific tricks rather than one general reasoning skill, and the narrow skills don't transfer to new domains or to combinations. Training jointly forces the model to find the *common* reasoning competence that serves all eight domains, which is both more general (it transfers) and more efficient (one skill instead of eight). This is the same logic as AgentBench's eight-environment design ([the benchmarks article](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/how-zhipu-measures-glm-benchmarks)) and AgentTuning's diverse-environment training ([the GLM-4 article](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/chatglm-to-glm-4-all-tools)): diversity in training is what produces a transferable skill rather than a collection of narrow ones. The cost — the coupling that lets a weak reward in one domain hurt the others — is the price of that generality, and managing it (with strong verifiers everywhere) is the engineering challenge.

The eight domains are also chosen to span the *practically valuable* multimodal capabilities, not just the academically interesting ones. STEM and charts are the "reason about structured visual information" capabilities; OCR and document understanding are the "read and use text in images" capabilities; grounding and spatial reasoning are the "understand where things are" capabilities; GUI agents and video are the "act on and understand dynamic visual content" capabilities. Together they cover most of what you'd actually want a multimodal model to do, which is why a model strong across all eight is a genuinely useful general VLM rather than a benchmark specialist. The domain selection is, in effect, a statement of what the team thinks multimodal capability *is*.

### The post-training pipeline

![A pipeline of the GLM-4.1V post-training: cold-start SFT for the thinking format, per-domain verifiers, then joint RLVR plus RLHF](/imgs/blogs/glm-4-1v-thinking-multimodal-reasoning-rl-3.png)

The pipeline above is the recipe, and it is recognizably the GLM-4.5 text recipe with vision substituted in. It starts with a base VLM (the ViT + GLM LLM). Then:

1. **Cold-start SFT** teaches the `<think>…</think><answer>…</answer>` format with full-parameter tuning at 32,768-token sequences. The model learns to produce an explicit reasoning trace before its answer — the same format that makes text reasoning models work.
2. **Boxed answers.** Answers are wrapped in `<|begin_of_box|>…<|end_of_box|>` markers so they can be *extracted* and checked. This is the small but crucial detail that makes verifiability possible: to give a verifiable reward, you need to reliably pull the model's final answer out of its reasoning trace, and a boxed format makes that extraction unambiguous.
3. **Per-domain verifiers.** Each domain gets a verifier — rule-based extraction where possible (a math answer, a bounding box, an OCR string) and model-based judgment where necessary. This is the hard part, as we'll see.
4. **Joint RLVR + RLHF.** Reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (for the checkable domains) plus RLHF (for the subjective ones) runs across all domains together, producing GLM-4.1V-Thinking.

```python
## The boxed-answer format makes the final answer extractable for verification.
## The model is trained to emit reasoning, then a boxed answer the verifier reads.
TEMPLATE = (
    "<think>{reasoning}</think>"
    "<answer>{prose} <|begin_of_box|>{final_answer}<|end_of_box|></answer>"
)

def reward(trace, domain, gold):
    answer = extract_boxed(trace)            # pull <|begin_of_box|>...<|end_of_box|>
    if answer is None:
        return 0.0                           # malformed: no extractable answer
    return VERIFIERS[domain](answer, gold)   # per-domain verifier: rule or model
```

![A before-after showing the GLM-4.5 text reasoning recipe ported almost unchanged to multimodal reasoning](/imgs/blogs/glm-4-1v-thinking-multimodal-reasoning-rl-6.png)

The before-after above makes the parallel explicit, and it's the thesis of the whole article. The left column is the GLM-4.5 *text* recipe: cold-start SFT on a chain-of-thought format, RL with verifiable rewards, a difficulty curriculum. The right column is the GLM-4.1V *multimodal* recipe: cold-start SFT on the `<think>/<answer>` format, RLVR across eight visual domains, RLCS curriculum sampling. The correspondence is nearly one-to-one. The report is, in effect, demonstrating that the text-reasoning recipe is *modality-agnostic* — the same three-part structure (teach the format, reward verifiable reasoning, curriculum the difficulty) produces reasoning whether the input is tokens or pixels. That modality-agnosticism is the strongest possible evidence that what the GLM team has is a method, not a text-specific trick.

The **boxed-answer format** is the small detail that makes the whole verifiable-reward approach work, and it's worth appreciating why. To give a verifiable reward, you have to *extract* the model's final answer from its output and check it — but the model's output is a long reasoning trace followed by an answer, and naively parsing "the answer" out of free-form text is error-prone. If your extraction is wrong (you pull the wrong part of the trace), your reward is noise, and noisy rewards derail RL. The boxed format (`<|begin_of_box|>…<|end_of_box|>`) makes extraction unambiguous: the model is trained to put its final answer inside the box, so the verifier just reads what's in the box. This is the multimodal analog of the structured tool-call formats that GLM-4.5 uses for the same reason — a clean, parseable output format is what makes automatic verification reliable. The lesson generalizes: if you're going to give verifiable rewards, *design the output format for extractability first*, because a reward you can't reliably extract is a reward you can't reliably train on. Format design is reward-quality engineering.

The **per-domain verifier** stage is where the real, mostly-unreported work lives, and the report is honest that it's the crux. Each domain needs a way to check whether an answer is correct. For some domains this is clean: a math answer can be checked against a gold value, a bounding box against a ground-truth region (IoU overlap), an OCR string against the reference text. For others it's genuinely hard: how do you verify a "describe this scene" answer, or judge whether a GUI agent took a reasonable action? The report uses a mix of *rule-based* verifiers (where the answer is checkable mechanically) and *model-based* verifiers (a judge model, where it isn't). The quality of these verifiers determines everything downstream, which is the approach's central vulnerability — and the engineering effort to build eight good verifiers is substantial and largely invisible in the headline result. Anyone trying to reproduce this should expect the verifier engineering to be the bulk of the work, not the RL algorithm.

### RLCS: keeping training at the frontier

![A graph of RLCS, where offline difficulty scoring and an online ability estimate combine to reweight samples to the model's frontier](/imgs/blogs/glm-4-1v-thinking-multimodal-reasoning-rl-4.png)

The one genuinely new technique relative to the text recipe is **RLCS (Reinforcement Learning with Curriculum Sampling)**, shown above. It's the multimodal analog of GLM-4.5's `pass@8=0/pass@512>0` difficulty filter, but made *continuous and adaptive*. Two signals combine: an **offline difficulty score** for each task (computed once, before training) and an **online ability estimate** of the model (updated as training progresses). The two feed an **adaptive reweighting** that increases the sampling weight of tasks at the model's current frontier — neither too easy (already solved, no gradient) nor too hard (never solved, no gradient) — and the RL update on those frontier tasks produces a stronger model, whose new ability is re-estimated, shifting the frontier outward. The loop keeps the model training on the edge of its competence as that edge moves.

The reason RLCS matters more for multimodal than for text is the *heterogeneity* of the domains. With eight domains of wildly different difficulty and the model improving at different rates in each, a fixed curriculum would be wrong almost everywhere — too easy in the domains the model is good at, too hard in the ones it's weak at. RLCS adapts per-domain and over time, keeping each domain's training at *its* frontier. This is the same "train at the frontier where the gradient lives" principle the [text training article](/blog/machine-learning/training-techniques/training-glm-4-5-distillation-agentic-rl) covered, generalized from a discrete filter to a continuous, adaptive reweighting suited to many heterogeneous domains.

The two-signal design (offline difficulty + online ability) is worth unpacking because each signal covers a blind spot of the other. The **offline difficulty score** is a fixed property of each task — how hard is this problem in absolute terms — computed once before training. It's cheap (you compute it once) but static (it doesn't know how good the *current* model is). The **online ability estimate** tracks the model's evolving competence — what can it currently solve — but is noisy and expensive to measure continuously. Combining them gives you what neither alone provides: the offline score tells you the absolute difficulty landscape, and the online estimate tells you where the model currently sits in that landscape, so together they identify "tasks that are hard in absolute terms *and* near the model's current edge." A purely offline curriculum would keep training on the same "medium" problems even after the model outgrew them; a purely online one would be too noisy and expensive to compute per-sample. The combination is the practical sweet spot, and it's a nice illustration of a general curriculum-design principle: a good curriculum needs both a model of *task difficulty* and a model of *learner ability*, because the right next task is a function of both.

It's also worth noting that RLCS is *necessary* for the multimodal setting in a way it wasn't strictly for text. GLM-4.5's text reasoning RL could get away with a coarse two-stage difficulty filter because the domain (math/code reasoning) was relatively homogeneous. Multimodal RL spans eight domains where the model's ability differs wildly — it might be near-frontier on charts and far-from-frontier on video — so a single global difficulty setting would be wrong for almost every domain. RLCS's *per-task adaptive* reweighting is what handles that heterogeneity, keeping each domain's training at *that domain's* frontier rather than a global one. The heterogeneity of multimodal capability is what forces the curriculum to be adaptive rather than fixed, which is a good example of how a new setting (many heterogeneous domains) demands a refinement (adaptive curriculum) of a technique that worked in a simpler setting (one homogeneous domain).

## Experiments: a 9B model beats a 72B one

The headline result is a parameter-efficiency story that's striking even by 2025 standards.

![A matrix of GLM-4.1V-9B versus Qwen2.5-VL-72B across multimodal reasoning, charts, screenshot-to-code, and GUI agency](/imgs/blogs/glm-4-1v-thinking-multimodal-reasoning-rl-5.png)

The matrix above is the head-to-head: GLM-4.1V-9B-Thinking versus Qwen2.5-VL-72B, a model *eight times its size*. The 9B model wins on every cell shown — **MMMU-Pro 57.1 vs 51.1** (hard college-level multimodal reasoning), **ChartMuseum 48.8 vs 39.6** (chart understanding), **Design2Code 64.7 vs 41.9** (screenshot-to-code), and **OSWorld 14.9 vs 8.8** (GUI agency). Across the full suite, GLM-4.1V-9B surpasses Qwen2.5-VL-72B on **29 of 42 benchmarks**. The larger GLM-4.5V (on the 106B/12B Air backbone) goes further, matching or beating closed models like Gemini-2.5-Flash on 22 benchmarks.

The GLM-4.5V result is worth a separate note because it tests whether the recipe *scales up* as well as down. GLM-4.1V-Thinking proves the recipe works at 9B; GLM-4.5V (on the much larger MoE Air backbone) proves it keeps working — and keeps winning against strong closed models — at larger scale. This two-point scaling story (9B and 106B/12B, both using the same recipe) is the same kind of evidence the [GLM-4.5 architecture article](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/glm-4-5-architecture) gave with the flagship-and-Air pair: a recipe that works across a wide scale range is a *principle*, not a fluke tuned to one size. The fact that the multimodal recipe, like the text recipe, scales cleanly is part of what justifies calling it a method. And because GLM-4.5V is built on GLM-4.5-Air — the same hybrid-reasoning MoE the [training article](/blog/machine-learning/training-techniques/training-glm-4-5-distillation-agentic-rl) covered — it inherits that model's thinking/non-thinking capability, so the multimodal model can also toggle between deliberate visual reasoning and quick direct answers, which is exactly the flexibility a deployed VLM wants.

What's load-bearing in this result, and what might not transfer? Three caveats:

1. **The win is concentrated in reasoning-heavy benchmarks.** The benchmarks where GLM-4.1V dominates — MMMU-Pro, ChartMuseum, Design2Code — are the ones that require multi-step reasoning over the image, which is exactly what the reasoning RL targets. On pure-perception benchmarks (does the model correctly see what's there), the gap narrows, because perception is what scale and SFT already gave Qwen. The result is best read as "reasoning RL adds what scale alone doesn't," not "9B beats 72B at everything."

This distinction — perception versus reasoning — is the most important lens for reading the results. Qwen2.5-VL-72B is an excellent *perceiver*: at 72B with extensive SFT, it sees what's in an image as well as almost anything. What it lacks, relative to GLM-4.1V-Thinking, is the *reasoning* layer on top of that perception — the careful, step-by-step inference that the reasoning RL instills. So the benchmarks where GLM-4.1V wins big are the ones where perception is necessary but not sufficient and reasoning is the bottleneck: MMMU-Pro (college-level problems that require multi-step inference over the image), ChartMuseum (reasoning about chart data, not just reading it), Design2Code (translating a visual layout into structured code, which requires reasoning about structure). On benchmarks that are mostly perception — recognize this object, read this text — the gap is much smaller, because there the 72B model's superior perception competes with the 9B model's superior reasoning, and perception is what those benchmarks reward. The clean takeaway is that **reasoning RL and scale buy different things**: scale (and SFT) buy perception, reasoning RL buys reasoning, and for the benchmarks that need reasoning, a smaller model with reasoning RL beats a larger one without it. This is the visual confirmation of the same lesson the text models taught — that past a certain point, *how* you post-train matters more than how big you pretrain.

The Design2Code result (64.7 vs 41.9) is worth singling out as the most striking, because the margin is enormous and the task is revealing. Design2Code asks the model to look at a screenshot of a web page and produce the HTML/CSS that renders it — which requires *reasoning about visual structure* (this is a header, this is a two-column layout, these are nav links) and translating it into code. It's a task where pure perception gets you almost nothing (you can see the page perfectly and still produce garbage code) and reasoning about structure is everything. A 23-point margin for the 9B reasoning model over the 72B perception model on exactly this task is the cleanest single piece of evidence in the report that the reasoning RL is doing the work.
2. **The verifier engineering is the hidden cost.** The result rests on having a good verifier for each of eight domains, which is substantial unreported effort. A team without that verifier infrastructure won't reproduce the result by copying the recipe.
3. **The backbone matters.** GLM-4.1V-Thinking inherits GLM-4-9B-0414, an already-strong reasoning backbone. The multimodal reasoning gains build *on* a base model that was already good at text reasoning; you can't necessarily get the same result starting from a weaker LLM. This is the most important caveat for anyone reading the 9B-beats-72B headline and hoping to reproduce it cheaply: the 9B model isn't a generic 9B, it's a 9B that was *already* one of the strongest open reasoning LLMs at its size before any vision was added. The multimodal reasoning is, to a large degree, that text reasoning made visual. Start from a mediocre LLM and you'll get mediocre multimodal reasoning no matter how good your vision encoder and your RL are, because the reasoning capacity simply isn't there to redirect. The practical sequencing lesson: build (or pick) your best reasoning LLM *first*, then add multimodality, rather than trying to train multimodal reasoning from a weak base — the order matters, and the report's result depends on it.

## Critique: the senior-engineer lens

### What's strong

The cross-modal transfer of the recipe is a genuinely important result, and the report demonstrates it cleanly. "The text reasoning recipe works on vision" is the kind of claim that's easy to assert and hard to show; the report shows it with a near-one-to-one correspondence between the text and multimodal pipelines and a 9B-beats-72B result that's hard to explain any other way. The joint-across-eight-domains training is also a strong, non-obvious choice that pays off — it would have been safer to train each domain separately, and the report's willingness to train jointly (and to report the failure mode that comes with it) is the kind of honest, informative engineering the whole lineage shows.

The architecture's restraint is also a strength worth naming. It would have been tempting, building a multimodal reasoner, to innovate on the vision encoder or the fusion mechanism — there's a long literature of clever cross-attention bridges, q-formers, and the like. GLM-4.1V instead uses the boring consensus design (ViT + MLP projector + LLM) and concentrates its novelty in the positional encodings (2D/3D-RoPE for extreme inputs) and the post-training (RLVR + RLCS). This means the result is *attributable*: because the architecture is conventional, the reasoning gains clearly come from the post-training, not from some confounded architectural cleverness. A reader can be confident that "reasoning RL produces visual reasoning" is the lesson, rather than "this specific fusion mechanism plus reasoning RL produces visual reasoning." The restraint is what makes the result a clean demonstration of the recipe's transferability rather than a muddier "this whole system works" claim.

### What's weak or unfalsifiable

![A before-after showing how a single weak per-domain reward can derail the entire unified multi-domain RL run](/imgs/blogs/glm-4-1v-thinking-multimodal-reasoning-rl-7.png)

The central vulnerability is shown above, and the report is admirably direct about it: in a unified multi-domain RL run, **a single weak verifier can derail training for all domains, not just its own.** If one domain's reward is noisy or gameable, the model learns to exploit it, and because the weights are shared, the corruption leaks into the model's general reasoning — degrading the domains that *did* have good rewards. This means the entire approach is only as strong as its *weakest* verifier, which is a demanding requirement: you need a strong, hard-to-game reward for every one of eight diverse domains, and some of those domains (open-ended description, aesthetic judgment) genuinely resist verification. The report's honesty about this is a strength, but it also exposes the approach's limit: it works beautifully where you can verify, and the verifiability is exactly hardest where vision is most subjective. The unfalsifiable-ish edge is that "we built good verifiers for all eight domains" is hard for a reader to audit — the verifier quality is the crux, and it's the least transparent part of the report.

There's a deeper structural concern the weak-verifier problem points to: **the approach has a single point of failure that scales with the number of domains.** Adding a ninth domain doesn't just add the work of building a ninth verifier — it adds the *risk* that the ninth verifier is the weak one that derails everything. So the approach gets harder to extend, not easier, as you add domains, because each new domain is another opportunity for the weakest-link failure. This is the opposite of how you'd want a system to scale, and it's a real limitation: the joint-training benefit (shared reasoning) comes bundled with a joint-training risk (shared failure), and the risk grows with domain count. A team wanting to push into many more domains would need either much more robust verifiers or some mechanism to *isolate* a weak domain's influence (per-domain gradient clipping, or detecting and down-weighting domains where the reward looks gamed) — neither of which the report describes. The honest reading is that the approach works well at eight carefully-verified domains and has an unproven scaling story beyond that.

A second weakness: the report's reasoning traces are evaluated by *outcome*, not by *faithfulness*. The model is rewarded for getting the answer right, which incentivizes a reasoning trace that *leads to* the right answer — but not necessarily a trace that *honestly reflects* how the model actually reached it. As with text reasoning models, there's a real possibility that the `<think>` trace is partly post-hoc rationalization rather than the true computation, and a multimodal model could be especially prone to this (it might "see" the answer perceptually and then generate a plausible-looking reasoning trace to justify it). The report doesn't probe trace faithfulness, which is a meaningful gap if you care about the reasoning being *interpretable* and not just *effective*. For the broader question of whether chain-of-thought reflects real computation, the blog's [is chain-of-thought a mirage](/blog/paper-reading/large-language-model/is-chain-of-thought-reasoning-of-llms-a-mirage-a-data-distribution-lens) read is the companion.

### What ablation is missing

The one I most want: a clean decomposition of how much of the win comes from the *reasoning format* (cold-start SFT on think-then-answer), how much from the *verifiable-reward RL*, and how much from *RLCS* specifically. The report shows the combination works, but a reader trying to port the approach with a limited budget would want to know which piece is most load-bearing — can you get most of the gain from just the format and basic RLVR, or is RLCS essential? The joint-vs-separate-domains ablation is also implied but not fully laid out: how much does joint training actually help versus training each domain separately and merging?

### What would change my mind

If a same-backbone VLM trained with *only* cold-start SFT on the thinking format (no RL) matched GLM-4.1V-Thinking's reasoning benchmarks, I'd downgrade the RL from "load-bearing" to "a refinement on top of a format that did the real work." The report's framing says the RL is essential, and the cross-modal parallel to the text models (where RL is clearly load-bearing) makes that plausible — but the cleanest evidence would be an SFT-only ablation showing how far the format alone gets you, and the report leaves that slightly implicit.

## What I'd build with this

1. **An SFT-only vs RL ablation on a small VLM.** Reproduce the recipe at, say, 2B scale and isolate the contribution of each stage (format SFT, RLVR, RLCS) on a couple of reasoning benchmarks. This is the missing ablation, and at small scale it's runnable — and it would tell you which piece to prioritize when budget is tight.
2. **A verifier toolkit for visual domains.** Since the approach is bottlenecked on per-domain verifiers, a reusable library of verifiers (rule-based extractors for math/OCR/bounding-boxes, calibrated model-judges for the subjective domains) would be the highest-leverage thing to build for anyone wanting to reproduce or extend this. The verifier quality is the crux, so making good verifiers reusable is the force multiplier.
3. **A subjective-domain extension.** The approach's limit is subjective domains that resist verification. Extending RLCS with a *calibrated judge* reward (in the style of [AlignBench](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/how-zhipu-measures-glm-benchmarks)) for the un-verifiable domains, and studying whether a calibrated-judge reward is strong enough to avoid the weak-verifier derailment, would push the approach into the domains it currently can't reach. This is the highest-stakes extension, because the subjective domains (aesthetic judgment, open-ended description, creative visual tasks) are exactly the ones a verifiable-reward approach currently can't serve, and they're a large share of what people actually want from a multimodal model.
4. **Video reasoning specifically.** The 3D-conv + 3D-RoPE machinery for video is the least-explored part of the architecture. A focused study of reasoning over video (temporal reasoning, action understanding) with this stack would test how far the recipe stretches into the hardest modality. Video is interesting precisely because it adds a dimension (time) where reasoning is least mature — answering "what happened, in what order, and why" over a video requires temporal reasoning that's harder to verify and harder to construct training data for than static-image reasoning. If the recipe holds up there, it's strong evidence the method is truly modality-agnostic; if it breaks, the break would tell you something about where reasoning RL's assumptions stop holding.

5. **A trace-faithfulness probe.** Given the concern that the reasoning trace may be post-hoc rationalization, I'd build an experiment that perturbs the image in ways the stated reasoning claims to depend on, and checks whether the answer changes accordingly. If the model says "I see the value is 47 in the third bar" but the answer doesn't change when you edit that bar, the trace isn't faithful. This would turn the faithfulness worry from a hand-wave into a measurement, and it matters for any application where you need to *trust* the model's stated reasoning, not just its answer.

The unifying thread across these is that the report establishes the *recipe transfers* and leaves open the *decomposition* (which piece matters most), the *scaling* (how many domains before the weak-link problem bites), and the *faithfulness* (is the reasoning real). Those are the natural follow-ups, and they're what I'd prioritize building to turn "the recipe works" into "here's exactly why, how far, and how trustworthy."
