There is a moment, somewhere around your third or fourth attempt at training a policy-gradient agent, where you stop trusting your learning curve. It climbs beautifully for two hundred thousand steps — a CartPole agent that finally keeps the pole upright, average return marching from 15 toward 400 — and then in a single update it falls off a cliff back to 9. The policy did not slowly forget. It took one catastrophically large gradient step, moved into a region of parameter space where it had no idea what to do, and never recovered. You restart, lower the learning rate, and the cliff comes back anyway, just later.

That cliff is the central pathology of vanilla policy gradients, and it is the problem Proximal Policy Optimization was built to kill. PPO is, by a wide margin, the most-used deep RL algorithm in production today. It trains the locomotion policies in MuJoCo benchmarks, it played Dota 2 at world-champion level as OpenAI Five, and — the reason most people reading this in 2026 care about it at all — it is the optimizer inside the RLHF loop that turned a raw GPT-style base model into InstructGPT and, downstream, into every aligned chat assistant that followed. When someone says "we fine-tuned the model with RL," nine times out of ten they mean PPO.

This post is a complete working understanding of PPO, built the way the [series](/blog/machine-learning/reinforcement-learning/reinforcement-learning-a-unified-map) builds everything: the theory of *why* the clipped objective stabilizes training, the algorithm in real code you can copy and run, and concrete measured results on named environments. We will derive the clipped surrogate objective and see exactly how it prevents the cliff, work through Generalized Advantage Estimation term by term, write a complete PPO agent in PyTorch from scratch, reproduce the Stable-Baselines3 recipe everyone actually ships, and trace the line from CartPole all the way to RLHF. The figure below is the whole algorithm in one picture — the branching structure of the loss that everything else in this post unpacks.

## The problem PPO solves: policy gradients are fragile

Recall the policy gradient setup. We have a stochastic policy $\pi_\theta(a \mid s)$ — a neural network that maps a state to a distribution over actions — and we want to maximize expected return $J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi_\theta}[R(\tau)]$. The policy gradient theorem gives us a usable gradient:

$$\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi_\theta}\left[\sum_{t} \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)\, A_t\right]$$

where $A_t$ is the advantage — how much better action $a_t$ was than the policy's average behavior at state $s_t$. The intuition is clean: push up the log-probability of actions that did better than expected, push down the ones that did worse. This is the REINFORCE family, and the [policy gradients post](/blog/machine-learning/reinforcement-learning/policy-gradient-methods-from-reinforce-to-actor-critic) covers its derivation in full.

The problem is the word "expectation." That gradient is an expectation over trajectories drawn *from the current policy*. The instant you take a gradient step, $\theta$ changes, so $\pi_\theta$ changes, so the data you just collected is now off-policy — it was generated by a policy that no longer exists. Vanilla policy gradient handles this by throwing the data away after one step and collecting fresh rollouts. That is staggeringly sample-inefficient: you do an enormous amount of environment interaction and extract a single gradient step from it.

To make this concrete, it helps to remember *why* the policy gradient is high-variance in the first place. It is a Monte Carlo estimate of the score-function gradient: you sample a handful of trajectories, multiply each action's log-probability gradient by the return that followed, and average. The return that follows any single action is a sum of many stochastic future rewards, so the per-sample signal is enormously noisy. A baseline (subtracting $V(s_t)$ to form the advantage) cuts the variance, but it does not eliminate it, and with a small batch the gradient direction can point almost anywhere on any given update. This is fine if every step is tiny and you average over enough updates. It becomes catastrophic the moment a single update is allowed to be large — and reusing data for multiple epochs is precisely a recipe for large updates, because you keep stepping in the same noisy direction.

There is also a second, subtler issue that motivates the entire trust-region family. The objective we care about, expected return, is a highly non-linear function of the policy parameters, and the surrogate we actually optimize (the importance-weighted advantage) is only a *local* approximation to it — accurate when the new policy is close to the old one, and increasingly meaningless as they diverge. Optimizing a local approximation past the region where it is valid is the optimization equivalent of trusting a Taylor expansion far from the expansion point. You will happily maximize a quantity that no longer has anything to do with the thing you wanted. Both TRPO and PPO are, at heart, mechanisms for refusing to leave the region where the surrogate is trustworthy.

It is worth being precise about where that surrogate comes from, because the precision is what makes the clip make sense. The quantity we would love to maximize is the *performance difference* between the new policy and the old one. A foundational identity (the one TRPO is built on) writes the new policy's expected return as the old policy's return plus an expectation, over states visited by the *new* policy, of the new policy's advantage measured against the old value function. That state distribution under the new policy is the inconvenient part — we have not run the new policy, so we cannot sample from its state distribution. The standard move is to approximate it with the *old* policy's state distribution, which turns the intractable object into the importance-weighted surrogate $\mathbb{E}_{s,a \sim \pi_{old}}[\,r(\theta)\,A^{\pi_{old}}\,]$ with $r(\theta) = \pi_\theta / \pi_{old}$. That approximation is exact when the two policies are identical and degrades smoothly as they separate. The size of the error is bounded by — you guessed it — a term proportional to the KL divergence between the two policies. So the surrogate is trustworthy *precisely to the extent that the policies are close*, and the entire trust-region program is the discipline of not optimizing the surrogate past the point where its bound has gone slack.

The natural fix is to reuse each batch of data for several gradient steps. But now you have a different problem. After a few steps the policy has drifted far from the one that collected the data, the importance-sampling correction needed to keep the estimate valid blows up, and a single minibatch with a large advantage and a large probability ratio can shove the policy into a region it has never explored. That is the cliff. The whole history of TRPO and PPO is the search for a way to *reuse data for multiple steps without letting the policy move too far in any one update*.

#### Worked example: how one step causes the cliff

Suppose at state $s$ the old policy assigned action $a$ probability $\pi_{old}(a \mid s) = 0.2$, and the measured advantage was $A = +4.0$ (a genuinely good action). The vanilla surrogate objective we maximize is the importance-weighted advantage:

$$L(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(a \mid s)}{\pi_{old}(a \mid s)} A_t = r_t(\theta)\, A_t$$

Gradient ascent happily increases $\pi_\theta(a \mid s)$ because $A$ is positive. Nothing in the objective stops it. After a handful of minibatch epochs the network might push $\pi_\theta(a \mid s)$ to 0.95, giving a ratio $r_t = 0.95 / 0.2 = 4.75$. The objective rewards this — bigger ratio, bigger value — but the policy has now collapsed almost all its probability mass onto one action it observed *once*, in *one* state, with a noisy advantage estimate. Entropy craters, exploration stops, and the next rollout reveals the action was not that good after all. Return falls off the cliff. The cure is to make the objective *stop caring* once the ratio leaves a trust band — and that is exactly what clipping does.

To make the failure quantitative: with the unclipped objective $r_t A_t = 4.75 \times 4.0 = 19.0$, and the gradient $\partial L / \partial r_t = A_t = 4.0$ is *constant and positive* no matter how large $r_t$ gets. There is no diminishing return, no satiation — the objective slope at $r_t = 4.75$ is identical to the slope at $r_t = 1.0$. The optimizer is being told "keep going, the reward per unit of ratio never drops." That is the mathematical statement of the cliff: an unbounded linear incentive paired with multi-epoch reuse of a single noisy sample.

## TRPO in one paragraph, and why we want something cheaper

Trust Region Policy Optimization (Schulman et al., 2015) solved the fragility problem rigorously. It maximizes the same surrogate objective but subject to a hard constraint that the KL divergence between the new and old policies stays below a small threshold $\delta$: $\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{KL}(\pi_{old} \,\|\, \pi_\theta)] \le \delta$. This guarantees the policy never moves too far, which guarantees monotonic improvement under some assumptions. The catch is the machinery. To solve a KL-constrained optimization at every update, TRPO computes the natural gradient using the conjugate gradient algorithm (which needs Fisher-vector products through the network) and then does a backtracking line search to find the largest step that respects the constraint. It works, but it is a mouthful to implement, it does not play nicely with architectures that share parameters between policy and value heads, and it does not vectorize over many parallel environments as cleanly as you would like.

PPO's thesis, in one line: *can we get TRPO's stability from a simpler first-order objective that any SGD optimizer can handle?* The answer is yes, and the trick is to bake the trust region directly into the loss function instead of enforcing it as a constraint. No conjugate gradient, no line search, no Fisher matrix. Just a clipped objective and ordinary Adam.

It is worth dwelling on *why* TRPO's implementation cost is so high, because it explains the entire design pressure that produced PPO. The KL constraint $\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{KL}(\pi_{old} \,\|\, \pi_\theta)] \le \delta$ defines a curved region in parameter space — its shape is governed by the Fisher information matrix, which captures how sensitively the policy distribution responds to parameter changes. To take the largest improving step that stays inside that region, TRPO needs the natural gradient $F^{-1} g$, where $F$ is the Fisher matrix and $g$ is the ordinary policy gradient. The Fisher matrix for a modern network has millions of rows and columns, so you never form it explicitly; instead you solve $F x = g$ iteratively with conjugate gradient, where each iteration needs a Fisher-vector product (a second backward pass through a KL term). Then, because the quadratic approximation that justified the step might be inaccurate, you backtrack along the proposed direction with a line search, shrinking the step until the *actual* measured KL is within $\delta$ and the surrogate actually improved. Every one of those pieces is a place where an implementation bug hides silently, and the whole thing assumes a clean separation between the policy you are differentiating and the value baseline. Sharing a network trunk between actor and critic — which you very much want to do for sample efficiency — muddies the Fisher computation. PPO throws all of it out and replaces "find the largest step inside a curved trust region" with "take ordinary gradient steps on an objective that goes flat once you have moved far enough." It is a profound simplification, and the remarkable empirical fact is that it costs you almost nothing in final performance.

## The clipped surrogate objective

Here is the entire idea. Define the probability ratio:

$$r_t(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)}{\pi_{\theta_{old}}(a_t \mid s_t)}$$

At the start of an update, $\theta = \theta_{old}$ so $r_t = 1$ everywhere. As we take gradient steps, $r_t$ drifts. The clipped surrogate objective PPO maximizes is:

$$L^{CLIP}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_t\left[\min\left(r_t(\theta)\, A_t,\; \mathrm{clip}(r_t(\theta),\, 1-\epsilon,\, 1+\epsilon)\, A_t\right)\right]$$

with $\epsilon$ typically 0.2. Read this slowly, because the genius is entirely in the interaction of the `min` and the `clip`.

There are two terms inside the `min`. The first, $r_t A_t$, is the ordinary surrogate. The second, $\mathrm{clip}(r_t, 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon) A_t$, is the surrogate with the ratio forced into the band $[0.8, 1.2]$. We take the *minimum* of the two. The minimum is what makes the objective pessimistic in exactly the right way.

### Deriving the clip case by case

Let us prove, rather than assert, that this objective is a pessimistic lower bound on the unclipped surrogate and that it kills the incentive for runaway updates. Write the per-sample objective as $\ell_t(\theta) = \min(r A,\; \mathrm{clip}(r, 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon) A)$, suppressing the $t$ subscript on $r$ for readability. The behavior splits cleanly on the sign of $A$, and the two cases must be analyzed separately because the `clip` is symmetric in $r$ but the `min` interacts with the sign of $A$ asymmetrically.

**Case 1: $A_t > 0$ (good action, we want $r$ to rise).** The clipped ratio $\mathrm{clip}(r, 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon)$ is never larger than $1+\epsilon$. Multiplying by the positive $A$, the clipped term $\mathrm{clip}(r,\cdot,\cdot)A$ is never larger than $(1+\epsilon)A$. Now consider three sub-regions for $r$:

- $r \le 1-\epsilon$: clip returns $1-\epsilon$, so the clipped term is $(1-\epsilon)A$. Is $rA$ or $(1-\epsilon)A$ smaller? Since $r \le 1-\epsilon$ and $A>0$, we have $rA \le (1-\epsilon)A$, so the `min` picks the *unclipped* term $rA$. The gradient flows: $\partial \ell / \partial r = A > 0$. Good — we are below the band and being pushed up.
- $1-\epsilon < r < 1+\epsilon$ (inside the band): clip is the identity, both terms equal $rA$, the `min` is $rA$, gradient $A>0$ flows. Normal learning.
- $r \ge 1+\epsilon$: clip returns $1+\epsilon$, the clipped term is the constant $(1+\epsilon)A$. Since $r \ge 1+\epsilon$ gives $rA \ge (1+\epsilon)A$, the `min` picks the *clipped* constant. Its derivative with respect to $r$ — and therefore with respect to $\theta$ — is **zero**. The incentive to push further evaporates exactly at the boundary.

So for a good action the objective rises linearly with $r$ until $r = 1+\epsilon$, then flattens to a horizontal line. The optimizer is rewarded for increasing the action's probability *by up to 20%* and not one bit more. This is the proof that PPO cannot produce the 4.75 ratio from the worked example above: past $1.2$ the gradient is identically zero.

**Case 2: $A_t < 0$ (bad action, we want $r$ to fall).** Now multiplying by a negative $A$ flips the inequalities. The clipped term is never *more negative* than $(1-\epsilon)A$ (since clip floors $r$ at $1-\epsilon$, and a smaller $r$ times negative $A$ is a larger product). Again three sub-regions:

- $r \ge 1+\epsilon$: clip returns $1+\epsilon$, clipped term $(1+\epsilon)A$ (very negative). With $A<0$ and $r \ge 1+\epsilon$, $rA \le (1+\epsilon)A$, so the `min` picks the unclipped $rA$, gradient $A<0$ flows — pushing $r$ down. Good, the action is too likely and we are correcting it.
- inside the band: identity, gradient flows normally.
- $r \le 1-\epsilon$: clip returns $1-\epsilon$, clipped term is the constant $(1-\epsilon)A$. With $A<0$ and $r \le 1-\epsilon$, $rA \ge (1-\epsilon)A$, so the `min` selects the *clipped constant*, derivative **zero**. We have cut the bad action's probability by 20% and the objective refuses to reward cutting it further.

It is worth pausing on the asymmetry between the two cases, because it is the cleanest statement of what "trust region" means here. In Case 1 the ceiling is at $1+\epsilon$: a good action can have its probability inflated by at most $\epsilon$ before the gradient cuts out. In Case 2 the floor is at $1-\epsilon$: a bad action can have its probability deflated by at most $\epsilon$. But notice what the clip does *not* bound. A bad action whose ratio is being driven down has no lower clip that stops it before $1-\epsilon$ — once below the floor the gradient is zero, but a *single* large negative-advantage sample can still drive $r$ toward zero within one step if the learning rate is large, because the unclipped branch governs the approach to the floor. This is the practical reason a too-high learning rate breaks PPO even with the clip: the clip bounds the *objective's reward* for moving, not the *parameter step's magnitude*. The clip removes the incentive past the band; it is Adam plus the gradient-norm clip that bound how far a single step actually travels. The two mechanisms are complementary, and dropping either is a common cause of an otherwise-correct PPO going unstable.

The two cases together establish the central claim: $L^{CLIP}$ is a lower bound on the unclipped surrogate $rA$ everywhere (the `min` can only ever return something $\le rA$), and the gradient is switched off precisely in the two regions — good action overshooting up, bad action overshooting down — where continuing would constitute a too-large move. That is what "pessimistic bound" means concretely: we optimize a floor on the true surrogate, and the floor goes flat exactly where the true surrogate would have led us astray.

The single subtlety that trips everyone up: the `min` is deliberately *not symmetric* with respect to the direction of the mistake. When the action was good and the ratio overshoots ($r > 1+\epsilon$), we clip — we refuse the reward. But when the action was good and the ratio went the *wrong* way ($r < 1-\epsilon$, i.e. we accidentally made a good action much less likely), the unclipped term $rA$ is smaller and gets selected, so the full gradient flows to fix the mistake. The clip only ever *removes* the incentive to move further in a direction we have already moved enough; it never removes the incentive to correct a move in the wrong direction. That asymmetry is why PPO is a one-sided trust region — it is fine to undo too-large moves, never fine to make them.

This is a far cry from a true KL constraint. PPO does not actually guarantee the KL stays small; a single minibatch can produce a large KL if many samples have ratios on the same side. In practice this rarely matters, and most implementations add a KL early-stopping heuristic (stop the epoch loop if the measured KL exceeds, say, $1.5 \times$ a target) as a cheap safety net. But the clipped objective alone, with ordinary Adam, recovers the bulk of TRPO's stability for a tiny fraction of the implementation cost.

#### Worked example: the gradient goes to zero at the clip boundary

Let us trace the gradient through a single good action so the flattening is unmistakable. Suppose $A_t = +2.0$ and we are partway through the epoch loop, with the current ratio at $r_t = 1.35$ — already past the upper clip of $1 + \epsilon = 1.2$. The two terms are $\text{surr1} = r_t A_t = 1.35 \times 2.0 = 2.70$ and $\text{surr2} = \mathrm{clip}(1.35, 0.8, 1.2) \times 2.0 = 1.2 \times 2.0 = 2.40$. Since $A_t > 0$, the clipped term is smaller, so $\min(2.70, 2.40) = 2.40$ — the constant. The derivative of a constant with respect to $\theta$ is zero. So for *this sample*, on *this step*, the actor receives no gradient at all: PPO has decided this action has been promoted enough and refuses to push it further. Contrast with a sample where $A_t = +2.0$ but $r_t = 1.05$ (inside the band): there $\min(2.10, 2.10) = 2.10$ comes from the unclipped term, the gradient flows normally, and the action's probability keeps rising. The clip is a per-sample, per-step gate that selectively switches off the samples that have already moved too far, while leaving the rest of the batch learning. That selectivity is why one rollout can survive ten epochs of updates without the policy running away.

## PPO-Clip versus PPO-KL

The 2017 paper actually proposed two variants. PPO-Clip is the one above. PPO-KL (the "adaptive KL penalty" variant) keeps the unclipped surrogate but subtracts a penalty proportional to the KL divergence, and *adapts the penalty coefficient* $\beta$ between updates: if the measured KL came out higher than a target $d_{targ}$, increase $\beta$ to penalize harder next time; if it came out lower, relax $\beta$.

$$L^{KLPEN}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_t\left[ r_t(\theta) A_t - \beta\, \mathrm{KL}\big(\pi_{\theta_{old}}(\cdot \mid s_t)\,\|\,\pi_\theta(\cdot \mid s_t)\big)\right]$$

The adaptive schedule is the part the paper actually pins down, and it is worth writing out because it is the same machinery RLHF reuses. After each policy update you measure the empirical KL $d = \hat{\mathbb{E}}_t[\mathrm{KL}]$ over the batch, then:

- if $d < d_{targ} / 1.5$, the penalty was too harsh — the policy barely moved — so halve it: $\beta \leftarrow \beta / 2$;
- if $d > d_{targ} \times 1.5$, the policy moved too far — so double it: $\beta \leftarrow \beta \times 2$;
- otherwise leave $\beta$ alone.

The constants $1.5$ and $2$ are not sacred; the paper notes the algorithm is insensitive to them. The point is that $\beta$ is a *controller* chasing a KL setpoint, not a fixed hyperparameter you tune by hand. The trouble is that this is a feedback loop with its own dynamics — $\beta$ can lag, overshoot, and oscillate — and you have introduced a target $d_{targ}$ to choose. The clip variant has no such loop and no $\beta$; the trust region is enforced sample-by-sample inside the loss with a single fixed $\epsilon$.

The paper's own ablations found PPO-Clip performed as well or better than PPO-KL across their benchmark suite, and it has one fewer moving part (no $\beta$ schedule to tune). So PPO-Clip won, and when people say "PPO" today they almost always mean PPO-Clip. There is one important exception: **RLHF**. The TRL `PPOTrainer` and the original InstructGPT setup use an *adaptive KL penalty against a frozen reference policy* — not the rollout-collecting old policy, but the supervised-fine-tuned model — to keep the language model from drifting into reward-hacked gibberish. That KL term is conceptually the PPO-KL variant, repurposed as an alignment leash rather than a trust region. We will return to it.

| Variant | Trust mechanism | Extra hyperparameter | Where it wins |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| PPO-Clip | Clip ratio to $[1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon]$ | $\epsilon$ (fixed) | General-purpose RL, control, games |
| PPO-KL (adaptive) | Penalty $\beta \cdot \mathrm{KL}$, $\beta$ adapted to a target | $d_{targ}$, $\beta$ schedule | RLHF (KL against a frozen reference) |

## Generalized Advantage Estimation

The objective needs advantages $A_t$, and how you estimate them dominates PPO's variance. This is where Generalized Advantage Estimation (Schulman et al., 2016) earns its place as the default. GAE is, frankly, half of why PPO works as well as it does.

Start with the building block, the one-step TD residual:

$$\delta_t = r_t + \gamma V(s_{t+1}) - V(s_t)$$

This is the temporal-difference error — the reward you got plus the discounted value of where you landed, minus the value of where you were. It is a one-step, low-variance, but biased estimate of the advantage (biased because it leans entirely on the learned value function $V$, which is wrong early in training).

At the other extreme, you could estimate the advantage from the full discounted return minus the baseline: low bias (it uses real rewards all the way to the end of the episode) but high variance (the sum of many noisy rewards). GAE interpolates between these two extremes with an exponentially weighted sum of TD residuals:

$$A_t^{GAE(\gamma, \lambda)} = \sum_{l=0}^{\infty} (\gamma\lambda)^l\, \delta_{t+l}$$

The parameter $\lambda \in [0, 1]$ is the knob. At $\lambda = 0$, the sum collapses to $A_t = \delta_t$ — pure one-step TD, low variance, high bias. At $\lambda = 1$, it becomes the full Monte Carlo advantage, high variance, low bias. In between, you get a smooth blend: each TD residual further into the future is downweighted by $(\gamma\lambda)^l$, so near-term residuals dominate but long-term signal still leaks in. The default $\lambda = 0.95$ keeps most of the variance reduction of short horizons while retaining enough long-horizon signal to not be badly biased. It is one of the most robust default hyperparameters in all of deep RL.

### The derivation: why the geometric weighting is the right one

It is worth seeing why the geometric weighting is the *right* way to interpolate, not just *a* way. Define the $k$-step advantage estimator that uses $k$ real rewards and then bootstraps off the value function:

$$\hat{A}_t^{(k)} = -V(s_t) + r_t + \gamma r_{t+1} + \dots + \gamma^{k-1} r_{t+k-1} + \gamma^k V(s_{t+k})$$

Each of these is a valid advantage estimate. The small-$k$ ones lean heavily on the (biased) value function and have low variance; the large-$k$ ones use more real rewards and have higher variance. Crucially, each $\hat{A}_t^{(k)}$ can be written as a partial sum of discounted TD residuals — telescope the value terms and you find $\hat{A}_t^{(k)} = \sum_{l=0}^{k-1} \gamma^l \delta_{t+l}$. GAE is then defined as the exponentially weighted average of *all* the $k$-step estimators:

$$\hat{A}_t^{GAE} = (1-\lambda)\big(\hat{A}_t^{(1)} + \lambda \hat{A}_t^{(2)} + \lambda^2 \hat{A}_t^{(3)} + \dots\big)$$

The $(1-\lambda)$ prefactor is the normalizer so the weights $\{(1-\lambda)\lambda^{k-1}\}$ sum to one. Substitute each $\hat{A}_t^{(k)} = \sum_{l=0}^{k-1}\gamma^l \delta_{t+l}$ and collect the coefficient of a fixed residual $\delta_{t+l}$. That residual appears in every estimator with $k > l$, so its total coefficient is $\gamma^l (1-\lambda)(\lambda^l + \lambda^{l+1} + \dots) = \gamma^l (1-\lambda)\lambda^l / (1-\lambda) = (\gamma\lambda)^l$. The normalizer cancels exactly, and what remains is precisely the discounted sum of TD residuals $\sum_l (\gamma\lambda)^l \delta_{t+l}$ from above.

The exponential weighting is therefore not arbitrary — it is the unique scheme under which the recursion $A_t = \delta_t + \gamma\lambda A_{t+1}$ holds, which is what lets you compute it in a single backward pass. The choice of $\lambda$ is then a direct dial on *how much you trust your value function*: a well-trained critic justifies a lower $\lambda$ (lean on it, cut variance); a critic you do not trust yet justifies a higher $\lambda$ (lean on real rewards, accept variance).

#### Worked example: λ on a three-step rollout

Let us make the bias-variance trade numerical. Suppose a short rollout produced TD residuals $\delta_t = 0.5$, $\delta_{t+1} = -2.0$, $\delta_{t+2} = 3.0$, with $\gamma = 0.99$. Take three settings of $\lambda$ and compute $A_t = \sum_l (\gamma\lambda)^l \delta_{t+l}$:

- $\lambda = 0$: $A_t = \delta_t = 0.5$. The estimate ignores the future entirely and trusts $V(s_{t+1})$ to summarize it. Low variance — it is one number — but if $V$ is wrong, that 0.5 is wrong, and notice it completely discards the genuine $+3.0$ signal two steps out.
- $\lambda = 0.95$: $\gamma\lambda = 0.9405$. $A_t = 0.5 + 0.9405(-2.0) + 0.9405^2(3.0) = 0.5 - 1.881 + 2.654 = 1.273$. Now the later residuals pull the estimate up substantially — the $+3.0$ two steps out is weighted by $0.88$ and changes the sign of the conclusion about this state. This is the blend most runs use.
- $\lambda = 1.0$: $\gamma\lambda = 0.99$. $A_t = 0.5 + 0.99(-2.0) + 0.99^2(3.0) = 0.5 - 1.98 + 2.94 = 1.46$. Full Monte Carlo (over this window): maximal use of real reward signal, but in a long noisy episode this sum keeps accumulating variance from every future residual.

The three numbers — $0.5$, $1.27$, $1.46$ — are the same quantity estimated with progressively more trust in observed rewards and less in the value function. The spread between them *is* the bias-variance trade-off made visible: at $\lambda=0$ you are confident but possibly biased; at $\lambda=1$ you are unbiased but at the mercy of every future reward's noise. The default $0.95$ deliberately sits where the variance reduction is still large but the bias is still small.

The reason GAE matters so much for PPO specifically: PPO reuses each rollout batch for many epochs, so any bias or variance baked into the advantages gets amplified across all those updates. A noisy advantage estimate (low $\lambda$ done wrong, or no GAE at all) means every one of the ten epochs is chasing noise. GAE gives you advantages clean enough to be worth optimizing against repeatedly.

The practical computation runs backward through the rollout, which makes it an $O(T)$ recursion rather than a naive $O(T^2)$ double sum:

$$A_t = \delta_t + \gamma\lambda\, A_{t+1}$$

with $A_{T} = \delta_{T}$ at the last step (and the bootstrap masked at episode boundaries). Here is the canonical implementation:

```python
import numpy as np

def compute_gae(rewards, values, dones, last_value, gamma=0.99, lam=0.95):
    """rewards, values, dones are arrays of length T over one rollout.
    last_value is V(s_T) used to bootstrap the final step."""
    T = len(rewards)
    advantages = np.zeros(T, dtype=np.float32)
    gae = 0.0
    # values augmented with the bootstrap value at the end
    values_ext = np.append(values, last_value)
    for t in reversed(range(T)):
        # if the step ended an episode, do not bootstrap past it
        mask = 1.0 - dones[t]
        delta = rewards[t] + gamma * values_ext[t + 1] * mask - values_ext[t]
        gae = delta + gamma * lam * mask * gae
        advantages[t] = gae
    returns = advantages + values  # value targets for the critic
    return advantages, returns
```

Two details that bite people. First, the `mask = 1.0 - dones[t]` term zeros out the bootstrap when a step ended an episode — without it, the advantage of the last step of episode A leaks the value of the first step of episode B, which is nonsense. Second, the `returns = advantages + values` line: the value-function targets are the advantages plus the old value estimates, which is the GAE-consistent way to train the critic (it is the same as a $\lambda$-return). Computing returns some other way (e.g. raw discounted reward-to-go) and pairing them with GAE advantages is a subtle, common, and performance-killing bug.

## The PPO training loop in full

Now we can assemble the loop. PPO is an on-policy actor-critic algorithm with a specific cadence: collect a fixed-size rollout with the current policy, freeze that policy as $\pi_{old}$, compute GAE advantages, then do several epochs of minibatch SGD on the combined loss, and finally let the just-updated policy become the new rollout collector. The combined objective for a shared actor-critic network is:

$$L(\theta) = \underbrace{L^{CLIP}(\theta)}_{\text{actor}} - c_1 \underbrace{L^{VF}(\theta)}_{\text{critic}} + c_2 \underbrace{S[\pi_\theta]}_{\text{entropy bonus}}$$

where $L^{VF} = (V_\theta(s_t) - V_t^{targ})^2$ is the value-function MSE, $S[\pi_\theta]$ is the policy entropy, and $c_1 \approx 0.5$, $c_2 \approx 0.01$ are weights. We maximize this, so the value loss is subtracted and the entropy bonus added.

![Stack diagram of one PPO iteration layering rollout collection, GAE computation, minibatch sampling, the combined clipped loss, the Adam step repeated over epochs, and the old-policy refresh](/imgs/blogs/proximal-policy-optimization-ppo-3.png)

The structure of one iteration:

1. **Rollout.** Run the current policy in the environment (often many parallel environments) for $N$ steps — `n_steps=2048` is the SB3 default per environment. Store states, actions, rewards, dones, the log-probabilities of the actions taken, and the value estimates. The stored log-probs *are* $\log \pi_{old}$; you freeze them here so the ratio is computable later.
2. **Advantages.** Run GAE over the rollout to get $A_t$ and value targets. Normalize advantages to zero mean and unit variance across the batch — this is not optional in practice; it stabilizes the gradient scale enormously.
3. **Optimize.** For `n_epochs` (default 10) passes over the rollout: shuffle, split into minibatches of `batch_size` (default 64), and for each minibatch recompute $\pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)$ and $V_\theta(s_t)$ under the *current* (being-updated) parameters, form the ratio against the stored old log-probs, compute the clipped loss, value loss, and entropy, and take an Adam step. Clip the global gradient norm to 0.5.
4. **Refresh.** The updated policy becomes the collector for the next rollout. The old log-probs from step 1 are discarded; new ones are recorded.

### Why PPO gets multiple epochs and A2C/TRPO do not

This is the single most important structural fact about PPO, and it is worth being explicit. A2C (synchronous advantage actor-critic) does exactly *one* gradient step per rollout, then throws the data away — it is essentially the vanilla policy gradient with a learned baseline. TRPO also does a single (carefully sized) update per batch. PPO does $K$ epochs — typically 10 — of minibatch SGD over the *same* rollout, which means it extracts `n_epochs * (n_steps / batch_size)` gradient steps from one batch of environment interaction. That data reuse is the entire source of PPO's sample-efficiency advantage over A2C.

Why can PPO get away with it when A2C cannot? Because of the ratio and the clip. On the *first* epoch, the parameters still equal $\theta_{old}$, so $r_t = 1$ for every sample and PPO's update is identical to a plain advantage-weighted policy gradient step — nothing is clipped yet. But on the second, third, and tenth epochs, the parameters have drifted, the data is now genuinely off-policy, and $r_t$ has moved away from 1. The importance ratio $r_t$ is exactly the correction that keeps the off-policy gradient valid, and the clip is exactly what stops the correction from exploding when a sample's ratio wanders too far. A2C has no ratio and no clip, so its update is only valid for on-policy data — it *must* discard after one step or its gradient becomes silently wrong. PPO turned "you may reuse this data" into a controlled operation: reuse it, correct with the ratio, and refuse to act on any sample whose ratio has left the trust band.

The drift is real and worth picturing. Right after a rollout, clip fraction is essentially zero (every $r_t \approx 1$). As epochs accumulate, more and more samples push their ratio to the $1\pm\epsilon$ boundary, the clip fraction climbs, and the *measured* KL between the current policy and $\pi_{old}$ grows. This is precisely why the **early-stopping trick** exists: many implementations (including SB3 via `target_kl`) compute the approximate KL after each epoch and break out of the epoch loop early if it exceeds a threshold. The first few epochs do most of the useful learning while the data is still fresh; the last few epochs, with the policy already drifted, are where a runaway update is most likely, so cutting them short is cheap insurance. If you set `n_epochs` very high and watch `approx_kl`, you will see it grow monotonically across epochs within a single update — that growth is the cost of data reuse, and the clip plus early stopping is the bound on that cost.

The reason this is sample-efficient relative to vanilla PG: step 3 extracts hundreds of gradient updates from a single rollout, and the clip is what makes reusing that increasingly-off-policy data safe.

## The entropy bonus, and what entropy collapse looks like

The entropy term $c_2 S[\pi_\theta]$ adds the policy's entropy to the objective, which rewards the policy for staying stochastic. Entropy of a categorical policy is $S = H(\pi) = -\sum_a \pi(a) \log \pi(a)$, maximized by the uniform distribution and zero for a deterministic policy. For $n$ discrete actions the maximum is $\ln n$ (so $\ln 2 \approx 0.69$ for two actions, $\ln 4 \approx 1.39$ for four). Adding a slice of it to the maximization objective gently resists premature determinism — it keeps the agent exploring while the advantages are still telling it useful things. The gradient of the entropy term pushes probability mass *away* from whatever action the clipped surrogate is concentrating on, so the two terms are in a deliberate tug-of-war: the surrogate sharpens the policy toward high-advantage actions, the entropy bonus flattens it back out, and `ent_coef` sets who wins.

The failure mode the bonus prevents is **entropy collapse**, and it is worth recognizing on sight because it is the single most common way a PPO run silently dies. Early in training, by luck, the policy finds one action that scores slightly above average. With $c_2 = 0$ or too small, the clipped objective happily concentrates probability on it, entropy drops toward zero, the policy becomes nearly deterministic, and exploration stops. From then on every rollout looks the same, every advantage is near zero, and the agent is frozen at a mediocre return — the learning curve flatlines at, say, 80 on CartPole and never moves. If you log policy entropy (you should always log policy entropy), you will see it fall off a cliff just before the return curve goes flat. The fix is to raise `ent_coef`; on sparse-reward or hard-exploration tasks people go as high as 0.01–0.1, where on dense easy tasks 0.0 is fine. Typical values: `0.0` for CartPole and most MuJoCo dense-reward tasks, `0.01` as a common default for Atari, and `0.01–0.1` for sparse or hard-exploration environments.

#### Worked example: reading an entropy curve

You train PPO on `LunarLander-v3` and the mean episode return climbs to 120 over 300k steps, then plateaus and even sags to 90, never reaching the ~200 that counts as solved. You pull up the entropy log: it started at 1.38 (close to $\ln 4$, the maximum for 4 discrete actions, meaning near-uniform) and dropped to 0.05 by 250k steps. That 0.05 is the smoking gun — the policy went nearly deterministic before it had actually found a good landing strategy. You bump `ent_coef` from 0.0 to 0.01 and re-run; entropy now holds around 0.4 through 400k steps, the agent keeps trying variations, and return reaches 235. The entropy bonus bought you the exploration you needed at the cost of slightly slower early progress. That trade is almost always worth it on anything harder than CartPole.

## Value function clipping

Because PPO reuses each rollout for many epochs, not just the policy but the *value function* can move too far within a single batch — and a value function that lurches around produces unstable advantage targets next iteration. A common (optional) trick mirrors the policy clip on the value side. Instead of plain MSE, you clip the value prediction to stay within $\epsilon$ of the old prediction:

$$L^{VF} = \max\left( (V_\theta - V^{targ})^2,\; (\,\mathrm{clip}(V_\theta, V_{old}-\epsilon, V_{old}+\epsilon) - V^{targ})^2 \right)$$

Note the `max` (we are minimizing the value loss, so the pessimistic choice is the larger error) — it is the mirror image of the policy clip's `min`. The logic is the same one-sided trust-region logic: the clipped value prediction is held within $\epsilon$ of $V_{old}$, and the `max` makes sure that if the unclipped prediction has moved far enough to *reduce* the loss artificially, we instead pay the larger error of the clipped term. This matters most with a *shared backbone* between actor and critic: there, a large value-loss gradient flows through the same trunk that the policy depends on, so a value function lurching across ten epochs can corrupt the features the actor is using, indirectly destabilizing the policy even though the policy clip is doing its job. Clipping the value bounds that backbone perturbation.

This is `clip_range_vf` in SB3 and it is on by default in many implementations. Honestly, its benefit is modest and benchmark-dependent; some careful studies (the "What Matters in On-Policy RL" line of work) found value clipping to be one of the *less* important PPO implementation details, well behind advantage normalization and reward scaling. Know it exists; do not lose sleep over tuning it.

A useful way to rank the implementation details by how much they actually move the needle, distilled from the empirical studies: **advantage normalization** and **reward scaling/clipping** are first-tier — turning them off measurably degrades nearly every task. **Orthogonal weight initialization** with a small gain on the policy head (so the initial policy is close to uniform) and **learning-rate annealing** are second-tier — reliable wins that are cheap to include. **Global gradient-norm clipping** at 0.5 is a third-tier safety belt that rarely hurts and occasionally saves a run from a single bad batch. **Value-function clipping**, **shared versus separate actor-critic trunks**, and the exact **entropy coefficient on dense-reward tasks** are fourth-tier — their effect is small and task-dependent, and reasonable people ship PPO with any combination of them. The practical takeaway is to spend your tuning budget on the learning rate, `clip_range`, `n_epochs`, and `ent_coef`, leave advantage normalization and gradient clipping permanently on, and treat the rest as defaults you only revisit when a diagnostic tells you to.

## A complete PPO in PyTorch from scratch

Here is a full, runnable PPO for a discrete-action Gymnasium environment. It is deliberately compact but complete — actor-critic network, rollout buffer, GAE, clipped update with all three loss terms. This is the thing to read until the math above becomes muscle memory.

```python
import gymnasium as gym
import numpy as np
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
from torch.distributions import Categorical

class ActorCritic(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, obs_dim, n_actions, hidden=64):
        super().__init__()
        self.shared = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(obs_dim, hidden), nn.Tanh(),
            nn.Linear(hidden, hidden), nn.Tanh(),
        )
        self.actor = nn.Linear(hidden, n_actions)   # logits over actions
        self.critic = nn.Linear(hidden, 1)           # state value V(s)

    def forward(self, x):
        h = self.shared(x)
        return self.actor(h), self.critic(h).squeeze(-1)

    def act(self, x):
        logits, value = self.forward(x)
        dist = Categorical(logits=logits)
        action = dist.sample()
        return action, dist.log_prob(action), value

    def evaluate(self, x, action):
        logits, value = self.forward(x)
        dist = Categorical(logits=logits)
        return dist.log_prob(action), dist.entropy(), value
```

The rollout buffer just accumulates one batch and then computes GAE over it:

```python
class RolloutBuffer:
    def __init__(self):
        self.reset()

    def reset(self):
        self.obs, self.actions, self.logprobs = [], [], []
        self.rewards, self.dones, self.values = [], [], []

    def add(self, obs, action, logprob, reward, done, value):
        self.obs.append(obs); self.actions.append(action)
        self.logprobs.append(logprob); self.rewards.append(reward)
        self.dones.append(done); self.values.append(value)

    def compute_gae(self, last_value, gamma=0.99, lam=0.95):
        rewards = np.array(self.rewards, dtype=np.float32)
        values = np.array(self.values, dtype=np.float32)
        dones = np.array(self.dones, dtype=np.float32)
        adv = np.zeros_like(rewards)
        gae, next_value = 0.0, last_value
        for t in reversed(range(len(rewards))):
            mask = 1.0 - dones[t]
            delta = rewards[t] + gamma * next_value * mask - values[t]
            gae = delta + gamma * lam * mask * gae
            adv[t] = gae
            next_value = values[t]
        returns = adv + values
        return adv, returns
```

And the training loop, which is where the clipped update lives:

```python
def train(env_id="CartPole-v1", total_steps=200_000, n_steps=2048,
          n_epochs=10, batch_size=64, clip=0.2, ent_coef=0.0,
          vf_coef=0.5, lr=3e-4, gamma=0.99, lam=0.95, target_kl=0.02):
    env = gym.make(env_id)
    obs_dim = env.observation_space.shape[0]
    n_actions = env.action_space.n
    model = ActorCritic(obs_dim, n_actions)
    opt = torch.optim.Adam(model.parameters(), lr=lr)
    buf = RolloutBuffer()

    obs, _ = env.reset(seed=0)
    steps_done = 0
    while steps_done < total_steps:
        buf.reset()
        # --- 1. collect a rollout with the current (old) policy ---
        for _ in range(n_steps):
            obs_t = torch.as_tensor(obs, dtype=torch.float32)
            with torch.no_grad():
                action, logp, value = model.act(obs_t)
            next_obs, reward, term, trunc, _ = env.step(action.item())
            done = term or trunc
            buf.add(obs, action.item(), logp.item(), reward, float(done), value.item())
            obs = next_obs if not done else env.reset()[0]
            steps_done += 1

        with torch.no_grad():
            last_value = model.act(torch.as_tensor(obs, dtype=torch.float32))[2].item()
        adv, returns = buf.compute_gae(last_value, gamma, lam)

        # tensors for the update
        b_obs = torch.as_tensor(np.array(buf.obs), dtype=torch.float32)
        b_act = torch.as_tensor(np.array(buf.actions))
        b_logp_old = torch.as_tensor(np.array(buf.logprobs), dtype=torch.float32)
        b_adv = torch.as_tensor(adv)
        b_ret = torch.as_tensor(returns)
        b_adv = (b_adv - b_adv.mean()) / (b_adv.std() + 1e-8)  # normalize

        # --- 2. multiple epochs of minibatch updates ---
        idx = np.arange(n_steps)
        for epoch in range(n_epochs):
            np.random.shuffle(idx)
            approx_kls = []
            for start in range(0, n_steps, batch_size):
                mb = idx[start:start + batch_size]
                logp, entropy, value = model.evaluate(b_obs[mb], b_act[mb])
                log_ratio = logp - b_logp_old[mb]
                ratio = torch.exp(log_ratio)                      # r_t(theta)
                surr1 = ratio * b_adv[mb]
                surr2 = torch.clamp(ratio, 1 - clip, 1 + clip) * b_adv[mb]
                actor_loss = -torch.min(surr1, surr2).mean()      # maximize -> negate
                value_loss = ((value - b_ret[mb]) ** 2).mean()
                ent = entropy.mean()
                loss = actor_loss + vf_coef * value_loss - ent_coef * ent
                opt.zero_grad()
                loss.backward()
                nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), 0.5)
                opt.step()
                with torch.no_grad():
                    # Schulman's low-variance KL estimator
                    approx_kls.append(((ratio - 1) - log_ratio).mean().item())
            if np.mean(approx_kls) > 1.5 * target_kl:
                break    # early-stop the epoch loop if the policy drifted too far
    return model
```

Every line of the theory is here: `ratio = torch.exp(logp - b_logp_old)` is $r_t$ computed in log-space for numerical stability, `torch.clamp(ratio, 1-clip, 1+clip)` is the trust band, `torch.min(surr1, surr2)` is the pessimistic `min`, and the negation turns maximization into the minimization PyTorch optimizers expect. The `((ratio - 1) - log_ratio).mean()` line is Schulman's low-variance, always-positive estimator of the KL between the new and old policy — cheaper and better behaved than the naive `(-log_ratio).mean()` — and it drives the early-stopping break. Run this on `CartPole-v1` and it solves it (return 500) within roughly 100k–150k steps on a laptop CPU. The advantage normalization line and the gradient-norm clip are not decoration — remove either and you will watch the run get noticeably less stable.

For a continuous-action environment like `LunarLanderContinuous-v3` or any MuJoCo task, the only change is the action distribution. Swap the categorical head for a Gaussian: the network outputs an action mean, you add a learned state-independent log-std parameter, and you build a `torch.distributions.Normal`. Everything else — the ratio, the clip, GAE, the loop — is byte-for-byte identical, which is exactly why PPO is so pleasant to deploy across action types:

```python
class ContinuousActorCritic(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, obs_dim, act_dim, hidden=64):
        super().__init__()
        self.shared = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(obs_dim, hidden), nn.Tanh(),
            nn.Linear(hidden, hidden), nn.Tanh(),
        )
        self.mean = nn.Linear(hidden, act_dim)
        self.log_std = nn.Parameter(torch.zeros(act_dim))  # state-independent
        self.critic = nn.Linear(hidden, 1)

    def dist(self, x):
        h = self.shared(x)
        mean = self.mean(h)
        std = self.log_std.exp()
        return torch.distributions.Normal(mean, std), self.critic(h).squeeze(-1)

    def evaluate(self, x, action):
        dist, value = self.dist(x)
        # sum log-probs over action dimensions (diagonal Gaussian)
        logp = dist.log_prob(action).sum(-1)
        entropy = dist.entropy().sum(-1)
        return logp, entropy, value
```

The one subtlety: for a multi-dimensional action you `.sum(-1)` the per-dimension log-probabilities, because a diagonal Gaussian treats the action components as independent, so the joint log-prob is the sum. Forgetting the sum is a classic continuous-PPO bug that silently scales the ratio wrong.

## Debugging a PPO run: the diagnostics that matter

When a PPO run misbehaves, you do not stare at the return curve alone — you read a small panel of training diagnostics that SB3 logs for free, because each one points at a different failure. Learn to read these four and you will diagnose most problems in minutes rather than reruns.

**Approximate KL divergence.** SB3 logs `approx_kl` per update — the measured KL between the new and old policy after the epoch loop. Healthy PPO keeps this around 0.01–0.02. If it spikes to 0.1 or higher, your updates are too aggressive: lower `clip_range`, lower `n_epochs`, or lower the learning rate. If `approx_kl` is essentially zero, the policy is barely moving and you are wasting compute — raise the learning rate or check that advantages are not all near zero (which would mean the critic has collapsed onto the policy). Many implementations use `approx_kl` for early stopping: bail out of the remaining epochs once it crosses a threshold, which prevents the rare runaway batch.

**Clip fraction.** This is the fraction of samples where the clip was active (the ratio left the band). A healthy value is roughly 0.1–0.3 — meaning the clip is doing real work on a minority of samples but most are still inside the trust region. A clip fraction near zero means your steps are tiny and the clip is irrelevant (you could afford a larger learning rate or wider clip). A clip fraction near one means almost every sample is being clipped, which usually means the policy is trying to move much further than the trust region allows — a sign of instability or a learning rate that is far too high.

**Explained variance of the value function.** SB3 logs `explained_variance` for the critic — how much of the variance in the returns the value function predicts, on a scale where 1.0 is perfect and 0.0 is no better than predicting the mean. Early in training this is near zero and that is fine. If it stays stuck near zero or goes *negative* late in training, your critic is not learning, your advantages are garbage, and no amount of actor tuning will help — increase `vf_coef`, check that your value targets are computed GAE-consistently, or widen the network.

**Entropy.** As covered above, a collapsing entropy curve is the signature of premature determinism. Always plot it alongside return — and read it *relative to its maximum* for your action count, since a raw entropy of 0.4 means something very different for a 2-action policy (max $\ln 2 \approx 0.69$) than for a 16-action one (max $\ln 16 \approx 2.77$). What you watch for is the *shape*: a steady, gradual decline as the policy sharpens onto a good strategy is healthy, whereas a sharp drop toward zero early in training, before the return has risen, is the collapse signature that tells you to raise `ent_coef`.

#### Worked example: diagnosing a stalled HalfCheetah run

You launch PPO on `HalfCheetah-v4`, and after 1M steps the return is stuck around 1200 (you expected 3000+). The return curve is flat, not crashing, so it is not the cliff. You pull the diagnostics: `approx_kl` is 0.003 (tiny), clip fraction is 0.02 (the clip almost never fires), entropy is healthy, and explained variance is 0.85 (the critic is fine). The story those numbers tell is unambiguous: the policy is barely updating — every step is microscopic. The clip is not the bottleneck because the policy never gets near the band. The fix is not more regularization but *less*: raise the learning rate from 3e-4 to 1e-3 and confirm `approx_kl` climbs to the healthy 0.01–0.02 band. After the change, the return climbs past 4000 by 2M steps. The lesson: a flat curve with a near-zero KL is an *under*-stepping problem, the mirror image of the cliff, and the diagnostics distinguish them instantly where the return curve alone cannot.

## The Stable-Baselines3 recipe everyone ships

You will rarely write PPO from scratch in production. You will use Stable-Baselines3, whose PPO is a careful, well-tested implementation with all the right defaults baked in. The from-scratch version above exists so you *understand* the SB3 one — not so you reimplement it. The production recipe is three lines:

```python
from stable_baselines3 import PPO
from stable_baselines3.common.env_util import make_vec_env

env = make_vec_env("CartPole-v1", n_envs=8)   # 8 parallel envs
model = PPO("MlpPolicy", env, n_steps=2048, batch_size=64, n_epochs=10,
            gamma=0.99, gae_lambda=0.95, clip_range=0.2, ent_coef=0.0,
            vf_coef=0.5, max_grad_norm=0.5, learning_rate=3e-4,
            target_kl=0.02, verbose=1)
model.learn(total_timesteps=1_000_000)
model.save("ppo_cartpole")
```

A few things worth knowing about this. The `n_envs=8` means the *effective* batch per rollout is `n_steps * n_envs = 2048 * 8 = 16384` steps — running parallel environments is the single biggest practical speedup for PPO, because the environment step is usually the bottleneck and PPO is embarrassingly parallel in data collection. `"MlpPolicy"` builds a shared-trunk actor-critic for vector observations; use `"CnnPolicy"` for image observations (Atari) and `"MultiInputPolicy"` for dict observation spaces. `target_kl` arms the early-stopping safety net described earlier. Evaluation is one call:

```python
from stable_baselines3.common.evaluation import evaluate_policy
mean_reward, std_reward = evaluate_policy(model, env, n_eval_episodes=20)
print(f"mean reward {mean_reward:.1f} +/- {std_reward:.1f}")
```

For continuous-action environments (MuJoCo, robotics) the only change is the environment and policy; PPO automatically uses a diagonal Gaussian policy (`torch.distributions.Normal`) instead of a categorical one, parameterizing the mean from the network and the log-std as a learned state-independent parameter — exactly the `ContinuousActorCritic` above. The clipped objective, GAE, and loop are identical. A common production touch is to anneal both the learning rate and the clip range linearly to zero over training, which SB3 supports by passing a schedule function instead of a float.

## Hyperparameter guide

PPO has a reputation for being robust, and it is — relative to its competitors. But "robust" means the defaults work on easy tasks, not that tuning never matters. Here is what each knob actually does.

![Matrix figure mapping PPO hyperparameters clip_range, n_epochs, gae_lambda and ent_coef to their effect, typical range, and the failure mode when each is set wrong](/imgs/blogs/proximal-policy-optimization-ppo-4.png)

| Hyperparameter | What it controls | Typical range | Effect of increasing | What goes wrong at the extremes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `n_steps` | rollout length per env before update | 512–4096 | more data per update, lower variance, slower iterations | too small: noisy advantages, unstable; too large: slow, stale value targets |
| `batch_size` | minibatch size in the epoch loop | 32–512 | smoother gradients, less stochasticity | too small: noisy steps; too large: fewer updates per epoch |
| `n_epochs` | passes over each rollout | 3–15 | more data reuse (sample-efficient) but risk of overfitting the batch and large KL | too high: KL blows up, the cliff; too low: wasted data, A2C-like inefficiency |
| `clip_range` | $\epsilon$, the trust band width | 0.1–0.3 | larger steps allowed | too large: reintroduces the cliff; too small: glacial learning, clip fraction near 1 |
| `ent_coef` | entropy bonus weight $c_2$ | 0.0–0.1 | more exploration, resists entropy collapse | too high: policy never sharpens, stays random; too low: entropy collapse |
| `vf_coef` | value-loss weight $c_1$ | 0.5–1.0 | critic learns faster, shares backbone gradient | too high: value loss dominates, actor starved; too low: bad advantages |
| `learning_rate` | Adam step size | 1e-4–3e-4 | faster but riskier; often linearly annealed to 0 | too high: KL spikes, the cliff; too low: under-stepping, flat curve |
| `gae_lambda` | $\lambda$, bias-variance of advantages | 0.9–0.97 | lower bias, higher variance | near 1: high-variance advantages; near 0: biased, value-function-dependent |
| `gamma` | discount factor | 0.99 (0.999 long-horizon) | values future reward more | too low: myopic; too high: high-variance returns, slow credit assignment |

The interactions matter more than any single value. `n_epochs` and `clip_range` together control how off-policy your data gets: more epochs with a wide clip is the fast route to instability, because each extra epoch drifts the policy further while a wide clip lets each step move more. If a run is unstable, the first two dials to turn down are `n_epochs` (try 4) and `clip_range` (try 0.1). If a run plateaus too early, suspect entropy collapse and turn `ent_coef` up. A standard, often-overlooked win is linearly annealing both `learning_rate` and `clip_range` to zero over training — it sharpens the final policy and is one of the implementation details the careful empirical studies found genuinely matters.

## Case studies: what PPO has actually done

**Atari (Schulman et al., 2017).** The original PPO paper benchmarked on 49 Atari games against A2C and ACER. PPO with a CNN policy, `clip_range=0.1` annealed, and 8 parallel environments matched or beat the alternatives on a majority of games in terms of both final score and wall-clock time, while being dramatically simpler than the alternatives. The headline was not "new state of the art on every game" — it was "competitive everywhere with one simple algorithm and almost no per-game tuning." That generality is exactly why it became the default.

**MuJoCo continuous control.** On continuous-control benchmarks like `HalfCheetah-v4`, `Hopper-v4`, and `Walker2d-v4`, PPO with `gae_lambda=0.95`, `clip_range=0.2`, and roughly 1–3M steps reaches strong returns (HalfCheetah comfortably past 3000–5000 depending on the exact env version and reward scaling). Compared to TRPO, PPO reaches similar asymptotic performance with far less implementation complexity and better wall-clock throughput from parallel envs. Compared to A2C (which is essentially PPO with `n_epochs=1` and no clip), PPO is markedly more sample-efficient because of the data reuse. The honest caveat: off-policy methods like SAC and TD3 are *more* sample-efficient than PPO on these same MuJoCo tasks — they reach a given return in fewer environment steps. PPO wins on simplicity, stability, and parallel wall-clock, not raw sample efficiency.

**OpenAI Five (Dota 2, 2018–2019).** OpenAI scaled PPO to train a team of agents to play Dota 2, ultimately defeating the world-champion human team OG. The algorithm was PPO essentially unchanged in its core — clipped surrogate, GAE — but scaled to thousands of GPUs collecting the equivalent of hundreds of years of gameplay per day, with a long discount horizon (`gamma` near 0.998) to handle the game's long credit-assignment chains. The lesson the field took from it: PPO's stability is what made it scalable — a more finicky algorithm would have been impossible to debug at that compute scale.

**InstructGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022).** The first widely deployed alignment of a large language model used PPO as its optimizer, with an adaptive KL penalty against the supervised-fine-tuned reference model. PPO took a base GPT-3-scale model and a reward model trained on human preference comparisons and produced a model that followed instructions and was preferred by labelers over a 175B base model despite having far fewer parameters in the instruction-tuned variant. This is the case study that put PPO on every ML engineer's radar; we unpack the mechanics in the next section.

| Algorithm | Policy type | Sample efficiency | Stability | Best use |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| PPO | on-policy | medium | high | general control, games, RLHF |
| SAC | off-policy | high | medium-high | continuous control, scarce samples |
| TD3 | off-policy | high | medium | continuous control, deterministic |
| DQN | off-policy | medium | medium | discrete actions, pixels |
| A2C | on-policy | low | medium | simple baselines, fast iteration |

## PPO for LLM fine-tuning: the RLHF connection

The reason PPO is on every ML engineer's radar in 2026 is RLHF. The recipe that produced InstructGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022) — and the template for essentially all instruction-tuned chat models since — uses PPO as its optimizer, and the mapping onto everything above is surprisingly direct.

Reframe a language model as a policy. The "state" is the prompt plus the tokens generated so far; the "action" at each step is the next token; the "policy" $\pi_\theta$ is the LLM's next-token distribution. A full generated response is a trajectory. The "reward" is *not* a per-token environment reward — it is a single scalar from a separately trained **reward model** that scores the complete response on how well it matches human preferences, applied at the final token. (Training that reward model from pairwise human comparisons is its own subject; the [RLHF post](/blog/machine-learning/training-techniques/rlhf-from-human-feedback-to-aligned-models) covers it.) So you generate a response, score it with the reward model, run GAE over the token sequence to spread that terminal reward back across the tokens that produced it, and update the LLM with the PPO clipped objective. It is the same loop — `n_steps` becomes a batch of generated responses, the action space is the vocabulary, and the value head estimates $V(s)$ at each token position.

Why is RLHF *on-policy* PPO and not, say, supervised learning on good responses? Because the reward model scores the model's *own* current samples. You generate from $\pi_\theta$, the reward model judges those generations, and you push the policy toward the ones the reward model liked — which only makes sense for samples drawn from the current policy. The moment you update, last batch's generations are off-policy, exactly the situation the ratio and clip were built for. This is the same on-policy data-reuse story as CartPole, transplanted to a 175-billion-parameter policy with a vocabulary-sized action space.

There is one critical addition, and it is the PPO-KL variant resurfacing. If you optimize the reward model's score with nothing holding you back, the LLM finds adversarial outputs that the reward model loves but humans hate — degenerate repetition, sycophantic filler, exploited quirks of the reward model. This is reward hacking. The fix is a per-token KL penalty against a *frozen reference policy* (the supervised-fine-tuned model you started from):

$$r_{total} = r_{RM}(\text{response}) - \beta\, \mathrm{KL}\big(\pi_\theta(\cdot \mid s) \,\|\, \pi_{ref}(\cdot \mid s)\big)$$

This is the alignment leash. It keeps the fine-tuned model close to the sensible base distribution so it cannot wander into reward-hacked nonsense, exactly the way the trust region keeps a control policy from falling off the cliff. Note the two distinct KL roles now in play: the *clip/old-policy* trust region keeps each PPO update stable (the same role as in control), while the *reference-policy* KL penalty keeps the whole training run anchored to a sane language model. They are different policies (the rollout-collecting $\pi_{old}$ versus the frozen $\pi_{ref}$) playing different roles. The TRL library packages all of this:

```python
from trl import PPOTrainer, PPOConfig, AutoModelForCausalLMWithValueHead
from trl import create_reference_model
from transformers import AutoTokenizer

config = PPOConfig(model_name="gpt2", learning_rate=1.41e-5,
                   batch_size=64, mini_batch_size=8, ppo_epochs=4,
                   init_kl_coef=0.2, target=6.0)   # adaptive KL to a target
model = AutoModelForCausalLMWithValueHead.from_pretrained(config.model_name)
ref_model = create_reference_model(model)          # frozen reference
tokenizer = AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained(config.model_name)
ppo_trainer = PPOTrainer(config, model, ref_model, tokenizer)
# loop: generate responses, score with reward model, ppo_trainer.step(...)
```

Note `ppo_epochs=4`, `init_kl_coef=0.2` with an adaptive `target=6.0` — the adaptive KL coefficient is PPO-KL, adapting $\beta$ to keep the measured KL near a target, precisely the variant and the adaptive schedule the original paper described. The `AutoModelForCausalLMWithValueHead` is the actor-critic in disguise: a value head bolted onto the transformer to estimate $V(s)$ for GAE. Everything you learned about clipping, advantages, and entropy applies; the environment is just "generate text, get scored." This is also why the field has since moved toward DPO and GRPO for some settings — PPO-for-LLMs needs a reward model, a reference model, and the policy all in memory at once, which is heavy; [DPO](/blog/machine-learning/training-techniques/direct-preference-optimization-dpo) sidesteps the RL loop entirely by deriving the optimal policy in closed form from the preference data. But PPO was the algorithm that made RLHF work first, and it remains a strong, well-understood choice when you have a reward model and want full on-policy control.

## When to use PPO (and when not to)

PPO is the right default for a huge swath of RL problems, but defaults are not universal answers.

![Decision tree routing from action-space type and sample budget to PPO with a CNN, PPO for cheap simulation, or SAC when samples are scarce](/imgs/blogs/proximal-policy-optimization-ppo-7.png)

**Reach for PPO when:** you have a fast simulator (sample efficiency is not your binding constraint), you want one algorithm that works across discrete and continuous actions with minimal retuning, you are doing RLHF, or you value debuggability and stability over squeezing out the last drop of sample efficiency. PPO's parallel data collection means if you can run many environment copies, wall-clock time is excellent. It is the safe first thing to try on almost any new control task.

**Prefer something else when:** environment interaction is expensive (real robots, costly simulations) — then an off-policy method like SAC or TD3 that replays old data many times is far more sample-efficient, and SAC's maximum-entropy formulation handles exploration more gracefully than PPO's entropy bonus. For purely discrete-action problems with small action spaces and a need for sample efficiency, DQN and its descendants (Rainbow) replay experience and often beat PPO per-sample. And if you have a *known, cheap model* of the environment dynamics, do not use model-free RL at all — value iteration or a planning method like MCTS will crush any model-free approach. ## Key takeaways

- **The clip is a trust region in disguise.** PPO bakes TRPO's "don't move too far" into the loss via $\min$ of clipped and unclipped surrogates, getting most of the stability for none of the conjugate-gradient machinery.
- **The `min` is a provable, one-sided pessimistic bound.** Case analysis on the sign of the advantage shows the gradient is identically zero exactly when a good action overshoots up or a bad action overshoots down, while moves in the wrong direction are always corrected.
- **GAE is half the magic.** $\lambda = 0.95$ blends short-horizon (low variance) and long-horizon (low bias) advantage estimates via the unique geometric weighting that admits the one-pass backward recursion; clean advantages are what make reusing a rollout for ten epochs safe.
- **Multiple epochs are the whole point.** The ratio plus clip make off-policy reuse of a single rollout safe, which is why PPO is sample-efficient relative to A2C; early-stopping on measured KL bounds the cost of that reuse.
- **Always normalize advantages** to zero mean and unit variance, and clip the gradient norm. These two "details" matter more than value clipping.
- **Watch the entropy curve.** A return plateau plus a collapsed entropy curve means premature determinism — raise `ent_coef`.
- **Parallel environments are the practical speedup.** PPO's data collection is embarrassingly parallel; `n_envs` is your best wall-clock lever.
- **PPO is the stability/simplicity choice, not the sample-efficiency choice.** On expensive environments, SAC or TD3 reach the same return in fewer steps.
- **RLHF is just on-policy PPO with a reward model and a KL leash** against a frozen reference policy — the same clipped objective, GAE, and loop you learned here.
