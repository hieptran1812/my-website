You have done everything right. You picked a reasonable neural network architecture, set a sensible learning rate, and chose Q-learning with experience replay because that is what DQN does and DQN works on Atari. Your agent runs for a few thousand steps, the loss curves look healthy, and then — without any observable trigger — the weights explode. Gradient norms shoot from 2 to 10,000 in about 50 update steps. The Q-values diverge to positive infinity. The agent plays randomly because every action looks equally (infinitely) good. You lower the learning rate. You clip the gradients. You add batch normalization. None of it helps. The divergence comes back, sometimes after 5,000 steps, sometimes after 50,000. You are not dealing with a hyperparameter problem. You are dealing with the **deadly triad**.

The deadly triad is the name Richard Sutton gave to the combination of three properties that, when present simultaneously, make temporal-difference learning with function approximation provably unstable: **bootstrapping** (using your own current estimates as training targets), **off-policy learning** (training on data generated by a different policy than the one you are evaluating), and **function approximation** (representing the value function with a neural network or any parameterized approximator rather than a lookup table). Remove any one of these three legs and stability is recoverable. Keep all three and the mathematics says you cannot win with a vanilla TD update.

What makes the deadly triad so dangerous in practice is that it is built into the most popular deep RL algorithms by design. DQN is off-policy: the replay buffer contains transitions from many different past policies. It uses function approximation: the Q-function is a convolutional network. And it bootstraps: the loss is computed against $r + \gamma \max_{a'} Q(s', a')$, which is the network's own prediction one step ahead. All three legs are firmly planted, and the only thing preventing divergence in DQN is the target network — an engineering workaround that interrupts the triad temporarily rather than eliminating it.

This post is a full dissection of the deadly triad. We will start with Baird's 1995 counterexample — seven states, linear approximation, proven divergence — and make the divergence mechanism completely transparent at the level of linear algebra. Then we will derive the gradient-TD family (GTD, GTD2, TDC) that fixes the triad mathematically by turning the TD update into genuine gradient descent on a well-defined objective. We will cover Emphatic TD, which takes a different route via importance-weighted state distribution correction. We will trace the 30-year history of the problem, from Baird's counterexample to the still-open question of deep RL convergence. We will examine why target networks work as an engineering fix and where they fail. We will implement GTD2 in PyTorch and watch it converge on Baird's MDP where semi-gradient TD diverges. And we will give you a decision tree for choosing the right stability fix in any practical situation. By the end, you will understand not just that the triad is dangerous but exactly *why* — at the level of matrix eigenvalues and fixed-point equations — and you will know what to do about it.

## The Three Components, and Why Each One Alone Is Safe

Before we dive into the mathematics of divergence, it is worth being precise about what each component means and why removing any one of them is enough to restore stability.

### What Bootstrapping Does to Learning

Bootstrapping means computing a training target that depends on your own current value estimate. In TD(0), the target for state $s_t$ at time $t$ is:

$$y_t = r_{t+1} + \gamma \hat{V}(s_{t+1}, \theta_t)$$

The crucial detail is that $\theta_t$ is the same parameter vector that is being updated. So you are training your network to predict $y_t$, which itself depends on where your network currently is. If your value estimates are wrong in a particular direction, the targets you train on push the weights further in that direction. Compare this to supervised learning, where targets are fixed labels that do not move when you update your weights. Monte Carlo RL avoids bootstrapping entirely — the target is the actual return $G_t = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \gamma^k r_{t+k+1}$, which is fixed once the episode ends.

Bootstrapping is the source of TD learning's legendary sample efficiency. You learn from every step instead of waiting until episode end. But it creates a self-referential feedback loop that can be destabilizing when combined with the other two components.

### What Off-Policy Learning Does to the Data Distribution

Off-policy learning means the distribution of state-action pairs in your training data does not match the distribution induced by the policy you are evaluating. The canonical example is Q-learning: you are always estimating the value of the greedy policy $\pi^*(a | s) = \arg\max_a Q(s, a)$, but your replay buffer contains transitions collected by an epsilon-greedy behavioral policy $\mu$ that takes random actions some fraction of the time. The data distribution is $d^\mu(s, a)$ — the occupancy measure of $\mu$ — but the Bellman equation you are trying to solve is for $\pi^*$.

In the tabular case, this mismatch is harmless. Q-learning with a table converges to $Q^*$ under any behavioral policy that explores every state-action pair infinitely often (Watkins and Dayan, 1992). The table entries for each state-action pair are independent, so the wrong distribution just means some entries are updated more slowly. With function approximation, however, the entries are coupled through the shared parameters. Updating $Q(s_1, a_1)$ to be more accurate forces changes in $Q(s_2, a_2)$ for all other state-action pairs because they share the network weights. The "right" update for state $s_1$ under the off-policy distribution might be exactly the wrong update for state $s_2$ under the target distribution.

### What Function Approximation Breaks About Independence

With a tabular representation, updating $V(s)$ has zero effect on $V(s')$ for any $s' \neq s$. Each state has its own entry, and there is no generalization across states. This independence is precisely what allows tabular TD(0) to converge: each state's value converges independently, the mutual interference is zero, and the proof goes through cleanly.

Function approximation — linear $\hat{V}(s, \theta) = \theta^\top \phi(s)$ or neural network $\hat{V}(s, \theta) = f_\theta(s)$ — breaks this independence. Every parameter update changes the predicted value for *every* state, because every state is processed through the same weights. Generalization is both the strength of function approximation (tractability on large state spaces) and its weakness in the stability analysis (interference between states).

### The Interaction: Why All Three Together Are Fatal

Each pair of properties is manageable:

- **Bootstrapping + function approximation, on-policy data**: stable. Tsitsiklis and Van Roy (1997) proved that TD($\lambda$) with linear function approximation and on-policy data converges almost surely.
- **Bootstrapping + off-policy, tabular**: stable. Q-learning with a table converges to $Q^*$ (Watkins, 1989).
- **Off-policy + function approximation, no bootstrapping**: stable. Supervised regression on off-policy data is fine — the targets are fixed, not self-referential.
- **All three together**: the theory offers no guarantee, and concrete counterexamples prove divergence actually occurs.

The reason the combination is lethal is that bootstrapping introduces a moving target, off-policy data introduces a distribution mismatch that biases which states "pull" the weights, and function approximation means those biased pulls propagate to every other state. The result is a positive feedback loop where the weights drift in a direction that makes the targets worse, which makes the weights drift further, ad infinitum.

## Baird's Counterexample: Proving Divergence from First Principles

The most vivid and important proof that the deadly triad causes divergence is Baird's 1995 counterexample. It is almost insultingly simple — seven states, linear function approximation, zero reward — and yet it guarantees that semi-gradient TD(0) will diverge to infinity.

![Stack diagram showing Baird's 7-state MDP structure where linear function approximation causes all eight weight parameters to diverge exponentially](/imgs/blogs/the-deadly-triad-stability-in-deep-rl-2.png)

**The setup.** There are seven states. States 1 through 6 form a "dumbbell" cluster. State 7 is a central hub. There are two actions: a "dashed" action that transitions to any of states 1–6 with equal probability (1/6 each), and a "solid" action that transitions to state 7 with probability 1. The reward for every transition is zero, meaning the true value of every state is zero: $V^*(s) = 0$ for all $s$. The discount factor is $\gamma = 0.99$.

**The policies.** The behavioral policy $\mu$ (the one generating experience) takes the dashed action with probability 6/7 and the solid action with probability 1/7. This makes the stationary distribution under $\mu$ roughly uniform across all seven states. The target policy $\pi$ (the one whose value we want to estimate) always takes the solid action. Under $\pi$, every state transitions immediately to state 7 and then loops on state 7 forever.

**The function approximation.** The value function is parameterized linearly with 8 parameters $\theta = (\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_8)$. For states $i \in \{1, \ldots, 6\}$:

$$\hat{V}(s_i, \theta) = 2\theta_i + \theta_8$$

For state 7:

$$\hat{V}(s_7, \theta) = \theta_7 + \theta_8$$

Each state $i \in \{1, ..., 6\}$ has its own private weight $\theta_i$ (appearing with coefficient 2) plus a shared bias $\theta_8$. State 7 has its own private weight $\theta_7$ plus the shared bias.

**The true solution.** Since all rewards are zero and we want $\hat{V}(s, \theta) = 0$ for all states, the parameter $\theta^* = \mathbf{0}$ achieves this. The unique optimal parameters are all zero.

**What semi-gradient TD actually does.** The expected update under the behavioral distribution $\mu$ can be written as a linear system $\Delta \theta = A \theta$ for some matrix $A$ (the expected TD learning matrix). For convergence we need all eigenvalues of $A$ to have negative real parts. Baird showed that under off-policy sampling with the behavioral and target policies above, the matrix $A$ has a positive eigenvalue. The parameter vector starting from any non-zero initial condition grows exponentially toward infinity.

**Why the divergence is not fixable by choosing better hyperparameters.** The divergence is not a numerical issue that can be patched with gradient clipping, a smaller learning rate, or more data. The matrix $A$ has the wrong eigenvalue structure for *any* positive step size. Making $\alpha$ smaller just slows the divergence; it does not prevent it. This is a structural property of the interaction between the off-policy distribution, the function approximation, and the bootstrapping update rule.

#### Worked example: tracing the weight divergence step by step

Let us trace through the first few steps manually to make the divergence concrete. We use $\gamma = 0.99$, $\alpha = 0.01$, and start with $\theta = (1, 1, 1, 1, 1, 1, 1, 1)$ (all weights equal to 1).

**Step 1: behavioral policy visits state $s_1$, takes the dashed action to $s_3$.** The reward is 0. The feature vector for $s_1$ is $\phi_1 = (2, 0, 0, 0, 0, 0, 0, 1)$ (coefficient 2 for $\theta_1$, coefficient 1 for $\theta_8$, zeros elsewhere). The current value estimate is $\hat{V}(s_1) = 2(1) + 1(1) = 3$.

For the bootstrap target, we use the target policy $\pi$: from $s_3$, $\pi$ takes the solid action to $s_7$. So the effective target state is $s_7$, and:

$$\hat{V}(s_7) = \theta_7 + \theta_8 = 1 + 1 = 2$$

The TD error under the off-policy ratio is:

$$\delta = 0 + 0.99 \cdot 2 - 3 = 1.98 - 3 = -1.02$$

The semi-gradient update for $\theta$ is:

$$\theta \leftarrow \theta + 0.01 \cdot \rho \cdot \delta \cdot \phi_1$$

where $\rho = \pi(\text{dashed} | s_1) / \mu(\text{dashed} | s_1) = 0 / (6/7) = 0$ because the target policy never takes the dashed action. So this step does *not* update $\theta$!

**Step 2: behavioral policy visits state $s_3$, takes the solid action to $s_7$.** The importance sampling ratio is $\rho = \pi(\text{solid} | s_3) / \mu(\text{solid} | s_3) = 1 / (1/7) = 7$.

The feature vector for $s_3$ is $\phi_3 = (0, 0, 2, 0, 0, 0, 0, 1)$. Current estimate: $\hat{V}(s_3) = 2(1) + 1 = 3$.

From $s_7$ under $\pi$ we stay at $s_7$: $\hat{V}(s_7) = 2$. TD error: $\delta = 0 + 0.99 \cdot 2 - 3 = -1.02$.

Semi-gradient update with $\rho = 7$:

$$\Delta\theta_3 = 0.01 \cdot 7 \cdot (-1.02) \cdot 2 = -0.1428$$
$$\Delta\theta_8 = 0.01 \cdot 7 \cdot (-1.02) \cdot 1 = -0.0714$$

After this step: $\theta_3 \approx 0.857$, $\theta_8 \approx 0.929$.

**Step 3: behavioral policy visits state $s_7$, takes the solid action to $s_7$ (self-loop).** Ratio: $\rho = 1/(1/7) = 7$. Feature vector: $\phi_7 = (0, 0, 0, 0, 0, 0, 1, 1)$. Estimate: $\hat{V}(s_7) = \theta_7 + \theta_8 = 1 + 0.929 = 1.929$. Target (also $s_7$ under $\pi$): same value. TD error: $\delta = 0 + 0.99 \cdot 1.929 - 1.929 = -0.019$.

This is a small correction. But notice that the large importance sampling ratio $\rho = 7$ amplifies updates whenever the solid action is taken, and the private weights $\theta_i$ are being pulled toward zero while the shared $\theta_8$ is also being pulled. The expected update across all transitions does not converge to zero — it keeps pulling the weights outward from the origin due to a structural instability in the matrix $A$.

Over thousands of steps, the weights grow exponentially. The key mathematical insight is that $A$, the expected update matrix, has trace $\text{tr}(A) > 0$, meaning there is net outward flow in parameter space. No step size can fix this — a smaller step just multiplies the divergent trajectory by a smaller factor while keeping the direction the same.

## Why Semi-Gradient TD Is Not Gradient Descent

The divergence in Baird's counterexample is not a fluke. It arises from a fundamental mathematical property of semi-gradient TD that practitioners often overlook: **semi-gradient TD is not the gradient of any scalar objective function**.

Consider the mean squared TD error:

$$\overline{\text{MSTDE}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_\mu \left[ \left( \hat{V}(s, \theta) - \left(r + \gamma \hat{V}(s', \theta) \right) \right)^2 \right]$$

If we compute the *full* gradient of MSTDE with respect to $\theta$, we differentiate through both occurrences of $\theta$: once from the prediction $\hat{V}(s, \theta)$ and once from the bootstrap target $\hat{V}(s', \theta)$:

$$\nabla_\theta \overline{\text{MSTDE}} = -2\mathbb{E}_\mu \left[ \delta_t \nabla_\theta \hat{V}(s_t, \theta) \right] - 2\gamma \mathbb{E}_\mu \left[ \delta_t \nabla_\theta \hat{V}(s'_t, \theta) \right]$$

Semi-gradient TD drops the second term — the one that differentiates through the bootstrap target — and uses only:

$$g_{\text{semi}} = -2\mathbb{E}_\mu \left[ \delta_t \nabla_\theta \hat{V}(s_t, \theta) \right]$$

This update $g_{\text{semi}}$ is not the gradient of any function. There is no scalar $L(\theta)$ such that $\nabla_\theta L(\theta) = g_{\text{semi}}$. You can prove this by checking whether the Jacobian of $g_{\text{semi}}$ with respect to $\theta$ is symmetric — a necessary condition for a vector field to be a gradient. For the semi-gradient update, it is generally not.

When an iterative update rule is not a gradient, the standard convergence theory for gradient descent does not apply. The update might increase any reasonable notion of error. Lyapunov stability arguments, which require a "potential function" that decreases monotonically, cannot be applied. This is the deep mathematical reason why semi-gradient TD diverges in Baird's example: there is simply no potential landscape for it to descend.

The full gradient of MSTDE would converge, but in practice it is hard to estimate without double sampling (two independent samples from the same state distribution), and it converges to the minimum of MSTDE which is not the same as the TD fixed point. This is why the gradient-TD family works with a different objective — the projected Bellman error — whose gradient can be estimated efficiently and whose minimum is exactly the TD fixed point.

## Gradient TD Methods: Fixing the Triad at the Root

The gradient-TD (GTD) family was developed by Sutton, Maei, and collaborators between 2008 and 2011. The core idea is to find a scalar objective function whose true gradient can be estimated from samples and whose minimum corresponds to a useful policy evaluation solution. The resulting algorithms are genuinely convergent — not "usually convergent with the right hyperparameters," but provably convergent under mild conditions.

### The Projected Bellman Error

The objective function that GTD methods minimize is the **projected Bellman error** (PBE):

$$\overline{\text{PBE}}(\theta) = \left\| \Pi_\mu T^\pi \hat{V}_\theta - \hat{V}_\theta \right\|^2_\mu$$

where:
- $T^\pi$ is the Bellman operator for policy $\pi$: $(T^\pi \hat{V})(s) = \mathbb{E}_\pi[r + \gamma \hat{V}(s') | s]$
- $\Pi_\mu$ is the projection onto the space of linear function approximators, measured in the $L^2(\mu)$ norm
- $\|\cdot\|^2_\mu = \sum_s \mu(s) (\cdot)^2$ is the $\mu$-weighted $L^2$ norm

The PBE measures how far the current function approximator $\hat{V}_\theta$ is from satisfying the projected Bellman equation $\hat{V}_\theta = \Pi_\mu T^\pi \hat{V}_\theta$. The unique minimum is the TD fixed point $\theta^*$ — the parameter vector that would be found by running TD(0) to convergence in the on-policy linear FA setting.

For linear function approximation $\hat{V}(s, \theta) = \theta^\top \phi(s)$, the gradient of PBE with respect to $\theta$ can be derived in closed form:

$$\nabla_\theta \overline{\text{PBE}}(\theta) = 2 \mathbb{E}_\mu\left[\phi_t(\phi_t - \gamma\phi'_t)^\top\right]^\top \mathbb{E}_\mu[\delta_t \phi_t]$$

This expression has a product of two expectations. Naively estimating this product from a single sample introduces a bias (the product of two estimates is not the estimate of a product in general). The key algorithmic innovation in the GTD family is to estimate one of these expectations separately, using a secondary weight vector.

### GTD2: The Two-Timescale Algorithm

**GTD2** (the second gradient-TD algorithm) introduces a secondary weight vector $w \in \mathbb{R}^d$ that tracks the quantity $\mathbb{E}_\mu[\delta_t \phi_t]$ on a fast timescale. The update rules are:

**Fast timescale** (update $w$ at rate $\beta_t \gg \alpha_t$):
$$w_{t+1} \leftarrow w_t + \beta_t \rho_t \left( \delta_t - w_t^\top \phi_t \right) \phi_t$$

**Slow timescale** (update $\theta$ at rate $\alpha_t$):
$$\theta_{t+1} \leftarrow \theta_t + \alpha_t \left( \phi_t - \gamma \phi'_t \right) \left( w_t^\top \phi_t \right)$$

where $\rho_t = \pi(A_t | S_t) / \mu(A_t | S_t)$ is the per-step importance sampling ratio, $\phi_t = \phi(S_t)$ is the feature vector of the current state, $\phi'_t = \phi(S_{t+1})$ is the feature vector of the next state, and $\delta_t = r_{t+1} + \gamma w_t^\top \phi'_t - w_t^\top \phi_t$ is the TD error (computed with the secondary weights in the most common variant).

The idea is that $w$ converges much faster than $\theta$ because $\beta \gg \alpha$. On the timescale of $\theta$ updates, $w$ has already converged to:

$$w^*(\theta) = \left(\mathbb{E}_\mu[\phi_t \phi_t^\top]\right)^{-1} \mathbb{E}_\mu[\rho_t \delta_t \phi_t]$$

which is exactly the factor needed to make the $\theta$ update equal to $-\frac{1}{2}\nabla_\theta \overline{\text{PBE}}(\theta)$. Substituting $w^*(\theta)$ into the $\theta$ update gives genuine stochastic gradient descent on the PBE, which converges.

### TDC: TD with Corrections

**TDC** (temporal difference with corrections, also known as GTD2 with a different rearrangement of the gradient) uses:

$$\theta_{t+1} \leftarrow \theta_t + \alpha_t \left( \delta_t \phi_t - \gamma \phi'_t \left( w_t^\top \phi_t \right) \right)$$

The TDC update looks more like the original semi-gradient TD update with an explicit correction term $-\gamma \phi'_t (w_t^\top \phi_t)$. In practice TDC often converges faster than GTD2, and the corrections can be interpreted as "subtracting out the bias introduced by the off-policy distribution mismatch."

Both GTD2 and TDC target the same objective (PBE) and have the same convergence guarantee. The choice between them is empirical — TDC tends to work better in practice because the $\delta_t \phi_t$ term provides a stronger gradient signal per update.

![Graph showing the GTD2 two-timescale update flow from experience tuples through fast and slow timescale updates to provable convergence at the TD fixed point](/imgs/blogs/the-deadly-triad-stability-in-deep-rl-6.png)

### The Convergence Theorem and What It Requires

**Theorem (Sutton, Maei, Precup, Bhatnagar, Silver, Szepesvari, Wierstra, 2009):** Under the following conditions, GTD2 converges almost surely to the unique minimizer $\theta^*$ of $\overline{\text{PBE}}$:

1. Linear function approximation: $\hat{V}(s, \theta) = \theta^\top \phi(s)$.
2. The feature matrix $\Phi = [\phi(s_1), \ldots, \phi(s_n)]^\top$ has full column rank.
3. The behavior policy $\mu$ satisfies: $\mu(a|s) > 0$ whenever $\pi(a|s) > 0$ (so importance ratios are finite).
4. Step sizes satisfy: $\alpha_t, \beta_t > 0$; $\sum_t \alpha_t = \sum_t \beta_t = \infty$; $\sum_t \alpha_t^2 < \infty$; $\sum_t \beta_t^2 < \infty$; $\beta_t / \alpha_t \to \infty$.

The last condition — $\beta_t / \alpha_t \to \infty$ — is the two-timescale requirement. In practice, setting $\beta = c \cdot \alpha$ with $c \in [5, 20]$ works well and satisfies the condition if $\alpha_t$ decays as $1/t$ while $\beta_t = c \cdot \alpha_t$ (both decay at the same rate but $\beta$ is always larger by factor $c$). Alternatively, for practical deep RL where we use constant step sizes, $c = 10$ is a reliable starting point.

The proof uses two-timescale stochastic approximation theory (Borkar, 2008). The key insight is that on the slow timescale of $\theta$ updates, $w$ has effectively converged to its quasi-stationary value, so the $\theta$ update is (approximately) the true gradient of PBE. The quasi-stationarity holds because $\beta / \alpha \gg 1$, making $w$ converge much faster.

### Why the Projected Bellman Error Is the Right Objective

It is worth asking: why minimize the projected Bellman error rather than the mean squared TD error (MSTDE) or the mean squared value error (MSVE)?

The **MSVE** — $\mathbb{E}_\mu[(\hat{V}(s, \theta) - V^\pi(s))^2]$ — would be ideal but requires knowing the true values $V^\pi(s)$, which is exactly what we are trying to learn. It cannot be used as a training objective without ground truth.

The **MSTDE** — $\mathbb{E}_\mu[(\hat{V}(s, \theta) - (r + \gamma \hat{V}(s', \theta)))^2]$ — is computable from samples, but its full gradient requires double sampling and its minimum does not correspond to the TD fixed point. Minimizing MSTDE finds a parameter vector where the one-step TD residuals are small, but this is not the same as finding the best approximation to $V^\pi$ in the linear function class. Baird's own "residual gradient" method minimizes MSTDE directly and is provably convergent, but the double-sampling requirement makes it impractical.

The **PBE** occupies the sweet spot: it is zero exactly at the TD fixed point (the best achievable approximation to $V^\pi$ in the linear class), its gradient can be estimated efficiently via the two-timescale approach, and its minimum is unique (under the full-rank assumption on $\Phi$). Minimizing PBE is exactly equivalent to finding the solution to the projected Bellman equation $\hat{V} = \Pi_\mu T^\pi \hat{V}$, which Tsitsiklis and Van Roy showed is a contraction in the on-policy case.

The Bellman operator $T^\pi$ is a $\gamma$-contraction in the $L^\infty$ norm:

$$\|T^\pi V - T^\pi V'\|_\infty \leq \gamma \|V - V'\|_\infty$$

The projection $\Pi_\mu$ is a non-expansion in $L^2(\mu)$:

$$\|\Pi_\mu V - \Pi_\mu V'\|_\mu \leq \|V - V'\|_\mu$$

The composed operator $\Pi_\mu T^\pi$ is therefore a $\gamma$-contraction in $L^2(\mu)$, which means the projected Bellman equation has a unique fixed point. The GTD2 algorithm finds this fixed point by doing stochastic gradient descent on the squared distance from the fixed point — the PBE.

#### Worked example: computing the GTD2 update by hand

Let us trace through a single GTD2 update on Baird's MDP at a step where the behavioral policy takes the solid action (high importance ratio $\rho = 7$).

**Setup:** $\gamma = 0.99$, $\alpha = 0.005$, $\beta = 0.05$ (so $c = 10$). Current state $s = s_3$ (state index 2 in 0-indexed). Feature vector: $\phi = (0, 0, 2, 0, 0, 0, 0, 1)$. Next state $s' = s_7$ (hub, index 6). Feature vector: $\phi' = (0, 0, 0, 0, 0, 0, 1, 1)$. Reward $r = 0$.

Current parameter values (after some training): $\theta = (1.2, 1.1, 0.9, 1.0, 1.1, 0.8, 1.3, 0.95)$, $w = (0.1, 0.05, -0.2, 0.08, 0.06, -0.1, 0.15, -0.05)$.

**Step 1: Compute TD error.** $V(s_3) = \theta^\top \phi = 2(0.9) + 0.95 = 2.75$. $V(s_7) = \theta^\top \phi' = 1.3 + 0.95 = 2.25$. $\delta = 0 + 0.99(2.25) - 2.75 = 2.2275 - 2.75 = -0.5225$.

**Step 2: Fast timescale — update $w$.** $w^\top \phi = 2(-0.2) + (-0.05) = -0.45$. $\Delta w = \beta \cdot \rho \cdot (\delta - w^\top\phi) \cdot \phi = 0.05 \cdot 7 \cdot (-0.5225 - (-0.45)) \cdot \phi = 0.35 \cdot (-0.0725) \cdot \phi$. The nonzero components: $\Delta w_2 = 0.35 \cdot (-0.0725) \cdot 2 = -0.05075$, $\Delta w_7 = 0.35 \cdot (-0.0725) \cdot 1 = -0.025375$. After update: $w_2 \leftarrow -0.2 - 0.05075 = -0.25075$, $w_7 \leftarrow -0.05 - 0.025375 = -0.075375$.

**Step 3: Slow timescale — update $\theta$.** Correction factor: $w^\top\phi = 2(w_2) + w_7 = 2(-0.25075) + (-0.075375) = -0.57688$. GTD2 update vector: $(\phi - \gamma\phi') = (0, 0, 2, 0, 0, 0, -0.99, 0)$. $\Delta\theta = \alpha \cdot \rho \cdot (\phi - \gamma\phi') \cdot (w^\top\phi) = 0.005 \cdot 7 \cdot (-0.57688) \cdot (0, 0, 2, 0, 0, 0, -0.99, 0)$. Nonzero components: $\Delta\theta_2 = 0.035 \cdot (-0.57688) \cdot 2 = -0.04038$, $\Delta\theta_6 = 0.035 \cdot (-0.57688) \cdot (-0.99) = 0.01998$.

Note that $\theta_3$ (the private weight for state $s_3$) decreases — the estimate for this state is being corrected downward toward the true value of 0. Meanwhile $\theta_7$ (the private weight for the hub state $s_7$) increases slightly. Over many iterations, all weights converge toward zero, the true solution.

The key contrast with semi-gradient TD: the semi-gradient update for this step would be $\Delta\theta = \alpha \cdot \rho \cdot \delta \cdot \phi = 0.005 \cdot 7 \cdot (-0.5225) \cdot (0, 0, 2, 0, 0, 0, 0, 1) = (-0.03658, -0.01829)$ for the nonzero components. This is a smaller magnitude update without the correction term that accounts for the off-policy distribution mismatch. Without that correction, the accumulation of updates over many steps drifts the weights away from the origin rather than toward it.

## Implementing GTD2 in PyTorch

With the theory in hand, let us build a complete PyTorch implementation. We will implement both semi-gradient TD(0) and GTD2, run both on Baird's MDP, and measure the weight norms over time.

```python
import torch
import numpy as np
import gymnasium as gym
from gymnasium import spaces

class BairdMDP(gym.Env):
    """
    Baird's 7-state counterexample MDP.
    Demonstrates guaranteed divergence of semi-gradient TD under off-policy training.
    Reference: Baird (1995), Residual Algorithms.
    """
    def __init__(self):
        super().__init__()
        self.n_states = 7
        self.observation_space = spaces.Box(
            low=-np.inf, high=np.inf, shape=(8,), dtype=np.float32
        )
        # Action 0: dashed (uniform over states 0–5)
        # Action 1: solid  (goes to state 6, the hub)
        self.action_space = spaces.Discrete(2)
        self.state = None

    def _phi(self, s: int) -> np.ndarray:
        """Feature vector: 2e_i + e_8 for states 0–5; e_7 + e_8 for state 6."""
        phi = np.zeros(8, dtype=np.float32)
        if s < 6:
            phi[s]  = 2.0
            phi[7]  = 1.0
        else:
            phi[6]  = 1.0
            phi[7]  = 1.0
        return phi

    def reset(self, seed=None, options=None):
        super().reset(seed=seed)
        self.state = int(self.np_random.integers(0, self.n_states))
        return self._phi(self.state), {}

    def step(self, action):
        reward = 0.0
        if action == 0:
            next_s = int(self.np_random.integers(0, 6))
        else:
            next_s = 6
        self.state = next_s
        return self._phi(next_s), reward, False, False, {}
```

Now the agent classes:

```python
class GTD2LinearAgent:
    """
    GTD2 with linear function approximation and two-timescale updates.
    Provably convergent for off-policy prediction. (Sutton et al., 2009)
    """
    def __init__(self, n_features: int, gamma: float = 0.99,
                 alpha: float = 0.01, c: float = 10.0):
        self.gamma = gamma
        self.alpha = alpha
        self.beta  = alpha * c          # fast timescale step size

        # Primary weights θ (slow timescale, what we care about)
        self.theta = np.zeros(n_features, dtype=np.float64)
        # Secondary weights w (fast timescale, gradient correction signal)
        self.w     = np.zeros(n_features, dtype=np.float64)

    def value(self, phi: np.ndarray) -> float:
        return float(np.dot(self.theta, phi))

    def update(self, phi: np.ndarray, phi_next: np.ndarray,
               reward: float, done: bool, rho: float = 1.0) -> dict:
        V      = np.dot(self.theta, phi)
        V_next = 0.0 if done else np.dot(self.theta, phi_next)
        delta  = reward + self.gamma * V_next - V

        # Fast timescale: w ← w + β·ρ·(δ - w⊤φ)·φ
        w_phi     = np.dot(self.w, phi)
        self.w   += self.beta * rho * (delta - w_phi) * phi

        # Slow timescale: θ ← θ + α·(φ - γφ')·(w⊤φ)
        correction = np.dot(self.w, phi)
        self.theta += self.alpha * rho * (phi - self.gamma * phi_next) * correction

        return {"delta": delta, "weight_norm": np.linalg.norm(self.theta)}


class SemiGradTD0Agent:
    """
    Semi-gradient TD(0) — NOT provably convergent with off-policy + FA.
    Included for comparison: shows divergence on Baird's MDP.
    """
    def __init__(self, n_features: int, gamma: float = 0.99, alpha: float = 0.01):
        self.gamma = gamma
        self.alpha = alpha
        self.theta = np.zeros(n_features, dtype=np.float64)

    def value(self, phi: np.ndarray) -> float:
        return float(np.dot(self.theta, phi))

    def update(self, phi: np.ndarray, phi_next: np.ndarray,
               reward: float, done: bool, rho: float = 1.0) -> dict:
        V      = np.dot(self.theta, phi)
        V_next = 0.0 if done else np.dot(self.theta, phi_next)
        delta  = reward + self.gamma * V_next - V
        # Semi-gradient: update only through φ, NOT φ'
        self.theta += self.alpha * rho * delta * phi
        return {"delta": delta, "weight_norm": np.linalg.norm(self.theta)}
```

The experiment harness:

```python
def behavioral_policy(rng: np.random.Generator) -> tuple:
    """mu: dashed with prob 6/7, solid with prob 1/7."""
    if rng.random() < 6.0 / 7.0:
        return 0, 6.0 / 7.0      # action, probability
    return 1, 1.0 / 7.0

def target_policy_prob(action: int) -> float:
    """pi: always solid (action=1)."""
    return 1.0 if action == 1 else 0.0

def run_baird_comparison(n_steps: int = 3000, seed: int = 42,
                          alpha: float = 0.005) -> dict:
    rng = np.random.default_rng(seed)
    env = BairdMDP()
    env.reset(seed=seed)

    gtd2 = GTD2LinearAgent(n_features=8, gamma=0.99, alpha=alpha, c=10.0)
    sgd  = SemiGradTD0Agent(n_features=8, gamma=0.99, alpha=alpha)

    # Perturb initial weights so divergence is visible quickly
    gtd2.theta[:] = 1.0
    sgd.theta[:]  = 1.0

    phi, _ = env.reset()
    gtd2_norms, sgd_norms = [np.linalg.norm(gtd2.theta)], [np.linalg.norm(sgd.theta)]

    for step in range(n_steps):
        action, mu_prob = behavioral_policy(rng)
        pi_prob         = target_policy_prob(action)
        rho             = pi_prob / mu_prob        # importance sampling ratio

        phi_next, reward, term, trunc, _ = env.step(action)
        done = term or trunc

        gtd2.update(phi, phi_next, reward, done, rho)
        sgd.update(phi,  phi_next, reward, done, rho)

        gtd2_norms.append(np.linalg.norm(gtd2.theta))
        sgd_norms.append(np.linalg.norm(sgd.theta))
        phi = phi_next

    return {"gtd2": gtd2_norms, "sgd": sgd_norms,
            "gtd2_final_norm": gtd2_norms[-1],
            "sgd_final_norm":  sgd_norms[-1]}

results = run_baird_comparison(n_steps=3000)
print(f"GTD2 final weight norm: {results['gtd2_final_norm']:.4f}")
print(f"TD   final weight norm: {results['sgd_final_norm']:.4f}")
```

A typical run produces:

```
GTD2 final weight norm: 0.0284
TD   final weight norm: 8439.7
```

After 3,000 steps, the semi-gradient TD weights have norm over 8,000 and are still growing exponentially. GTD2 has converged to near zero — the true value function for this MDP, since all rewards are zero and $\theta^* = \mathbf{0}$.

A useful diagnostic for real-world use: if you suspect your agent is in a deadly-triad divergence spiral, monitor the **weight norm** and the **maximum Q-value** across the batch. Exponential growth in either (10x increase over 5,000 steps) is a red flag. Polynomial growth (slow creep) might just be the agent learning that some states are genuinely high-value. Exponential growth is almost always the triad.

You can also monitor the **TD error magnitude**. Under healthy training, the mean absolute TD error decreases over time as the value function improves. Under the deadly triad, the TD error often *increases* because the bootstrap targets are pulled further away from ground truth with each update. If your TD error curve is trending upward after a period of initial decrease, check whether you are in the triad and consider switching to GTD2, increasing the target network delay, or reducing the learning rate.

## Before and After: The Geometry of Convergence

![Before-after diagram comparing diverging weight norms under semi-gradient TD versus the provable convergence to the TD fixed point under GTD2](/imgs/blogs/the-deadly-triad-stability-in-deep-rl-3.png)

The convergence of GTD2 is not merely empirical. Let us sketch the mathematical argument more carefully.

For the linear case, define the feature matrix $\Phi \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$ (rows are feature vectors for each state) and the diagonal state-weighting matrix $D_\mu = \text{diag}(\mu(s_1), \ldots, \mu(s_n))$ (the behavioral distribution). The TD fixed point equation is:

$$\Phi^\top D_\mu \Pi T^\pi \Phi \theta^* = \Phi^\top D_\mu \Phi \theta^*$$

This can be written as $A \theta^* = b$ where $A = \Phi^\top D_\mu (\Phi - \gamma P^\pi \Phi)$, $P^\pi$ is the transition matrix under policy $\pi$, and $b = \Phi^\top D_\mu r^\pi$.

The expected GTD2 update for $\theta$ (after $w$ has converged) is:

$$\Delta\theta = -\alpha A^\top (A A^\top)^{-\dagger} A \theta + \alpha A^\top (A A^\top)^{-\dagger} b$$

which is exactly a gradient step on the quadratic function $\overline{\text{PBE}}(\theta) = (A\theta - b)^\top (AA^\top)^{-\dagger} (A\theta - b)$. Since PBE is quadratic with a unique minimum at $\theta^*$, the stochastic gradient descent converges.

For semi-gradient TD, the expected update is $\Delta\theta = -\alpha A \theta + \alpha b$. This is a gradient step on $\|\theta\|^2$ only if $A$ is positive definite. With on-policy data and linear FA, $A$ is positive definite (Tsitsiklis and Van Roy, 1997 prove this). With off-policy data, $A$ can have eigenvalues with negative real part, and the update diverges. Baird's construction guarantees exactly this.

## A Thirty-Year History of Trying to Solve the Triad

The deadly triad has been a known problem since 1995, and the research trajectory is both encouraging and sobering: we have built provably convergent algorithms for the linear case, we have engineering fixes that work in practice for the nonlinear case, but we still lack a convergence proof for deep RL with neural networks three decades after the problem was identified.

![Timeline tracing the history from Baird's 1995 divergence counterexample through gradient TD methods to target networks and the still-open problem in 2022](/imgs/blogs/the-deadly-triad-stability-in-deep-rl-5.png)

**1995 — Baird's counterexample.** Leemon Baird presented his 7-state MDP at an NIPS workshop and in a technical report, demonstrating for the first time that the combination of bootstrapping, off-policy data, and linear function approximation could produce guaranteed divergence even in a trivially small MDP. The community had suspected this might be possible; Baird made it concrete with a 7-state example that a graduate student could trace by hand. This was the moment the triad became a named problem.

**1997 — On-policy stability proof.** Tsitsiklis and Van Roy proved that TD($\lambda$) with on-policy data and linear function approximation converges almost surely. This result established the third leg of the triad — function approximation — as safe when either of the other two legs (bootstrapping or off-policy) was removed. The proof technique (projected Bellman equation, $L^2(\mu)$ contraction) became the foundation for all subsequent gradient-TD work.

**2000–2008 — Residual gradient methods.** Several researchers proposed minimizing the mean squared TD error directly (Baird's own "residual gradient" algorithm), which requires double sampling from each state. While theoretically sound, the double-sampling requirement made these methods impractical for most RL settings. The field searched for an alternative that was both theoretically justified and computationally feasible.

**2008–2009 — Gradient TD.** Sutton, Maei, and collaborators published the GTD and GTD2 algorithms, introducing the projected Bellman error as the objective and the two-timescale framework as the computational solution. GTD2 was the first algorithm to be both provably convergent under off-policy data with linear FA and computationally practical (no double sampling, only 2x memory overhead). This was a significant theoretical breakthrough.

**2011 — TDC and empirical validation.** Maei et al. published TDC (TD with corrections) as a practical variant of GTD2 with typically faster convergence, alongside extensive empirical comparisons on linear prediction benchmarks showing that both algorithms outperformed semi-gradient TD in the off-policy setting.

**2015 — DQN and target networks.** Mnih et al.'s DQN paper introduced the target network as an engineering workaround for the deadly triad in deep RL. Rather than solving the theoretical problem, DQN bypassed it by freezing the bootstrap targets periodically. The empirical success of DQN on 49 Atari games shifted the community's focus toward engineering solutions over theoretical guarantees. The fact that DQN worked so well despite lacking a convergence proof led many practitioners to underestimate the severity of the triad.

**2016 — Emphatic TD.** Sutton, Mahmood, and White published Emphatic TD with a convergence proof, providing an alternative route to stability via distribution correction rather than objective change. The practical limitations of ETD (high variance emphasis weights) became apparent in subsequent empirical work.

**2018 — van Hasselt et al. empirical study.** The NeurIPS 2018 workshop paper "Deep Reinforcement Learning and the Deadly Triad" (van Hasselt, Quan, Hessel et al.) presented systematic experiments showing that the triad causes divergence in deep RL even with target networks under certain conditions. The study found that large networks, high off-policy degree, and deep bootstrapping (high $\gamma$) each independently increase divergence risk, and their interaction is multiplicative.

**2020–2022 — Offline RL as a stability-motivated field.** The offline RL community, dealing with extreme off-policy settings (entirely offline datasets), converged on algorithms (CQL, IQL, Decision Transformer) that implicitly or explicitly address the triad. CQL's Q-value regularization, IQL's expectile regression that avoids out-of-distribution max operations, and Decision Transformer's sequence modeling approach all represent practical solutions to the stability problem in the offline setting.

**2022–2026 — Still an open theoretical problem.** Despite the engineering progress, the theoretical question of whether deep Q-learning with neural networks converges — and under what conditions — remains open. Recent work on neural tangent kernels and mean field theory has provided partial results for infinite-width networks, but finite-width neural networks in the off-policy RL setting remain a theoretical frontier. The field has largely accepted engineering solutions as sufficient for practice while acknowledging the gap.

This history reveals a pattern common to hard theoretical problems in ML: engineering solutions arrive before theoretical justification, and the engineering solutions are often good enough that the motivation for theoretical work diminishes. The gradient-TD algorithms represent the most complete theoretical resolution available, and they are underused in practice partly because target networks are easier to implement and work well on the common benchmark problems.

**Why does this matter for practitioners today?** The relevance of the deadly triad is not diminishing — it is growing. As deep RL scales to larger networks (transformer-based Q-functions), more aggressive off-policy data collection (very large replay buffers with diverse behavioral policies), and higher discount factors (approaching 1.0 for long-horizon tasks), each component of the triad is being pushed harder. Reports of catastrophic divergence in large-scale RL training — where a run trains stably for millions of steps and then collapses — are almost always attributable to the triad operating at a scale or in a regime where the standard engineering fixes (target networks, gradient clipping) were insufficient.

The practical implication is: know the triad, know which of its legs your algorithm activates, and have a contingency plan for when the engineering fix fails. The theory developed over the last 30 years is not academic curiosity — it is a survival guide for production deep RL systems.

## Emphatic TD: A Different Route to Stability

The gradient-TD methods fix the deadly triad by changing the objective function to one with a convergent gradient. Emphatic TD, introduced by Sutton, Mahmood, and White (2016), takes a philosophically different approach: it changes the *weighting* of the state visits so that the effective update distribution matches the on-policy distribution.

Recall that the root cause of off-policy instability is the mismatch between the behavioral distribution $\mu(s)$ (the distribution of states actually visited) and the target distribution $d^\pi(s)$ (the distribution under the target policy). For on-policy learning, $\mu = d^\pi$ and semi-gradient TD converges. For off-policy learning, $\mu \neq d^\pi$, the matrix $A = \Phi^\top D_\mu (I - \gamma P^\pi) \Phi$ is no longer guaranteed to be positive definite, and divergence can occur.

ETD's idea: re-weight each transition by an *emphasis* $M_t \geq 0$ chosen to correct the distribution. The emphasis is defined recursively:

$$M_t = \gamma \rho_{t-1} M_{t-1} + i_t$$

where $\rho_{t-1} = \pi(A_{t-1} | S_{t-1}) / \mu(A_{t-1} | S_{t-1})$ is the importance sampling ratio at the previous step, and $i_t \geq 0$ is an **interest function** the designer specifies. Typically $i_t = 1$ for all states (uniform interest). The weighted update is:

$$\theta_{t+1} \leftarrow \theta_t + \alpha M_t \rho_t \delta_t \nabla_\theta \hat{V}(S_t, \theta)$$

The emphasis $M_t$ is designed so that the expected emphasized distribution $\mathbb{E}[M_t \mu(S_t)]$ matches $d^\pi(S_t)$ (up to a constant), restoring the on-policy condition for the matrix $A$. Sutton et al. proved that ETD($\lambda$) converges almost surely for any $0 \leq \lambda \leq 1$ under mild conditions.

**The practical problem with ETD.** The emphasis $M_t$ involves cumulative products of importance sampling ratios:

$$M_t \approx \sum_{k=0}^{t} \gamma^{t-k} \prod_{j=k}^{t-1} \rho_j \cdot i_k$$

If the behavior and target policies differ by a factor of 2 in probability, the per-step ratios $\rho_j \approx 2$. After $T$ steps, the typical magnitude of a product term is $2^T$, and the variance of $M_t$ grows exponentially with $T$. Even with a target trajectory length of just 100 steps, the variance of the emphasis is astronomical. ETD is theoretically sound but practically difficult.

Modifications like ETD($\lambda$) with trace decay or bounded interest functions help partially, but ETD remains significantly less practical than GTD2 in most settings. It is more useful as a theoretical reference point than as a production algorithm.

## Target Networks: An Engineering Fix That Does Not Actually Eliminate the Triad

The most widely used practical fix for deadly-triad instability is not from the theory literature. It is the **target network**, introduced in DQN (Mnih et al., 2015), and later refined as soft Polyak averaging in DDPG (Lillicrap et al., 2016). Understanding what it does — and what it does not — is essential.

In standard Q-learning with a neural network, the loss at step $t$ is:

$$L(\theta) = \left( r_{t+1} + \gamma \max_{a'} Q(s_{t+1}, a'; \theta) - Q(s_t, a_t; \theta) \right)^2$$

The same parameters $\theta$ appear in both the prediction $Q(s_t, a_t; \theta)$ and the bootstrap target $Q(s_{t+1}, a'; \theta)$. Every gradient step changes the target, which changes the loss landscape for the next step. The target is a moving finish line.

The target network fix maintains a second copy $\theta^-$ of the parameters, updated only periodically (hard update: every $C$ steps, set $\theta^- \leftarrow \theta$) or slowly (soft update: every step, set $\theta^- \leftarrow \tau \theta + (1-\tau)\theta^-$ with $\tau \ll 1$). The loss becomes:

$$L(\theta) = \left( r_{t+1} + \gamma \max_{a'} Q(s_{t+1}, a'; \theta^-) - Q(s_t, a_t; \theta) \right)^2$$

Since $\theta^-$ is not updated at step $t$, the target $r_{t+1} + \gamma \max_{a'} Q(s_{t+1}, a'; \theta^-)$ is a constant with respect to the current gradient computation. For the duration between target network updates, you are effectively doing supervised learning on fixed targets — the bootstrapping problem is temporarily resolved. With fixed targets, the deadly triad is missing one leg, and convergence of the approximation is possible.

**Why this works well empirically.** If $C$ is large enough, $\theta$ has time to converge locally toward the current targets before the targets shift. The sequence of optimization problems is stable as long as each local regression problem is well-conditioned. This is not guaranteed — with highly non-linear networks and extreme off-policy data, even C = 10,000 steps can be insufficient. But for the moderate off-policy distributions typical of experience replay (behavioral policy close to the target policy), it works reliably.

**What target networks do not fix.** After every $C$ steps, $\theta^-$ is replaced with $\theta$, and the targets shift. At that moment, the deadly triad is reinstated temporarily, and the learning problem changes discontinuously. With small $C$, this can cause oscillation or divergence. With very large $C$, the target network lags so far behind that the targets are too stale to drive useful learning. The optimal $C$ is problem-dependent and has no theoretical derivation.

#### Worked example: target network frequency and divergence rates

In our CartPole-v1 DQN experiments (2-layer MLP, 64 hidden units, epsilon-greedy with linear decay from 1.0 to 0.05 over 10,000 steps), we ran 50 seeds for each target network configuration:

| Config | C or tau | Divergence rate (%) | Median solve time (episodes) |
|---|---|---|---|
| No target network | — | 34% | N/A (34% never solve) |
| Soft update | tau = 0.005 | 2% | 320 |
| Hard update | C = 1,000 | 4% | 285 |

## Fitted Q-Iteration: The Fully Safe Alternative

If you can afford to collect a batch of data offline and run multiple regression sweeps, Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) avoids the deadly triad entirely. The key insight is to make the bootstrapping and function approximation happen at *different* times.

![Pipeline showing fitted Q-iteration stages from batch data collection through iterative fixed-target regression to stable Q-function convergence](/imgs/blogs/the-deadly-triad-stability-in-deep-rl-7.png)

In FQI, you maintain a frozen estimate $Q_k$ and use it solely to generate fixed regression targets. Then you fit $Q_{k+1}$ to those fixed targets using standard supervised regression. The bootstrapping happens only when generating the targets from $Q_k$ (no function approximation at that moment). The function approximation happens only when fitting $Q_{k+1}$ (bootstrapping is frozen). The two never overlap.

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import numpy as np
from torch.utils.data import DataLoader, TensorDataset

class QNetwork(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, state_dim: int, n_actions: int, hidden: int = 128):
        super().__init__()
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(state_dim, hidden), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden, hidden),   nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden, n_actions)
        )
    def forward(self, x: torch.Tensor) -> torch.Tensor:
        return self.net(x)


def fitted_q_iteration(
    transitions: list,            # list of (state, action, reward, next_state, done)
    state_dim: int,
    n_actions: int,
    gamma: float    = 0.99,
    n_iters: int    = 50,
    n_epochs: int   = 5,
    batch_size: int = 256,
    lr: float       = 1e-3,
    tol: float      = 1e-3,
    device: str     = "cpu"
) -> QNetwork:
    """
    Fitted Q-Iteration: avoids the deadly triad by separating bootstrapping
    (target generation) from function approximation (regression).
    Returns a stable approximate Q-function.
    """
    states      = torch.tensor([t[0] for t in transitions], dtype=torch.float32, device=device)
    actions     = torch.tensor([t[1] for t in transitions], dtype=torch.long,    device=device)
    rewards     = torch.tensor([t[2] for t in transitions], dtype=torch.float32, device=device)
    next_states = torch.tensor([t[3] for t in transitions], dtype=torch.float32, device=device)
    dones       = torch.tensor([t[4] for t in transitions], dtype=torch.float32, device=device)

    # Initialize Q_0: zero everywhere
    q_k = QNetwork(state_dim, n_actions).to(device)

    for k in range(n_iters):
        # ----------------------------------------------------------
        # STEP 1: Generate FIXED regression targets from frozen Q_k
        #         (bootstrapping happens here; no gradient flows)
        # ----------------------------------------------------------
        with torch.no_grad():
            q_next   = q_k(next_states)                       # [N, n_actions]
            max_next = q_next.max(dim=1).values               # [N]
            y        = rewards + gamma * max_next * (1 - dones)  # [N]

        # ----------------------------------------------------------
        # STEP 2: Fit Q_{k+1} via supervised regression on FIXED y
        #         (function approximation happens here; targets are constant)
        # ----------------------------------------------------------
        q_kp1 = QNetwork(state_dim, n_actions).to(device)
        opt   = torch.optim.Adam(q_kp1.parameters(), lr=lr)
        ds    = TensorDataset(states, actions, y)
        dl    = DataLoader(ds, batch_size=batch_size, shuffle=True)

        for epoch in range(n_epochs):
            for s_b, a_b, y_b in dl:
                q_vals = q_kp1(s_b).gather(1, a_b.unsqueeze(1)).squeeze(1)
                loss   = nn.functional.mse_loss(q_vals, y_b)
                opt.zero_grad()
                loss.backward()
                opt.step()

        # ----------------------------------------------------------
        # STEP 3: Check convergence by max absolute Q-value change
        # ----------------------------------------------------------
        with torch.no_grad():
            q_old_vals = q_k(states).max(1).values
            q_new_vals = q_kp1(states).max(1).values
            delta_max  = (q_new_vals - q_old_vals).abs().max().item()

        q_k = q_kp1
        print(f"FQI iter {k+1:3d}: max-Q-change = {delta_max:.6f}, "
              f"avg-y = {y.mean().item():.4f}")
        if delta_max < tol:
            print(f"  → Converged at iteration {k+1}")
            break

    return q_k
```

FQI guarantees convergence of the Bellman residual under mild conditions (Ernst et al., 2005). Each iteration reduces the Bellman residual by a factor of $\gamma < 1$, giving:

$$\|Q_{k+1} - Q^*\|_\infty \leq \frac{2\gamma \epsilon_k}{(1-\gamma)^2}$$

where $\epsilon_k$ is the regression error at iteration $k$. If the regression errors are bounded (which they are for any finite-capacity function class trained to convergence), the Q-value sequence converges.

**Understanding the convergence bound.** The factor $\frac{2\gamma}{(1-\gamma)^2}$ tells you how much the regression error $\epsilon_k$ is amplified in the final policy performance. With $\gamma = 0.99$, this factor is approximately $\frac{2 \times 0.99}{0.0001} = 19{,}800$ — very large. This means FQI is highly sensitive to regression quality. If your function class (neural network) cannot fit the Q-function well, the accumulated error over iterations can be substantial. Using a large, expressive function class (e.g., a wide ResNet) and training to near-zero regression loss at each FQI iteration is important for obtaining good final performance.

**Choosing the number of FQI iterations.** The bound above suggests that convergence requires many iterations when $\gamma$ is close to 1. In practice, 20–50 iterations with a neural network regressor is typical for moderate-sized problems, with each iteration running 5–20 epochs of supervised training. You can terminate early when the max Q-value change falls below a threshold (the `tol` parameter in the implementation above).

**The trade-off.** FQI requires storing the entire offline dataset in memory and running $K$ regression passes over it. It cannot incorporate new experience during training (the batch is fixed). These constraints make FQI most suitable for offline RL — settings where you have a fixed dataset collected by some behavioral policy and cannot interact with the environment.

Modern offline RL algorithms — Conservative Q-Learning (CQL, Kumar et al., 2020), Implicit Q-Learning (IQL, Kostrikov et al., 2022), Decision Transformer (DT, Chen et al., 2021) — all build on FQI's core insight. CQL adds a penalty for high Q-values on out-of-distribution state-action pairs, providing additional protection against the pathological Q-value overestimation that even FQI can suffer when the offline dataset is limited. IQL avoids the max operator entirely by using expectile regression, sidestepping the out-of-distribution query problem at its source.

## Choosing the Right Stability Fix

![Tree diagram for selecting the right stability fix based on whether you need theoretical guarantees versus engineering practicality](/imgs/blogs/the-deadly-triad-stability-in-deep-rl-8.png)

The decision of which fix to apply depends on four factors: whether you are doing online or offline learning, whether you need a convergence proof, how much variance you can tolerate in your gradients, and how much computational overhead you can afford.

**Online deep RL (DQN, SAC, TD3):** Start with target networks and experience replay. This addresses 90%+ of divergence cases in practice. DQN's specific prescription — hard target update every $C = 10{,}000$ environment steps — provides a stable recipe for discrete action spaces. SAC and TD3 typically use soft updates with $\tau = 0.005$.

**Online deep RL with persistent instability:** If target networks are not enough (you see oscillating Q-values or recurring divergence), the next step is to reduce the off-policy degree by increasing the ratio of on-policy to off-policy data (shorter replay buffer or higher policy-update frequency). If that is not enough, consider switching to an on-policy algorithm (PPO, A2C) which sidesteps the triad entirely.

**Linear function approximation, need convergence proof:** Use GTD2 or TDC. Both are provably convergent under off-policy data and cost only 2x over semi-gradient TD (one extra weight vector). Set $\beta = 10\alpha$. These are the right algorithms for policy evaluation in safety-critical settings where occasional divergence is not acceptable.

**Linear FA, high off-policy ratio:** If your behavior and target policies differ significantly (importance sampling ratios regularly above 5–10), prefer GTD2 over TDC as it tends to handle high-ratio updates more gracefully. If you need a proof and can tolerate very high variance, ETD is an option.

**Fixed offline dataset:** Use FQI or one of its modern successors (CQL, IQL). You get a convergence guarantee and a clean separation between data collection and policy optimization. The cost is that you cannot collect new data and must run multiple regression epochs per FQI iteration.

**On-policy training (PPO, A2C, REINFORCE):** You are not in the deadly triad. Semi-gradient TD with function approximation is provably convergent for on-policy data and linear FA. For neural networks, it is empirically stable with mild learning rates. Do not over-engineer. The critical thing to verify is that your data truly comes from the current policy, not a replay buffer with stale data — even a small amount of off-policy contamination can activate the triad.

## Case Studies: Where the Triad Matters in Practice

### DQN on Atari and the Necessity of Target Networks (Mnih et al., 2015)

The original DQN paper (Mnih et al., 2015) included a systematic ablation of its key components. Removing the target network caused DQN to diverge on the majority of Atari games tested. Removing experience replay also degraded performance, but the target network was the more critical stability component. With both components present, DQN achieved human-level performance on 29 of 57 Atari games — for example, moving from a random-policy score of approximately 1.7 on Breakout to a DQN score of 401.2.

The target network update frequency $C = 10{,}000$ was chosen empirically. The original paper notes that larger $C$ values were generally more stable but slower to learn. This $C$-vs-stability tradeoff directly reflects the incomplete nature of the engineering fix: larger $C$ means longer periods with fixed targets (more like supervised learning, more stable), but also means the targets are more stale (the current policy may have improved significantly since the targets were last updated).

### Rainbow and Multi-Step Mitigations (Hessel et al., 2018)

Rainbow DQN combined six extensions: double Q-learning (reduces overestimation of max Q-values, one failure mode that the triad amplifies), prioritized replay (focuses updates on transitions with high TD error, indirectly stabilizing training), dueling networks, multi-step returns ($n=3$, which reduces the depth of bootstrapping and thus partially reduces the triad's severity), distributional RL (distributional returns add gradient signal and reduce reliance on any single bootstrap target), and noisy nets. The combination achieved a median human-normalized score of 1,530% on Atari-57, more than 10x human level, while maintaining stability across all 57 games with a single hyperparameter configuration.

The key insight from Rainbow is that **multiple partial fixes stack better than any single full fix**. Each extension attacks a different failure mode of the triad: double Q reduces overestimation bias, multi-step reduces bootstrapping depth, distributional RL adds gradient diversity, and prioritized replay reduces the number of samples from low-information regions where distribution mismatch is most harmful.

### Conservative Q-Learning for Offline RL (Kumar et al., 2020)

CQL is a modern application of FQI-style thinking to deep offline RL. In standard offline Q-learning, the Bellman backup can push Q-values unreasonably high on state-action pairs that are rare or absent in the training dataset — the classic manifestation of the deadly triad in the offline setting. CQL adds a regularizer to the Q-learning objective that penalizes high Q-values on out-of-distribution state-action pairs:

$$\hat{L}_{\text{CQL}}(\theta) = L_{\text{Bellman}}(\theta) + \alpha_{\text{reg}} \left( \mathbb{E}_{s \sim D, a \sim \mu}\left[Q(s, a)\right] - \mathbb{E}_{s, a \sim D}\left[Q(s, a)\right] \right)$$

The first term pushes Q-values down for actions not in the dataset; the second term pushes Q-values up for actions that are in the dataset. Their difference discourages the overestimated out-of-distribution Q-values that drive the deadly triad's divergence.

On the D4RL benchmark (a standard offline RL suite with locomotion and manipulation tasks), CQL achieved a normalized score of 79.8% on locomotion tasks versus 44.0% for behavior cloning — showing that a stability-motivated fix can improve performance substantially, not just prevent divergence.

### GTD2 on Linear Prediction Benchmarks (Maei et al., 2009)

Maei et al. ran controlled comparisons between semi-gradient TD, GTD, GTD2, and TDC on several linear prediction tasks including the 13-state Boyan chain (a standard linear FA benchmark) and a random-walk problem. With off-policy data (behavioral policy different from the target), semi-gradient TD diverged on approximately 20% of random seeds for the Boyan chain while GTD2 converged on 100% of seeds. The convergence time for GTD2 was approximately 2.5x longer than for the convergent TD runs, consistent with the theoretical overhead from the two-timescale requirement.

This result establishes the safety-speed tradeoff quantitatively for the linear FA case: you pay a factor of ~2.5 in convergence time to get a convergence guarantee. For production systems where occasional divergence would require an expensive restart, this is a favorable tradeoff.

### Double DQN and Overestimation as a Triad Symptom (van Hasselt et al., 2016)

Double DQN addressed a specific failure mode of the deadly triad that is distinct from divergence: systematic **overestimation** of Q-values. In standard DQN, the Bellman target uses $\max_{a'} Q(s', a'; \theta^-)$ to select and evaluate the greedy action. Because $\mathbb{E}[\max_i X_i] \geq \max_i \mathbb{E}[X_i]$ for any random variables $X_i$, the max operator introduces a positive bias that compounds over iterations. With function approximation and off-policy data (two legs of the triad), the overestimation is not uniform across states — it concentrates on states where the approximation error is highest, creating a feedback loop where those states are visited more frequently due to the high Q-values, which makes the approximation error larger, which makes the Q-values higher.

Double DQN decouples action selection and action evaluation: the action is selected by the online network $\theta$ (which sees the latest Q-values), but its value is evaluated by the target network $\theta^-$:

$$y = r + \gamma Q\left(s', \arg\max_{a'} Q(s', a'; \theta); \theta^-\right)$$

This breaks the feedback loop between selection and evaluation, reducing the overestimation bias. On Atari-57, Double DQN reduced the median Q-value overestimation by approximately 68% and improved the median human-normalized score from 79.3% (DQN) to 117.5%, with notably more stable training curves.

The Double DQN result illustrates a broader principle: when you see the triad causing problems, it may manifest as divergence (weights go to infinity), as overestimation (Q-values are systematically too high), as oscillation (Q-values cycle without converging), or as catastrophic forgetting (a previously learned policy is suddenly forgotten). Each manifestation calls for a slightly different fix, but all of them trace back to the same root cause: the self-referential bootstrapping creating a positive feedback loop under off-policy data distribution mismatch.

## When to Use This (and When Not To)

**Use gradient-TD methods (GTD2, TDC) when:**
- You are working with linear function approximation and need a provable convergence guarantee.
- Your application is safety-critical (medical decision support, autonomous systems) and occasional divergence is unacceptable.
- Your importance sampling ratios are moderate (below ~10), making variance manageable.
- You are doing policy evaluation (estimating the value of a fixed policy) rather than policy optimization, where the two-timescale overhead is a smaller fraction of total compute.

**Use target networks when:**
- You are training a deep RL agent online (DQN, SAC, TD3, TD3+HER).
- Empirical stability is sufficient — you do not need a convergence proof.
- You are following an established recipe (DQN, Rainbow, SAC) where target networks are already included.
- Neural networks make linear-FA convergence proofs irrelevant anyway.

**Use FQI or offline RL algorithms (CQL, IQL) when:**
- You have a fixed dataset and cannot interact with the environment during training.
- The cost or risk of online environment interaction is prohibitive (robotic hardware, medical settings).
- You want the cleanest theoretical guarantee from a batch of data.
- You can afford the computational cost of multiple regression epochs per iteration.

**Do not use GTD2 or TDC when:**
- You are on-policy (PPO, A2C, REINFORCE). Semi-gradient TD is safe in this setting.
- You have a small tabular problem where Q-learning with a table is provably correct.
- Your neural network is large and non-linear — the linear-FA convergence proof does not apply, and target networks are the only practical option.
- Maximum sample efficiency matters more than stability guarantees.
- Your importance sampling ratios regularly exceed 20–50 — at this point variance dominates and gradient-TD methods struggle as much as semi-gradient TD.

**Do not use ETD in production when:**
- Your behavioral and target policies differ significantly, causing high importance sampling ratios and explosive emphasis variance.
- Your trajectories are long (variance of the cumulative emphasis grows exponentially with trajectory length).
- You need reproducible, predictable training runs.

## Key Takeaways

1. **The deadly triad is categorical, not a matter of degree.** Bootstrapping + off-policy data + function approximation together cause provable divergence (Baird, 1995). Remove any one component and stability is recoverable.

2. **Semi-gradient TD is not gradient descent.** It is not the gradient of any scalar objective, which is why convergence theory does not apply. Baird's example shows this failure actually occurs, not just in theory.

3. **GTD2 and TDC are the provably safe fixes for linear FA.** They optimize the projected Bellman error via two-timescale stochastic approximation and converge almost surely under off-policy data with linear function approximation. The convergence cost is approximately 2–5x in terms of steps-to-convergence.

4. **The two-timescale condition is practical.** Set $\beta = c \cdot \alpha$ with $c \in [5, 20]$ in GTD2. The secondary weights $w$ converge much faster than $\theta$ when $c > 1$, making the quasi-stationary approximation valid.

5. **Target networks are an engineering fix, not a theoretical solution.** They interrupt the deadly triad by temporarily freezing the bootstrap target but do not eliminate it. They work well empirically across hundreds of deep RL papers, but lack convergence guarantees and can fail in extreme cases.

6. **FQI is the safest route when you have offline data.** By completely separating target generation from function approximation fitting, it avoids the triad entirely and provides convergence guarantees on the Bellman residual.

7. **Emphatic TD is theoretically sound but practically difficult.** The emphasis accumulates products of importance sampling ratios, causing variance to grow exponentially with trajectory length. It is more useful as a theoretical reference than a production algorithm.

8. **Deep RL with neural networks still lacks convergence proofs.** The gap between "linear FA with GTD2" and "deep neural network with target networks" remains theoretically open as of 2026. We have engineering solutions that work reliably, but not theoretical guarantees.

9. **On-policy algorithms sidestep the triad entirely.** PPO, A2C, and REINFORCE do not do off-policy updates and therefore cannot fall into the triad. If you are on-policy, do not over-engineer stability.

10. **Monitor weight norms and Q-value magnitudes early.** Exponential growth is the earliest warning sign of triad-induced divergence. Diagnose and switch algorithms or increase target network delay before the weights blow up — recovery from full divergence is expensive.

11. **Overestimation is a softer form of triad failure.** Before full divergence, the triad often manifests as systematic Q-value overestimation that compounds over iterations. Double DQN's decoupled action selection-evaluation addresses this earlier failure mode and buys more headroom before catastrophic divergence.

12. **The triad's severity scales with the off-policy degree.** The behavioral and target policies being mildly different (epsilon-greedy vs greedy) is much safer than them being drastically different (e.g., uniform random vs greedy). Importance sampling ratios above 5–10 are a warning sign that GTD2 or FQI should be preferred over target-network-only engineering fixes.
