Two engineers on the same team are training agents for the same robot arm. One picks PPO; the other picks SAC. A week later the PPO run has burned through 40 million simulator steps and is still climbing. The SAC run plateaued at a higher score after 1 million steps — forty times fewer — but it crashed twice during training, once after a single bad batch sent the policy off a cliff it never recovered from. Same task. Same reward. Wildly different stories. The entire difference traces back to one decision neither engineer thought hard about: **on-policy or off-policy?**

This is the most consequential and most quietly misunderstood axis in all of reinforcement learning. It is not about discrete versus continuous actions, not about value-based versus policy-based, not about model-free versus model-based — though it correlates with all three. It is about a deceptively simple question: **can your algorithm learn from data that some other policy generated?** On-policy methods cannot; they must learn from fresh data produced by the very policy being updated, and they throw that data away after one use. Off-policy methods can; they hoard every transition they have ever seen in a replay buffer and squeeze gradient after gradient out of it. That one bit — reuse or discard — cascades into sample efficiency, stability, wall-clock time, hardware footprint, whether you can use offline logs, and whether your training run survives a single unlucky minibatch.

By the end of this post you will be able to look at a new RL problem and confidently say "this is an off-policy SAC job" or "this has to be on-policy PPO" and defend the choice with the math behind it. We will derive *why* off-policy learning needs importance sampling, *why* those importance ratios blow up the variance, and *why* on-policy methods get convergence guarantees that off-policy methods forfeit. We will write real PyTorch and Stable-Baselines3 code for both camps, look at measured sample-efficiency curves, and end with a five-question framework you can apply in thirty seconds. The figure below is the destination — five canonical algorithms scored across the axes this whole post unpacks.

Throughout, hold onto the spine of this whole series: **the RL loop is an agent interacting with an environment, collecting rewards, and updating a policy.** Every algorithm is a different answer to two questions — *which objective to optimize* and *how to estimate its gradient*. On-policy versus off-policy is fundamentally a statement about *whose data you are allowed to use* when estimating that gradient.

## 1. The fundamental distinction: whose data are you learning from?

Let me set up the running example we'll refine all the way through. You have a CartPole agent — the pole balancing on a cart, the "hello world" of RL. The agent has a policy $\pi$, a function from states (cart position, velocity, pole angle, angular velocity) to a distribution over actions (push left, push right). Training means repeatedly: run the policy to collect experience, then nudge the policy's parameters so it earns more reward.

The fork in the road is what "experience" you are allowed to learn from.

An **on-policy** algorithm requires that the experience used for the update was generated by the *current* policy — the same policy you are about to improve. The instant you update the parameters, all the data you collected becomes stale, because it was produced by the old policy, which no longer exists. You must throw it away and collect fresh rollouts. PPO, A2C, TRPO, REINFORCE, and vanilla actor-critic all live here.

An **off-policy** algorithm has no such requirement. It can learn a policy (the *target* policy) from data generated by a completely different policy (the *behavior* policy). The behavior policy might be an older version of the target, a deliberately exploratory policy, a random policy, a heuristic, or even a human demonstrator. Because the data does not go stale the moment you update, you can store it and reuse it indefinitely. Q-learning, DQN, SAC, TD3, and DDPG all live here.

Here is the intuition that makes everything else click. On-policy learning asks: "given that I act according to $\pi$, how good is $\pi$, and how do I make it better?" The evaluation and the improvement are locked to the same policy. Off-policy learning asks a subtly different and more powerful question: "regardless of how this data was collected, what is the best policy I can infer?" That decoupling — learning about one policy while following another — is what lets DQN learn the *optimal* greedy policy while behaving with an exploratory $\epsilon$-greedy policy.

The word "policy" in "on/off-policy" refers to the policy that *generated the data*, not the policy being learned. The terminology trips up everyone at first. The clean definitions:

- **Behavior policy** $\mu$ (or $\pi_{\text{old}}$, or $b$): the policy that actually selected the actions in your data.
- **Target policy** $\pi$: the policy you are evaluating or improving.
- **On-policy:** $\mu = \pi$. Behavior and target are the same.
- **Off-policy:** $\mu \neq \pi$. They differ, and you must correct for the difference.

That last clause — "you must correct for the difference" — is where the mathematics begins, and it is the source of every headache and every superpower off-policy methods have.

To make the distinction visceral, picture two ways of learning to cook. The on-policy cook tries a recipe, tastes the result, adjusts, and tries again — every lesson comes from a dish *they themselves just made* with their *current* technique. The off-policy cook can also learn from a stack of old cooking videos, a friend's failed attempts, and recipes from chefs with completely different styles, then reason about what the *best* dish would taste like even though they never made it that way. The off-policy cook learns faster from limited kitchen time because nothing is wasted — but they also risk confidently concluding that an untested combination is great, because they are reasoning about food they never actually tasted. That gap between "data I generated" and "behavior I am reasoning about" is exactly the distributional-shift problem we will hit again and again.

| Property | On-policy | Off-policy |
|---|---|---|
| Data source | Current policy only | Any policy (old, random, demos) |
| Data reuse | Discard after one update | Replay indefinitely |
| Behavior vs target | $\mu = \pi$ | $\mu \neq \pi$ |
| Needs correction | No | Yes (importance sampling or bootstrapping) |
| Canonical methods | PPO, A2C, TRPO | DQN, SAC, TD3, DDPG |
| Sample efficiency | Lower | Higher |
| Stability | Higher | Lower |

## 2. The formalism: a one-line definition that explains everything

Strip away the prose and the distinction is one equation. Recall the policy gradient — the gradient of expected return $J(\theta)$ with respect to policy parameters $\theta$. The policy gradient theorem (we derive it in full in the policy-gradient post of this series; here we use the result) gives:

$$
\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi_\theta}\left[ \sum_{t} \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t) \, A^{\pi_\theta}(s_t, a_t) \right]
$$

Read the subscript on the expectation carefully: $\tau \sim \pi_\theta$. The trajectory $\tau$ must be sampled *from the policy we are differentiating*. That subscript is the entire definition of on-policy. The expectation is over data drawn from $\pi_\theta$, so to estimate it with a Monte Carlo average we need samples from $\pi_\theta$. Use samples from any other distribution and the estimate is biased — you are averaging the right integrand against the wrong measure.

### On-policy in Bellman form: SARSA

To anchor this in a value-based update rather than a policy gradient, look at SARSA, the cleanest on-policy temporal-difference method. SARSA evaluates the action-value function $Q^\pi$ of the *policy it is following*. Its update is named after the tuple it uses, $(s, a, r, s', a')$:

$$
Q(s,a) \leftarrow Q(s,a) + \alpha \left[ r + \gamma\, Q(s', a') - Q(s,a) \right], \qquad a' \sim \pi(\cdot \mid s')
$$

The crucial detail is that $a'$ — the action used to form the bootstrap target — is *the action the current policy actually takes* in $s'$. The target tracks the behavior. If the policy is $\epsilon$-greedy, SARSA learns the value of that $\epsilon$-greedy policy, exploration mistakes and all. This is why a SARSA agent learning to walk along a cliff edge will learn a *safe* path that stays away from the edge: the value of the edge states is dragged down by the $\epsilon$ chance of stepping off, and because SARSA evaluates the policy it actually follows, it respects that risk. The behavior and the thing being evaluated are one and the same — the definition of on-policy.

### Off-policy correction: importance sampling

Off-policy methods confront the wrong-measure problem head-on. Suppose your data came from a behavior policy $\mu \neq \pi_\theta$. You want an expectation under $\pi_\theta$ but you only have samples from $\mu$. The fix is **importance sampling**, a classical Monte Carlo trick: to estimate $\mathbb{E}_{x \sim p}[f(x)]$ using samples from $q$, reweight by the ratio $p(x)/q(x)$:

$$
\mathbb{E}_{x \sim p}[f(x)] = \int p(x) f(x)\, dx = \int q(x) \frac{p(x)}{q(x)} f(x)\, dx = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim q}\!\left[\frac{p(x)}{q(x)} f(x)\right]
$$

Apply it to the policy gradient. With single-step actions drawn from $\mu$, the corrected estimator is:

$$
\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{a \sim \mu}\left[ \frac{\pi_\theta(a \mid s)}{\mu(a \mid s)} \, \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a \mid s) \, A(s, a) \right]
$$

The ratio $\rho(s,a) = \pi_\theta(a\mid s) / \mu(a\mid s)$ is the **importance sampling ratio**. When $\mu = \pi_\theta$ the ratio is identically 1 and the correction vanishes — on-policy is just the special case where no correction is needed. That is the single most clarifying sentence in this whole topic: *on-policy learning is off-policy learning with the importance ratio pinned to one.*

So why does anyone ever bother being on-policy if the off-policy estimator is strictly more general? Because that ratio is a loaded weapon. The next section is about how it goes off in your hand.

### Off-policy correction: the Bellman optimality operator (Q-learning)

There is a second route to off-policy correctness that sidesteps importance sampling entirely: **bootstrapping with the Bellman optimality operator.** Q-learning never computes an importance ratio. Instead it uses the update

$$
Q(s,a) \leftarrow Q(s,a) + \alpha \left[ r + \gamma \max_{a'} Q(s', a') - Q(s,a) \right]
$$

The $\max_{a'}$ term evaluates the *greedy* (target) policy regardless of which action the behavior policy actually took next. The target is defined by the Bellman optimality operator $\mathcal{T}^* Q(s,a) = \mathbb{E}_{s'}[r + \gamma \max_{a'} Q(s',a')]$, which is a $\gamma$-contraction in the sup-norm and therefore has a unique fixed point — the optimal $Q^*$ — independent of the sampling distribution, as long as every state-action pair keeps getting visited. That is why Q-learning is "natively" off-policy without any IS ratio: it learns about the greedy policy while you behave however you like.

Contrast the cliff-walking story again. Q-learning, unlike SARSA, will learn the *optimal* path that hugs the cliff edge, because its $\max_{a'}$ target assumes it will behave greedily (never accidentally step off), even though during training its $\epsilon$-greedy behavior occasionally does step off. Q-learning evaluates the greedy policy while behaving exploratorily — the textbook demonstration that one operator choice ($\max_{a'}$ versus the sampled $a'$) sets the entire policy class. One character flips the algorithm from on-policy to off-policy.

![A dataflow graph showing the behavior policy generating transitions, the importance ratio formed from target over behavior probabilities, and the corrected gradient feeding a policy update](/imgs/blogs/on-policy-vs-off-policy-a-practical-guide-3.png)

There is one more subtlety worth pinning down before we move on. Q-learning needs no importance ratio precisely because the quantity it bootstraps from — $\max_{a'} Q(s', a')$ — does *not* depend on which action the behavior policy chose at $s'$. It only depends on $s'$ itself, which the environment handed you regardless of policy. The action the behavior policy took to *get* to the transition $(s, a, r, s')$ does not need correcting either, because Q-learning learns a separate value for *every* $(s,a)$ pair; it is not averaging over the behavior policy's action distribution. Policy gradients, by contrast, *are* averaging over the policy's action distribution (that is what the expectation $\mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi}$ means), which is exactly why they cannot escape importance sampling when the data is off-policy. The deep lesson: **value-based bootstrapping dodges importance sampling by never taking an expectation over the behavior policy's actions; policy gradients cannot dodge it because that expectation is the whole point.**

## 3. Importance sampling and the variance explosion

The importance ratio is exact in expectation but vicious in variance. Here is the failure mode that has wrecked more off-policy policy-gradient runs than any other.

Consider a single action where the target policy assigns probability 0.9 and the behavior policy assigned 0.1. The ratio is 9. Now consider a trajectory — a sequence of actions. The trajectory-level importance ratio is the *product* of the per-step ratios:

$$
\rho_{0:T} = \prod_{t=0}^{T} \frac{\pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)}{\mu(a_t \mid s_t)}
$$

Products of ratios are explosive. If each step contributes a ratio that is on average a little above 1, a 100-step trajectory can produce a total weight of $e^{100 \times 0.1} \approx 22{,}000$; if a little below 1, it collapses toward zero. The estimator becomes dominated by a handful of trajectories with astronomically large weights while the rest contribute nothing. The variance of the gradient estimate grows, in the worst case, *exponentially* in the horizon. You see it in practice as a loss curve that looks fine for a thousand updates and then detonates — one batch happens to contain a high-ratio trajectory, the gradient norm spikes by three orders of magnitude, and the policy is destroyed.

#### Worked example: how fast the ratio blows up

Take a 50-step episode. Suppose at each step the target and behavior policies differ modestly: the target assigns probability 0.3 to the action the behavior policy took, and the behavior assigned 0.2. Per-step ratio: $0.3 / 0.2 = 1.5$. Sounds harmless. But over the full trajectory the cumulative weight is $1.5^{50} \approx 6.4 \times 10^{8}$. A single such trajectory in a batch of 64 carries a weight 64-million times larger than a "ratio-1" trajectory, so it single-handedly determines the gradient. The effective sample size of your batch collapses from 64 to roughly 1. You paid for 64 rollouts and got the statistical power of one. This is why naive trajectory-level importance-sampled policy gradients are essentially unusable beyond very short horizons.

#### Worked example: variance and effective sample size, made quantitative

Let me put a sharper number on "the batch collapses to one." A standard diagnostic for importance-sampling health is the **effective sample size** (ESS), which for a batch of $N$ weights $w_i$ is

$$
\text{ESS} = \frac{\left(\sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i\right)^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i^2}
$$

ESS ranges from 1 (one weight dominates everything) to $N$ (all weights equal — the on-policy ideal). Suppose a batch of $N = 64$ trajectories has 63 trajectories with weight $w = 1$ and one trajectory with the $6.4 \times 10^8$ weight from the example above. Then $\sum w_i \approx 6.4 \times 10^8$, $\sum w_i^2 \approx (6.4 \times 10^8)^2 = 4.1 \times 10^{17}$, and

$$
\text{ESS} \approx \frac{(6.4 \times 10^8)^2}{4.1 \times 10^{17}} \approx 1.0
$$

You collected 64 trajectories and your estimator has the statistical strength of *one*. The gradient is whatever that single outlier trajectory says it is, noise and all. Now run the same calculation after truncating every per-step ratio at $\bar{c} = 1$: every trajectory weight becomes $\le 1$, the weights are near-uniform, and the ESS climbs back toward 64. You have traded a little bias (the truncation) for two orders of magnitude more effective data. In production off-policy systems, logging ESS per batch is one of the cheapest and most informative health checks you can add — when it craters, your correction has stopped working.

The standard defenses, in increasing order of sophistication:

1. **Per-decision importance sampling.** Don't multiply ratios across the whole trajectory; only weight the reward at time $t$ by the product of ratios *up to* time $t$. This is unbiased and lower-variance because later rewards aren't weighted by ratios from before they occurred.
2. **Truncation / clipping.** Cap each ratio at some $\bar{c}$, e.g. $\rho \leftarrow \min(\bar{c}, \rho)$. This introduces bias but slashes variance. V-trace (the engine behind IMPALA) does exactly this with two separate clip thresholds, $\bar{c}$ and $\bar{\rho}$, which we dissect in section 11.
3. **Weighted importance sampling.** Normalize the weights to sum to 1 within the batch. Biased for finite samples but consistent, and dramatically lower variance — the standard choice when you must do IS at the trajectory level.
4. **Avoid IS entirely.** Use a bootstrapped value-based method (Q-learning family) whose off-policy correctness comes from the Bellman operator, not from reweighting. This is why DQN and SAC scale to long horizons and millions of steps while off-policy *policy gradients* historically did not.

```python
import numpy as np

def naive_is_weight(target_probs, behavior_probs):
    """Trajectory-level IS weight: product of per-step ratios. Explodes."""
    ratios = np.array(target_probs) / np.array(behavior_probs)
    return np.prod(ratios)

def truncated_is_weights(target_probs, behavior_probs, c_bar=1.0):
    """Per-step truncated ratios, as used by V-trace. Bounded variance."""
    ratios = np.array(target_probs) / np.array(behavior_probs)
    return np.minimum(c_bar, ratios)

def effective_sample_size(weights):
    """ESS: ranges from 1 (one weight dominates) to N (uniform)."""
    w = np.asarray(weights, dtype=float)
    return (w.sum() ** 2) / (np.square(w).sum() + 1e-12)

# 50-step episode, modest per-step divergence
target = [0.3] * 50
behavior = [0.2] * 50
print("Naive trajectory weight:", naive_is_weight(target, behavior))   # ~6.4e8
print("Truncated per-step weights (first 5):", truncated_is_weights(target, behavior)[:5])

# A batch of 64: 63 well-behaved trajectories + 1 outlier
batch = [1.0] * 63 + [naive_is_weight(target, behavior)]
print("ESS of raw batch:", effective_sample_size(batch))               # ~1.0
print("ESS after clipping:", effective_sample_size([min(1.0, w) for w in batch]))  # ~64
```

Run it and the naive weight prints something like `638449924.5` while the truncated weights are a sober vector of `1.0`s, and the ESS jumps from ~1.0 to ~64.0. That gap — eight orders of magnitude in the weight, sixty-fold in effective data — is the whole reason variance reduction is not optional in off-policy learning.

## 4. Sample efficiency: the off-policy superpower

Now the payoff that makes off-policy worth all that variance trouble. **Sample efficiency** is how much reward improvement you extract per unit of environment interaction. In many real domains — robotics, healthcare, recommendation, finance — environment interaction is the expensive resource. A real robot moves in real time and wears out. A clinical decision affects a real patient. A trading action costs real spread. You want to learn as much as possible from every interaction.

Off-policy methods win here, often by 10–40×, for one structural reason captured in the figure below: **they reuse data.**

![A before-and-after comparison contrasting on-policy collect-update-discard with off-policy collect-store-sample using a replay buffer](/imgs/blogs/on-policy-vs-off-policy-a-practical-guide-2.png)

An on-policy method like PPO runs a fixed number of steps (say 2048), computes advantages, does a handful of gradient epochs over that batch, and then **throws all 2048 transitions away** because the policy has changed and they are now off-policy with respect to it. Every gradient step costs fresh environment interaction.

An off-policy method like SAC takes a single environment step, stores the transition $(s, a, r, s')$ into a replay buffer holding up to a million transitions, and then samples a random minibatch of 256 from the *entire history* to do a gradient update. A transition collected at step 10,000 is still being sampled and learned from at step 900,000. The ratio of gradient updates to environment steps — the **replay ratio** or **update-to-data ratio** — can be 1, 4, 20, or more. SAC defaults to one gradient step per environment step but learns from a 256-sample batch each time, so each transition participates in many updates over its lifetime in the buffer.

The arithmetic is stark. To do one million gradient updates:

- **PPO** needs roughly one million × batch-size fresh transitions if each batch is used once — in practice PPO reuses each batch for ~10 epochs, so call it 10 gradient passes per fresh batch. Still: every new batch is new environment interaction.
- **SAC** can do one million gradient updates having collected as few as one million environment steps (update-to-data ratio of 1), and you can push that ratio higher to learn even more per step, bounded by overfitting to the buffer.

Here is the replay buffer itself — the single component that the entire off-policy sample-efficiency story rests on. Notice how little it is: a ring buffer of transitions and a uniform sampler.

```python
import numpy as np

class ReplayBuffer:
    """The off-policy data-efficiency multiplier: store everything, sample anything."""
    def __init__(self, capacity, state_dim, action_dim):
        self.capacity = capacity
        self.ptr = 0
        self.size = 0
        self.states      = np.zeros((capacity, state_dim),  dtype=np.float32)
        self.actions     = np.zeros((capacity, action_dim), dtype=np.float32)
        self.rewards     = np.zeros((capacity, 1),          dtype=np.float32)
        self.next_states = np.zeros((capacity, state_dim),  dtype=np.float32)
        self.dones       = np.zeros((capacity, 1),          dtype=np.float32)

    def add(self, s, a, r, s2, done):
        i = self.ptr
        self.states[i], self.actions[i] = s, a
        self.rewards[i], self.next_states[i], self.dones[i] = r, s2, done
        self.ptr = (self.ptr + 1) % self.capacity   # ring buffer: overwrite oldest
        self.size = min(self.size + 1, self.capacity)

    def sample(self, batch_size):
        # Uniformly sample from the ENTIRE history -> data collected long ago
        # is still learned from. This is the whole trick.
        idx = np.random.randint(0, self.size, size=batch_size)
        return (self.states[idx], self.actions[idx], self.rewards[idx],
                self.next_states[idx], self.dones[idx])
```

For a 1M-capacity buffer over an 18-dimensional state and a 6-dimensional action, this object holds roughly $10^6 \times (18 + 6 + 1 + 18 + 1) \times 4$ bytes $\approx 176$ MB of float32 — small for MuJoCo, but a serious concern for image observations, which we quantify in section 7.

#### Worked example: HalfCheetah-v4 sample budget

On the MuJoCo HalfCheetah-v4 benchmark, published Stable-Baselines3 and original-paper results put SAC at roughly 10,000–12,000 average return after about 1 million environment steps. PPO reaches a respectable but lower return (roughly 1,500–3,000 with default hyperparameters, more with heavy tuning) and needs several million steps to get there. If a HalfCheetah step in your real-robot analogue cost one second of wall-clock and one unit of hardware wear, SAC's 1M-step solution costs about 11.5 days of robot time; PPO's 5M-step solution costs about 58 days. When interaction is the bottleneck, that 5× is the difference between a feasible project and an infeasible one. (These are approximate, hyperparameter-sensitive figures from the SAC and PPO papers and the SB3 RL Zoo benchmark tables; reproduce with the RL Zoo configs before quoting them in a paper.)

#### Worked example: replay ratio as a data multiplier

Let me quantify the "every transition is learned from many times" claim. SAC with `train_freq=1`, `gradient_steps=1`, `batch_size=256`, and a buffer that fills to 1M does one 256-sample gradient update per environment step. Over a transition's lifetime in the buffer — it survives roughly 1M steps before being overwritten — the probability it is *not* sampled in a given update of size 256 from a full buffer is $(1 - 256/10^6) \approx 0.999744$. Over 1M updates, the expected number of times that one transition is sampled is $10^6 \times 256 / 10^6 = 256$. So on average **each transition contributes to 256 gradient updates** before it ages out. PPO, by contrast, touches each transition for exactly `n_epochs = 10` updates and then discards it. That is the 25× reuse advantage in one line of arithmetic — and if you raise SAC's `gradient_steps` to 4, it becomes 100×, at the cost of increased risk of overfitting the critic to the buffer (the primary failure mode of high replay ratios, which is why algorithms like REDQ pair high replay ratios with critic ensembles and regularization).

This is the core reason robotics and offline-RL communities are overwhelmingly off-policy: you simply cannot afford to discard data when each datum is expensive.

## 5. Stability: the on-policy superpower

If off-policy wins sample efficiency, on-policy wins **stability and predictability**, and the reason is the mirror image of the variance story.

An on-policy method only ever learns from data its current policy generated, so the data distribution and the policy move together, in small synchronized steps. On top of that, modern on-policy methods *deliberately bound how far the policy can move per update.* PPO's clipped surrogate objective is the canonical example:

$$
L^{\text{CLIP}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_t\left[ \min\left( r_t(\theta) A_t, \; \text{clip}(r_t(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon) A_t \right) \right]
$$

where $r_t(\theta) = \pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t) / \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(a_t\mid s_t)$ is the ratio between the new policy and the policy that *collected this batch a few gradient steps ago*. (Note: PPO is "on-policy" but it does a few epochs over each batch, so within those epochs $\pi_\theta$ drifts slightly from $\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$ — the clip exists precisely to keep that drift small. PPO is on-policy in the sense that batches are not reused across iterations, but the clip is doing mild off-policy correction within an iteration.) The $\text{clip}$ caps the ratio inside $[1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon]$ with $\epsilon \approx 0.2$, so no single update can move the policy probability by more than ~20% on any action. TRPO enforces an even harder guarantee — a trust region constraining the KL divergence between old and new policy:

$$
\max_\theta \; \mathbb{E}_t\!\left[ \frac{\pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(a_t\mid s_t)} A_t \right] \quad \text{subject to} \quad \mathbb{E}_t\!\left[ D_{\text{KL}}\!\left(\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}} \,\|\, \pi_\theta\right) \right] \le \delta
$$

These bounded-step guarantees give on-policy methods their characteristic *monotonic-ish, robust* learning curves. They rarely catastrophically collapse, and the same hyperparameters tend to work across many environments — PPO's reputation as the "just works" algorithm comes from exactly this.

Off-policy methods have no such governor by default. A Q-learning update can change the greedy action for a state from one update to the next, swinging the implied policy wildly. Combine that with function approximation and bootstrapping and you get the infamous deadly triad, which gets its own section next.

The trade-off has no free lunch, which the next figure makes explicit: each property flips which camp wins.

### The deadly triad

The deadly triad is the precise statement of *why* deep off-policy RL is dangerous. It says that when you combine three ingredients, your value estimates can diverge to infinity:

1. **Function approximation** — a neural network (or any parametric function) representing $Q$ or $V$, so that updating one state's value bleeds into nearby states through shared weights.
2. **Bootstrapping** — updating an estimate toward a target that itself contains an estimate (the $\gamma \max_{a'} Q(s', a')$ term), rather than toward a full Monte Carlo return.
3. **Off-policy training** — updating on a distribution of states different from the one the current policy would induce.

Any two of the three are safe. On-policy bootstrapping with function approximation (TD with the policy's own data) converges. Off-policy bootstrapping in the tabular case (no function approximation) converges. Off-policy Monte Carlo with function approximation (no bootstrapping) converges. It is specifically the *combination of all three* that can blow up, and the mechanism is a positive feedback loop: an over-estimated $Q(s', a')$ inflates the bootstrap target for $Q(s, a)$, the shared network weights propagate that inflation to *other* states including $s'$ itself, and because the off-policy state distribution does not self-correct the way on-policy sampling does, nothing pulls it back. Baird's counterexample is the classic minimal demonstration where this provably diverges.

#### Worked example: why a target network tames the loop

Here is the feedback loop in numbers. Suppose $Q(s', a') = 10$ is correctly estimated, $r = 0$, $\gamma = 0.99$, and the network shares enough capacity that nudging $Q(s,a)$ up by $\delta$ also nudges $Q(s', a')$ up by $0.5\delta$ (weight sharing). A single Q-learning update sets the target to $0 + 0.99 \times 10 = 9.9$ and moves $Q(s,a)$ toward it. But if the *current* network already overestimates $Q(s', a') = 12$ instead of 10, the target becomes $11.88$, the update raises $Q(s,a)$, which through weight sharing raises $Q(s', a')$ to perhaps $12.3$, which on the next update raises the target to $12.18$, and so on — a runaway. Now insert a **target network**: the bootstrap target uses a *frozen* copy $Q_{\bar\theta}(s', a')$ that is only synced every, say, 10,000 steps. The target stays pinned at $0.99 \times 12 = 11.88$ no matter how much the online network's $Q(s', a')$ drifts during those 10,000 steps, breaking the within-update feedback loop. The online network chases a stationary target for a while, which is exactly the stability deep Q-learning needs.

This is why DQN's two innovations — the **replay buffer** (which de-correlates and *stabilizes the data distribution*, partially defusing the "off-policy" leg by mixing many policies into a slowly-changing average) and the **target network** (which *slows the bootstrap leg*) — are not incidental tricks. They are direct, surgical mitigations of two of the three deadly-triad ingredients. SAC and TD3 layer on twin critics (clipped double-Q to fight the *over*-estimation bias that feeds the loop) and, in TD3, delayed policy updates and target smoothing. All of it is engineering scaffolding erected to tame instabilities that on-policy methods do not have in the first place. They work well in practice now, but they are mitigations, not guarantees — which is the deepest reason the stability column in every table in this post favors on-policy.

## 6. Wall-clock vs sample efficiency: the trade-off everyone conflates

Here is the distinction that separates engineers who have shipped RL from those who have only read about it. **Sample efficiency and wall-clock efficiency are different things, and on-policy methods often win the second even while losing the first.**

Sample efficiency = reward per environment step. Wall-clock efficiency = reward per second of real time. They diverge because on-policy methods **parallelize trivially** and off-policy methods do not.

On-policy collection is embarrassingly parallel. Spin up 16, 64, or 512 environment workers, each running the *same* current policy, and gather their experience into one big batch. A2C and PPO do exactly this with `SubprocVecEnv`. If you have a simulator that runs cheaply and a fat machine or a cluster, you can collect millions of steps per minute. OpenAI Five collected the equivalent of *900 years of Dota per day* this way, using PPO across thousands of CPU cores. The agent was atrociously sample-inefficient — it needed astronomically many steps — but because steps were cheap and parallelizable, wall-clock training was feasible.

Off-policy methods are harder to parallelize cleanly because the replay buffer is shared mutable state and the learner wants the most recent policy. You *can* distribute them — Ape-X and R2D2 use many actors feeding a central prioritized buffer, and IMPALA uses V-trace precisely to let actors lag the learner — but you pay an off-policy correction cost (the actors' data was generated by a slightly stale policy, so you are back to importance ratios) and the engineering is heavier.

The structural reason is worth stating sharply. On-policy parallelism scales the *data-collection* axis, and collection is stateless: every worker holds the same policy weights, runs independently, and you just concatenate their transitions. There is no shared mutable state, so it scales near-linearly with cores. Off-policy parallelism wants to scale the *gradient-update* axis against a *single shared replay buffer*, which is mutable state that actors write and the learner reads — and the moment many actors run a policy that lags the learner's latest weights, their data is off-policy with respect to the learner, reintroducing the importance-ratio variance problem of section 3. This is precisely the gap that distributed architectures like Ape-X (many actors, one prioritized buffer, one learner) and IMPALA (V-trace to correct actor-learner staleness) were invented to bridge. The asymmetry is fundamental, not incidental: collecting on-policy data is trivially shardable; reusing off-policy data at scale requires correction machinery.

The practical decision rule:

- **Simulator is cheap and parallelizable, you have lots of cores** → on-policy (PPO/A2C) often wins wall-clock even though it's sample-inefficient. You burn steps to save time.
- **Each environment step is expensive (real robot, real money, real patient)** → off-policy (SAC/TD3/DQN) wins because samples, not seconds, are the binding constraint.

| Axis | On-policy (PPO/A2C) | Off-policy (SAC/DQN) |
|---|---|---|
| Reward per env step | Lower | Higher (5–40×) |
| Reward per wall-clock second | Higher *if* cheap parallel sim | Lower (hard to parallelize) |
| Parallel collection | Trivial (many workers, one policy) | Hard (shared buffer, staleness) |
| Memory footprint | Small (one batch) | Large (1M-transition buffer) |
| Binding constraint it suits | Compute / time | Sample budget |

#### Worked example: choosing for a cheap sim vs an expensive robot

You're solving a 6-DOF manipulation task. **Scenario A:** you have a fast MuJoCo sim (5,000 steps/sec/core) and a 64-core box. PPO across 64 workers collects ~320k steps/sec; even needing 50M steps, that's ~2.6 minutes of collection plus update overhead — call it well under an hour to a good policy. SAC, single-threaded collection at maybe 5k steps/sec, needs 2M steps = ~7 minutes of collection but its gradient updates are the bottleneck, and you can't easily 64× it. PPO likely wins wall-clock. **Scenario B:** you must train on the *real* arm at 20 steps/sec, no parallel copies. 50M PPO steps = 29 days of robot time; 2M SAC steps = 28 hours. SAC wins by 25×, and the robot survives. Same algorithm pair, opposite conclusion, decided entirely by whether steps or seconds are scarce.

Here is on-policy parallel collection in real Stable-Baselines3 — the one line (`n_envs=64`) that buys the wall-clock win in Scenario A:

There is no equivalent one-liner that 64×-es SAC's *gradient* throughput, because the bottleneck is a single learner reading a single buffer — which is the whole point.

## 7. The on-policy advantage, stated precisely

Beyond stability, on-policy methods carry three concrete advantages worth naming so you know when they are decisive.

**Theoretical convergence guarantees.** On-policy policy-gradient methods are honest stochastic gradient ascent on the true objective $J(\theta)$ — the gradient estimate is unbiased (no importance-ratio approximation, no bootstrap bias), so under standard step-size conditions (Robbins–Monro: step sizes that sum to infinity but whose squares sum to a finite value) they converge to a local optimum of the expected return. Off-policy value methods with function approximation have no general convergence guarantee; the deadly triad can diverge, and the guarantees that do exist require restrictive conditions (e.g., linear function approximation with on-policy distribution, or gradient-TD methods that explicitly fix the off-policy leg). When you need to *trust* that more training won't suddenly make things worse, on-policy is the safer bet.

**No distributional shift between data and objective.** Because the data is always from the current policy, the states you train on are the states you'll actually visit at deployment. Off-policy methods train on a replay buffer that mixes states from many past policies, some of which the current policy would never visit — so the critic can be confidently wrong about regions that matter, a problem that becomes acute in offline RL (extrapolation error on out-of-distribution actions). This is not a subtle effect: the dominant failure mode of offline RL is the critic assigning spuriously high value to actions the data never demonstrated, and the policy then exploiting that delusion. On-policy methods are immune by construction because they never evaluate an action the current policy would not take.

**Small, predictable memory footprint.** An on-policy method holds exactly one batch in memory: `n_steps × n_envs` transitions, typically a few thousand to a few hundred thousand. For MuJoCo with an 18-dim state that is megabytes. There is no replay buffer to size, no decision about capacity, no RAM ceiling to hit. This becomes decisive with image observations — see the contrast in the next section.

**Natural fit for RLHF.** This one is enormous in practice and gets its own section (13). In a nutshell: when you fine-tune a language model with RL, you generate completions *from the current model*, score them with a reward model, and update *the current model*. The data is, by construction, on-policy. PPO slots in naturally. Off-policy would mean reusing completions from earlier checkpoints, which drift out of distribution and break the reward model's calibration. That is why InstructGPT and the overwhelming majority of RLHF pipelines use PPO.

## 8. The off-policy advantage, stated precisely

The mirror advantages:

**Learning from offline data.** This is the single most important capability off-policy methods have and on-policy methods structurally cannot. If you have a fixed dataset — logged production traffic, historical trades, recorded human demonstrations, a medical database — and you cannot collect new interactions, you *must* be off-policy, because all your data was generated by some other policy. This is the entire field of **offline RL** (CQL, IQL, AWAC, decision transformers). On-policy methods are dead on arrival here: they have no current policy that generated the data, so there is nothing to be "on."

**Sample efficiency via reuse**, as covered in section 4. Every transition can be learned from hundreds of times.

**No need to regenerate data every step.** In a setting where generating a rollout is slow or risky but you have a good buffer, off-policy lets you keep improving the policy purely from stored experience between rare collection bursts. SAC can run dozens of gradient updates per collected step; PPO cannot productively do this because the extra updates would be increasingly off-policy and the clip would zero out their contribution.

### Memory requirements: the off-policy cost everyone forgets

The replay buffer that buys all that sample efficiency has a price tag in RAM, and it is easy to underestimate. The buffer stores, for each transition, the state, action, reward, next state, and done flag. The state is stored *twice* (once as $s$, once as the previous transition's $s'$ unless you deduplicate), so a transition's footprint is roughly $2 \times (\text{state size}) + (\text{action size}) + 2$ scalars.

#### Worked example: MuJoCo buffer vs Atari buffer

For **MuJoCo HalfCheetah** the state is 17 floats and the action is 6 floats. A transition is about $(2 \times 17 + 6 + 2) \times 4 \approx 168$ bytes. A 1M-transition buffer is $\approx 168$ MB — trivial for any modern machine.

For **Atari with image observations**, the state is an $84 \times 84 \times 4$ stacked grayscale frame = 28,224 bytes per state even at uint8. Stored naively (state and next-state separately) a transition is $\approx 56$ KB, so a 1M-transition replay buffer is **56 GB** — far more than most GPUs' host RAM budget. This is why every serious Atari DQN implementation uses a *frame-stacking trick*: store individual 84×84 uint8 frames once and reconstruct the 4-frame stacks on sampling, cutting memory by roughly 8×, down to ~7 GB for 1M transitions. The point: off-policy's data reuse is not free; it trades environment-interaction cost for memory and engineering cost, and at image scale that trade becomes a real budget line. On-policy PPO on the same Atari task holds only `n_steps × n_envs` frames — for 128 steps × 8 envs, about 29 MB. Three orders of magnitude less RAM.

| Decision axis | Favors on-policy | Favors off-policy |
|---|---|---|
| Data source | Online, fresh interaction | Offline logs / demos / replay |
| Scarce resource | Compute / wall-clock | Environment samples |
| Stability requirement | High (must not collapse) | Tolerable (have mitigations) |
| Convergence guarantee | Needed | Not critical |
| Memory budget | Tight (small batch only) | Generous (GBs for buffer) |
| Parallelism available | Abundant cores | Limited |
| LLM fine-tuning (RLHF) | Strong fit | Awkward fit |

## 9. Algorithm survey by category

Let me put concrete algorithms against each axis so the abstractions have faces. The decision tree below is the compressed version; the prose expands it.

![A decision tree routing from action space through data source and budget questions to DQN, SAC, or PPO as the recommended algorithm](/imgs/blogs/on-policy-vs-off-policy-a-practical-guide-5.png)

**On-policy camp:**

- **REINFORCE** — the original Monte Carlo policy gradient. On-policy, high variance, no value baseline. Pedagogically essential, rarely used raw.
- **A2C / A3C** — actor-critic with a value baseline to cut variance. On-policy, parallelizes beautifully (A3C's whole point was asynchronous parallel actors). Fast wall-clock, modest sample efficiency.
- **TRPO** — trust-region policy optimization. On-policy with a hard KL constraint for monotonic improvement guarantees. Powerful but expensive (conjugate gradient + line search per update).
- **PPO** — the practical descendant of TRPO, replacing the hard constraint with the soft clip. On-policy, robust, the default for continuous control benchmarks and RLHF. Slightly off-policy within an iteration (multiple epochs per batch) but discards batches between iterations.

**Off-policy camp:**

- **Q-learning / SARSA** — tabular value methods. Q-learning off-policy (learns greedy via $\max$), SARSA on-policy (learns the behavior policy via sampled $a'$). The cleanest illustration that one operator choice sets the policy class.
- **DQN (and Rainbow)** — deep Q-learning with a replay buffer and target network. Off-policy, discrete actions only, very sample-efficient relative to on-policy, but needs target nets and double-Q to be stable. Rainbow stacks six improvements (double, dueling, prioritized replay, multi-step, distributional, noisy nets).
- **DDPG** — deterministic policy gradient for continuous actions, off-policy with a replay buffer. Sample-efficient but notoriously brittle to hyperparameters.
- **TD3** — twin-delayed DDPG. Adds twin critics (clipped double-Q to fight overestimation), delayed policy updates, and target-policy smoothing. Off-policy, continuous, much more stable than DDPG.
- **SAC** — soft actor-critic. Off-policy, continuous, maximum-entropy objective (adds an entropy bonus to encourage exploration and robustness), twin critics, automatically tuned temperature. Currently the default off-policy continuous-control workhorse; excellent sample efficiency *and* relatively stable.

### The master comparison table

Here is every algorithm above against the axes that decide a project:

| Algorithm | Policy class | Replay buffer | IS correction | Action space | Sample efficiency | MuJoCo HalfCheetah (~1M steps) | Stability |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| PPO | On-policy | No | Implicit (clip, within-iter) | Both | Low | ~1.5k–3k | Very high |
| A2C | On-policy | No | None (pure on-policy) | Both | Low | ~1k–2k | High |
| TRPO | On-policy | No | KL trust region | Both | Low | ~1.5k–2.5k | Very high |
| DQN | Off-policy | Yes | None (Bellman max) | Discrete only | High | n/a (discrete) | Medium (needs target net) |
| DDPG | Off-policy | Yes | None (det. policy grad) | Continuous | High | ~8k–10k | Low (brittle) |
| TD3 | Off-policy | Yes | None (twin-Q) | Continuous | High | ~9k–10k | Medium-high |
| SAC | Off-policy | Yes | None (max-entropy + twin-Q) | Continuous | Very high | ~10k–12k | Medium-high |

The MuJoCo numbers are approximate and hyperparameter-sensitive (RL Zoo / original papers); the column is for *relative* orientation, not citation. Two patterns jump out. First, every off-policy method has a replay buffer and none of them needs importance sampling — they all dodge it via either the Bellman max (DQN) or the reparameterized/deterministic policy gradient (DDPG/TD3/SAC), which evaluate the critic at the *current* policy's actions and so never reweight stored actions. This is the practical reason off-policy *deep* RL is dominated by value/critic methods, not off-policy *policy gradients*: the former sidestep the variance explosion entirely. Second, the sample-efficiency and stability columns are almost perfectly anti-correlated with each other — the central trade-off of this whole post, visible in one glance.

## 10. A five-question decision framework

When a new problem lands on your desk, walk these five gates in order. The pipeline figure is the map; each step prunes the options until one survives.

![A five-step pipeline gating on action space, data source, budget, stability need, and final algorithm choice](/imgs/blogs/on-policy-vs-off-policy-a-practical-guide-8.png)

**Question 1 — Discrete or continuous actions?** Discrete and small → DQN/Rainbow is the natural off-policy fit; PPO also works. Continuous → SAC/TD3 (off-policy) or PPO (on-policy); DQN is out (no $\max$ over a continuous action set without extra machinery, because you cannot enumerate infinitely many actions to take a max over). *Example:* a board game with 100 legal moves is discrete → DQN is viable. A robot joint with continuous torque is continuous → SAC/TD3/PPO.

**Question 2 — Online or offline data?** If you only have a fixed logged dataset and cannot interact → you *must* go off-policy, specifically an offline-RL algorithm (CQL, IQL). On-policy is impossible — there is no "current policy" that produced the data. If you can interact freely → both camps are open; proceed. *Example:* you have a year of logged recommender clicks but cannot A/B test a new policy on live users until launch → offline RL. You have a simulator you can query freely → either camp.

**Question 3 — Sample budget or compute budget?** If environment steps are the scarce resource (real robot, real money, real patients) → off-policy for sample efficiency. If compute/time is scarce but the simulator is cheap and parallelizable → on-policy, parallelize across many workers and burn steps to save wall-clock. *Example:* a physical drone that flies at 30 Hz and breaks props → samples are scarce → SAC. A fast game simulator on a 128-core cluster → seconds are scarcer than steps → PPO.

**Question 4 — How much exploration risk and how much stability do you need?** If a single bad update destroying the policy is catastrophic and you need predictable, monotonic-ish curves → on-policy (PPO's clip / TRPO's trust region). If you can tolerate the occasional dip and rely on engineering mitigations (target nets, twin critics) → off-policy is fine. *Example:* a production system where a policy collapse means a customer-facing outage → PPO's predictability. A research sweep where you can restart from a checkpoint → off-policy's occasional crash is acceptable.

**Question 5 — Do you need a convergence guarantee or are you fine-tuning an LLM?** Need theoretical guarantees or doing RLHF → on-policy (PPO). Otherwise let questions 1–4 decide. *Example:* aligning a chat model from human preferences → PPO, by construction (section 13).

```python
def choose_algorithm(action_space, data, scarce_resource, needs_stability, is_rlhf):
    if data == "offline":
        return "Offline RL: CQL or IQL (off-policy, no interaction)"
    if is_rlhf:
        return "PPO (on-policy) — RLHF generates from the current model"
    if action_space == "discrete":
        if scarce_resource == "samples":
            return "DQN / Rainbow (off-policy)"
        return "PPO (on-policy) — easy to parallelize"
    # continuous
    if scarce_resource == "samples":
        return "SAC (off-policy, sample-efficient, stable)"
    if needs_stability:
        return "PPO (on-policy, robust)"
    return "SAC or TD3 (off-policy)"

print(choose_algorithm("continuous", "online", "samples", True, False))
# -> SAC (off-policy, sample-efficient, stable)
print(choose_algorithm("discrete", "online", "compute", False, False))
# -> PPO (on-policy) — easy to parallelize
print(choose_algorithm("continuous", "offline", "samples", True, False))
# -> Offline RL: CQL or IQL (off-policy, no interaction)
```

Notice the two parameters that *are* the distinction made concrete: SAC has `buffer_size=1_000_000` (it hoards data) and no clip; PPO has `n_steps` + `n_epochs` (collect, reuse a few times, discard) and `clip_range=0.2` (bound the step). Everything else is shared. The API barely changes; the learning dynamics change completely.

### A minimal off-policy SAC training loop, from scratch

To see what `sac.learn()` is actually doing — and where the replay buffer plugs in — here is the bare bones of an off-policy actor-critic loop. It interleaves single-step collection with replay-sampled updates, the defining rhythm of off-policy learning:

```python
import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

def sac_train_loop(env, actor, critic, critic_target, buffer,
                   actor_opt, critic_opt, total_steps=1_000_000,
                   gamma=0.99, tau=0.005, alpha=0.2, batch_size=256,
                   warmup=10_000):
    state, _ = env.reset()
    for step in range(total_steps):
        # --- COLLECT one transition with the CURRENT (behavior) policy ---
        if step < warmup:
            action = env.action_space.sample()           # random warmup fills buffer
        else:
            with torch.no_grad():
                action, _ = actor.sample(torch.as_tensor(state).float())
                action = action.numpy()
        next_state, reward, term, trunc, _ = env.step(action)
        buffer.add(state, action, reward, next_state, float(term))
        state = env.reset()[0] if (term or trunc) else next_state

        if buffer.size < batch_size:
            continue

        # --- UPDATE from a random minibatch of the ENTIRE history (off-policy) ---
        s, a, r, s2, d = (torch.as_tensor(x).float() for x in buffer.sample(batch_size))

        with torch.no_grad():
            a2, logp2 = actor.sample(s2)                 # next action from CURRENT policy
            q_next = critic_target(s2, a2) - alpha * logp2   # entropy-regularized target
            target_q = r + gamma * (1 - d) * q_next
        # critic regression toward the bootstrapped target
        critic_loss = F.mse_loss(critic(s, a), target_q)
        critic_opt.zero_grad(); critic_loss.backward(); critic_opt.step()

        # actor maximizes Q minus entropy temperature * log-prob (reparameterized)
        a_new, logp = actor.sample(s)
        actor_loss = (alpha * logp - critic(s, a_new)).mean()
        actor_opt.zero_grad(); actor_loss.backward(); actor_opt.step()

        # Polyak-averaged target network: slows the bootstrap leg of the deadly triad
        with torch.no_grad():
            for p, pt in zip(critic.parameters(), critic_target.parameters()):
                pt.mul_(1 - tau).add_(tau * p)
```

Three lines carry the entire off-policy character: `buffer.add(...)` (store everything), `buffer.sample(batch_size)` (learn from the whole history), and the Polyak target-network update (tame the deadly triad). Note that SAC dodges importance sampling because the critic target is evaluated at `a2` — an action freshly drawn from the *current* policy in $s'$ — not at the *stored* action from the behavior policy. It never reweights the stored action, so no IS ratio appears. Compare this to the next loop.

### A minimal on-policy PPO training loop, from scratch

The on-policy rhythm is the opposite: collect a *fresh* batch under the current policy, update on it a few times, then *discard it entirely* and collect again. No buffer survives across iterations.

```python
import torch

def ppo_train_loop(envs, actor_critic, optimizer, total_iters=2000,
                   n_steps=2048, n_epochs=10, clip=0.2, gamma=0.99, lam=0.95):
    for it in range(total_iters):
        # --- COLLECT a fresh on-policy batch with the CURRENT policy ---
        states, actions, logp_old, rewards, values, dones = [], [], [], [], [], []
        s = envs.reset()
        for _ in range(n_steps):
            with torch.no_grad():
                a, lp, v = actor_critic.act(s)           # act under CURRENT policy
            s2, r, done, _ = envs.step(a)
            states.append(s); actions.append(a); logp_old.append(lp)
            rewards.append(r); values.append(v); dones.append(done)
            s = s2
        adv, returns = compute_gae(rewards, values, dones, gamma, lam)  # advantages

        S, A, LP, ADV, RET = map(torch.as_tensor, (states, actions, logp_old, adv, returns))
        ADV = (ADV - ADV.mean()) / (ADV.std() + 1e-8)

        # --- UPDATE: reuse THIS batch for a few epochs, with the clip as governor ---
        for _ in range(n_epochs):
            logp, value, entropy = actor_critic.evaluate(S, A)
            ratio = torch.exp(logp - LP)                  # pi_new / pi_old, the IS ratio
            unclipped = ratio * ADV
            clipped = torch.clamp(ratio, 1 - clip, 1 + clip) * ADV  # bound the step
            policy_loss = -torch.min(unclipped, clipped).mean()
            value_loss = (value - RET).pow(2).mean()
            loss = policy_loss + 0.5 * value_loss - 0.01 * entropy.mean()
            optimizer.zero_grad(); loss.backward(); optimizer.step()
        # The batch is now discarded: pi has moved, the data is stale. Collect fresh.
```

Put the two loops side by side and the entire post is in the diff. SAC's outer loop is *single-step collect, then sample the whole history*; PPO's is *collect a big fresh batch, reuse it n_epochs times, throw it away*. SAC has `buffer.add`/`buffer.sample` and a target network; PPO has the explicit `ratio` and `clamp`. The `ratio` in PPO is literally the importance ratio of section 2 — but because PPO only does a handful of epochs before refreshing, the ratio stays near 1 and the clip keeps it there, so PPO never suffers the variance explosion that a full off-policy policy gradient would.

## 11. Hybrid approaches: blurring the line

The on/off-policy dichotomy is a spectrum, not a binary, and the most interesting modern algorithms live in the middle.

**Dyna-Q** bridges via a *learned model*. It does standard Q-learning on real off-policy transitions, but *also* learns a model of the environment and generates synthetic transitions from it to do extra Q-updates. The synthetic data is "off-policy" in an even deeper sense — it didn't come from any real policy at all. Dyna-Q shows that the real axis underneath is "where does my training data come from," and a model is just another source. The practical appeal is exactly the off-policy appeal amplified: if real interaction is expensive but a learned model is cheap to query, you can do many planning updates per real step, pushing sample efficiency even further than a plain replay buffer. The risk is that a wrong model generates confidently wrong synthetic transitions — model bias contaminating the value function — which is why Dyna-style methods pair best with well-calibrated, short-horizon models.

**Retrace($\lambda$)** is a safe, low-variance off-policy multi-step return. It uses truncated importance ratios $c_s = \lambda \min(1, \pi(a_s\mid s_s)/\mu(a_s\mid s_s))$ so that the per-step weights never exceed 1, guaranteeing the variance can't explode the way naive trajectory IS does, while remaining a contraction toward $Q^\pi$. It's the correction mechanism inside ACER and several distributed agents. The genius of the $\min(1, \cdot)$ is that it makes Retrace fall back gracefully: when behavior and target agree, it uses full multi-step returns (fast learning); when they diverge, it automatically shortens the effective horizon (safe learning). It interpolates between Monte Carlo and one-step TD based on how off-policy the data is, without you tuning anything.

**Prioritized Experience Replay (PER)** deserves a mention as a subtler bridge. It is still off-policy — it samples from the replay buffer — but it samples *non-uniformly*, preferring transitions with high TD error, then corrects the resulting sampling bias with importance-sampling weights $w_i = (N \cdot P(i))^{-\beta}$. So PER reintroduces importance sampling not to correct for the *behavior policy* but to correct for its own *non-uniform sampling of the buffer*. It shows that "importance sampling" appears any time your sampling distribution differs from the distribution your estimator assumes — whether that mismatch comes from an old policy or from your own prioritization scheme.

**V-trace** is the IMPALA correction and the most important hybrid to understand, because it's how you do *distributed on-policy-ish* learning. In IMPALA, many actors run slightly stale copies of the policy and stream trajectories to a central learner. By the time the learner uses them, the data is mildly off-policy (the learner's policy $\pi$ has moved past the actors' behavior policy $\mu$). V-trace corrects this with *two* truncated ratios:

$$
\rho_s = \min(\bar{\rho},\, \pi(a_s\mid s_s)/\mu(a_s\mid s_s)), \qquad c_s = \min(\bar{c},\, \pi(a_s\mid s_s)/\mu(a_s\mid s_s))
$$

The $\rho_s$ (clipped at $\bar{\rho}$, typically 1) controls *which fixed point* you converge to, and the $c_s$ (clipped at $\bar{c}$) controls *how fast* the correction propagates back through the trajectory while bounding variance. The V-trace value target is:

$$
v_s = V(s_s) + \sum_{t=s}^{s+n-1} \gamma^{t-s} \left(\prod_{i=s}^{t-1} c_i\right) \rho_t \left( r_t + \gamma V(s_{t+1}) - V(s_t) \right)
$$

The product of $c_i$ terms is what would explode in naive IS — clipping each at $\bar{c}=1$ bounds it. The figure traces the two ratios into the value target and gradient.

![A graph showing the raw importance ratio split into truncated trace and weight terms feeding a multi-step advantage and V-trace value target into the policy gradient](/imgs/blogs/on-policy-vs-off-policy-a-practical-guide-7.png)

```python
import torch

def vtrace_targets(behavior_logp, target_logp, rewards, values, bootstrap,
                   gamma=0.99, rho_bar=1.0, c_bar=1.0):
    """Minimal V-trace value targets. Tensors are shape [T] for one trajectory."""
    log_ratios = target_logp - behavior_logp           # log(pi/mu)
    ratios = torch.exp(log_ratios)
    rho = torch.clamp(ratios, max=rho_bar)             # clipped weight
    c = torch.clamp(ratios, max=c_bar)                 # clipped trace
    values_tp1 = torch.cat([values[1:], bootstrap.unsqueeze(0)])
    deltas = rho * (rewards + gamma * values_tp1 - values)
    vs_minus_v = torch.zeros_like(values)
    acc = torch.zeros(())
    for t in reversed(range(len(rewards))):            # backward recursion
        acc = deltas[t] + gamma * c[t] * acc
        vs_minus_v[t] = acc
    return values + vs_minus_v                          # V-trace targets v_s
```

And here is an IMPALA-style distributed actor — the worker that runs a stale policy, generates trajectories, and ships them to the learner queue, leaving the V-trace correction to absorb the staleness:

```python
import torch

def impala_actor(env, get_latest_policy, trajectory_queue, unroll_length=20):
    """One async actor: run a (possibly stale) policy copy, push trajectories.

    The learner pulls these, computes V-trace targets to correct for the fact
    that this actor's behavior policy mu lags the learner's target policy pi.
    """
    policy = get_latest_policy()       # snapshot: this is the BEHAVIOR policy mu
    state, _ = env.reset()
    while True:
        states, actions, behavior_logps, rewards, dones = [], [], [], [], []
        for _ in range(unroll_length):
            with torch.no_grad():
                action, behavior_logp = policy.act(torch.as_tensor(state).float())
            next_state, reward, term, trunc, _ = env.step(action.numpy())
            states.append(state); actions.append(action)
            behavior_logps.append(behavior_logp)        # logged for the IS ratio later
            rewards.append(reward); dones.append(float(term))
            state = env.reset()[0] if (term or trunc) else next_state
        # Ship the trajectory WITH the behavior log-probs so the learner can
        # recompute pi/mu. The actor never waits for the learner -> async, scalable.
        trajectory_queue.put((states, actions, behavior_logps, rewards, dones))
        policy = get_latest_policy()    # refresh; new mu will lag pi by a few updates
```

The key line is `behavior_logps.append(behavior_logp)`: the actor *logs the behavior policy's probabilities* so that the learner, holding a newer policy, can later form the $\pi/\mu$ ratio that V-trace truncates. This is the whole architecture of scalable RL — decouple cheap, parallel, slightly-stale collection from a single learner, and pay for the staleness with a bounded importance correction.

These hybrids all answer the same engineering pressure: *I want off-policy data reuse and distribution, but I need on-policy-grade variance control.* The truncated ratio is the universal tool that buys you both.

## 12. Case studies with concrete comparisons

**DQN on Atari (Mnih et al., 2015).** The landmark "Human-level control" paper trained a single off-policy DQN architecture on 49 Atari games, exceeding human performance on 29 of them. The replay buffer (1M frames) and target network were the two innovations that made off-policy deep Q-learning stable — and, as section 5 showed, they are precisely the surgical mitigations for two legs of the deadly triad. DQN's sample budget was large by today's standards — 50M frames (200M with frame-skip) per game — but it was *off-policy reuse* of that replay buffer that made deep value learning work at all. An on-policy method discarding every frame after one update could never have extracted enough signal from 50M frames, because each frame would have contributed to exactly one gradient step instead of the ~hundreds it contributes to under replay. The off-policy choice was not an optimization here; it was the enabling condition.

**SAC vs PPO on MuJoCo.** The cleanest head-to-head in the literature. On HalfCheetah-v4, Hopper-v4, and Walker2d-v4, SAC's sample-efficiency curves reach strong returns in ~1M steps; PPO needs several million and often plateaus lower without tuning. The timeline below shows the canonical shape: off-policy reaches the target score far sooner in *steps*. But run both on a 64-core box with a cheap sim and PPO can finish sooner in *seconds*. The benchmark you cite depends entirely on whether the x-axis is steps or wall-clock — which is the entire lesson of section 6 reduced to a single plot. A reviewer who shows you a sample-efficiency curve has told you who wins on a real robot; a reviewer who shows you a wall-clock curve on a cheap sim has told you who wins in a research sweep. They can be the *opposite* algorithms.

![A timeline showing SAC reaching a target score around 300k steps, PPO around 5M steps, and DQN around 10M steps](/imgs/blogs/on-policy-vs-off-policy-a-practical-guide-6.png)

**OpenAI Five (Dota 2, 2019).** The extreme on-policy case. OpenAI Five used PPO with Generalized Advantage Estimation across thousands of CPU cores, collecting the equivalent of ~900 years of gameplay per day. Per-sample it was staggeringly inefficient. But because the game simulated fast and PPO parallelized across the whole cluster, wall-clock training to superhuman play was feasible. This is the canonical demonstration that *on-policy + massive parallelism beats off-policy when samples are cheap.* Crucially, OpenAI Five could *not* have used a single-learner off-policy method at that scale — the bottleneck would have been the learner reading one replay buffer, exactly the asymmetry of section 6. Their architecture scaled the collection axis, which only on-policy parallelizes for free.

**AlphaGo / AlphaZero.** A model-based hybrid: self-play generates data, and the policy/value nets are trained on a sliding window of recent self-play games — slightly off-policy in that the data comes from earlier network versions, with MCTS providing the improvement operator. The sliding window is itself a deliberate off-policy/on-policy compromise: keep enough recent games to learn efficiently, but discard old ones whose generating policy is too stale to be representative — a hand-tuned version of the same staleness trade-off V-trace solves automatically. It sits between the camps and shows that at the frontier the clean dichotomy dissolves into "where does the data come from and how do I correct for staleness."

## 13. The RLHF case: why InstructGPT uses PPO

This deserves its own treatment because it's where most readers actually encounter the on/off-policy choice today, and the reasoning is subtle.

In RLHF you have a language model policy $\pi_\theta$. Training a step looks like: sample a prompt, generate a completion *from the current $\pi_\theta$*, score that completion with a learned reward model $r_\phi$, and update $\pi_\theta$ to increase expected reward. The objective, with the all-important KL penalty to a frozen reference policy $\pi_{\text{ref}}$ (usually the SFT model), is:

$$
\max_\theta \; \mathbb{E}_{x \sim D,\, y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot\mid x)}\!\left[ r_\phi(x, y) - \beta\, D_{\text{KL}}\!\left(\pi_\theta(\cdot\mid x) \,\|\, \pi_{\text{ref}}(\cdot\mid x)\right) \right]
$$

Notice $y \sim \pi_\theta$: the completions are generated *from the current model.* This is on-policy by construction. PPO fits like a glove — generate, score, take a clipped policy-gradient step, repeat. The KL term is doing a job analogous to PPO's clip and TRPO's trust region: keeping $\pi_\theta$ close to the reference so it doesn't drift into a region where the reward model is uncalibrated and starts hallucinating high reward (reward hacking). It is worth noticing the layered correspondence: the reference model $\pi_{\text{ref}}$ plays the role of a behavior anchor (much like a behavior policy in off-policy learning), the KL penalty plays the role of the importance-sampling correction (penalizing divergence rather than reweighting), and generating fresh from $\pi_\theta$ keeps the actual training data on-policy so the ratio inside PPO's clip stays near 1. RLHF is, in effect, a carefully engineered way to get the *benefits* of a behavior anchor without paying the *variance cost* of true off-policy correction.

Why not off-policy? Because reusing old completions creates a vicious distributional-shift problem unique to this setting. A completion generated by a checkpoint from 1,000 steps ago was sampled from a policy that no longer exists. The reward model's scores on it no longer reflect what the current policy would produce, and the importance ratios across a 500-token completion are the trajectory-IS variance explosion from section 3 in its purest form — a product of 500 per-token ratios.

#### Worked example: why a 500-token completion is hopeless off-policy

Take the section-3 arithmetic and apply it to language. A completion is a 500-token trajectory. Suppose the current policy and the 1,000-steps-ago checkpoint differ by a very modest per-token ratio averaging 1.01 (a 1% disagreement per token — far smaller than the divergence you actually get after 1,000 gradient steps). The trajectory-level importance weight is $1.01^{500} \approx 145$. At a more realistic 1.05 average per-token ratio the weight is $1.05^{500} \approx 4 \times 10^{10}$. With sequences this long, even a whisper of per-token policy drift compounds into an importance weight that makes the off-policy gradient pure noise — the effective sample size of an entire batch of completions collapses to essentially one. This is *the* mathematical reason RLHF cannot reuse stale completions and must regenerate on-policy every iteration. The horizon length that makes language modeling powerful is exactly what makes off-policy correction hopeless for it.

So RLHF stays on-policy: generate fresh completions every iteration.

```python
from trl import PPOTrainer, PPOConfig, AutoModelForCausalLMWithValueHead
from trl.core import respond_to_batch
from transformers import AutoTokenizer
import torch

model = AutoModelForCausalLMWithValueHead.from_pretrained("gpt2")
ref_model = AutoModelForCausalLMWithValueHead.from_pretrained("gpt2")  # frozen reference
tokenizer = AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained("gpt2")
tokenizer.pad_token = tokenizer.eos_token

config = PPOConfig(batch_size=8, learning_rate=1.41e-5, init_kl_coef=0.2)  # the KL governor
ppo_trainer = PPOTrainer(config, model, ref_model, tokenizer)

query = tokenizer("Explain reinforcement learning to a beginner:", return_tensors="pt")
# 1. Generate FRESH from the CURRENT policy -> this is what makes it on-policy
response_tensor = respond_to_batch(model, query["input_ids"], txt_len=40)
# 2. Score with a reward model (here a placeholder scalar)
reward = [torch.tensor(1.2)]
# 3. On-policy PPO step on the just-generated data, then it's discarded
ppo_trainer.step([query["input_ids"][0]], [response_tensor[0]], reward)
```

The interesting recent twist: **DPO (Direct Preference Optimization)** sidesteps the whole RL loop by reformulating the KL-regularized objective so it can be optimized directly on a *fixed* preference dataset with a simple classification-style loss — which makes it effectively *off-policy* (it learns from logged preference pairs, no generation loop). DPO's popularity is partly a reaction to PPO's on-policy cost: generating completions every step is expensive (you run a full autoregressive decode for every training example, every iteration). So the field is actively pushing back toward off-policy even in alignment, trading some of PPO's on-policy fidelity for DPO's data reuse and simplicity. The catch is the catch of all off-policy methods — DPO can drift toward regions of completion space its fixed preference dataset never covered, and a chorus of follow-up work (iterative DPO, online DPO, identity-preference variants) exists precisely to claw back some on-policy freshness. The on/off-policy axis is *the* lens through which to understand why DPO exists *and* why its successors keep reintroducing on-policy generation. For the mechanics of DPO see the training-techniques series.

## 14. When to use this (and when not to)

A decisive section, because indecision here wastes weeks.

**Use off-policy (SAC / TD3 / DQN) when:**
- Environment steps are expensive (real robots, real trades, real users).
- You have offline data and can't interact — then you *must* (CQL/IQL).
- The action space is continuous and you want maximum sample efficiency → SAC.
- The action space is discrete and you want sample efficiency → DQN/Rainbow.
- You have ample RAM for a large buffer and the engineering budget to size and tune it.

**Use on-policy (PPO / A2C) when:**
- The simulator is cheap and you have many cores → parallelize, win wall-clock.
- You need stability and predictable curves and can't risk catastrophic collapse.
- You're doing RLHF / LLM fine-tuning → PPO is the default for the reasons in section 13.
- You need theoretical convergence comfort.
- Memory is tight (image observations, embedded targets) and a multi-GB buffer is infeasible.

**Don't use deep model-free RL *at all* when:**
- You have a known, cheap-to-simulate model and a small state space → use value iteration or MCTS; they're exact and faster.
- The problem is genuinely a bandit (no state transitions) → use a bandit algorithm (UCB, Thompson sampling), not full RL.
- You have abundant labeled examples of the right behavior → supervised/imitation learning is simpler and more stable than RL; only reach for RL when you need to optimize a reward that supervised learning can't express.

A blunt heuristic that's right more often than it should be: **continuous control with a real-world sample budget → SAC. LLM fine-tuning → PPO. Discrete Atari-like with a cheap sim → either, lean PPO for simplicity or DQN for sample efficiency. Fixed dataset → offline RL.**

## 15. Key takeaways

- **The defining question is data reuse:** on-policy must learn from the current policy's fresh data and discards it; off-policy learns from any policy's data and replays it. Everything else follows.
- **On-policy is off-policy with the importance ratio pinned to 1.** The off-policy estimator is strictly more general but pays in variance.
- **Importance ratios multiply across a trajectory and explode** — variance can grow exponentially in the horizon, and the effective sample size of a batch can collapse to one. Truncation (V-trace, Retrace) or avoiding IS via bootstrapping (Q-learning) is mandatory.
- **Off-policy wins sample efficiency (often 5–40×)** because the replay buffer lets every transition be learned from many times — ~256 times under default SAC settings.
- **On-policy wins stability** because data and policy move together in bounded steps (PPO clip, TRPO trust region), and it gets honest convergence guarantees; off-policy must fight the deadly triad with target networks and twin critics.
- **Sample efficiency ≠ wall-clock efficiency.** On-policy parallelizes trivially (stateless collection) and can win wall-clock when the sim is cheap, even while losing sample efficiency badly; off-policy parallelism fights a single shared buffer and a single learner.
- **Memory is a real off-policy cost:** an image-observation replay buffer can be tens of GBs, while on-policy holds only one small batch.
- **Offline data forces off-policy.** There is no "current policy" that generated logged data, so on-policy is structurally impossible.
- **RLHF is on-policy by construction** (you generate from the current model), and the 500-token horizon makes off-policy correction mathematically hopeless — which is why PPO dominates alignment, though DPO and its online successors keep renegotiating that line.
- **The frontier algorithms are hybrids** (Dyna-Q, V-trace, Retrace, PER, AlphaZero) that buy off-policy reuse with on-policy-grade variance control via truncated ratios.
