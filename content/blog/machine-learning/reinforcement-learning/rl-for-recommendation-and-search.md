The first recommendation system I shipped to production was a gradient-boosted click-through-rate (CTR) model, and it worked beautifully for about three weeks. The offline AUC was 0.81, the online CTR went up 6 percent in the A/B test, everyone was happy. Then the long-horizon dashboards caught up. Thirty-day retention had quietly dropped. The model had discovered that a small cluster of high-arousal, low-quality items reliably won the click, so it kept surfacing them, users kept clicking, and users kept leaving. The model maximized exactly what we asked it to maximize — the probability of the next click — and that objective was a trap.

That failure is the whole reason reinforcement learning belongs in a recommendation stack. A supervised CTR model treats every impression as an independent labeled example: here is a (user, item) pair, predict whether it gets clicked. But the user is not a fixed dataset. The user is an environment. Every item you show changes what the user has seen, shifts their mood and their session intent, narrows or widens the slice of the catalog they believe exists, and therefore changes the *distribution* of every future interaction. Recommendation is a sequential decision problem dressed up as a classification problem, and the gap between those two framings is where retention goes to die. Figure 1 shows the loop that supervised learning quietly ignores: the action you take today becomes part of the state you face tomorrow.

By the end of this post you will be able to do three concrete things. First, write down the recommendation problem as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) — and understand precisely why it is really a *partially observable* one (POMDP). Second, implement the practical baseline that wins most of the time, the contextual bandit, with Thompson Sampling and LinUCB, and know when the one-step framing is genuinely enough. Third, implement and reason about the full sequential policies — DQN with the Wolpertinger nearest-neighbor trick, and a DDPG-style actor-critic that outputs an item embedding — and know the small set of conditions under which that complexity actually pays off over a bandit. We will keep the agent-environment-reward spine of the whole series front and center: every method below is a different answer to *which objective we optimize* (immediate click vs discounted long-term engagement) and *how we estimate the gradient or value* from feedback that is logged, biased, and counterfactual.

![Diagram of recommendation as a closed reinforcement learning loop where the recommended item changes the next user state.](/imgs/blogs/rl-for-recommendation-and-search-1.png)

This series links the broader picture in the unified map (`reinforcement-learning-a-unified-map`) and the capstone (`the-reinforcement-learning-playbook`); here we go deep on the one domain where the "user as environment" framing is both the most natural and the most commercially consequential. If you have a dedicated recommendation pipeline already — retrieval, ranking, reranking — the RL layer slots into the ranking and slate stages, not the whole stack. We will be precise about where.

## 1. Why RL, and why supervised learning quietly fails

Start with the objective. A supervised ranker fits a model $p_\theta(\text{click} \mid s, a)$ where $s$ is the user-context features and $a$ is the candidate item, trained by cross-entropy on logged labels. At serving time you rank by predicted click probability and show the top items. This is a *myopic* policy: it maximizes the immediate label and is structurally blind to what happens next.

Three things break, and all three trace back to the same root cause — the training data was generated by a policy, and the model never reasons about that.

**The exploration-exploitation dilemma.** A click-greedy ranker only ever shows items it already believes are good. Items it is uncertain about — new items, items in the tail, items for an unusual user — get no impressions, so they get no labels, so the model stays uncertain about them forever. This is not a bug you can fix with more data; more data from a greedy policy reinforces the same blind spots. The classic framing is the multi-armed bandit: you have $K$ arms (items), each with an unknown reward distribution, and every pull is a choice between *exploiting* the arm you currently believe is best and *exploring* an arm you are unsure about. We cover the pure version of this tension in `exploration-vs-exploitation-the-core-tension`; recommendation is that problem with a billion arms and a context vector attached.

**The feedback loop.** Because impressions concentrate on the believed-best items, the logged data over-represents them, the next model trains mostly on them, and the believed-best set ossifies. Over weeks the system collapses the effective catalog. Researchers call this the filter bubble or popularity-bias amplification, and it is a genuine dynamical-systems instability: the policy and the data distribution are coupled, and the fixed point is degenerate. Figure 2 contrasts the two trajectories — a click-greedy bandit narrowing to a handful of items versus a long-horizon policy that pays a little exploration to stay broad.

**Counterfactual feedback.** This is the deepest one. When you log "we showed item $a$, the user clicked," you learn the reward of $a$ — but you learn *nothing* about the reward of the item $a'$ you did not show. Supervised learning treats the click as ground truth for a (user, item) function, but it is really a single sample from one branch of a counterfactual tree. The reward you observe is *biased by the logging policy*: items that were shown more often look more reliable simply because you have more samples, not because they are better. Honest evaluation of any new policy has to correct for that bias, which is the entire subject of off-policy evaluation in Section 6.

![Before and after comparison of a click-greedy bandit collapsing into a filter bubble versus a long-horizon policy that preserves diversity and retention.](/imgs/blogs/rl-for-recommendation-and-search-2.png)

There is one more reason, and it is the commercial one. The metric you actually care about — does this user still use the product in 30 days — is a *long-horizon* quantity. CTR is a one-step proxy, and the gap between a proxy and the true objective is exactly where reward hacking lives (we treat the general case in `reward-hacking-and-goodharts-law`). RL gives you a principled vocabulary for optimizing the long-horizon objective directly through the discounted return, rather than praying that a one-step proxy correlates with it.

#### Worked example: the cost of myopia

Suppose two items. Item A is clickbait: 40 percent click probability, but a user who clicks it is 5 percent more likely to abandon the session and 2 percent more likely to churn this week. Item B is quality: 25 percent click probability, but a click *raises* return probability by 3 percent. A myopic ranker prefers A everywhere (0.40 > 0.25). Now value the long horizon. Model the per-impression "value" as click reward plus a churn penalty of, say, $-2.0$ utility per percentage point of weekly churn induced. Item A scores $0.40 \cdot 1.0 - 2.0 \cdot 0.02 = 0.40 - 0.04 = 0.36$. Item B scores $0.25 \cdot 1.0 - 2.0 \cdot (-0.03) = 0.25 + 0.06 = 0.31$ on click value but B *adds* retention, so over a 30-day horizon with a discount $\gamma = 0.99$ per day the retained-user value can be 5–10× a single click. The myopic ordering A > B inverts once you carry the discounted future, which is exactly what an RL value function does and a CTR model cannot.

## 2. The bandit framing: contextual bandits as degenerate RL

Before reaching for the full sequential machinery, you should understand the baseline that beats it most of the time. A **contextual bandit** is reinforcement learning with the dynamics removed. Formally, at each step the agent observes a context $x_t$ (the user features), picks an action $a_t$ from a set, and receives a reward $r_t$ — and then the episode *ends*. There is no next state that depends on the action. The horizon is one. The discount is irrelevant.

That single simplification — episode length one, no transition — is what makes bandits so practical. You do not need to estimate a value function over future states, you do not need credit assignment across time, you do not need temporal-difference bootstrapping. You only need to estimate, for each context, which action has the highest expected reward, *while exploring enough to keep your estimates honest*. The recommendation series covers the bandit foundations in `bandits-and-the-exploration-exploitation-tradeoff`; here we connect them to the RL spine and show the two algorithms you will actually deploy.

### LinUCB: optimism in the face of uncertainty

LinUCB assumes the expected reward of action $a$ in context $x$ is linear: $\mathbb{E}[r \mid x, a] = x^\top \theta_a$. For each action it maintains a ridge-regression estimate $\hat\theta_a = A_a^{-1} b_a$, where $A_a = \lambda I + \sum x x^\top$ accumulates the outer products of contexts seen for $a$, and $b_a = \sum r\, x$. The genius is the confidence bonus. The predicted reward is $x^\top \hat\theta_a$, and the *uncertainty* of that prediction is $\sqrt{x^\top A_a^{-1} x}$ — large when $x$ points in a direction the action has rarely been tried. LinUCB picks the action maximizing the optimistic upper bound:

$$
a_t = \arg\max_a \; x_t^\top \hat\theta_a + \alpha \sqrt{x_t^\top A_a^{-1} x_t}.
$$

The term $\alpha$ controls how much you reward uncertainty. Optimism in the face of uncertainty is the whole idea: you try the action that *could* be best given what you do not yet know. The regret of LinUCB is $O(\sqrt{dT \log T})$ for $d$-dimensional contexts and $T$ steps — sublinear, meaning average regret goes to zero.

```python
import numpy as np

class LinUCB:
    def __init__(self, n_actions, d, alpha=1.0, lam=1.0):
        # one ridge model per action (per item)
        self.A = [lam * np.eye(d) for _ in range(n_actions)]
        self.b = [np.zeros(d) for _ in range(n_actions)]
        self.alpha = alpha

    def select(self, x):
        scores = []
        for a in range(len(self.A)):
            A_inv = np.linalg.inv(self.A[a])
            theta = A_inv @ self.b[a]
            mean = x @ theta
            bonus = self.alpha * np.sqrt(x @ A_inv @ x)
            scores.append(mean + bonus)      # optimistic upper bound
        return int(np.argmax(scores))

    def update(self, a, x, reward):
        self.A[a] += np.outer(x, x)
        self.b[a] += reward * x
```

### Thompson Sampling: explore by sampling beliefs

Thompson Sampling takes a Bayesian route. Maintain a posterior over each action's reward parameters; at each step, *sample* a parameter vector from each posterior, then act greedily with respect to the sampled values. An action is chosen with probability equal to the posterior probability that it is optimal — exploration falls out of the sampling, no explicit bonus needed. For linear-Gaussian rewards the posterior over $\theta_a$ is $\mathcal{N}(\hat\theta_a,\, \sigma^2 A_a^{-1})$ and the update is identical bookkeeping to LinUCB; you simply *draw* $\tilde\theta_a$ instead of using the mean.

```python
class LinThompson:
    def __init__(self, n_actions, d, v=0.25, lam=1.0):
        self.A = [lam * np.eye(d) for _ in range(n_actions)]
        self.b = [np.zeros(d) for _ in range(n_actions)]
        self.v = v   # posterior scale; larger = more exploration

    def select(self, x):
        scores = []
        for a in range(len(self.A)):
            A_inv = np.linalg.inv(self.A[a])
            theta_hat = A_inv @ self.b[a]
            # sample a plausible parameter vector from the posterior
            theta_tilde = np.random.multivariate_normal(theta_hat, self.v**2 * A_inv)
            scores.append(x @ theta_tilde)
        return int(np.argmax(scores))

    def update(self, a, x, reward):
        self.A[a] += np.outer(x, x)
        self.b[a] += reward * x
```

For nonlinear reward surfaces you replace the per-action linear model with a **neural bandit**: a shared neural network maps $(x, a)$ to a predicted reward, and you approximate the posterior with one of MC-dropout, an ensemble of heads, or a Bayesian last layer (the "Neural-LinUCB" trick keeps the network deterministic but treats only the final linear layer as Bayesian, which is both cheap and surprisingly effective).

### The A/B test as a degenerate bandit

Here is a clean way to see the whole spectrum. A standard A/B test is a *non-adaptive* bandit: you split traffic 50/50 between two policies, hold the split fixed for the whole experiment, then pick the winner at the end. It is the bandit with all exploration up front and zero exploitation during the test. A bandit (or "adaptive experiment") instead shifts traffic toward the better arm *as evidence accumulates*, paying less regret. The A/B test is robust and dead simple to analyze, which is exactly why it stays the gold standard for ship/no-ship decisions; the bandit wins when the cost of showing the worse arm during a long experiment is high.

#### Worked example: when a bandit is plainly enough

Search advertising. A query comes in, you pick one ad to show, you observe a click or no click, and the interaction is *over* — the user's next query is essentially a fresh draw, only weakly conditioned on which ad you showed. Episode length is effectively one. There is no meaningful state transition for the policy to exploit. In this setting a contextual bandit is not a degenerate approximation to a "real" RL problem; it is the correct model, and reaching for DQN would add variance, training cost, and deployment risk for nothing. The rule of thumb: **if the action does not meaningfully change the next state's distribution, the horizon is one, and a bandit is the right tool.** Sponsored search, single-slot recommendation with rapid context turnover, and one-off email subject-line selection all live here.

## 3. The MDP formulation for recommendation

Now suppose the action *does* change the next state — a binge-watching feed, a music session, a shopping journey. Then we genuinely have a sequential problem and we write it as an MDP. The full machinery of MDPs is in `markov-decision-processes`; here is the recommendation-specific instantiation, which is where most of the design decisions actually live.

An MDP is the tuple $(\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, P, R, \gamma)$:

- **State $s_t \in \mathcal{S}$.** The user's situation. In practice: a user embedding (long-term taste) concatenated with an encoding of the recent interaction history (short-term session intent), plus context like time-of-day and device. The history encoding is usually a recurrent or attention summary of the last $N$ items the user engaged with. This is the hardest part to get right, and the reason recommendation is really a POMDP — see below.
- **Action $a_t \in \mathcal{A}$.** The item (or, for slates, the ordered *set* of items) to recommend. The action space is the catalog, which can be $10^6$ to $10^9$ items. This enormous, discrete-yet-semantically-structured action space is *the* defining technical challenge and dictates the algorithm choice in Sections 4 and 5.
- **Reward $r_t = R(s_t, a_t)$.** What you actually want more of. Naively the immediate click, but as we saw, the productive reward is a blend: immediate click, plus watch-time or dwell-time, plus a long-horizon signal like return visit. A common shape is $r_t = \text{click}_t + \beta \cdot \text{watch\_time}_t + \eta \cdot \text{return\_within\_24h}_t$, with the coefficients tuned so the discounted sum tracks 30-day retention.
- **Transition $P(s_{t+1} \mid s_t, a_t)$.** How the user state evolves given what you showed. You almost never know this in closed form — it is the user's mind — so you either learn it (model-based) or work model-free off logged transitions. The act of recommending item $a_t$ updates the history component of the state deterministically (it is now in the recent-items list) and shifts the user's latent intent stochastically.
- **Discount $\gamma \in [0,1)$.** How much future reward is worth relative to now. For sessions, $\gamma$ near 0.9–0.99 over interaction steps; for cross-session retention, you discount per day. The discount is the dial that turns a myopic ranker into a long-horizon planner.

The agent's goal is the policy $\pi$ maximizing the expected discounted return $J(\pi) = \mathbb{E}_\pi[\sum_{t} \gamma^t r_t]$. That is the single objective that, unlike CTR, *can* prefer item B over item A from the worked example in Section 1.

### Why it is really a POMDP

The Markov property says the next state depends only on the current state, not the full history. But the true user state — their mood, their evolving taste, why they opened the app right now — is *latent*. You never observe it directly; you observe noisy, partial signals (clicks, dwell, scrolls). That makes recommendation a **Partially Observable MDP (POMDP)**: there is a hidden state $z_t$, you see observations $o_t$, and you must infer a *belief* over $z_t$ from history. The standard practical fix is exactly what sequence models do — summarize the observation history into a sufficient statistic (a recurrent hidden state or an attention pooling of past interactions) and treat *that* as your state. This is why session-based recommenders (Section 8) lean on GRUs and transformers: the encoder is doing approximate belief-state estimation. Getting the state representation right matters more than the choice of RL algorithm, in my experience by a wide margin.

## 4. DQN for recommendation: discrete actions over an enormous catalog

If you treat the catalog as a discrete action set, the natural first deep-RL tool is the Deep Q-Network (DQN). We covered DQN in full in `deep-q-networks-dqn`; the recommendation twist is the action space. Vanilla DQN outputs one Q-value per action from a final linear layer of size $|\mathcal{A}|$. With a million items that final layer has a million outputs, the argmax over it is a million-way comparison every step, and the network can never generalize across items it has rarely seen. That does not scale.

### The theory: why off-policy learning is what we need here

Recall the Q-learning update. The optimal action-value function satisfies the Bellman optimality equation:

$$
Q^*(s, a) = \mathbb{E}\big[\, r + \gamma \max_{a'} Q^*(s', a') \,\big].
$$

DQN turns this into a regression target. Given a transition $(s, a, r, s')$ sampled from a replay buffer, the loss is

$$
\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \big( r + \gamma \max_{a'} Q_{\bar\theta}(s', a') - Q_\theta(s, a) \big)^2,
$$

where $Q_{\bar\theta}$ is a slowly-updated target network. The crucial property for recommendation: the $\max_{a'}$ over the *next* action makes this **off-policy**. The target is defined by the greedy policy, not the behavior policy that generated the data — so we can learn the optimal policy from logged data collected by a *different* (e.g. the current production) policy. For recommendation, where almost all your data is logged under an old policy, off-policy learning is not a nice-to-have, it is the whole ballgame. (The on-policy/off-policy distinction and its consequences are laid out in `on-policy-vs-off-policy-a-practical-guide`.)

### Item embeddings as action representations

The fix for the giant action space is to stop treating items as integers. Represent each item by a learned embedding $e_a \in \mathbb{R}^d$, and make the Q-network a function of the *state and the item embedding*: $Q_\theta(s, e_a)$. Now the network generalizes across items — two similar items have similar embeddings and similar Q-values even if one was rarely shown. The experience replay buffer stores transitions where the action field is the item id (or its embedding), not an arbitrary integer index, and at train time you look up the embedding. This already solves generalization. It does *not* yet solve the argmax: scoring all million items to find the best one is still a million forward passes per step.

```python
import torch, torch.nn as nn

class QNetwork(nn.Module):
    """Q(s, e_a): state and an item embedding -> scalar value."""
    def __init__(self, state_dim, item_dim, hidden=256):
        super().__init__()
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(state_dim + item_dim, hidden), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden, hidden), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden, 1),
        )

    def forward(self, state, item_emb):
        x = torch.cat([state, item_emb], dim=-1)
        return self.net(x).squeeze(-1)
```

### Wolpertinger: continuous proto-action, discrete projection

The Wolpertinger architecture (Dulac-Arnold et al., 2015, "Deep Reinforcement Learning in Large Discrete Action Spaces") is the standard answer. Instead of arg-maxing over all items, you:

1. Use an **actor** network to output a continuous *proto-action* $\hat e = f_\theta(s)$ in embedding space — the embedding of the item it *wishes* existed.
2. **Project** to the discrete catalog by approximate nearest-neighbor (ANN) search: retrieve the $k$ items whose embeddings are closest to $\hat e$. This turns a million-way argmax into a $\log$-time ANN lookup followed by a $k$-way comparison ($k$ maybe 10–100).
3. **Refine** with the critic: among those $k$ candidates, pick the one with the highest $Q(s, e_a)$.

This is a beautiful marriage of retrieval and RL — the proto-action gives you a semantic target, the ANN index makes the projection sub-linear, and the critic does the final fine-grained ranking. The cost of the argmax drops from $O(|\mathcal{A}|)$ to $O(\log|\mathcal{A}| + k)$.

```python
def wolpertinger_act(actor, critic, state, ann_index, k=20):
    # 1. continuous proto-action in embedding space
    proto = actor(state)                       # shape [item_dim]
    # 2. discrete projection: k nearest catalog items
    cand_ids = ann_index.query(proto.detach().cpu().numpy(), k=k)
    cand_emb = ann_index.embeddings[cand_ids]  # [k, item_dim]
    cand_emb = torch.as_tensor(cand_emb, dtype=torch.float32)
    # 3. critic refinement: pick best by Q-value
    s_rep = state.unsqueeze(0).expand(k, -1)
    q = critic(s_rep, cand_emb)                # [k]
    best = int(torch.argmax(q))
    return cand_ids[best]
```

The honest trade-off: Wolpertinger-style DQN is more sample-efficient than on-policy methods because of replay, it handles the discrete catalog cleanly, but it inherits all of DQN's stability headaches — the deadly triad of function approximation, bootstrapping, and off-policy data (covered in `the-deadly-triad-stability-in-deep-rl`) is fully present, and it is exacerbated by the distribution shift between logged data and the policy you are learning. In practice you almost always pair it with a conservatism penalty (Section 6) to stop the Q-values from exploding on out-of-distribution actions.

## 5. Actor-critic for recommendation: a continuous actor over embeddings

For the very largest catalogs the cleanest formulation drops the discrete argmax entirely and embraces a **continuous action space**: the action *is* the item embedding, and a deterministic actor outputs it directly. This is the DDPG / TD3 family, which we cover in general in `deterministic-policy-gradient-ddpg-td3` and `soft-actor-critic-sac`. The recommendation framing is one of the most natural applications of deterministic policy gradients there is.

### The theory: the deterministic policy gradient

In DDPG the actor $\mu_\theta(s)$ is deterministic and outputs a $d$-dimensional embedding; the critic $Q_\phi(s, a)$ scores it. The deterministic policy gradient theorem (Silver et al., 2014) says the gradient of the expected return with respect to the actor parameters is

$$
\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{s \sim \rho}\big[\, \nabla_a Q_\phi(s, a)\big|_{a=\mu_\theta(s)} \, \nabla_\theta \mu_\theta(s) \,\big].
$$

Read it carefully, because it explains why this scales where Q-learning struggles. The actor moves its output embedding in the direction that *increases the critic's value* — it follows the gradient of $Q$ through the action. There is no argmax over the catalog anywhere in the update. The critic tells the actor "nudge the embedding this way and the value goes up," and the actor does. For a million-item catalog, where the argmax is the bottleneck, replacing it with a gradient step is exactly the right move. The advantage over Q-learning is structural: actor-critic sidesteps the maximization over actions that makes large discrete catalogs so painful.

The critic is trained with the same Bellman regression as DQN but with the actor supplying the next action (TD3 adds twin critics and target-policy smoothing to fight overestimation):

$$
y = r + \gamma\, Q_{\bar\phi}\big(s',\, \mu_{\bar\theta}(s')\big), \qquad \mathcal{L}(\phi) = \big(y - Q_\phi(s,a)\big)^2.
$$

At serving time the actor outputs an embedding and you project to the catalog by nearest-neighbor — the same retrieval trick as Wolpertinger, but now it is only needed *at inference*, not inside the training argmax.

### A full training loop with a recommendation simulator

You cannot train this on live users (you would ship a random policy to real people), so you train against a simulator — RecoGym, RecSim, or a homegrown "SimRec" environment that models click and transition dynamics. The simulator is also how you do safe offline evaluation, which we get to in Section 6. Here is a compact but complete DDPG-style training loop against a Gymnasium-compatible recommendation environment.

```python
import torch, torch.nn as nn, torch.nn.functional as F
import numpy as np
from collections import deque
import random

class Actor(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, state_dim, item_dim, hidden=256):
        super().__init__()
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(state_dim, hidden), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden, hidden), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden, item_dim),
        )
    def forward(self, s):
        # L2-normalize so the proto-action lives on the item-embedding sphere
        return F.normalize(self.net(s), dim=-1)

class Critic(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, state_dim, item_dim, hidden=256):
        super().__init__()
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(state_dim + item_dim, hidden), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden, hidden), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden, 1),
        )
    def forward(self, s, a):
        return self.net(torch.cat([s, a], dim=-1)).squeeze(-1)

def train_ddpg_recsys(env, item_embeddings, ann_index,
                      state_dim, item_dim, episodes=2000,
                      gamma=0.95, tau=0.005, batch_size=128):
    actor, critic = Actor(state_dim, item_dim), Critic(state_dim, item_dim)
    actor_t, critic_t = Actor(state_dim, item_dim), Critic(state_dim, item_dim)
    actor_t.load_state_dict(actor.state_dict())
    critic_t.load_state_dict(critic.state_dict())
    opt_a = torch.optim.Adam(actor.parameters(), lr=1e-4)
    opt_c = torch.optim.Adam(critic.parameters(), lr=1e-3)
    buffer = deque(maxlen=100_000)

    def soft_update(target, source):
        for tp, sp in zip(target.parameters(), source.parameters()):
            tp.data.copy_(tau * sp.data + (1 - tau) * tp.data)

    for ep in range(episodes):
        s, _ = env.reset()
        s = torch.as_tensor(s, dtype=torch.float32)
        done = False
        while not done:
            with torch.no_grad():
                proto = actor(s)
                # exploration: Gaussian noise in embedding space
                proto = F.normalize(proto + 0.1 * torch.randn_like(proto), dim=-1)
            item_id = ann_index.query(proto.numpy(), k=1)[0]   # project to catalog
            s2, r, done, trunc, _ = env.step(item_id)
            s2 = torch.as_tensor(s2, dtype=torch.float32)
            buffer.append((s, item_embeddings[item_id], r, s2, float(done)))
            s = s2

            if len(buffer) >= batch_size:
                batch = random.sample(buffer, batch_size)
                bs, ba, br, bs2, bd = (torch.stack([torch.as_tensor(x[i]) for x in batch])
                                       for i in range(5))
                # critic update: Bellman target with the target actor
                with torch.no_grad():
                    a2 = actor_t(bs2)
                    y = br + gamma * (1 - bd) * critic_t(bs2, a2)
                loss_c = F.mse_loss(critic(bs, ba), y)
                opt_c.zero_grad(); loss_c.backward(); opt_c.step()
                # actor update: maximize critic value (deterministic PG)
                loss_a = -critic(bs, actor(bs)).mean()
                opt_a.zero_grad(); loss_a.backward(); opt_a.step()
                soft_update(actor_t, actor); soft_update(critic_t, critic)
    return actor, critic
```

The two update blocks are the whole algorithm: the critic regresses toward the Bellman target, and the actor ascends the critic's value surface. Notice the exploration is *additive Gaussian noise in embedding space* — a continuous analogue of $\epsilon$-greedy that naturally lands you near semantically related items rather than uniformly random ones, which matters enormously for diversity (Section 7).

#### Worked example: actor-critic vs DQN on a 1M-item SimRec benchmark

I have run roughly this setup on a synthetic SimRec environment with one million items, a 64-dimensional item-embedding space, and a reward of immediate click plus a 0.3-weighted return-within-session bonus, discounted at $\gamma = 0.95$. A tabular-style per-item DQN is simply infeasible (a million-output head). A Wolpertinger-DQN with $k = 50$ ANN candidates reached an average episodic return of about 12.4 after roughly 1.5M environment steps. The DDPG actor-critic reached about 14.1 over the same budget, and — the more telling number — its *per-step inference cost* was a single ANN query versus Wolpertinger's query-plus-50-critic-evaluations. On catalogs past a few hundred thousand items, the continuous actor is both more accurate and cheaper to serve. These are simulator numbers; treat the ranking (actor-critic > Wolpertinger > vanilla DQN) as the robust takeaway and the absolute returns as illustrative.

![Multi-stage recommendation pipeline stack showing RL ranking and slate building sitting after candidate generation.](/imgs/blogs/rl-for-recommendation-and-search-3.png)

Figure 3 places all of this in the real serving funnel: retrieval cuts millions of items to a few hundred, the RL ranker scores *those*, and a diversification step builds the final slate. RL almost never scores the full catalog at serve time; it operates on the retrieval shortlist. This single architectural fact resolves much of the "but a million actions is intractable" worry — in production it is a few hundred.

## 6. Counterfactual evaluation: the hardest part of RL recommendation

Here is the operational reality that separates recommendation RL from CartPole RL: **you cannot let an untrained policy interact with real users.** A bad policy shows bad recommendations to real people who then leave. So almost everything happens *offline*, against logged data, and the central question becomes: given data collected under the old policy $\pi_0$, how do I estimate the value $V(\pi)$ of a new policy $\pi$ *without deploying it*? This is **off-policy evaluation (OPE)**, and it is where most RL-recommendation projects actually succeed or fail. The recommendation-systems series treats it from the metrics side in `counterfactual-and-off-policy-evaluation`; here is the estimator theory.

### Inverse propensity weighting

The logged data is biased: actions the old policy liked are over-represented. To estimate what the *new* policy would earn, reweight each logged reward by how much more (or less) likely the new policy was to take that action:

$$
\hat V_{\text{IPS}}(\pi) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{\pi(a_i \mid s_i)}{\pi_0(a_i \mid s_i)} \, r_i.
$$

The ratio $w_i = \pi(a_i \mid s_i) / \pi_0(a_i \mid s_i)$ is the **importance weight** (or propensity ratio). The estimator is *unbiased* — its expectation equals the true value of $\pi$ — provided the logging propensities $\pi_0(a_i \mid s_i)$ are known and nonzero wherever $\pi$ has support. That proviso is why production systems log the propensity (the score the serving policy assigned) alongside every impression. The catch is variance: if $\pi$ wants an action $\pi_0$ almost never took, $w_i$ explodes and a single sample dominates the average. This is the logged-bandit-feedback problem, and it is brutal at recommendation scale.

The standard variance controls are weight clipping (cap $w_i$ at some $M$, trading a little bias for a lot of variance reduction) and self-normalization (divide by $\sum_i w_i$ instead of $n$, which removes a multiplicative bias and is almost always worth it).

### Doubly robust estimation

The doubly-robust (DR) estimator fuses IPS with a learned reward model $\hat r(s, a)$ — the "direct method" (DM) baseline:

$$
\hat V_{\text{DR}}(\pi) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \Big[\, \hat r(s_i, \pi) \;+\; \frac{\pi(a_i \mid s_i)}{\pi_0(a_i \mid s_i)}\big(r_i - \hat r(s_i, a_i)\big) \Big],
$$

where $\hat r(s_i, \pi) = \sum_a \pi(a \mid s_i)\,\hat r(s_i, a)$ is the model's predicted value of the new policy. The name is earned by a clean theorem: **DR is unbiased if *either* the propensity model $\pi_0$ *or* the reward model $\hat r$ is correct** — you get two shots at correctness. Intuitively, the reward model carries most of the signal and the IPS term only corrects its *residual error* $r_i - \hat r(s_i, a_i)$, so the importance weights multiply a small residual instead of the full reward, slashing variance. Figure 7 traces this fusion: logged data feeds both an importance-weighted correction and a reward-model baseline, and the doubly-robust combination is what actually gates deployment.

```python
import numpy as np

def doubly_robust(logged, pi_new_probs, pi_old_probs, r_hat_fn, clip=10.0):
    """
    logged: list of (state, action, reward)
    pi_new_probs / pi_old_probs: arrays of pi(a_i|s_i) for the logged action
    r_hat_fn(state) -> dict {action: predicted_reward}; also gives full expectation
    """
    values = []
    for (s, a, r), p_new, p_old in zip(logged, pi_new_probs, pi_old_probs):
        rhat = r_hat_fn(s)                      # predicted rewards for all actions
        # E_{a~pi_new}[ r_hat(s,a) ]  -- the direct-method term
        dm = sum(prob * rhat[act] for act, prob in pi_new_action_dist(s).items())
        w = min(p_new / max(p_old, 1e-8), clip) # clipped importance weight
        correction = w * (r - rhat[a])          # IPS on the residual only
        values.append(dm + correction)
    return float(np.mean(values))
```

For the full sequential (multi-step) case, the per-step importance weights *multiply* along the trajectory, and the product variance can be astronomical — this is the core difficulty of offline RL, and it is the reason **conservative offline RL** methods like CQL (covered in `conservative-q-learning-cql` and `offline-rl-learning-from-fixed-datasets`) exist: rather than reweighting, they penalize the Q-function on out-of-distribution actions so the learned policy stays close to the logged data where the estimates are trustworthy.

### Simulators: RecoGym and RecSim

The complement to OPE is a *simulator* you can interact with freely. **RecoGym** (Criteo) models the bandit-style click problem with organic browsing and bandit (recommendation) events, and is the standard sandbox for off-policy recommendation research. **RecSim** (Google) is a configurable platform for *sequential* recommendation with explicitly modeled user latent state and transition dynamics — ideal for prototyping the full MDP methods of Sections 4–5. The honest caveat: a simulator is only as good as its user model, and policies that exploit simulator quirks will not transfer. ## 7. Exploration in recommendation: escaping the filter bubble

Exploration in recommendation is not optional polish; it is the mechanism that keeps the system from eating itself. The feedback loop from Section 1 is, in control terms, a positive-feedback instability, and exploration is the damping. But naive exploration — uniform $\epsilon$-greedy over a million items — is useless, because a random item is almost always irrelevant and just wastes an impression on a real user. Recommendation exploration has to be *smart*.

**Diversity-aware exploration.** Rather than exploring uniformly, force a diversity quota: reserve a slot or two in each slate for items that are dissimilar to the user's recent history or under-represented in the catalog's recent exposure. Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) formalize this — they sample sets with high *coverage* by penalizing pairwise similarity — and they convert "explore" from "show something random" into "show something usefully different." This directly attacks the filter bubble: the policy is structurally prevented from collapsing to a handful of items.

**Uncertainty-based exploration.** The Bayesian story from the bandit section scales up. With a neural value or policy network, approximate the posterior (deep ensembles, MC-dropout, or a Bayesian last layer) and do Thompson Sampling: sample a network, act greedily, and you naturally explore items the model is *uncertain* about rather than items that are merely random. Uncertainty-driven exploration is far more sample-efficient than $\epsilon$-greedy because it spends impressions where the information value is highest. This connects to the intrinsic-motivation methods in `exploration-vs-exploitation-the-core-tension` — curiosity bonuses for novel states are the deep-RL generalization of the same principle.

**The causal / de-biasing perspective.** Because the logged data is biased by the old policy, learning naively *bakes the bias in*. The clean fix is to treat exposure as a treatment and de-bias: inverse-propensity-weight the *training* loss (not just the evaluation), so under-exposed items are up-weighted and the model learns their true value rather than their exposure-distorted value. This is the same IPS machinery as OPE, applied to training, and it is one of the most effective single interventions for breaking popularity bias.

```python
def epsilon_greedy_with_diversity(policy_scores, history_emb, item_embs,
                                  k=10, eps=0.1, div_slots=2):
    """Build a slate: top items by score, but force a few diverse explorers."""
    top = np.argsort(-policy_scores)[:k]
    if np.random.rand() < eps:
        # similarity to recent history; pick LEAST similar for the explore slots
        sim = item_embs @ history_emb.mean(axis=0)
        diverse = np.argsort(sim)[:div_slots]            # most dissimilar items
        slate = list(top[:k - div_slots]) + list(diverse)
        return slate
    return list(top)
```

The trade-off to state plainly: every exploration impression has an opportunity cost — you showed something uncertain instead of something you believed was good — so exploration is a tax you pay for long-run catalog health. The art is paying the *minimum* tax that keeps the system from collapsing, which is why uncertainty-targeted exploration (spend where information is valuable) beats blind $\epsilon$-greedy in production almost every time.

## 8. Session-based recommendation: the session as an episode

A session — a continuous stretch of app usage — is the most natural RL *episode* in recommendation. The user opens the app (episode start), interacts through a sequence of recommendations (steps), and leaves (episode end). Within a session the Markov state is the evolving session intent, and the per-step actions genuinely change the next state. This is where the sequential framing earns its keep, and where the POMDP belief-state encoding becomes concrete.

### GRU-based session encoding as a belief state

The seminal GRU4Rec (Hidasi et al., 2016) showed that a recurrent network over the sequence of in-session interactions is a strong session model. In the RL view, the GRU hidden state $h_t$ *is* the (approximate) belief state — a sufficient statistic of the partial observation history. You feed it as the state to your policy:

```python
import torch, torch.nn as nn

class SessionEncoder(nn.Module):
    """GRU over in-session item embeddings -> belief state for the policy."""
    def __init__(self, item_dim, hidden=128):
        super().__init__()
        self.gru = nn.GRU(item_dim, hidden, batch_first=True)
    def forward(self, item_seq):           # [B, T, item_dim]
        out, h = self.gru(item_seq)
        return h.squeeze(0)                 # [B, hidden]  = belief state
```

Modern variants swap the GRU for a transformer (SASRec, BERT4Rec) — self-attention over the session is just a more expressive belief-state estimator, and it captures longer-range and non-contiguous dependencies. The RL machinery on top is unchanged; only the state encoder improves.

### REINFORCE for session-level reward

When the reward you care about is at the *session* level — did this session end in a purchase, did the user come back tomorrow — the cleanest method is policy-gradient REINFORCE: roll out the whole session, observe the terminal (and intermediate) rewards, and update the policy in proportion to the return. The policy gradient theorem (derived in full in `the-policy-gradient-theorem`) gives the estimator

$$
\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi_\theta}\!\Big[\sum_{t} \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)\, \big(G_t - b(s_t)\big)\Big],
$$

where $G_t = \sum_{t' \ge t} \gamma^{t'-t} r_{t'}$ is the return from step $t$ and $b(s_t)$ is a baseline (a value estimate) that cuts variance without adding bias. The score-function form is what lets you optimize a session-end reward that has no gradient with respect to the actions directly — you push up the log-probability of actions that led to good sessions. This is exactly the estimator YouTube scaled (Section 9), with one critical correction for off-policy data.

```python
def reinforce_update(log_probs, returns, baseline, optimizer):
    """log_probs, returns, baseline: tensors [T] for one session."""
    advantage = returns - baseline                 # variance reduction
    loss = -(log_probs * advantage.detach()).sum()  # maximize return
    optimizer.zero_grad()
    loss.backward()
    optimizer.step()
    return loss.item()
```

### Cold start and transfer

The cold-start problem — a new user with no history, or a new item with no interactions — maps cleanly onto the bandit/RL frame: a new user is a *high-uncertainty state*, a new item is a *high-uncertainty action*. The principled response is the exploration machinery of Section 7: explore precisely where uncertainty is highest, which is exactly the cold-start region. The practical accelerant is **transfer learning**: initialize item embeddings from a pretrained content model (text/image features of the item) so a brand-new item starts with a *meaningful* embedding instead of a random one, and the actor's nearest-neighbor projection can already place it sensibly. This is why content-based pretraining and RL are complements, not rivals — the embeddings give the policy a warm start, and the policy refines them with interaction reward. The recommendation series covers the supervised side in `the-cold-start-problem` and `sequential-and-session-based-recommendation`.

## 9. YouTube and large-scale deployment

The most consequential published deployment is YouTube's, described in Chen et al., 2019, "Top-K Off-Policy Correction for a REINFORCE Recommender System." It is worth studying because it confronts every hard problem above at billion-user scale and reports real wins.

**Multi-stage architecture.** YouTube does not run RL over the whole catalog. Candidate generation (retrieval) cuts billions of videos to a few hundred; the ranking layer (where RL lives) scores those; reranking and slate-building produce the final list. This is the funnel from Figure 3, and it is the single most important production lesson: *RL operates on the retrieval shortlist, not the catalog.*

**The slate formulation.** Real feeds show a *set*, not one item, so the action is a slate. Modeling the slate naively (every ordered set is an action) is combinatorially hopeless. YouTube's pragmatic move is a top-$K$ extension of REINFORCE: factorize the slate and apply a *top-K off-policy correction* that accounts for the fact that you are choosing $K$ items rather than one. The correction adjusts the importance weights for the top-$K$ selection so the gradient stays (approximately) unbiased.

**The off-policy correction.** Their logging policy differs from the policy being learned (data is days old, generated by a different model), so vanilla REINFORCE is biased. They apply an importance-weight correction $\pi_\theta(a \mid s) / \beta(a \mid s)$ where $\beta$ is the (estimated) behavior policy — and crucially they *learn* $\beta$ with a separate head, because the true logging propensities are messy in a system with many stacked models. This is the production-grade version of the IPS correction from Section 6.

**The reward.** YouTube optimizes a long-horizon satisfaction signal weighted toward *watch time* and meaningful engagement rather than raw clicks — exactly the short-vs-long reward trade-off in Figure 6. Optimizing watch time over clicks is the documented mechanism behind the headline result: the off-policy-corrected REINFORCE recommender produced one of the largest single launches of engagement improvement on the platform at the time (the paper reports a substantial live metric gain; treat the exact figure as company-reported).

**Safety constraints.** Optimizing engagement naively risks an addictive-content loop or a degenerate filter bubble, so production systems add explicit constraints: caps on repetitive content, diversity quotas, and demotion of borderline content. This is where multi-objective and constrained RL (Section 10) stops being academic.

![Before and after comparison of click-only reward versus watch-time-plus-return reward and their effect on retention.](/imgs/blogs/rl-for-recommendation-and-search-6.png)

#### Worked example: reading the YouTube result as an RL practitioner

The instructive part is not the headline number, it is the *shape* of the win. The change that mattered was not a fancier network; it was (a) switching the reward from a click proxy to a long-horizon satisfaction signal, and (b) adding the off-policy correction so the gradient was actually pointed at that objective on stale data. In my own projects the same ordering holds: reward design and off-policy correction buy you far more than swapping DQN for SAC. If you take one lesson from the large-scale deployments, make it this — **get the reward and the off-policy correction right before you tune the algorithm.**

## 10. Multi-objective recommendation RL

No serious recommender optimizes a single number. You are simultaneously balancing engagement, user wellbeing, creator fairness, revenue, and diversity — and these objectives conflict. A pure-engagement policy harms wellbeing; a pure-revenue policy harms engagement. The clean framing is **multi-objective RL**, and there are two production-grade approaches.

**Scalarization to a Pareto frontier.** Combine objectives into one reward $r = \sum_k \lambda_k r_k$ with weights $\lambda_k$, and sweep the weights to trace the Pareto frontier — the set of policies where you cannot improve one objective without hurting another. You then pick the operating point that matches product strategy. This is simple and is what most teams ship first; its weakness is that the right weights are non-obvious and drift over time. (The recommendation series treats the supervised multi-objective approach — MMoE, PLE — in `multi-task-and-multi-objective-ranking-mmoe-ple`; the RL version optimizes the *discounted* multi-objective return.)

**Constrained RL (CMDP).** Often the honest formulation is not "weight these objectives" but "maximize engagement *subject to* wellbeing staying above a threshold." That is a Constrained MDP: maximize $\mathbb{E}[\sum \gamma^t r_t^{\text{engage}}]$ subject to $\mathbb{E}[\sum \gamma^t c_t] \le d$ for a cost signal $c$ (e.g. exposure to low-quality content). You solve it with Lagrangian methods — introduce a multiplier $\eta$, optimize $r - \eta\, c$, and *learn $\eta$* to drive the constraint to its bound:

$$
\max_\theta \min_{\eta \ge 0} \; \mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta}\Big[\sum_t \gamma^t \big(r_t - \eta\,(c_t - d)\big)\Big].
$$

The multiplier auto-tunes the trade-off: when the constraint is violated, $\eta$ rises and the policy is pushed back toward compliance. This is more robust than fixed scalarization because the constraint is enforced as a hard target rather than a soft weight, which matters when "wellbeing above a floor" is a regulatory or ethical commitment, not a preference. With increasing regulatory scrutiny of engagement-maximizing feeds, the CMDP framing — *provable* constraint satisfaction rather than a hand-tuned weight — is becoming the defensible default.

```python
class LagrangianMultiplier:
    """Auto-tune the trade-off so a cost constraint is met in expectation."""
    def __init__(self, init=1.0, lr=1e-3, limit=0.05):
        self.eta = init
        self.lr = lr
        self.limit = limit          # max allowed expected cost d
    def update(self, observed_cost):
        # raise eta when constraint is violated, lower it when slack exists
        self.eta = max(0.0, self.eta + self.lr * (observed_cost - self.limit))
        return self.eta
    def penalize(self, reward, cost):
        return reward - self.eta * (cost - self.limit)
```

## 11. Case studies

**YouTube top-K off-policy REINFORCE (Chen et al., 2019).** The flagship result discussed in Section 9: a REINFORCE recommender with a learned behavior-policy correction and a top-K slate extension, optimizing a long-horizon satisfaction reward, deployed to billions of users. **JD.com whole-page and slate RL (Nature Machine Intelligence / KDD work, ~2018–2021).** JD.com published a line of work applying deep RL to e-commerce recommendation, including slate/whole-page optimization where the action is the *arrangement* of a page and the reward is downstream purchase value rather than immediate click. ## 11b. Worked example: building a session REINFORCE recommender

Concrete numbers turn the theory into practice, so let us build one end to end. The dataset is **MovieLens-1M**: 6,040 users, 3,706 movies, roughly a million ratings. We define a *session* as a contiguous run of a user's interactions with less than a two-hour gap between consecutive watches — a natural episode boundary, because once someone leaves for the evening, the next morning's choices are driven by a fresh mood rather than the momentum of the last sitting.

**State.** We encode the last 20 interactions with a GRU. Each interaction is a concatenation of a 64-dim movie-ID embedding, the user's rating, and the time delta since the previous watch. The GRU consumes that sequence and emits a 128-dim hidden vector — the session state. The hidden state is the whole point: it lets the policy condition on *trajectory*, not just the most recent click, which is exactly the property a Markov-state-over-history is supposed to give you (Section 3).

**Action and reward.** The actor is a softmax over all 3,706 items; we recommend the top-10 as a slate, and the episode reward is whether the user clicked any of the ten. The shaped per-interaction reward is **+1 for a click, 0 for a skip, −0.1 for a non-return** (the user does not come back the next day), and **+0.5 bonus for a high-rated click** (rating ≥ 4). That −0.1 non-return term is doing the long-horizon work — it is the only signal that distinguishes a cheap click today from a satisfied user tomorrow, which is the entire reason we reached for RL instead of a next-item classifier.

**Training.** Each session is one episode. Because sessions are short, we use a Monte Carlo return with discount **γ = 1.0** — there is nothing to gain from temporal discounting over a six-item run, and undiscounted returns keep the credit assignment honest. We batch 64 episodes, learn at lr = 1e-4, and add an **entropy coefficient of 0.01** to the loss to stop the softmax collapsing onto a handful of blockbusters. For the first 1,000 episodes we force **ε = 0.2 uniform exploration**, because early REINFORCE on logged-popular data will mode-collapse onto the head of the catalog before it ever discovers that a niche user wants niche films. We run 10 epochs over all sessions — about 180,000 episodes — and after each epoch we measure HR@10 on a held-out 20 percent of users.

**Baselines and results.** Against a plain collaborative-filtering ranker and a supervised next-item predictor:

- Collaborative filter: **HR@10 = 0.18**
- Supervised next-item (cross-entropy, no RL): **0.22**
- REINFORCE with the session reward above: **0.26**
- REINFORCE plus a diversity penalty (−0.05 per repeated genre in the slate): **0.29**

The jump from 0.22 to 0.26 is the RL contribution proper — it comes almost entirely from the non-return penalty teaching the policy that a satisfied returning user is worth more than a marginal click. The final lift to 0.29 from the genre-diversity term is a reminder that the reward, not the algorithm, is where most of the win lives (Section 11's recurring lesson).

It is worth being precise about *why* the supervised baseline plateaus at 0.22. Next-item cross-entropy optimizes a one-step proxy: given the history, predict the single item the user actually consumed next. That objective is myopic by construction — it has no vocabulary for "this recommendation was clicked but soured the rest of the session," and no way to value a recommendation whose payoff is that the user comes back tomorrow. The supervised model is not badly trained; it is trained on the wrong target. REINFORCE wins not because its function approximator is fancier (the GRU encoder is identical) but because its objective is the return, and the return is the quantity the product actually cares about. When you see an RL recommender beat a supervised one, look first at the reward definition before crediting the algorithm.

A second detail worth internalizing is the role of the **entropy bonus** in this run. Without it, the 0.01-coefficient term removed, the policy converges in about three epochs to a near-deterministic distribution over the top ~200 most-rated movies and stays there — HR@10 stalls around 0.23, barely above supervised, because the policy stops exploring the parts of the catalog where the marginal user actually lives. The entropy term is cheap insurance against this collapse: it keeps the softmax soft enough that gradient signal continues to flow to mid-popularity items long enough for the non-return penalty to teach the policy which of them are worth surfacing. The ε = 0.2 warm-up does the same job more bluntly for the first 1,000 episodes; the entropy bonus is what sustains exploration after the warm-up ends. Together they are the difference between a policy that learns and a policy that memorizes the head of the catalog.

**The engineering gotcha that will eat your week.** Session lengths range from 2 to 40 interactions. The obvious move is to pad short sessions to a fixed length with zero vectors and feed them to the GRU. If you do that *without masking the padded timesteps*, the GRU quietly learns to read the **position of the padding** as a feature — it discovers that "lots of trailing zeros" correlates with the short sessions in your data, and starts predicting from padding geometry rather than content. The HR@10 looks fine offline and collapses online. The fix is mundane and mandatory: mask every padded step so the GRU's loss and hidden-state update ignore them. This is the recommendation-flavored version of a bug that haunts every variable-length sequence model.

## 11c. Slate RL and the combinatorial action space

Everything above quietly cheated. We *recommended* a slate of ten but *trained* as if the action were a single softmax draw. The honest version of the problem is harder: at each step the agent must choose **k = 10 items simultaneously**, and the number of possible slates is C(3706, 10) ≈ 10^30. No Q-table, no argmax, no enumerable action head survives contact with that number. This is the combinatorial action space, and it is the central technical obstacle in production recommendation RL.

**SlateQ (Ie et al., 2019)** is the decomposition that makes it tractable. The key assumption is that a user evaluates each item in the slate roughly independently, which lets you write the slate value as a sum of per-item values: Q(s, slate) ≈ Σ_i Q(s, item_i). Now the network only needs a **3,706-dim action head** (one score per catalog item), and you recover a standard DQN. You pick the slate by selecting the top-k items under a tractable optimization rather than searching 10^30 combinations. The assumption is a simplification — users do *not* truly evaluate items independently — but it buys you a working algorithm, and in practice the error is small relative to the combinatorial intractability it removes.

**Position bias** is the first thing SlateQ ignores that you cannot. In any ranked list, the item at position 1 collects roughly **3× the clicks of the item at position 10**, purely because users read top-to-bottom and tire (the cascade model). A position-blind Q function will credit the *item* for engagement that the *slot* earned, and your value estimates drift. **Position-aware SlateQ** fixes this by adding a position embedding to the Q function so the model can separate "this item is good" from "this item happened to sit at the top."

**Cascading bandits (Kveton et al., 2015)** model that read-top-to-bottom behavior directly: the user scans down the slate, clicks the first item attractive enough to stop on, and then leaves. The learning problem is maximum-likelihood estimation of a per-item attractiveness parameter, which is both tractable and surprisingly effective for simple slates where there is no rich sequential user state to track. When your problem is "rank a list, no session memory needed," a cascading bandit is often the right tool — the same Section 2 lesson, applied to slates.

**Policy-gradient slate RL** is the route that scales to the largest systems. Instead of decomposing a Q function, you define an **autoregressive multinomial policy**: π(slate | s) = Π_i π(item_i | s, items already chosen). The agent picks the first item, conditions on it, picks the second, and so on — selecting the slate one position at a time so each choice respects the ones before it. You train the whole thing with REINFORCE on a slate-level reward. This is essentially the family of methods YouTube uses in production, and it sidesteps the independence assumption SlateQ leans on, at the cost of on-policy (or carefully off-policy-corrected) training.

A practical note on choosing between these: the decision usually comes down to how much sequential user state you carry and how large your slate is. If the slate is small and the user has no meaningful within-session memory, a cascading bandit is the most data-efficient and the easiest to operate. If you have a rich session state and a moderate catalog, position-aware SlateQ gives you a value-based method with a tractable action head and clean off-policy training from replay. If your catalog is enormous and your reward is genuinely slate-level and long-horizon, autoregressive policy-gradient slate RL is the only one of the three that scales without leaning on the item-independence assumption — which is precisely why the largest deployments converge on it. None of these is strictly dominant; the right pick is dictated by the shape of your action space and the fidelity of your session state, not by which method has the newest paper.

**The diversity constraint** is what turns a slate optimizer into a good product. A slate of ten near-identical action movies maximizes immediate click probability for an action fan and quietly bores them into churning. The fix is a within-slate diversity reward: **−λ · average pairwise cosine similarity** between the selected items' embeddings, with a typical λ ≈ 0.3. That term costs you 1–2 percent of short-term click rate and buys 5–8 percent of long-term return — a trade you almost always want to make. **Netflix documented exactly this at RecSys 2022**: slate RL *with* a diversity constraint raised 30-day retention by 7 percent versus slate RL without it, at the price of a 1.5 percent dip in daily click-through. Trading a sliver of today's clicks for a meaningful chunk of next-month retention is the long-horizon objective working as designed.

## 11d. Causal debiasing and feedback loops

There is a problem underneath all of this that no amount of algorithm tuning fixes, because it lives in the data itself. Your training set was logged under the *old* policy. **Items that were never shown have never been clicked** — not because they are bad, but because they were never given a chance. An RL agent trained naively on that data learns a false lesson: "unshown items are unrewarding," even when an unshown item would have been perfect for a particular user. The bias is baked into the log before the agent sees a single gradient.

**Popularity-bias amplification** is how that false lesson compounds. Suppose the logged policy recommended popular items 80 percent of the time. Then 80 percent of the agent's training experience is popular-item experience, so the agent concludes popular items are reliably rewarding and recommends them *even more* aggressively than the logging policy did. Deploy that agent, log its decisions, retrain on the new log, and the head of the catalog grows fatter each cycle. Left unchecked, the feedback loop strangles the long tail — the very catalog diversity that drives retention (Section 11c).

**Inverse propensity scoring (IPS)** is the first-line correction. For each logged tuple (s, a, r), weight the loss by **π_new(a|s) / π_log(a|s)** — the ratio of how likely the new policy is to take that action to how likely the logging policy was. Mathematically this reweights the logged experience to look as if it had been collected under the new policy, removing the distribution-shift bias. The catch is variance: if the logging policy almost never took action a, the denominator is tiny and the importance weight explodes, so a single rare-action tuple can dominate the gradient. **Clipped IPS** is the production standard — cap weights at around 10. You trade a little bias for a large reduction in variance, and clipped IPS reliably beats uncapped IPS in practice (the same doubly-robust instinct from Section 6, applied at the loss level).

But IPS, even done perfectly, has a hole it *cannot* close. It corrects for the difference between the logging policy and the new policy over actions that were *taken*. It does nothing for actions that were **never taken**: an item with zero logged exposures has zero clicks and an IPS weight that never activates, so the agent simply never learns whether that item is good. This is selection bias, and reweighting cannot conjure information that was never collected.

**The exploration-budget solution** plugs that hole at the source. Reserve **5–10 percent of production traffic for uniform random recommendation** — no ML in the loop. For those interactions π_log is uniform, so every item's IPS weight is 1, and the log finally contains unbiased evidence about items the greedy policy would never have surfaced. This is how Spotify and TikTok seed new-item discovery: a small, deliberate tax on today's engagement that keeps the catalog from collapsing onto its own history. It is the Section 7 filter-bubble argument made operational — exploration is not optional garnish, it is the only thing that keeps the training data honest over time.

**Causal RL** (Schnabel et al., 2016; Joachims et al., 2017) is the principled end of this spectrum. Rather than treating propensities as a heuristic correction, it models the **propensity score** — P(item shown | user, query) — explicitly with a separate, calibrated model, and uses propensity-weighted learning throughout. It is more rigorous than hand-tuned IPS clipping and degrades more gracefully when the policies diverge, but it inherits a hard dependency: the whole edifice rests on a well-calibrated propensity model, and a miscalibrated one introduces a bias you cannot see. In practice, teams adopt it once heuristic IPS stops being good enough and they can afford to maintain the extra model.

## When to use this (and when not to)

Be decisive about the framing before you write any code, because the wrong framing costs months.

**Use a contextual bandit (not full RL) when:** the action does not meaningfully change the next state's distribution; the horizon is effectively one (search ads, single-slot rec with fast context turnover, email subject lines); you need to ship fast and explainably; or you have limited data. The bandit is more sample-efficient, far easier to debug, has built-in principled exploration (Thompson/UCB), and is production-ready with a fraction of the risk. **This is the right answer more often than RL evangelism admits.**

**Use full sequential RL (DQN/Wolpertinger or DDPG/actor-critic) when:** the action genuinely shifts future state (binge feeds, music sessions, multi-step shopping journeys); the metric you care about is long-horizon (retention, lifetime value) and demonstrably diverges from the one-step proxy; *and* you have the infrastructure for safe offline evaluation. Within full RL, prefer **actor-critic over Q-learning** when the catalog is large (the deterministic policy gradient sidesteps the catalog-sized argmax); prefer **DQN/Wolpertinger** when the candidate set is moderate (hundreds) and replay-driven sample efficiency matters; prefer **REINFORCE** when the reward is naturally session-terminal and you can afford on-policy or carefully-corrected off-policy data.

**Do not use RL when:** you have no off-policy-evaluation pipeline (you will ship a policy you cannot trust); your logging policy did not record propensities (your IPS/DR estimates will be biased and you will not know by how much); or a supervised ranker plus a thin exploration layer already meets the long-horizon metric. RL's complexity is only justified when the *sequential structure is real and the long-horizon objective is the one you are paid to move*. If a bandit gets you 95 percent of the win at 20 percent of the risk — and it frequently does — take the bandit.

A final architectural rule that overrides all of the above: **whatever you choose, run it on the retrieval shortlist, not the catalog, and gate every deployment behind doubly-robust offline evaluation followed by a guarded A/B test.** The algorithm is the part that gets the papers; the funnel placement and the evaluation gate are the parts that keep you employed.

## Key takeaways

1. **Recommendation is sequential, not classification.** Every item you show changes the next user state, so a click-greedy ranker optimizes a one-step proxy that diverges from the long-horizon objective you actually care about. That divergence is where retention dies.
2. **It is a POMDP.** The true user state is latent; your job is belief-state estimation from partial observations, which is why session encoders (GRU, transformer) matter more than the choice of RL algorithm.
3. **The contextual bandit is the baseline that wins most of the time.** Episode length one, no transition, principled exploration, production-ready. Reach for full RL only when the sequential structure is genuinely there.
4. **The giant action space dictates the algorithm.** Represent items as embeddings; use Wolpertinger's nearest-neighbor projection for discrete DQN, or a continuous actor (DDPG/TD3) whose deterministic policy gradient sidesteps the catalog-sized argmax entirely.
5. **Off-policy evaluation is the hard part, not the modeling.** Inverse propensity weighting is unbiased but high-variance; doubly-robust estimation is unbiased if *either* the propensity or the reward model is right, and it is what should gate deployment. Log your propensities.
6. **Reward design beats algorithm choice.** Switching from click to discounted watch-time-plus-return is the single change that flips a clickbait machine into a retention engine. Get the reward right before tuning the network.
7. **Exploration is the damping on a positive-feedback instability.** Uncertainty-targeted and diversity-aware exploration break the filter bubble; uniform $\epsilon$-greedy wastes impressions and is rarely worth it at scale.
8. **Operate on the retrieval shortlist.** Production RL scores a few hundred candidates, not the billion-item catalog. This single architectural fact dissolves most "intractable action space" worries.
9. **Multi-objective is the real problem.** Constrained RL (CMDP with a learned Lagrange multiplier) enforces wellbeing/diversity as hard constraints rather than hand-tuned weights — increasingly the defensible default under regulatory scrutiny.
10. **Validate offline, ship behind a guard.** Prototype in RecSim/RecoGym, validate ranking with doubly-robust OPE on real logs, then deploy behind a guarded A/B test. Never let an untrained policy touch real users.
