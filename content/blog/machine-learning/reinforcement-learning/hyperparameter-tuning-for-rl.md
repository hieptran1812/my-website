Here is a story that has happened to almost everyone who has trained a reinforcement learning agent more than twice. You take a clean PPO implementation, point it at `CartPole-v1`, and watch the agent learn to balance the pole in about three minutes. Encouraged, you copy the exact same code and the exact same hyperparameters over to `HalfCheetah-v4`, hit run, and come back an hour later to a flat reward curve hovering near zero. Nothing has learned. You did not change the algorithm. You changed the *task*, and the hyperparameters that were perfect for one were quietly catastrophic for the other.

This is the central, frustrating truth of applied RL: the algorithm is maybe a third of the battle, and the hyperparameters are most of the rest. A textbook will tell you that Proximal Policy Optimization optimizes a clipped surrogate objective, and that is true and important. What the textbook usually will not tell you is that the difference between a PPO run that reaches a return of 487 out of 500 and one that flatlines at 9 is frequently a single number — a learning rate that was 3× too large, a discount factor that was set for the wrong horizon, or an entropy coefficient that let the policy collapse before it found anything good. The math is identical in both runs. The outcome is not.

This post is the field manual I wish I had when I was burning GPU-weeks on phantom convergence. We are going to go through the hyperparameters that *actually* move the needle in the three workhorse algorithm families — PPO, SAC, and DQN — and exactly how to reason about each one. We will derive why some of them matter (the discount factor is not an arbitrary knob; it literally sets your planning horizon, and we will prove that). We will write runnable code for the tuning loops, not pseudocode. And we will end with two automated strategies — population-based training and Optuna's Bayesian search — that turn tuning from a superstition into a procedure. The figure below is the map: it shows, per algorithm, the one hyperparameter that dominates everything else, and where most people waste their time.

By the end, you should be able to take any of the standard RL algorithms, look at your task, and make an *informed* first guess at the hyperparameters — then run a disciplined search that converges to something good instead of spinning grid-search wheels for a week. This post sits in the "Reinforcement Learning: From Rewards to Real Systems" series; it assumes you have met the basic RL loop (an agent takes actions in an environment, collects rewards, and updates a policy) but nothing more advanced than that. ## 1. Why RL hyperparameter tuning is genuinely harder than supervised learning

If you come from supervised deep learning, you have a mental model of hyperparameter tuning that is roughly: lower the learning rate until the loss stops exploding, pick a batch size that fits in memory, add some weight decay, and sweep a few values on a held-out validation set. That model is *wrong* for RL in three specific and important ways, and understanding why is the foundation for everything else in this post.

**First, the hyperparameter-to-performance relationship is non-monotone, and worse, it is non-monotone *across time*.** In supervised learning, if a learning rate of 1e-3 gives lower validation loss than 1e-4 at the end of training, you usually trust that ordering. In RL, a learning rate that helps in the first 100k steps can actively destroy your policy in the next 100k. The reason is that the data distribution in RL is *generated by the policy you are training*. A large step early on lets the agent escape the random-initialization plateau quickly, which looks great. But once the policy is near something good, that same large step overshoots, the new policy collects worse data, and the next update is computed on that worse data — a feedback loop that supervised learning simply does not have because its dataset is fixed. The curve of "final performance vs learning rate" is not a smooth bowl; it is jagged, and the location of its minimum depends on how long you train.

**Second, hyperparameters interact strongly, so you cannot tune them one at a time.** The classic example is batch size and learning rate. In PPO, the gradient you apply is an average over a mini-batch of transitions. A larger mini-batch gives a lower-variance gradient estimate, which means you can *afford* a larger learning rate without the update blowing up. So the "best" learning rate is not a fixed number — it is a function of your batch size. Tune the learning rate at batch size 64, then bump the batch size to 2048 to "be safe," and you will find your previously-tuned learning rate is now too small and learning has slowed to a crawl. The discount factor interacts with the value function's scale, which interacts with the value-loss coefficient, which interacts with the learning rate. These are not independent knobs; they form a coupled system.

**Third, and most insidiously, evaluation in RL is *noisy*.** When you train a classifier and measure 91.2% accuracy on the test set, you can trust that number to within a fraction of a percent. When you train an RL agent and measure an average return of 300, the *same hyperparameters with a different random seed* might give you 180 or 420. Stochasticity enters from environment randomness, from exploration noise, from the random initialization of the network, and from the order in which transitions land in the replay buffer. A frequently-cited result from the RL reproducibility literature (Henderson et al., "Deep Reinforcement Learning that Matters," 2018) is that the variance across random seeds can be so large that two runs of the *identical* algorithm and hyperparameters can look like two different algorithms. This means that if you compare hyperparameter A against hyperparameter B using a single seed each, you are mostly comparing the luck of two random number generators.

This last point gives us the single most important practical rule in this entire post, so it gets its own heading.

#### Worked example: why one seed lies to you

Suppose you are tuning the entropy coefficient for PPO on `LunarLander-v2`, choosing between 0.0 and 0.01. You run each once. The 0.0 run finishes at return 245; the 0.01 run finishes at 198. You conclude 0.0 is better and move on. But the true distribution of final returns for these settings — which you would only see by running many seeds — might be: for 0.0, a mean of 210 with a standard deviation of 60 (it sometimes collapses to a bad mode); for 0.01, a mean of 250 with a standard deviation of 25 (more reliable). Your single-seed comparison landed on a lucky 0.0 run and an unlucky 0.01 run, and you picked the *worse* setting. With five seeds each, you would have seen 0.01's mean of 250 clearly beat 0.0's mean of 210, and you would have noticed the 0.0 runs were also far less reliable — exactly the property you care about when you ship.

**The 5-seed minimum rule:** never make a hyperparameter decision from fewer than five random seeds, and report the median (not the mean — RL return distributions are skewed by occasional collapses, and the median is robust to those). If five seeds is too expensive for your full training budget, run the comparison at a *shorter* horizon with five seeds rather than the full horizon with one. A noisy comparison at the right horizon with enough seeds beats a clean-looking comparison with one seed every single time. This rule will save you from more wrong conclusions than any other piece of advice in this post.

There is one more structural reason RL tuning is hard that is worth naming. In supervised learning, your loss is a direct, differentiable proxy for the thing you care about. In RL, the quantity you optimize (a policy-gradient surrogate, a TD error) is only *indirectly* related to the return you actually want to maximize. You can have a TD error that is dropping beautifully while your agent's return goes nowhere, because the value function is becoming internally consistent around a bad policy. So you cannot tune by watching the loss; you must tune by watching the *return*, evaluated with enough seeds to be trustworthy. Keep that distinction front of mind: **loss going down is necessary but nowhere near sufficient.**

## 2. The hyperparameters that matter, by algorithm family

Before we go deep on individual knobs, you need a map of which knobs even exist for each algorithm, because they are genuinely different. Reaching for the entropy coefficient when you are training DQN is a category error — DQN does not have one in the PPO sense. Here is the honest accounting, organized by the three families we will use throughout.

For **PPO** (an on-policy actor-critic that updates a clipped surrogate objective), the hyperparameters that matter, roughly in order of how much grief they cause:

- **Learning rate** — dominant, as always.
- **Clipping coefficient ε** — controls how far each update can move the policy; PPO's whole stability story lives here.
- **Entropy coefficient β** — keeps the policy exploring; too low and it collapses.
- **GAE λ** — the bias-variance dial on the advantage estimator.
- **Number of epochs per rollout** — how many times you reuse each batch of on-policy data.
- **Mini-batch size** — couples with the learning rate.
- **Discount factor γ** — sets the horizon (shared across all families).

For **SAC** (an off-policy actor-critic with a maximum-entropy objective), the relevant knobs:

- **Learning rates** — and there are *three*: actor, critic, and the temperature/entropy parameter. You often share one value, but they can be split.
- **Target smoothing coefficient τ** — how fast the target networks track the online networks.
- **Replay buffer size** — how much history you keep.
- **Number of gradient steps per environment step** — the "update-to-data" ratio.
- **Target entropy** — the setpoint for automatic temperature tuning.
- **Discount factor γ**.

For **DQN** (an off-policy value-based method that learns Q-values and acts greedily plus exploration), the knobs:

- **Learning rate**.
- **Epsilon decay schedule** — the exploration-to-exploitation transition.
- **Replay buffer size**.
- **Target network update frequency** — the hard-update interval.
- **Discount factor γ**.
- **Prioritized replay parameters** (α, β) if you use PER.

Notice three things. The learning rate and discount factor appear in *every* list — they are universal. The clipping/entropy knobs are PPO's stability story; the τ and buffer knobs are the off-policy story shared by SAC and DQN; and each family has one or two knobs nobody else has. The rest of this post walks through these in order of impact, starting with the one that matters everywhere.

## 3. Learning rate: the one knob to tune first

If you can only tune one hyperparameter, tune the learning rate. It is the single most impactful number in nearly every RL run, and it is also the one most likely to be silently wrong when you port code between tasks. The general range for deep RL is roughly **1e-5 to 1e-3**, with **3e-4** being the famous "Adam default for RL" that works as a starting point across a startling range of tasks — so much so that it has become something of a running joke in the community (Karpathy once called 3e-4 "the best learning rate for Adam, hands down"). Joke or not, it is a genuinely sensible default, and you should start there unless you have a reason not to.

Here is the figure that captures what a wrong learning rate looks like, side by side with a right one. The diagnostic signature is unmistakable once you have seen it: oscillating reward and a gradient norm that spikes into the tens means your step is too big; a smooth reward climb with a gradient norm parked at 3 to 5 means you are in the good regime.

![Before and after comparison showing an oscillating reward curve with gradient norm spikes versus a smooth reward climb with stable gradient norm](/imgs/blogs/hyperparameter-tuning-for-rl-2.png)

**The "divide by 10 if diverging" rule.** When a run diverges — reward suddenly tanks, loss explodes, the value function outputs `nan` — the first thing to try, before changing anything structural, is dropping the learning rate by an order of magnitude. From 1e-3 to 1e-4, or 3e-4 to 3e-5. This works astonishingly often because divergence in RL is usually an instability in the optimization, and a smaller step damps it. It is the cheapest possible intervention and you should reach for it reflexively.

Let me show you how to actually watch for the divergence signature, because "the gradient norm spikes" is only useful if you are logging the gradient norm. Here is a clipped, instrumented PyTorch training step:

```python
import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

def ppo_update_step(policy, optimizer, batch, clip_eps=0.2, max_grad_norm=0.5):
    states, actions, old_log_probs, advantages, returns = batch

    dist = policy.distribution(states)
    new_log_probs = dist.log_prob(actions)
    ratio = torch.exp(new_log_probs - old_log_probs)

    # Clipped surrogate objective
    surr1 = ratio * advantages
    surr2 = torch.clamp(ratio, 1.0 - clip_eps, 1.0 + clip_eps) * advantages
    policy_loss = -torch.min(surr1, surr2).mean()

    value_loss = F.mse_loss(policy.value(states), returns)
    entropy = dist.entropy().mean()
    loss = policy_loss + 0.5 * value_loss - 0.0 * entropy

    optimizer.zero_grad()
    loss.backward()
    # Measure BEFORE clipping so the log reflects the true gradient size
    grad_norm = torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(policy.parameters(), max_grad_norm)
    optimizer.step()

    return {
        "policy_loss": policy_loss.item(),
        "value_loss": value_loss.item(),
        "grad_norm": grad_norm.item(),   # watch this number
    }
```

The line `clip_grad_norm_` returns the gradient norm *before* it clips, which is exactly what you want to log. If `grad_norm` is consistently riding the `max_grad_norm` ceiling and your reward is jagged, your learning rate is too high. If `grad_norm` is hovering near zero and nothing is learning, it may be too low (or you have a different problem, like a value function that has collapsed).

**Learning rate schedules.** A constant learning rate is fine as a default, but RL frequently benefits from decay, and for the same reason supervised learning does: large steps early to escape the plateau, small steps late to settle into the optimum without overshooting. The most common schedule in RL is **linear decay to zero over the course of training** — this is the default in many PPO implementations, including the original OpenAI baselines and Stable-Baselines3 when you pass a schedule. Cosine annealing (a smooth decay following a half-cosine curve) is also popular and slightly gentler at the end. Cyclical schedules (repeatedly ramping up and down) exist but are less common in RL than in supervised learning; they can help escape local optima but add a hyperparameter (the cycle length) and a lot of variance.

In Stable-Baselines3, a linear schedule is a one-liner — you pass a *callable* that maps remaining-progress (1.0 at start, 0.0 at end) to the current learning rate:

```python
from stable_baselines3 import PPO

def linear_schedule(initial_value: float):
    def schedule(progress_remaining: float) -> float:
        return progress_remaining * initial_value
    return schedule

model = PPO(
    "MlpPolicy",
    "HalfCheetah-v4",
    learning_rate=linear_schedule(3e-4),  # decays 3e-4 -> 0 linearly
    n_steps=2048,
    batch_size=64,
    gamma=0.99,
    verbose=1,
)
model.learn(total_timesteps=1_000_000)
```

**The warmup trick.** Borrowed from transformer training, a linear *warmup* — ramping the learning rate from near zero up to its target over the first 5k to 10k steps — can help RL specifically because the very first gradients are computed from an untrained value function. Early in training the advantage estimates are mostly noise (the critic has not learned anything yet), so taking large policy steps based on that noise is a recipe for moving the policy in a random direction before it has any signal. A warmup lets the critic find its footing before the actor commits to big moves. It is most valuable in actor-critic methods where the actor depends on the critic's estimates; it matters less in pure value-based methods.

**Separate learning rates for actor and critic.** In actor-critic methods, the actor and critic are learning different things and often want different step sizes. The critic is doing regression (fitting returns), which is relatively forgiving and can take a larger learning rate. The actor is doing something far more delicate — moving a policy that generates its own data — and often wants a *smaller* step. A common pattern is a critic learning rate 2× to 3× the actor's. SAC takes this further and gives the temperature parameter its own learning rate too. In SB3, PPO shares one learning rate by default, but you can split them with custom optimizer groups; SAC exposes the structure more directly. Do not over-engineer this early — start with a shared rate and only split if you see the critic lagging (value loss not dropping) or the actor thrashing (policy entropy collapsing while value loss is fine).

#### Worked example: rescuing a diverging HalfCheetah run

I once had a `HalfCheetah-v4` PPO run that climbed nicely to a return of about 2,400 by 600k steps, then over the next 200k steps cratered to 400 and stayed there. The value loss had spiked, and the gradient norm log showed it riding the clip ceiling of 0.5 almost continuously after step 600k. The diagnosis: the learning rate of 3e-4, fine early, became too aggressive once the policy was near a good basin — a textbook non-monotone-across-time failure. The fix was not a new algorithm; it was a linear-decay schedule starting from 3e-4, so that by step 600k the effective rate was down around 1e-4. The rerun climbed to 2,400 and kept going to 3,100 without the collapse. One scheduled number, a 30% improvement, and a run that no longer destroyed itself. This is the kind of result that makes you believe learning rate is the first thing to tune.

## 4. Discount factor: it is your planning horizon, and here is the proof

The discount factor γ (gamma) is the hyperparameter people most often treat as arbitrary — "0.99 seems standard, I'll use that" — when it is in fact the most *interpretable* knob you have. Gamma is not a vague preference for the future; it sets, in a precise and provable sense, how many steps into the future your agent can effectively plan. Let me derive that, because once you internalize it you will never set gamma by superstition again.

The return the agent maximizes from time $t$ is the discounted sum of future rewards:

$$G_t = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \gamma^k \, r_{t+k+1}.$$

Each reward $k$ steps in the future is weighted by $\gamma^k$. Now ask: how far into the future does this sum actually "see"? The weights $\gamma^k$ form a geometric series, and we can ask for the *effective horizon* — the characteristic number of steps over which the weights remain meaningful. One clean way to define it is the point at which the cumulative weight reaches a fixed fraction of the total, but the cleanest and most-used definition comes from the sum of the weights itself:

$$\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \gamma^k = \frac{1}{1 - \gamma}.$$

This is the total "mass" of the discount weights. A reward stream of constant value 1 evaluates to exactly $\frac{1}{1-\gamma}$, so this quantity behaves like the number of steps the agent meaningfully integrates over. We therefore define the **effective horizon** as:

$$H_{\text{eff}} = \frac{1}{1 - \gamma}.$$

Plug in numbers and the consequences are stark. With γ = 0.9, $H_{\text{eff}} = 10$ — the agent essentially plans ten steps ahead. With γ = 0.99, $H_{\text{eff}} = 100$. With γ = 0.999, $H_{\text{eff}} = 1000$. **Each additional nine in gamma multiplies your planning horizon by roughly ten.** This is the single most useful fact about gamma, and it means you should pick gamma by asking "how many steps separate my agent's actions from their consequences?" rather than by copying a default. The figure below shows this relationship and maps real tasks onto it.

![Graph showing how the discount factor sets the effective horizon, with gamma 0.9 giving horizon 10, gamma 0.99 giving horizon 100, and gamma 0.999 giving horizon 1000, annotated with example tasks](/imgs/blogs/hyperparameter-tuning-for-rl-3.png)

So why not just set γ = 0.9999 and plan over a 10,000-step horizon for everything? Because there is a tension, and it is a bias-variance tension. A higher gamma lets you account for distant rewards, but it also *amplifies the uncertainty* in those distant rewards. The value of a state under a high gamma depends on a long chain of future events, each of which is stochastic — the environment's transitions, the policy's action choices, the rewards themselves. The further out you look, the more noise accumulates in your value estimate. High gamma means a high-variance value function, which means noisier gradients, which means harder, slower, less stable learning. Low gamma throws away genuinely useful long-term information (bias) but gives you a low-variance, easy-to-learn value function. Gamma *is* the bias-variance dial for the temporal credit assignment problem.

The practical ranges that fall out of this reasoning:

| Task type | Reward structure | Typical γ | Effective horizon |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dense, short-horizon | Reward every step, quick consequences | 0.95–0.99 | 20–100 |
| Standard control / Atari | Reward fairly frequent | 0.99 | 100 |
| Locomotion (MuJoCo) | Dense reward, longer episodes | 0.99 | 100 |
| Sparse, long-horizon | Reward only at goal, far away | 0.999 | 1000 |
| Very long-horizon (some trading) | Consequences over thousands of steps | 0.999–0.9999 | 1000–10000 |

A few field notes. For Atari, 0.99 is nearly universal and was used in the original DQN paper. For MuJoCo locomotion, 0.99 is also standard despite long episodes, because the reward is dense (you get a small reward every step for moving forward), so the agent does not need to plan far to get signal. The 0.999 regime is for genuinely sparse-reward tasks where the payoff is hundreds or thousands of steps after the action that earned it — think a robot that only gets reward upon completing a long manipulation sequence. And be careful at the extreme: gamma above 0.999 can make the value function so high-variance that learning becomes unstable, and you may need to compensate with a larger batch size, a lower learning rate, or techniques like reward normalization.

#### Worked example: a sparse-reward maze that 0.99 could not solve

Consider a gridworld maze where the agent gets a reward of +1 only upon reaching the exit, which is 400 steps away under the optimal policy, and 0 reward everywhere else. With γ = 0.99, the discounted value of that exit reward at the start state is $0.99^{400} \approx 0.018$ — almost indistinguishable from zero. The gradient signal pointing toward the exit is so attenuated by discounting that the agent never learns to head there; it wanders. Bump gamma to 0.999, and $0.999^{400} \approx 0.67$ — now the exit reward is clearly visible from the start, and the agent learns the route. The *only* thing that changed was gamma, and it was the difference between solving the task and not. The lesson: when your reward is sparse and far, a too-small gamma can make the problem literally invisible to the agent, no matter how long you train.

## 5. Batch size and mini-batch size in PPO

PPO's data flow is fundamentally different from off-policy methods, and that difference drives how you think about batch sizes. PPO is **on-policy**: it collects a batch of rollouts using the current policy, performs several gradient updates on that batch, and then *throws the data away* and collects fresh rollouts with the now-updated policy. It must throw the data away because the data was generated by the old policy, and after a few updates the policy has changed enough that the data is no longer representative. (PPO's clipping is precisely the mechanism that lets you safely do a *few* updates on slightly-stale data without the policy running away — but only a few.)

This gives PPO two distinct batch-size knobs that people constantly confuse:

- **Rollout length / batch size (`n_steps` in SB3)** — how many environment steps you collect before each update phase. Multiplied by the number of parallel environments, this is the total batch of on-policy data. Typical values: 2048 per environment for continuous control, 128 to 256 per environment for Atari with many parallel envs.
- **Mini-batch size (`batch_size` in SB3)** — within the update phase, the rollout batch is shuffled and split into mini-batches, and each mini-batch is one gradient step. Typical values: 64 to 2048.

The trade-offs run in opposite directions. A **larger rollout batch** gives a more accurate advantage estimate and more stable learning, but means slower iteration (you collect a lot before you update) and more wall-clock between policy improvements. A **larger mini-batch** gives lower-variance gradient estimates per step, which — and here is the coupling again — lets you use a larger learning rate. This is the **"batch size proportional to learning rate" rule**: when you increase the mini-batch size, you can and often should increase the learning rate to match, because the gradient noise has dropped and you can afford a bigger step. The rough heuristic from supervised learning (linear scaling: double the batch, double the learning rate, up to a point) is a reasonable starting intuition in RL too, though RL's noisier landscape means you should validate it rather than trust it blindly.

The number of update epochs per rollout (`n_epochs` in SB3) is the third member of this trio. It controls how many times you pass over the collected rollout data before discarding it. Too few epochs (1–2) wastes the data you paid for; too many (15+) over-fits to that specific batch and, worse, pushes the policy so far from the data-collecting policy that the clipping starts working against you and the KL divergence between old and new policy blows up. The sweet spot for most tasks is **3 to 10 epochs**. SB3's default of 10 for continuous control and the original PPO paper's 3–4 for Atari bracket the useful range.

Here is a complete, runnable PPO configuration in Stable-Baselines3 that shows these knobs together, with the coupling made explicit in comments:

```python
from stable_baselines3 import PPO
from stable_baselines3.common.env_util import make_vec_env
from stable_baselines3.common.vec_env import SubprocVecEnv

# 8 parallel envs: total rollout batch = 8 * 2048 = 16384 transitions
vec_env = make_vec_env("HalfCheetah-v4", n_envs=8, vec_env_cls=SubprocVecEnv)

model = PPO(
    "MlpPolicy",
    vec_env,
    learning_rate=3e-4,   # paired with batch_size below
    n_steps=2048,         # per-env rollout length (16384 total per update)
    batch_size=256,       # mini-batch; larger -> can raise learning_rate
    n_epochs=10,          # passes over each rollout before discarding it
    gamma=0.99,           # horizon ~100 steps, right for dense locomotion
    gae_lambda=0.95,      # advantage estimator bias-variance dial
    clip_range=0.2,       # PPO trust region
    ent_coef=0.0,         # locomotion often needs none; raise if collapsing
    vf_coef=0.5,
    max_grad_norm=0.5,
    verbose=1,
)
model.learn(total_timesteps=2_000_000)
```

**Sync vs async PPO.** The configuration above is synchronous: all parallel environments step in lockstep, you wait for the slowest one, then update. This is simple and reproducible and is what SB3 does. Asynchronous variants (like IMPALA's architecture, or APPO in RLlib) let actors run continuously and send experience to a central learner without waiting, which dramatically improves throughput on many machines — but introduces *policy lag* (the actors' policy is slightly behind the learner's), which is itself a hyperparameter-like concern you must manage with importance-sampling corrections (V-trace). For a single machine with a handful of environments, stick with synchronous PPO; reach for async only when you are scaling to hundreds of actors and throughput is your bottleneck.

The **GAE λ** (lambda) parameter deserves a sentence here because it sits in the same conceptual slot as gamma — it is a bias-variance dial, but for the *advantage estimator* rather than the return. Generalized Advantage Estimation interpolates between a high-bias, low-variance one-step TD estimate (λ = 0) and a low-bias, high-variance Monte Carlo estimate (λ = 1). The standard value is **0.95**, which leans toward low variance, and it is robust enough that you rarely need to tune it; if you do, the useful range is 0.9 to 0.99. Higher λ when your rewards are reliable and you want less bias; lower λ when your value function is good and you want to lean on it for variance reduction.

## 6. The entropy coefficient: keeping the policy alive

Exploration in policy-gradient methods is governed largely by one number: the entropy coefficient (β in PPO, or the temperature α in SAC). Entropy, here, is the entropy of the action distribution — a measure of how *spread out* the policy's action choices are. A high-entropy policy is hedging, trying many actions; a low-entropy policy has committed, picking one action nearly deterministically. The entropy coefficient adds a bonus to the objective proportional to the policy's entropy, which rewards the agent for *staying uncertain*, which keeps it exploring.

The failure modes at both ends are sharp. **Too high**, and the agent is paid to stay random; it never commits to the good actions it has found, and its return plateaus below what it could achieve because it keeps throwing away good behavior to maintain randomness. **Too low (or zero)**, and the policy can collapse: it commits early to whatever looked best in the first few thousand steps, drives its own entropy to zero, and thereafter cannot explore its way out of that local optimum because it has stopped trying alternatives. Once entropy hits zero, the policy is effectively deterministic and the gradient that would push it toward a better mode has nothing to work with. The figure below shows exactly this collapse and the small bonus that prevents it.

![Before and after comparison showing policy entropy collapsing to zero with no entropy bonus versus stable entropy and continued reward improvement with an entropy coefficient of 0.01](/imgs/blogs/hyperparameter-tuning-for-rl-5.png)

Typical values for the PPO entropy coefficient are **0.0 to 0.1**. For dense-reward continuous control (locomotion), you can often get away with 0.0 because the reward gradient is informative enough on its own — the SB3 defaults use 0.0 for MuJoCo. For discrete-action tasks and anything with sparse or deceptive rewards, a small positive value like **0.01** is good insurance against early collapse, and 0.001 to 0.01 is the most common range. The cost of a small entropy bonus is low; the cost of a collapse is total, so when in doubt, add a little.

**Entropy scheduling.** Just as with the learning rate, you can schedule the entropy coefficient — start high to encourage broad exploration early, then decay it so the policy can commit late in training. This mirrors the explore-then-exploit arc you want from any RL agent. It is not always necessary, but for hard-exploration tasks a schedule from, say, 0.05 down to 0.001 over training can outperform any fixed value. Implement it the same way as the learning-rate schedule — a callable on remaining progress.

**SAC's automatic temperature tuning** is the elegant answer to "what should the entropy coefficient be?" — it says, *don't pick one, target an entropy level instead.* SAC reframes the problem: instead of choosing the coefficient α directly, you choose a **target entropy** $\bar{\mathcal{H}}$, and α is adjusted automatically by gradient descent to keep the policy's actual entropy near that target. The standard heuristic for the target entropy is:

$$\bar{\mathcal{H}} = -\dim(\mathcal{A}),$$

the negative of the action-space dimensionality. For a 6-dimensional continuous action space (like HalfCheetah), the target entropy is −6. The intuition is that this corresponds to roughly one unit of entropy "budget" per action dimension, which empirically balances exploration and exploitation across a wide range of continuous-control tasks. SAC then optimizes α to satisfy this constraint, which removes one of the most finicky hyperparameters from your search entirely. This is a large part of why SAC is so robust out of the box — it tuned its own entropy knob.

Here is the automatic temperature update, which is genuinely simple once you see it:

```python
import torch

# log_alpha is a learnable scalar; target_entropy = -action_dim
log_alpha = torch.zeros(1, requires_grad=True)
alpha_optimizer = torch.optim.Adam([log_alpha], lr=3e-4)
target_entropy = -float(action_dim)   # e.g. -6.0 for HalfCheetah

def update_temperature(log_probs):
    # log_probs: log pi(a|s) for the current batch of sampled actions
    alpha = log_alpha.exp()
    # Push alpha up when entropy (-log_probs) is below target, down when above
    alpha_loss = -(log_alpha * (log_probs + target_entropy).detach()).mean()

    alpha_optimizer.zero_grad()
    alpha_loss.backward()
    alpha_optimizer.step()
    return log_alpha.exp().item()
```

Read the `alpha_loss` carefully — it is the whole trick. When the policy's entropy ($-\log\pi$, on average) is *below* the target, the term $(\log\pi + \bar{\mathcal{H}})$ is positive, and minimizing $-\log\alpha \cdot (\text{positive})$ pushes $\log\alpha$ up, raising the entropy bonus and encouraging more exploration. When entropy is *above* target, the same mechanism pushes α down. It is a simple feedback controller that keeps entropy at the setpoint, and it is why you almost never tune SAC's exploration by hand.

**The KL penalty as an alternative.** There is a different lever for controlling how much the policy moves, used heavily in RLHF: instead of (or alongside) an entropy bonus, penalize the KL divergence between the current policy and a reference policy. This is the mechanism that keeps RLHF fine-tunes from reward-hacking — the KL term anchors the language model to its pretrained behavior so it cannot drift into gibberish that happens to score well. PPO can also use an *adaptive KL penalty* as an alternative to clipping (it was in the original PPO paper), automatically raising the penalty when the policy moves too far and lowering it when it is too conservative. In practice the clipped objective won out for most control tasks because it is simpler, but the KL-penalty variant is conceptually cleaner and is the dominant approach in the LLM-alignment world. If you are working on RLHF specifically, the training-techniques series covers that mechanism in depth.

## 7. Replay buffer size and fill ratio

We now move from on-policy PPO to the off-policy world of SAC, DQN, and DDPG, where a **replay buffer** stores past transitions and the agent learns by repeatedly sampling from them. This decoupling of data collection from learning is what makes off-policy methods sample-efficient — every transition can be reused many times — but it introduces a new family of hyperparameters about *which* transitions to keep and how to sample them.

**Buffer size** is the headline knob. The standard is **1 million transitions** for continuous control (SAC, DDPG) and Atari (DQN), and it is a default for good reasons, but it is a default you should question per task. A larger buffer holds more diverse history, which stabilizes learning by de-correlating the samples in each mini-batch (consecutive transitions are highly correlated; sampling from a big buffer breaks that correlation, which is the whole point of experience replay). But a larger buffer also holds *older* data — and old data was generated by an old, worse policy. There is a tension between diversity (favoring a big buffer) and recency (favoring a small one).

**Minimum fill before training** (`learning_starts` in SB3) controls how many transitions you collect with a random policy before the first gradient update. The standard is around 10% of the buffer, or a fixed number like 10,000 to 25,000 steps. The reason is twofold: you want enough diverse data that the first updates are not over-fitting to a tiny correlated sample, and you want the early exploration to be genuinely random rather than driven by a policy that has not learned anything yet. Set this too low and your early Q-values latch onto noise; set it too high and you waste steps not learning.

The figure below shows the right way to think about a buffer's contents — not as a uniform pool, but as a distribution of *ages*, where freshness correlates with usefulness.

![Stack diagram showing replay buffer transitions organized by age, with the newest 10 percent at high priority and the oldest 10 percent nearly useless for non-stationary environments](/imgs/blogs/hyperparameter-tuning-for-rl-6.png)

**Prioritized Experience Replay (PER)** addresses the observation that not all transitions are equally informative. PER samples transitions in proportion to their TD error — the surprising transitions, where the agent's prediction was most wrong, get sampled more often, because that is where there is the most to learn. PER adds two hyperparameters:

- **α (priority exponent)** — how strongly to prioritize. α = 0 recovers uniform sampling; α = 1 samples purely by TD error. The standard is **0.6**, a middle ground that prioritizes without ignoring low-error transitions entirely.
- **β (importance-sampling exponent)** — PER biases the sampling distribution, so you must correct for that bias with importance-sampling weights, and β controls the strength of the correction. It is typically *annealed* from **0.4 to 1.0** over training, because the bias matters most late in training when you are near convergence. The original PER paper (Schaul et al., 2016) uses exactly this schedule.

**Buffer age and non-stationarity.** Here is a subtlety that trips people up. Are transitions from 10 million steps ago still useful? In a *stationary* environment — one whose dynamics do not change — yes, old transitions are still valid samples of the same MDP, and a big buffer is pure benefit. But in a *non-stationary* environment — multi-agent settings where opponents are also learning, or financial markets whose regime shifts, or any curriculum where the task changes — old transitions describe a world that no longer exists, and training on them actively hurts. For non-stationary problems, use a *smaller* buffer (so old data ages out faster) or an explicit buffer-decay scheme that down-weights or evicts stale transitions. This is one of the clearest cases where copying the 1M default blindly will sabotage you: a 1M buffer in a fast-changing environment means you are perpetually training on a stale average of many past worlds.

```python
from stable_baselines3 import SAC

model = SAC(
    "MlpPolicy",
    "HalfCheetah-v4",
    learning_rate=3e-4,
    buffer_size=1_000_000,     # 1M for stationary control; shrink if non-stationary
    learning_starts=10_000,    # random exploration before first update
    batch_size=256,
    tau=0.005,                 # target smoothing (next section)
    gamma=0.99,
    train_freq=1,              # gradient step every env step
    gradient_steps=1,          # update-to-data ratio = 1
    verbose=1,
)
model.learn(total_timesteps=1_000_000)
```

## 8. Target network updates: the stability-tracking trade-off

Off-policy value learning has a structural instability that target networks were invented to fix, and the hyperparameter that governs them is one of the most consequential — and most misunderstood — in DQN, SAC, and DDPG. To understand the knob, you have to understand the problem it solves.

In Q-learning, you update the Q-network toward a target that is *itself computed from the Q-network*:

$$\text{target} = r + \gamma \max_{a'} Q(s', a').$$

The network is chasing a moving target made out of its own outputs. Every time you update Q, the target shifts, which is like trying to hit a dartboard that lurches whenever you throw. This feedback loop is a primary source of the notorious instability and divergence in deep Q-learning. The fix (from the 2015 DQN paper) is a **target network** — a delayed copy of the Q-network whose weights are held fixed for a while, so the target stops moving while you fit toward it. The question is *how* to update that delayed copy, and that gives us two styles.

**Hard updates** copy the online network's weights into the target network wholesale, every N steps, and leave the target frozen in between. This is DQN's approach. The hyperparameter is **N, the update frequency**, typically **500 to 10,000 steps** (the original DQN used 10,000 frames). The trade-off is a clean stability-vs-tracking dial, and the extremes make it concrete:

- **N = 1** means the target updates every step — there is effectively no target network, and you are back to chasing your own tail. This is plain online Q-learning, and it is unstable; it was exactly the problem target networks were meant to solve.
- **N = ∞** means the target never updates — it is a static, stale target. It is perfectly stable (the target does not move) but useless, because it can never reflect what the network has learned; the agent stops improving.

The right N is in between: large enough that the target is stable across many gradient steps, small enough that it does not lag the online network so badly that you are fitting toward outdated values. Smaller N (more frequent updates) tracks better but is less stable; larger N is more stable but tracks worse.

**Soft updates** (Polyak averaging), used by SAC and DDPG, take a smoother approach: at every step, nudge the target weights a tiny fraction τ of the way toward the online weights:

$$\theta_{\text{target}} \leftarrow \tau \, \theta_{\text{online}} + (1 - \tau) \, \theta_{\text{target}}.$$

The hyperparameter is **τ (tau)**, typically **0.005** in SAC and DDPG. This is equivalent in spirit to a hard update with a smoothly-decaying memory — instead of a discrete jump every N steps, the target continuously trails the online network with an exponential-moving-average lag. The extremes again clarify the trade-off:

- **τ too small** (e.g. 0.0001) means the target barely moves; it tracks the online network very slowly, learning crawls, because you are perpetually fitting toward a target far behind where the network actually is.
- **τ too large** (e.g. 0.1, approaching 1.0) means the target snaps almost immediately to the online weights — at τ = 1 it *is* the online network, and you have lost the stabilizing lag entirely, recovering the instability.

The standard τ = 0.005 corresponds to a target that takes roughly $1/\tau = 200$ steps to substantially catch up — a soft analog of N ≈ 200 to 1000 hard updates. The relationship between the two is direct: a soft update with coefficient τ behaves like a hard update with period roughly $1/\tau$, but smoother and without the periodic jolt of a sudden weight copy. Here is the soft update in code; it is genuinely two lines:

```python
import torch

@torch.no_grad()
def soft_update(target_net, online_net, tau=0.005):
    for target_param, online_param in zip(target_net.parameters(),
                                          online_net.parameters()):
        target_param.data.mul_(1.0 - tau)
        target_param.data.add_(tau * online_param.data)

# Called every gradient step in SAC/DDPG
soft_update(critic_target, critic, tau=0.005)
```

#### Worked example: a DQN that would not stabilize

A common DQN failure on Atari looks like this: training reward climbs, then crashes, then climbs, then crashes — a sawtooth that never settles. One of the first things to check is the target update frequency. If someone has set N = 100 (far too frequent for Atari), the target is moving almost as fast as the online network, and the chasing-your-own-tail instability is back; the sawtooth is the network and its target oscillating against each other. Raising N to 8,000 (close to the DQN paper's setting) stabilizes the target for long enough that the online network can fit toward it and converge, and the sawtooth smooths into a steady climb. The reverse mistake — N = 50,000 — produces a different pathology: learning is glacially slow because the target is so stale the agent spends most of training fitting toward Q-values it surpassed long ago. The diagnostic is the shape of the curve: oscillation means N too small, painfully slow improvement means N too large.

## 9. Population-based training: tuning *inside* the training run

Everything so far has assumed you tune hyperparameters by trying configurations and comparing them. Population-based training (PBT), introduced by DeepMind (Jaderberg et al., 2017), is a genuinely different idea: it folds the hyperparameter search *into* the training run itself, so that a single PBT job produces both a trained agent and a tuned hyperparameter *schedule* — and it does so for roughly the cost of training a handful of agents in parallel.

The mechanism is an evolutionary loop over a population of M agents, each training with its own hyperparameters. Periodically, you evaluate them all, and then two operations run:

- **Exploit:** the worst-performing agents copy the *weights and hyperparameters* of the best-performing agents. A weak agent's whole state is replaced by a strong one's — it abandons its losing trajectory and resumes from a winner's checkpoint.
- **Explore:** after copying, the formerly-weak agents *perturb* their inherited hyperparameters — multiply the learning rate by 0.8 or 1.2, nudge the entropy coefficient — so the population keeps searching the neighborhood of what is currently working rather than all converging to one identical configuration.

The figure below shows the loop. The thing to notice is that exploit and explore together produce something no static search can: a *non-stationary hyperparameter schedule discovered automatically*. Because PBT perturbs and re-selects throughout training, it naturally finds, for example, that a high learning rate is good early and a low one is good late — it discovers the decay schedule rather than you specifying it. This directly attacks the non-monotone-across-time problem from Section 1: the best hyperparameters change over the course of training, and PBT is the one method that adapts to that automatically.

![Pipeline showing the population-based training loop from initializing M agents with random hyperparameters through parallel training, evaluation, exploit, and explore phases](/imgs/blogs/hyperparameter-tuning-for-rl-4.png)

The reported results are strong. On Atari and on DeepMind's harder tasks, PBT achieved roughly **3× better sample efficiency than grid search** in the original paper, and it has since been used in flagship systems — OpenAI Five used PBT-style hyperparameter adaptation for its Dota 2 agents, and AlphaStar used population-based methods for StarCraft II. The cost model is favorable too: if you were going to train one agent anyway, training a population of 8–16 in parallel and getting a tuned schedule out of it is a far better use of the same total compute than training one agent with hand-guessed hyperparameters and hoping.

Here is PBT in Ray Tune, which has a first-class scheduler for it. This is real, runnable code — the kind you would actually launch on a multi-GPU box:

```python
from ray import tune
from ray.tune.schedulers import PopulationBasedTraining
from ray.rllib.algorithms.ppo import PPOConfig

pbt = PopulationBasedTraining(
    time_attr="training_iteration",
    perturbation_interval=10,        # evaluate + exploit/explore every 10 iters
    quantile_fraction=0.25,          # bottom 25% copy from top 25%
    resample_probability=0.25,
    hyperparam_mutations={
        # explore ranges PBT perturbs around
        "lr": [1e-5, 3e-5, 1e-4, 3e-4, 1e-3],
        "clip_param": [0.1, 0.2, 0.3],
        "entropy_coeff": [0.0, 0.001, 0.01],
        "num_sgd_iter": [3, 5, 10],
    },
)

base = (
    PPOConfig()
    .environment("HalfCheetah-v4")
    .training(gamma=0.99, lambda_=0.95)
)

tuner = tune.Tuner(
    "PPO",
    param_space=base.to_dict(),
    tune_config=tune.TuneConfig(
        scheduler=pbt,
        num_samples=8,               # population of 8 agents
        metric="episode_reward_mean",
        mode="max",
    ),
)
results = tuner.fit()
```

The two parameters that define PBT's character are `quantile_fraction` (here 0.25 — the bottom quarter exploits from the top quarter; 0.2 to 0.25 is standard) and `perturbation_interval` (how often the exploit/explore cycle runs — too frequent and you do not give configurations a fair trial, too rare and you waste compute on losers). The `hyperparam_mutations` dict defines the search space, and crucially, PBT only ever explores *near* current values, which is both its strength (efficient local search) and its limitation (it will not discover a configuration far outside its initial population, so seed the population sensibly).

One honest caveat: PBT needs real parallelism. With a population of 8 and a perturbation interval that gives each configuration a fair trial, you are committing 8× the single-run compute, on hardware that can run them concurrently. If you have one GPU, PBT is not for you; use the literature defaults or a cheaper search. PBT shines exactly when you have a cluster and a hard task where the right hyperparameters are genuinely unknown — which is precisely the regime where the flagship game-playing agents used it.

## 10. Optuna and Bayesian optimization for noisy RL objectives

When you have moderate compute — enough for tens of parallel trials but not a PBT cluster, or when you want a *single* good static configuration rather than a schedule — Bayesian optimization is the workhorse, and Optuna is the most ergonomic tool for it. The core idea is smarter-than-random search: rather than sweeping a grid or sampling uniformly, build a probabilistic model of "configuration → performance" and use it to propose the configurations most likely to be good next.

Optuna's default sampler is the **Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE)**. Where a Gaussian-process Bayesian optimizer models $p(\text{performance} \mid \text{config})$ directly, TPE models the inverse — it maintains two densities over the configuration space: $l(x)$, the density of configurations that produced *good* results, and $g(x)$, the density of configurations that produced *bad* results. It then proposes the configuration maximizing the ratio $l(x)/g(x)$ — the point most likely to be good and least likely to be bad. TPE scales gracefully to many dimensions and handles conditional/tree-structured search spaces (hence the name), which is why it is the default for hyperparameter work and a good fit for RL's mixed continuous-and-categorical knobs.

**The RL-specific challenge is the noisy objective**, and it is the thing that makes naive Optuna-on-RL fail. Recall Section 1: a single training run's return is a noisy sample. If each Optuna trial runs *one seed* and reports its return, TPE will be fooled by lucky and unlucky runs exactly as you would be — it will chase configurations that happened to get a good seed and avoid good configurations that got a bad one. The fix is non-negotiable: **run multiple seeds per trial (3 is a practical minimum, 5 is better) and report the median return as the objective.** This makes each trial more expensive, but a Bayesian search built on noisy single-seed objectives is worse than no search at all because it confidently converges to noise.

Two more tricks make Optuna-for-RL practical. The **warm-start trick:** do not search the whole space from scratch. Start from a known-good configuration from the literature (the defaults in this very post) and search a radius of roughly 2× around each value — learning rate in [1e-4, 1e-3] around the 3e-4 default rather than [1e-6, 1e-1]. This dramatically shrinks the search space and lets a modest trial budget find real improvements instead of rediscovering that 1e-1 diverges. And **pruning:** Optuna can kill unpromising trials early (using a median pruner that stops a trial if it is below the median of completed trials at the same step), which reclaims compute from obvious losers and redirects it to promising configurations.

Here is a complete Optuna study for tuning SAC on HalfCheetah, with the multi-seed median objective and warm-started ranges. This is the script I would actually run:

```python
import optuna
import numpy as np
from stable_baselines3 import SAC
from stable_baselines3.common.evaluation import evaluate_policy
import gymnasium as gym

def objective(trial):
    # Warm-started ranges: ~2x around known-good SAC defaults
    lr = trial.suggest_float("lr", 1e-4, 1e-3, log=True)
    tau = trial.suggest_float("tau", 0.001, 0.02, log=True)
    gamma = trial.suggest_float("gamma", 0.97, 0.999)
    batch_size = trial.suggest_categorical("batch_size", [128, 256, 512])

    # Noisy objective -> average over 3 seeds, report the MEDIAN
    seed_returns = []
    for seed in range(3):
        env = gym.make("HalfCheetah-v4")
        model = SAC(
            "MlpPolicy", env,
            learning_rate=lr, tau=tau, gamma=gamma,
            batch_size=batch_size, buffer_size=300_000,
            learning_starts=10_000, seed=seed, verbose=0,
        )
        model.learn(total_timesteps=100_000)  # short horizon for the search
        mean_r, _ = evaluate_policy(model, env, n_eval_episodes=10)
        seed_returns.append(mean_r)
        env.close()

    return float(np.median(seed_returns))   # robust to seed outliers

study = optuna.create_study(
    direction="maximize",
    sampler=optuna.samplers.TPESampler(seed=0),
    pruner=optuna.pruners.MedianPruner(),
)
study.optimize(objective, n_trials=50, n_jobs=4)   # 4 trials in parallel
print("Best HPs:", study.best_params)
print("Best median return:", study.best_value)
```

In about 50 trials — a few GPU-days, or less with `n_jobs` parallelism — this kind of study reliably finds a HalfCheetah SAC configuration that beats the naive defaults by a meaningful margin, and crucially, because the objective is a 3-seed median, the winner is genuinely better rather than luckier. The short 100k-step horizon during search (you train the final winner for the full budget) is itself a deliberate choice: tune at a shorter horizon to afford more trials and more seeds, then commit your compute to training the best configuration to convergence.

The comparison between the two automated approaches is worth stating plainly:

| Approach | What it produces | Best when | Cost model |
|---|---|---|---|
| Literature defaults | A known-decent config | Budget < 100 GPU-hours | Near zero |
| Optuna / TPE | One tuned static config | Tens of trials affordable; want reproducibility | Trials × seeds × short horizon |
| Population-based training | A tuned config *schedule* | Cluster available; hard task | Population × full horizon |
| Grid search | Exhaustive over a small grid | Few HPs, reproducibility critical | Grid size × seeds (expensive) |

## 11. Practical recipes: known-good starting points by algorithm

Theory and tuning machinery aside, most of the time you want a configuration that *works* so you can start iterating, and then you tune from there only if you need to. These are battle-tested starting points — the configurations that the literature and the major implementations converge on for the canonical task in each algorithm's home territory. Start here, confirm learning happens, *then* tune.

**PPO for locomotion (the canonical continuous-control recipe).** This is the configuration that gets MuJoCo tasks like HalfCheetah, Hopper, and Walker2d learning reliably:

```python
from stable_baselines3 import PPO

model = PPO(
    "MlpPolicy", "HalfCheetah-v4",
    learning_rate=3e-4,    # the workhorse default
    n_steps=2048,          # rollout length per env
    batch_size=64,         # mini-batch
    n_epochs=10,           # epochs per rollout
    gamma=0.99,            # ~100-step horizon, right for dense reward
    gae_lambda=0.95,       # standard advantage smoothing
    clip_range=0.2,        # standard trust region
    ent_coef=0.0,          # dense reward needs no entropy bonus
    vf_coef=0.5,
    max_grad_norm=0.5,
    verbose=1,
)
model.learn(total_timesteps=1_000_000)
```

**SAC for robotics and continuous control.** SAC's auto-temperature makes it remarkably robust; this configuration is close to the original SAC paper and the SB3 defaults, and it is what I reach for first on any continuous-control task where sample efficiency matters:

```python
from stable_baselines3 import SAC

model = SAC(
    "MlpPolicy", "HalfCheetah-v4",
    learning_rate=3e-4,        # shared across actor, critic, temperature
    tau=0.005,                 # soft target update
    gamma=0.99,
    buffer_size=1_000_000,     # 1M for stationary control
    learning_starts=10_000,    # random warmup
    batch_size=256,
    train_freq=1,
    gradient_steps=1,
    ent_coef="auto",           # automatic temperature: target entropy -dim(A)
    verbose=1,
)
model.learn(total_timesteps=1_000_000)
```

**DQN for Atari.** Value-based, discrete actions, the recipe that launched deep RL. The numbers here trace directly to the Rainbow and DQN literature:

```python
from stable_baselines3 import DQN

model = DQN(
    "CnnPolicy", "ALE/Breakout-v5",
    learning_rate=6.25e-5,         # the Atari DQN learning rate
    buffer_size=1_000_000,
    learning_starts=50_000,
    batch_size=32,
    gamma=0.99,
    target_update_interval=8_000,  # hard target update (~N=8k)
    train_freq=4,
    gradient_steps=1,
    exploration_fraction=0.1,      # decay epsilon over first 10% of training
    exploration_initial_eps=1.0,   # start fully exploring
    exploration_final_eps=0.01,    # end nearly greedy
    verbose=1,
)
model.learn(total_timesteps=10_000_000)
```

A note on DQN's epsilon-greedy exploration, since it is DQN's analog of the entropy coefficient. The agent acts randomly with probability ε and greedily otherwise, and ε is *annealed* from 1.0 (fully random) down to a small floor like 0.01 (almost always greedy) over the early part of training. The two knobs are the floor (`exploration_final_eps`, usually 0.01–0.05 — never zero, because you want a trickle of exploration forever) and the decay length (`exploration_fraction`, the fraction of total training over which ε reaches its floor — 0.1 means ε hits 0.01 after 10% of training, then stays there). The classic Atari setting decays ε over the first million frames. Decay too fast and the agent commits before it has explored; decay too slow and it wastes most of training acting randomly.

The decision of *which* tuning strategy to even use comes down mostly to your compute budget, and the figure below makes that explicit as a decision tree. The honest summary: under 100 GPU-hours, do not tune at all — use these defaults and spend your compute on training. Between 100 and 1000 GPU-hours, tune the top two knobs only (learning rate plus one algorithm-specific knob). Above 1000 GPU-hours with a cluster, run PBT. And if exact reproducibility is the priority over peak performance, fix the seed and run a small grid.

![Tree showing how to choose a hyperparameter tuning strategy based on compute budget, from using literature defaults under 100 GPU-hours to population-based training above 1000 GPU-hours](/imgs/blogs/hyperparameter-tuning-for-rl-8.png)

This is worth dwelling on because it is the most common mistake I see: people with a small budget over-investing in tuning. If you have 50 GPU-hours, running a 50-trial Optuna study at 3 seeds each leaves almost nothing for actual training, and you will end up with a beautifully-tuned configuration trained for far too few steps. The defaults in this section are *good*. Use them, train fully, and only reach for automated tuning when your budget genuinely supports it on top of a full training run.

## 12. Case studies: what tuning bought in real systems

Abstract advice is cheap; here are four named results where hyperparameter choices were the visible difference between success and failure.

**Atari DQN (Mnih et al., 2015).** The DQN paper that achieved human-level play on 49 Atari games is, read carefully, as much a hyperparameter-and-engineering paper as an algorithmic one. The headline algorithm — Q-learning with a neural network — had been tried before and was known to be unstable. What made it *work* was a specific bundle of choices: a 1M-transition replay buffer to de-correlate samples, a target network updated every 10,000 steps to stabilize the bootstrap target, reward clipping to [−1, 1] to make a single learning rate work across games with wildly different score scales, and an ε-greedy schedule annealing from 1.0 to 0.1 over the first million frames. Change any one of those and the published results degrade or vanish. The same hyperparameters were used across all 49 games — a deliberate demonstration that a single robust configuration could generalize, which is itself the strongest possible argument for the existence of good defaults.

**SAC's automatic temperature (Haarnoja et al., 2018).** The original SAC paper had a fixed temperature α that needed per-task tuning, and the follow-up's central contribution was making it automatic via the target-entropy mechanism in Section 6. The measured impact was that SAC went from "competitive but finicky" to "robust across tasks with a single configuration" — the auto-temperature version matched or beat the hand-tuned version on every benchmark task *without* per-task tuning. This is the cleanest example in the literature of *removing* a hyperparameter (by making it self-tuning) being a major contribution, and it is why SAC is the default recommendation for continuous control today.

**OpenAI Five (2019).** The Dota 2 agent used PPO at a scale that made hyperparameters existential — training ran for months across thousands of GPUs, and a bad hyperparameter choice meant burning enormous compute. The team used population-based methods to adapt hyperparameters during training, and reported that the discount factor in particular had to be carefully managed and even *increased over training* (from 0.998 toward 0.9997) as the agent learned to value longer-horizon strategies — a moving-gamma schedule that no static value could capture, and a direct real-world instance of the non-monotone-across-time principle from Section 1.

**DreamerV3 (Hafner et al., 2023).** The frontier of this topic is making tuning *unnecessary*. DreamerV3, a model-based agent, made the explicit claim of using a *single fixed set of hyperparameters* across more than 150 tasks spanning Atari, continuous control, Minecraft, and more — domains that previously each demanded their own tuning. It achieved this not by magic but by careful normalization techniques (symlog transforms on rewards and values, normalized returns) that make the algorithm's behavior invariant to the scale differences that usually force per-task tuning. DreamerV3 is the proof of concept that the long-term answer to "how do I tune this?" might be "you don't, if the algorithm is designed to be scale-invariant." It is the destination the whole timeline below is heading toward.

![Timeline of milestones in RL hyperparameter tuning from the 2015 DQN sensitivity analysis through 2023 near hyperparameter-free agents like DreamerV3](/imgs/blogs/hyperparameter-tuning-for-rl-7.png)

That arc — from hand-tuning fragile knobs (DQN's careful bundle), to automating the search (PBT, Optuna), to self-tuning individual knobs (SAC's temperature), to algorithms designed to need almost no tuning (DreamerV3) — is the real story of RL hyperparameter tuning over the last decade. We are not there yet for most algorithms, which is why this post exists, but the direction is unmistakable.

## When to use this (and when not to)

A decisive section, because over-tuning is as common a failure as under-tuning.

**Do not tune at all when your budget is small.** Under roughly 100 GPU-hours, the literature defaults in Section 11 beat any tuning you could afford, because tuning steals compute from training. Use the defaults, train fully, ship.

**Tune only the top two knobs when your budget is moderate.** Between 100 and 1000 GPU-hours, the highest return on tuning effort comes from the learning rate plus one algorithm-specific knob — clipping for PPO, τ for SAC, target-update frequency for DQN. Tuning more than two or three knobs at once with a modest budget spreads your seeds too thin to draw reliable conclusions (remember the 5-seed rule), and the interactions make the results hard to interpret anyway.

**Reach for PBT only with real parallelism and a hard task.** PBT's 3× sample-efficiency win is real, but it requires running a population concurrently. With one GPU it is not an option. It shines on cluster-scale problems where the right hyperparameters are genuinely unknown and a schedule (not a fixed config) is what you need — the flagship game agents are the canonical use case.

**Prefer the self-tuning algorithm when one exists.** If you are doing continuous control and choosing between DDPG (manual exploration noise) and SAC (automatic temperature), the fact that SAC tunes its own exploration is a strong reason to pick it. Choosing an algorithm that removes a hyperparameter is better than tuning that hyperparameter well.

**Do not tune when a non-RL method would work.** This is the meta-point. If your problem has a known model and a small state space, value iteration needs no hyperparameter tuning at all and gives the exact optimal policy — do that instead of tuning a deep RL agent. If you can frame your problem as supervised learning (behavior cloning from demonstrations), supervised hyperparameter tuning is far more forgiving than RL's. The best way to win at RL hyperparameter tuning is sometimes to not be doing deep RL.

**Always tune at the right horizon with enough seeds.** Whatever you tune, do it at a horizon short enough to afford 3–5 seeds per configuration, then train the winner to full convergence. A clean-looking single-seed comparison is worse than useless — it is *confidently misleading*.

## Key takeaways

1. **The learning rate is the first and most important knob.** Start at 3e-4, divide by 10 if anything diverges, and use linear decay for long runs. The diagnostic signature of "too high" is oscillating reward and gradient norms spiking into the tens.

2. **Gamma is your planning horizon, not a vibe.** Effective horizon is $1/(1-\gamma)$: γ=0.99 sees ~100 steps, γ=0.999 sees ~1000. Pick it from how far your rewards are from the actions that earn them. Sparse, distant rewards need a higher gamma or they become invisible.

3. **Never decide from one seed.** RL evaluation is noisy enough that single-seed comparisons mostly compare luck. Use 5 seeds minimum and report the median, even if you have to shorten the horizon to afford them.

4. **Hyperparameters interact; tune the coupled ones together.** Batch size and learning rate co-determine the effective step — raise the learning rate when you raise the batch. Gamma couples with value-function scale. You cannot tune these one at a time.

5. **The entropy coefficient prevents policy collapse.** Too low and the policy commits early and stalls; 0.0 is fine for dense-reward locomotion, 0.01 is cheap insurance for sparse or discrete tasks. SAC's automatic temperature removes this knob entirely — prefer it.

6. **Target updates trade stability for tracking.** Hard updates every N=500–10k steps (DQN) or soft updates with τ=0.005 (SAC/DDPG); a soft τ behaves like a hard update with period ~1/τ. Oscillating reward means the target moves too fast; glacial learning means it moves too slow.

7. **Match the tuning strategy to the budget.** Under 100 GPU-hours, use defaults and don't tune. Moderate budget, tune the top two knobs. Cluster-scale with a hard task, use PBT. The most common mistake is over-tuning on a small budget.

8. **For automated search, fix the noise first.** Optuna with TPE works well for RL *only* if each trial reports a multi-seed median; warm-start the search around literature defaults and prune losers early.

9. **PBT discovers schedules, not just configs.** Because it perturbs and re-selects throughout training, it automatically finds that the best hyperparameters change over time — the exact thing static search cannot capture.

10. **The frontier is needing no tuning.** SAC's auto-temperature and DreamerV3's single-config-across-150-tasks point at the destination: algorithms designed with scale-invariant normalization so the knobs tune themselves.

## Further reading

- Sutton & Barto, *Reinforcement Learning: An Introduction* (2nd ed., 2018) — the foundational text; the chapters on TD learning and function approximation explain *why* gamma, step size, and bootstrapping interact the way they do.
- Mnih et al., "Human-level control through deep reinforcement learning" (Nature, 2015) — the DQN paper; read it as a hyperparameter case study (replay buffer, target network, reward clipping, ε schedule).
- Schulman et al., "Proximal Policy Optimization Algorithms" (2017) — the source for clip range, epochs, and the adaptive-KL alternative; the ablations show how each knob matters.
- Haarnoja et al., "Soft Actor-Critic Algorithms and Applications" (2018) — the automatic-temperature mechanism and the target-entropy heuristic $-\dim(\mathcal{A})$.
- Jaderberg et al., "Population Based Training of Neural Networks" (2017) — the original PBT paper with the Atari sample-efficiency results.
- Henderson et al., "Deep Reinforcement Learning that Matters" (2018) — the empirical case for the 5-seed rule; required reading on RL evaluation noise.
- Hafner et al., "Mastering Diverse Domains through World Models" (DreamerV3, 2023) — the single-fixed-hyperparameter-set result across 150+ tasks.
- The Stable-Baselines3 documentation and RL Zoo — tuned hyperparameters for dozens of environments, and a practical reference for the SB3 API used throughout this post.
