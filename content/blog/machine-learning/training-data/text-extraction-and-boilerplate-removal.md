Every team building a pretraining corpus eventually argues about the glamorous parts: which quality classifier to train, how aggressive to make the dedup, what mixture weights to assign Wikipedia versus code versus the long tail of the open web. Almost nobody argues about text extraction. It is treated as plumbing — a one-liner that turns a downloaded web page into a string you can feed everything else. That instinct is exactly backwards. Extraction is the single step that sets the ceiling on every quality metric you will ever measure, and you cannot tell from the dashboard that you hit it.

Here is the uncomfortable physics of it. A web page is roughly ninety percent chrome — navigation, search bars, login widgets, cookie banners, ad iframes, "related stories" rails, comment threads, and a footer stuffed with links and inline scripts. The article you actually want to train on is a small island in that sea. Text extraction is the act of finding the island and throwing the ocean away. Do it well and the rest of your pipeline has a chance. Do it badly and there is no filter, no classifier, no dedup pass, and no clever tokenizer that can put back content the extractor deleted, or repair text the extractor mangled. The damage is permanent the moment the WARC record becomes a string.

This post is a deep tour of that step: what boilerplate actually is, the five families of extractors and where each one wins, why bad extraction is a *cliff* rather than a *slope*, the structure-preservation hard cases that quietly destroy code and tables and math, a runnable benchmark you can point at your own pages, the named decisions that RefinedWeb and FineWeb made, and a troubleshooting guide for the defects that surface three pipeline stages after the bug. It is the back half of [sourcing and collecting training data](/blog/machine-learning/training-data/sourcing-and-collecting-training-data): once the bytes are on disk, extraction is what determines whether those bytes were worth downloading.

## The problem: a web page is mostly chrome

![Anatomy of a web page showing the small central article body surrounded by navigation, sidebar, ad, comment, and footer boilerplate](/imgs/blogs/text-extraction-and-boilerplate-removal-1.webp)

The diagram above is the mental model for the entire post: the article body is the green block in the middle, and everything around it is reusable scaffolding that appears, byte-for-byte, on thousands of other pages from the same site. The percentages are illustrative, but they are not exaggerated. On a typical news or blog page, the main article is often under fifteen percent of the rendered DOM and frequently under ten percent of the raw HTML once you count inline `<style>`, `<script>`, JSON-LD blobs, and SVG icon sprites. The chrome dominates because chrome is *templated*: the same header, the same nav tree, the same footer, generated by the CMS on every URL.

That structural fact has three consequences that ripple through the whole corpus:

- **The signal-to-noise ratio at the byte level is terrible.** If you concatenate raw HTML, the model spends most of its attention budget learning the shape of `<div class="nav-item">` instead of learning language.
- **The boilerplate is near-identical across pages**, which means it is the single largest manufacturer of duplicates in your corpus. A site with 100,000 pages ships its 2 KB footer 100,000 times. Your dedup stage will be drowning in it — see [deduplication at scale](/blog/machine-learning/training-data/deduplication-at-scale) for what that does to a MinHash index.
- **The content is structurally distinct from the chrome**, which is the only reason extraction is possible at all. Article text lives in long runs of prose-shaped DOM nodes; chrome lives in short, link-dense, repeated nodes. Every extractor is, at heart, a classifier that exploits that difference.

### Why text extraction is harder than it looks

The reason extraction gets under-budgeted is that the naive version *appears* to work. You run a stripper, you get text out, the text has English words in it, you move on. The failure is invisible because the output is plausible. Here is the gap between the common assumption and the reality.

| Assumption | The naive view | The reality |
| --- | --- | --- |
| "Stripping HTML tags gives you the text." | `BeautifulSoup(html).get_text()` returns a string, done. | That string is the *whole page* — nav, ads, comments, footer — concatenated into a wall of link text. It is ~50-60% boilerplate by character. |
| "Common Crawl already gives me text (WET files)." | WET is free and pre-extracted, so use it. | WET is a generic tag-strip with no content detection. It keeps the chrome. Every serious corpus since 2023 stopped using it. |
| "Extraction quality is a rounding error next to filtering." | Filters and classifiers do the real cleanup. | A filter can delete a bad *document*; it cannot recover the good document the extractor wrecked. Extraction is a hard ceiling, not a contribution. |
| "All extractors are roughly the same." | Pick whichever has the nicest API. | They differ by 15-30 points of content F1 and by **two orders of magnitude** in throughput. The choice is a real engineering decision. |
| "Tables, code, and math are edge cases." | The prose is what matters. | For technical corpora these *are* the value, and they are exactly what generic extractors flatten or drop. |

The throughline: the output of bad extraction is not empty or obviously broken. It is *subtly wrong* in a way that survives a casual eyeball, passes through your filters, corrupts your tokenizer statistics, and only reveals itself as a soft degradation in benchmark scores that you will spend weeks misattributing to your learning rate.

## What "boilerplate" actually is

Boilerplate is any text on the page that is not the content the page exists to deliver. That sounds circular until you enumerate it, because the categories are remarkably stable across the web:

- **Navigational chrome** — the top nav, breadcrumb trails, mega-menus, "skip to content" links, the hamburger menu's expanded contents.
- **Site furniture** — sidebars, "popular posts," "you might also like," tag clouds, author bios that repeat on every post.
- **Legal and consent** — cookie banners, GDPR consent text, privacy notices, "this site uses cookies" overlays, copyright footers.
- **Monetization** — ad slot labels ("Advertisement," "Sponsored"), newsletter signup prompts, paywall teasers, "subscribe to read more."
- **Social and engagement** — share buttons rendered as text, like/comment counts, "follow us on" rows.
- **User-generated tail** — comment sections, which are sometimes valuable and usually a swamp of spam, "first!", and quote-pyramids.
- **Machine furniture** — timestamps in twelve formats, "reading time: 4 min," "last updated," pagination controls ("1 2 3 ... Next").

The reason removing it matters is not aesthetic. Boilerplate actively *poisons* downstream stages, and it does so in ways that compound:

- **It inflates your token bill.** Boilerplate is real tokens that cost real GPU-hours to train on and teach the model nothing it wants to know. On a WET-based corpus, you can easily spend 40-50% of your compute attending to navigation menus.
- **It manufactures duplicates.** The same footer on a million pages is a million near-duplicate spans. Your dedup will either spend enormous effort on it or, worse, will *fail* to catch it because each page's footer is wrapped in slightly different surrounding text, so the document hashes differ even though half the content is identical.
- **It teaches degenerate patterns.** Models trained on link-dense boilerplate learn to emit "Home | About | Contact | Privacy Policy" and "Click here to subscribe." This is a real, observed failure mode of under-cleaned corpora.
- **It corrupts your statistics.** Every measurement you take downstream — average document length, language ID confidence, quality-classifier scores — is computed over text that is half chrome. Your filters are tuned against a distribution that does not exist in clean data.

The job of an extractor is to delete all of that and keep the body, the title, the publication date if you want it, and the *structure* of the content — paragraphs, headings, lists, tables, code — without keeping anything else.

## Extraction approaches: five ways to turn HTML into text

There are five families of approaches in wide use, and they span a huge range on both axes that matter: how accurately they separate content from boilerplate, and how fast they run. At a trillion-token scale, throughput is not a nicety — a 100x speed difference is the difference between a one-day extraction job on a modest cluster and a hundred-day one.

![Comparison matrix of five extraction methods across mechanism, quality F1, throughput, and structure preservation](/imgs/blogs/text-extraction-and-boilerplate-removal-2.webp)

The matrix is the summary; the rest of this section is the detail behind each row.

### 1. Prebuilt WET text (the tempting trap)

Common Crawl publishes three file types per crawl: WARC (the raw HTTP responses, including full HTML), WAT (metadata), and **WET (extracted plain text)**. WET is produced by a generic HTML-to-text conversion that strips tags and emits the visible text. It is free, it is already computed, and it is a trap.

WET does no content detection whatsoever. It keeps the nav, the sidebar, the ads, the comments, and the footer, because to a tag-stripper those are all just visible text. A WET record for a blog post is the blog post *plus* the entire site template, concatenated with no structure. Its content precision — the fraction of characters that are actually article text — is often below 0.5. Its recall is near-perfect (it keeps everything, so it keeps the content too), which is exactly why it looks fine if you only spot-check for "is the article in here?" The article is in there, drowning.

```python
# Reading WET vs WARC from a Common Crawl segment with fastwarc.
# WET is free but uncontrolled; WARC is the raw HTML you re-extract yourself.
from fastwarc.warc import ArchiveIterator, WarcRecordType

def iter_wet(path):
    with open(path, "rb") as f:
        for rec in ArchiveIterator(f, record_types=WarcRecordType.conversion):
            yield rec.reader.read().decode("utf-8", "replace")

def iter_warc_html(path):
    with open(path, "rb") as f:
        for rec in ArchiveIterator(f, record_types=WarcRecordType.response):
            payload = rec.reader.read()
            # crude: real code sniffs Content-Type and the encoding first
            yield payload  # raw bytes, headers + HTML
```

The verdict every serious corpus reached: WET is a fine way to *prototype* a pipeline and a bad way to *ship* one. Every major open corpus from RefinedWeb (2023) onward re-extracts from WARC rather than using WET.

### 2. DOM / readability heuristics

The "readability" family — Mozilla's Readability.js, the Python `readability-lxml` port, Arc90's original algorithm — parses the DOM into a tree and scores each node by how content-like it looks. The features are intuitive: long text runs score high, high link density scores low, certain tags and class names (`article`, `content`, `post-body`) get bonuses, others (`comment`, `sidebar`, `footer`, `nav`) get penalties. It then walks up from the highest-scoring node to grab the smallest subtree that contains the bulk of the content.

This works remarkably well on the article-shaped pages it was designed for (it powers "reader mode" in browsers) and it preserves *some* structure because it returns a DOM subtree, not flat text. But it is slow — it builds and scores a full DOM — and it is brittle on pages that do not match the article template: forums, documentation sites, product pages, anything with multiple content regions. Content F1 around 0.85 is typical, with the misses concentrated on non-article layouts.

```python
from readability import Document  # pip install readability-lxml
import requests

html = requests.get("https://example.com/some-article").text
doc = Document(html)
title = doc.title()
content_html = doc.summary()      # cleaned HTML subtree, not plain text
# You still have to convert content_html -> text/markdown yourself,
# which is a second place structure quietly gets lost.
```

### 3. jusText (the stopword-density classic)

jusText takes a different and elegant angle: it splits the page into blocks (roughly, paragraphs) and classifies each block as "good," "bad," or "near-good" using **stopword density** as the primary signal. The insight is that real prose has a high density of function words ("the," "of," "and," "to") while boilerplate — link lists, menus, button labels — does not. It then does a context-aware pass so that a short "near-good" block surrounded by good blocks gets kept.

jusText is fast, language-aware (it ships stopword lists per language, which couples it to [language identification](/blog/machine-learning/training-data/language-identification-and-heuristic-quality-filters)), and was the extractor behind The Pile's web component. Its weakness is the same as its strength: stopword density is a blunt instrument. Short legitimate content (captions, code, lists) looks like boilerplate, and verbose boilerplate (long cookie notices, prose-y ads) looks like content. Content F1 around 0.83.

```python
import justext, requests

html = requests.get("https://example.com/some-article").content
paragraphs = justext.justext(html, justext.get_stoplist("English"))
text = "\n\n".join(p.text for p in paragraphs if not p.is_boilerplate)
```

### 4. trafilatura (the quality default)

trafilatura combines everything: it runs DOM heuristics (with a readability-style fallback and a jusText-style fallback), uses a cascade of rules and lightweight machine-learned signals, detects and preserves structure (headings, lists, **tables**, code, formatting), extracts metadata (title, author, date, sitename), and outputs to text, markdown, or XML. In published comparisons it consistently posts the best content F1 of the open extractors — around 0.92 — which is why it became the default for high-quality corpora.

The cost is speed. trafilatura is pure Python over lxml and runs at low tens of documents per second per core. At small-to-medium corpus scale that is irrelevant; at trillion-token scale it is the thing that makes you reach for the next option or for heavy parallelism.

```python
import trafilatura

downloaded = trafilatura.fetch_url("https://example.com/some-article")
text = trafilatura.extract(
    downloaded,
    output_format="markdown",   # keep heading/list/table structure
    include_tables=True,
    include_formatting=True,
    include_links=False,        # links in prose are usually noise for pretraining
    include_comments=False,     # drop the comment swamp
    favor_precision=True,       # bias toward dropping boilerplate over keeping it
    deduplicate=True,           # trafilatura can drop repeated blocks itself
)
```

The `favor_precision` versus `favor_recall` knob is the single most important option and the one most people never touch. For pretraining you almost always want precision: a little real content lost is far cheaper than boilerplate leaking through, because the corpus is enormous and the boilerplate is what poisons downstream stages.

### 5. resiliparse (the throughput champion)

resiliparse, from the ChatNoir / Web Archive research group, is a C++-backed extraction library with Python bindings. Its `extract_plain_text` is built for exactly the WARC-at-scale problem: robust HTML parsing, fast encoding detection, and content extraction that runs **one to two orders of magnitude faster** than trafilatura — well into the hundreds of documents per second per core. Its content F1 (around 0.90) is a hair below trafilatura's, but on a trillion-token job that small quality gap can be worth the 16x throughput, which is precisely the trade DCLM made.

```python
from resiliparse.extract.html2text import extract_plain_text
from resiliparse.parse.encoding import detect_encoding, bytes_to_str

raw = open("page.html", "rb").read()
encoding = detect_encoding(raw)          # robust charset detection up front
html = bytes_to_str(raw, encoding)
text = extract_plain_text(
    html,
    main_content=True,        # run main-content detection, not whole-page strip
    list_bullets=True,        # keep list structure
    preserve_formatting=True, # keep paragraph/line structure
)
```

#### Second-order: the choice is a function of corpus scale, not taste

The non-obvious point is that there is no single right answer. The right extractor is a function of how big your corpus is and how much you care about the last two points of content F1. Below ~100B tokens, use trafilatura and never think about throughput. Above ~1T tokens, the extraction job's wall-clock and cost become a line item, and resiliparse's speed earns its slightly lower F1. The teams who got this right did not pick an extractor; they picked an extractor *for a corpus size*.

## The quality cliff: bad extraction caps everything downstream

Now the central argument of the post, and the reason "extraction is just plumbing" is a dangerous belief. Extraction quality is not one contribution among many that average together. It is a **ceiling**. Every stage after it can only subtract.

![The quality cliff: a ceiling banner over the pipeline stages of extract, quality filter, dedup, tokenizer, and pretrain, showing no stage can rise above the extraction-set ceiling](/imgs/blogs/text-extraction-and-boilerplate-removal-3.webp)

Walk the chain and notice that information only ever leaves it. The extractor produces a string. The quality filter can *delete* documents but cannot *create* the clean version of a document the extractor mangled — it has no access to the original HTML. Dedup can *remove* copies but cannot *un-merge* a paragraph that was glued to a menu. The tokenizer can only *segment* the bytes it is given; if those bytes are mojibake or boilerplate, it dutifully learns merges for garbage. Pretraining can only *fit* the distribution it is shown. At no point does any stage reach back to the HTML and fix the extraction. The ceiling is set at step one.

This is why it is a *cliff* and not a *slope*. With a slope, you could compensate for a weak extractor by tuning the filter harder. With a ceiling, harder filtering past a point just deletes good data along with bad, because the bad data is *interleaved with* the good inside individual documents. Consider what happens when a menu leaks into the middle of an article: a document-level filter sees a document that is 85% good prose and 15% menu, scores it "keep," and now the menu is in your corpus forever, in the worst possible place — inside an otherwise-clean document where dedup will never find it as a standalone duplicate.

A concrete way to feel the ceiling: suppose your extractor has 0.80 content precision (one character in five is boilerplate). No downstream stage knows *which* characters are the bad ones — the boilerplate is mixed into real documents. Your best move is a document-level filter, which throws away whole documents that look too boilerplate-heavy. To get the corpus to 0.95 precision that way, you would have to discard a huge fraction of perfectly good documents that happen to carry a normal amount of leaked chrome. You will trade away recall by the bucketload to buy back precision the extractor should have given you for free. The cheapest precision in the entire pipeline is the precision you get at extraction time.

### Second-order: the ceiling is invisible on every dashboard

The cruelest part is that the ceiling does not show up where you look for problems. Your data dashboard shows document counts, token counts, language distribution, average length, dedup ratio — all of which look *fine* with a bad extractor, because boilerplate is valid text that counts as tokens and passes language ID. The ceiling only manifests as a benchmark score that is a few points lower than it should be, with no single document you can point at as "the bug." This is why extraction errors are routinely misdiagnosed as modeling problems. The fix is to measure extraction quality *directly*, as its own metric, before any of it reaches training — which is the benchmark section below.

## Structure preservation: the hard cases

The discussion so far has been about prose. The hardest, highest-value part of extraction is everything that is *not* prose: tables, code blocks, math, and nested lists. These are where generic extractors do the most damage, and they are precisely the content that makes a corpus good at reasoning, coding, and technical tasks.

![Four structure-preservation hard cases: tables flattened to one line, code blocks with indentation stripped, math dropped, and nested lists with depth collapsed](/imgs/blogs/text-extraction-and-boilerplate-removal-5.webp)

Each case fails in its own way:

- **Tables.** A generic strip reads a table row by row, cell by cell, and emits the cell text with no delimiters. A 2D financial table becomes a 1D run-on string of numbers with no way to recover which number was in which column. The model sees `Revenue 2021 2022 2023 412 489 533 Costs 201 240 266` and learns nothing about the relationship. A good extractor emits a markdown or pipe-delimited table that preserves the grid.
- **Code blocks.** Code's meaning lives in its whitespace and line breaks. Strip the `<pre>` formatting and you get every line joined into one, indentation gone, so `def f():\n    return x` becomes `def f(): return x` at best and `deff(): returnx` at worst. For a model that is supposed to learn to *write* code, this is catastrophic — and it feeds directly into [how the tokenizer learns its merges](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/bpe-tokenizer), because the tokenizer's whitespace handling assumes code whitespace is meaningful.
- **Math.** Math arrives as LaTeX in `<script type="math/tex">`, as MathML, as rendered images with alt text, or as Unicode symbols. Generic extractors drop the script blocks entirely (so the equation vanishes), turn MathML into a soup of element names, or mojibake the Unicode. The model trains on prose that references "the equation below" with no equation below it.
- **Nested lists.** A numbered list with sub-bullets carries meaning in its indentation and numbering. Flatten it and `1. First 2. Second a. sub b. sub 3. Third` collapses into an undifferentiated run where the hierarchy — which sub-points belong to which top-level item — is gone.

The reason this matters more than it seems: these structures are *over-represented in the content you most want*. Documentation, tutorials, scientific papers, financial filings, and Stack Overflow answers are dense with tables, code, and math. A pipeline that extracts prose perfectly but flattens structure will systematically degrade exactly the high-value technical slice of your corpus while leaving the low-value chatter intact.

```python
# trafilatura preserves tables and code when you ask it to; the default
# text output does not. Always extract to markdown/xml for technical corpora.
import trafilatura

# WRONG for technical pages: flat text drops table/code structure.
flat = trafilatura.extract(html)

# RIGHT: markdown keeps pipe-tables, fenced code, list nesting.
structured = trafilatura.extract(
    html,
    output_format="markdown",
    include_tables=True,
    include_formatting=True,
)
```

### Second-order: structure preservation interacts with your tokenizer

If you preserve markdown structure, your corpus now contains pipe characters, backtick fences, and consistent indentation. That is good — but it means your tokenizer should be trained on the *same* representation you will train the model on. If you extract to markdown but trained your tokenizer on flat text, the tokenizer has no efficient merges for ` | ` or ```` ``` ```` and will shatter every table and code fence into single characters, inflating your token count and starving the model of context. Extraction format and tokenizer training data must agree. This is one of the quiet couplings that makes "just swap the extractor" a bigger change than it looks.

## A worked benchmark: trafilatura vs resiliparse vs raw WET

Enough assertion. Here is a concrete scenario with numbers. Take a sample of 1,000 pages pulled from a single Common Crawl WARC segment, filtered to English and a realistic mix of news, blogs, documentation, and forums. Run three extractors over the same pages — raw WET (the free baseline), trafilatura (the quality default), and resiliparse (the throughput champion) — plus python-readability and jusText for context. Measure three things: **yield** (characters kept per page), **cleanliness** (content precision: fraction of kept characters that are actually article text, scored against a hand-labeled gold set), and **throughput** (documents per second per core).

![Before and after of a single page extraction: raw WET keeps nav, cookie banner, ads, article, and comments, while trafilatura keeps only title, article, code block, and structure](/imgs/blogs/text-extraction-and-boilerplate-removal-4.webp)

The figure shows the qualitative difference on one page; the table shows the aggregate over all 1,000.

| Extractor | Chars kept / page | Content precision | Content recall | F1 | Throughput (docs/s/core) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| raw WET | ~14,200 | 0.45 | 0.99 | 0.62 | n/a (precut) |
| python-readability | ~4,800 | 0.88 | 0.82 | 0.85 | ~6 |
| jusText | ~4,200 | 0.86 | 0.80 | 0.83 | ~28 |
| trafilatura | ~3,100 | 0.95 | 0.89 | 0.92 | ~11 |
| resiliparse | ~3,400 | 0.92 | 0.88 | 0.90 | ~180 |

Read the table carefully, because the headline number (chars kept) is the *misleading* one. WET keeps 4-5x more characters than trafilatura — and that is precisely the problem. Those extra characters are the nav, the comments, and the footer. WET's recall is 0.99 (it keeps essentially all the real content) but its precision is 0.45 (more than half of what it keeps is junk). trafilatura keeps a quarter as much text but 95% of it is real article content. **Higher yield is a red flag, not a feature.** When you swap a naive extractor for a good one and your corpus shrinks by 60%, that is the system working: you just deleted the boilerplate you were about to pay to train on.

The throughput column is where the trafilatura-vs-resiliparse decision lives. resiliparse is roughly 16x faster for a two-point F1 cost. On a 100B-token corpus, both finish overnight and you take trafilatura's quality. On a 5T-token corpus extracted from tens of thousands of WARC files, resiliparse's speed is the difference between a feasible job and one that dominates your data-pipeline budget — so DCLM took the F1 hit and the speed.

Here is a compact version of the harness that produced numbers like these. It is deliberately runnable: point it at a directory of saved HTML files (or WARC payloads) and it prints yield and throughput per extractor.

```python
import time, glob, statistics as stats
import trafilatura
import justext
from readability import Document
from resiliparse.extract.html2text import extract_plain_text

def extract_wet(html):                       # naive baseline: strip tags only
    from selectolax.parser import HTMLParser
    return HTMLParser(html).text(separator="\n")

def extract_readability(html):
    return Document(html).summary()          # HTML subtree; len() over-counts

def extract_justext(html):
    ps = justext.justext(html.encode("utf-8"), justext.get_stoplist("English"))
    return "\n\n".join(p.text for p in ps if not p.is_boilerplate)

def extract_trafilatura(html):
    return trafilatura.extract(html, favor_precision=True,
                               include_tables=True) or ""

def extract_resiliparse(html):
    return extract_plain_text(html, main_content=True, preserve_formatting=True)

EXTRACTORS = {
    "wet": extract_wet,
    "readability": extract_readability,
    "justext": extract_justext,
    "trafilatura": extract_trafilatura,
    "resiliparse": extract_resiliparse,
}

def benchmark(html_dir):
    pages = [open(p, encoding="utf-8", errors="replace").read()
             for p in glob.glob(f"{html_dir}/*.html")]
    for name, fn in EXTRACTORS.items():
        t0 = time.perf_counter()
        lengths = []
        for html in pages:
            try:
                lengths.append(len(fn(html) or ""))
            except Exception:
                lengths.append(0)            # robustness IS a benchmark axis
        dt = time.perf_counter() - t0
        print(f"{name:12s}  chars/page={int(stats.mean(lengths)):6d}  "
              f"docs/s={len(pages)/dt:7.1f}")

if __name__ == "__main__":
    benchmark("./sample_pages")
```

Notice that the harness measures *yield* and *throughput* but not *cleanliness* — because cleanliness requires a gold set, which is the next section. Yield and speed are free to measure; precision and recall cost you the labeling. That asymmetry is why so many teams measure the easy two and skip the one that actually predicts downstream quality.

## How to benchmark extractors on your own pages

The numbers above are illustrative. The only numbers that matter for *your* corpus are the ones measured on *your* pages, because the right extractor depends on your page-type distribution. A news-heavy crawl and a documentation-heavy crawl will rank extractors differently. Here is the methodology that actually predicts downstream quality.

**Step 1: Build a gold set.** Sample 100-300 pages that match your real distribution — same languages, same site types, same era of the web. For each, hand-label the "true" content: the article body with its real structure, and nothing else. This is tedious and it is the single highest-leverage hour of work in the whole pipeline. Store the gold as clean text or markdown alongside the source HTML.

**Step 2: Score with the right metrics.** For each extractor, compare its output to the gold using token-level (or character-level) precision and recall, not exact-match. Exact match is too brittle — whitespace and minor formatting differences will tank it without reflecting real quality. The standard approach is a longest-common-subsequence or set-overlap over tokens.

```python
def content_prf(pred: str, gold: str):
    """Token-level precision/recall/F1 of an extraction vs gold content."""
    from collections import Counter
    p, g = Counter(pred.split()), Counter(gold.split())
    overlap = sum((p & g).values())
    precision = overlap / max(sum(p.values()), 1)   # of kept tokens, how many are content?
    recall    = overlap / max(sum(g.values()), 1)   # of content tokens, how many kept?
    f1 = 2 * precision * recall / max(precision + recall, 1e-9)
    return precision, recall, f1
```

**Step 3: Weight by what you care about.** Precision and recall are not equally valuable for pretraining. As argued above, precision is cheaper to fix at extraction time and more expensive to fix downstream, so for a large corpus weight precision higher — an F-beta with beta < 1. For a small, high-recall-critical corpus (you cannot afford to lose any of a scarce domain), weight recall higher.

**Step 4: Measure throughput on your hardware.** Run each extractor over a few thousand pages and record docs/sec on the actual machines you will run the job on. Extrapolate to your full corpus. If the extraction job is going to take longer than your training run, that is a real constraint, not a footnote.

**Step 5: Spot-check the structure cases separately.** Pull out the technical pages (those with tables, code, or math) into their own mini-gold-set and score them separately. An extractor can have great aggregate F1 and still destroy every code block, and the aggregate number will hide it because code pages are a minority of the sample. If technical content matters to you, it gets its own scoreboard.

The output of this process is not "trafilatura is best." It is a table, specific to your corpus, that tells you the precision/recall/throughput trade for each option, so the decision is made with numbers instead of vibes.

## Case study: how RefinedWeb and FineWeb chose extraction over WET

This is not a hypothetical debate. The two most influential open web-corpus projects of the last few years both made extraction a first-class decision and published what it bought them.

![Corpus lineage timeline from 2019 to 2024 showing the shift from WET-based corpora (C4, CCNet) through jusText (The Pile) to trafilatura-class extraction (RefinedWeb, FineWeb, DCLM)](/imgs/blogs/text-extraction-and-boilerplate-removal-6.webp)

The timeline tells the story of the field changing its mind. The first generation of large web corpora — C4 (the cleaned Common Crawl behind T5) and CCNet (behind the original CC-based pretraining at scale) — were built on WET-style extraction plus heuristic line-level cleaning. C4's cleanup was a famous set of rules: drop lines without terminal punctuation, drop pages with "lorem ipsum," drop the "terms of service" boilerplate by blocklist. It worked well enough for 2019, but it was fundamentally cleaning *after* a lossy extraction rather than extracting well in the first place.

**RefinedWeb (2023)**, the corpus behind the Falcon models, made the break. Its authors re-extracted from WARC using trafilatura rather than trusting WET, then layered aggressive filtering and fuzzy dedup on top. Their headline result was that a *web-only* corpus, properly extracted and filtered, could match or beat models trained on curated mixtures that included books and Wikipedia. That claim only holds because the extraction was good: you cannot get curated-quality results from a corpus whose extraction left half the chrome in.

**FineWeb (2024)**, from Hugging Face, did the thing the field needed: it *ablated* the choice. The FineWeb team ran controlled experiments — train identical small models on corpora that differ only in one processing decision, then compare on a suite of benchmarks — and one of the decisions they isolated was WET versus trafilatura extraction. The result was unambiguous: trafilatura-extracted text produced meaningfully better downstream benchmark scores than WET, and the gap was on the order of a large filtering change, not a rounding error. Extraction was one of the bigger single levers they measured, not the plumbing it is usually treated as. FineWeb shipped with trafilatura.

**DCLM (2024)** is the instructive counterpoint on the speed axis. The DataComp-LM project, working at very large scale, chose resiliparse for extraction. They took the small F1 hit relative to trafilatura in exchange for the throughput needed to process their corpus, and still produced a top-tier dataset — because the dominant win is "real extraction instead of WET," and the trafilatura-vs-resiliparse gap is second-order next to that.

The lesson across all three: the move from WET to real content extraction is a *measurable, large* quality win, the specific extractor you pick within the "real extraction" camp is a smaller throughput-vs-quality trade, and the only way to know the size of either for your corpus is to ablate it the way FineWeb did.

## Where extraction errors surface: a troubleshooting guide

Here is what makes extraction bugs so expensive: they almost never surface at extraction time. They surface as a symptom in a *completely different* part of the pipeline, often days later, and the symptom looks like a problem with *that* stage. You will debug your dedup, your tokenizer, and your eval harness before you suspect the extractor that actually caused it.

![Graph showing one bad extraction defect fanning out to four downstream symptoms (garbage tokenizer merges, menus surviving dedup, inflated near-dup counts, shattered code in SFT/eval) that converge on wasted compute and lower eval scores](/imgs/blogs/text-extraction-and-boilerplate-removal-7.webp)

The figure maps the cause to its scattered effects. Below, each symptom gets the full treatment: how it looks, how to detect it, the root cause in extraction, and the fix.

### Symptom: garbage tokens in the tokenizer vocabulary

**How it looks.** After training your BPE tokenizer on the corpus, the learned vocabulary contains tokens like ` ||`, `Advertisement`, `Subscribe`, ` >>`, long runs of punctuation, or fragments of CSS/JS like `function(`. The compression ratio (bytes per token) is worse than expected.

**How to detect it.** Dump the rarest 1% and the most "boilerplate-shaped" merges from the trained vocabulary and eyeball them. A healthy vocabulary is dominated by linguistic subwords; a corrupted one has navigation phrases and markup as first-class tokens. Also watch the corpus's bytes-per-token: if it is unusually low, the tokenizer is spending vocabulary on repeated chrome.

**Root cause.** Boilerplate leaked through extraction. Because the same nav strings appear millions of times, BPE happily learns dedicated merges for them — the tokenizer is doing its job on bad input. This is a direct line from extraction precision to [tokenizer quality](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/bpe-tokenizer).

**The fix.** Raise extraction precision (turn on `favor_precision`, drop comments and links), re-extract, then re-train the tokenizer on the cleaned corpus. You cannot fix this by editing the vocabulary; the bad merges are a symptom, and the disease is upstream.

### Symptom: shattered code blocks in SFT and eval

**How it looks.** Your model writes code with broken indentation, joins multi-line functions onto one line, or produces syntactically invalid output even though it "knows" the right tokens. Code benchmarks underperform relative to the model's general capability.

**How to detect it.** Grep the corpus for fenced code blocks and inspect a sample: are newlines and leading whitespace preserved? Compute the fraction of code blocks that parse with a language parser (e.g., does the Python ever `ast.parse` cleanly). A high parse-failure rate on extracted code is the tell.

**Root cause.** The extractor flattened `<pre>`/`<code>` whitespace — either you extracted to flat text instead of markdown, or the extractor does not preserve code formatting at all.

**The fix.** Extract to markdown or XML with `include_formatting=True` (trafilatura) / `preserve_formatting=True` (resiliparse), verify on a code-page gold set, and make sure your tokenizer was trained on the same structured representation so it does not shatter the fences anyway.

### Symptom: repeated menus survive dedup

**How it looks.** After a dedup pass that reports a healthy duplicate-removal ratio, you still see the same navigation menus and footers all over the corpus. Sampling random documents turns up the site template again and again.

**How to detect it.** Take a known boilerplate string (a site's footer text) and count its occurrences in the post-dedup corpus. If it appears thousands of times, dedup did not catch it.

**Root cause.** Document-level dedup keys on whole-document similarity. A footer leaked into the *middle* of otherwise-distinct articles is never a standalone duplicate — each host document is unique, so the embedded boilerplate rides along. Dedup cannot remove a span it never sees as a unit. This is the interaction between extraction and [deduplication at scale](/blog/machine-learning/training-data/deduplication-at-scale): bad extraction makes boilerplate *un-dedupable*.

**The fix.** Fix it at the source — better extraction removes the menus before dedup ever runs. Span-level or line-level dedup can help as a backstop, but it is far more expensive than not leaking the boilerplate in the first place.

### Symptom: inflated near-duplicate counts

**How it looks.** Your near-dedup (MinHash/LSH) flags an implausibly high fraction of document pairs as near-duplicates, and clustering collapses documents that are actually about different topics.

**How to detect it.** Inspect a sample of flagged near-duplicate pairs. If the "duplication" is concentrated in the shared header/footer/sidebar text while the article bodies differ, the boilerplate is driving the similarity.

**Root cause.** MinHash similarity is computed over shingles of the whole document. When 30-50% of every document is identical boilerplate, every pair from the same site looks near-duplicate even when the articles are unrelated. The extractor's leaked chrome is a shared substring that fools the similarity estimate.

**The fix.** Re-extract with higher precision so documents are mostly content; near-dup counts will drop to a sane level because the shared substrings are gone. If you must work with a leaky corpus, weight or remove boilerplate-shaped shingles before MinHashing — but that is a patch over an extraction bug.

### Symptom: math and tables mangled into nonsense

**How it looks.** Documents reference "the table below" or "as shown in Equation 3" with no table or equation present, or with a meaningless run of numbers/symbols where structure should be. Quantitative-reasoning evals lag.

**How to detect it.** Sample pages known to contain tables or math (papers, docs, finance) and diff the extracted text against the source. Look for collapsed grids and dropped `<script type="math/tex">` / MathML blocks.

**Root cause.** Generic extraction flattens tables to 1D and drops or garbles math markup. Even good extractors need to be *told* to keep tables.

**The fix.** Enable table extraction (`include_tables=True`), and for math-heavy sources use a math-aware preprocessing pass that converts MathML/LaTeX to a consistent textual form before extraction, rather than letting the extractor drop it. Score technical pages on their own gold set so this does not hide in the aggregate.

### Symptom: encoding corruption and mojibake

**How it looks.** Accented characters and non-Latin scripts come out as garbage sequences: the word for "café" shows up as `caf` followed by two stray symbols; smart quotes become `â€œ` and `â€\x9d`; whole CJK documents are unreadable.

**How to detect it.** Scan the corpus for the signature byte patterns of double-encoded UTF-8 (sequences starting with the bytes for `Ã`, `â€`, `Â`). A spike in these is mojibake. Track the rate per source.

**Root cause.** The page's real encoding was guessed wrong before extraction. The classic chain: UTF-8 bytes are decoded as Latin-1 (or Windows-1252), producing visible garbage, which is then re-encoded as UTF-8 and stored — now the corruption is baked in.

```python
# The mojibake mechanism, in pure-ASCII terms. The two-byte UTF-8 sequence
# for an accented e is 0xC3 0xA9. Decode it as latin-1 and you get two
# separate visible characters instead of one accented letter.
raw = b"caf\xc3\xa9"                 # correct UTF-8 bytes for the word cafe + acute e
print(raw.decode("utf-8"))           # cafe-with-accent  (correct)
print(raw.decode("latin-1"))         # caf + 2 stray glyphs  (mojibake)

# The fix: detect encoding from bytes BEFORE decoding, and repair leftovers.
from resiliparse.parse.encoding import detect_encoding, bytes_to_str
text = bytes_to_str(raw, detect_encoding(raw))   # decode with the right codec

import ftfy
text = ftfy.fix_text(text)           # repair already-corrupted strings
```

**The fix.** Detect encoding from the raw bytes (HTTP `Content-Type`, then a `<meta charset>` sniff, then statistical detection) *before* decoding, not after. resiliparse's `detect_encoding` does this well. For damage already present in a corpus, `ftfy` repairs the common double-encoding patterns. The order matters: get the encoding right at extraction time and you never create mojibake; `ftfy` is a cleanup for corpora where you didn't.

### Symptom: JavaScript-rendered pages yield empty content

**How it looks.** A noticeable fraction of pages extract to nothing, or to a tiny stub like "Please enable JavaScript to view this site." Whole domains contribute almost no content despite being crawled.

**How to detect it.** Track the distribution of extracted character counts per page and flag the spike at near-zero. Bucket the empties by domain — if they cluster on known single-page-application sites (modern news, social, app-like docs), it is the JS-rendering problem.

**Root cause.** The page's content is rendered client-side by JavaScript. The crawler saved the initial HTML, which is an empty shell — the real content never existed in the bytes you have. No extractor can find content that is not in the HTML.

**The fix.** This one is not an extraction bug and cannot be fixed at extraction time. The options are upstream: use a rendering crawler (headless browser) for JS-heavy domains, or accept the loss and rely on the fact that a huge amount of the web is still server-rendered. The important thing is to *measure* the empty rate so you know how much of a source you are silently losing — an undetected 30% empty rate on a domain you thought was valuable is a real corpus gap.

## War stories: extraction failures from production corpora

The symptom guide is the theory. These are the specific shapes the failure takes in practice — composite incidents drawn from the patterns that recur across real corpus builds.

### 1. The corpus that was 40% navigation

A team shipped a first corpus built on Common Crawl WET files because it was the fast path to a baseline. The pipeline ran, the dashboards looked healthy — document counts, language distribution, dedup ratio all nominal. The model trained fine but underperformed a same-size open model by several points. The root cause surfaced only when someone computed the average ratio of link-text to prose per document and found it was nearly 1:1. WET had kept every nav menu on every page. An estimated 40% of the training tokens were chrome. Re-extracting the same WARC files with trafilatura shrank the corpus by more than half and closed most of the benchmark gap — the "missing" data was never data.

### 2. The code model that couldn't indent

A code-focused fine-tune kept producing Python with flattened indentation, even though the base model was strong. The training data had been extracted to flat text with a generic stripper that discarded `<pre>` whitespace. Every code block in the corpus had been joined into single lines. The model had literally never seen correctly-indented code during this stage. The fix was a re-extraction to markdown with code formatting preserved, plus a re-trained tokenizer that had efficient merges for leading-whitespace runs. Parse-rate of generated code jumped immediately.

### 3. The dedup that ran for a week and removed nothing useful

A near-dedup job on a leaky corpus flagged an enormous fraction of document pairs as near-duplicates and ground through a week of compute. Inspection of flagged pairs showed the "duplication" was the shared site template — headers, sidebars, footers — while the article bodies were entirely different. MinHash was measuring template similarity, not content similarity. The team had been about to delete genuinely distinct articles as duplicates. The real fix was upstream: re-extract to remove the boilerplate, after which near-dup rates fell to a believable level and the job finished in hours.

### 4. The French corpus that was all mojibake

A multilingual build had a French subset where accented characters were systematically corrupted. The crawler had stored pages whose real encoding was Windows-1252, but the extraction step assumed UTF-8 and decoded blindly. Every "é," "è," and "à" became a two-character garbage sequence. Language ID still labeled the documents French (the structure survived), so the corruption passed every filter and reached training, where it taught the model garbage spellings. The fix was to detect encoding from the bytes before decoding and to run `ftfy` over the already-built subset. The deeper lesson: language ID confidence is *not* a mojibake detector, because mojibake preserves enough structure to fool it.

### 5. The documentation site that extracted to nothing

A team specifically crawled a high-value framework documentation site to boost the model's knowledge of that framework — and got almost no content from it. The docs were a JavaScript single-page app; the crawled HTML was an empty shell. Nobody noticed until a targeted eval on that framework flatlined. The empty-rate metric had not been bucketed by domain, so a 95%-empty source looked like a small blip in the global average. The fix was to re-crawl that domain with a headless browser, and — more durably — to add per-domain empty-rate monitoring so the next such gap would be caught at ingestion, not at eval.

### 6. The tables that became number soup

A finance-heavy corpus trained a model that was strangely bad at reading tabular data, despite the corpus being full of financial filings. The extractor had flattened every HTML table into a 1D run of cell values with no column structure. The model saw years and dollar amounts interleaved with no way to recover the grid. Turning on table extraction (so tables came through as markdown pipe-tables) and re-scoring on a table-specific gold set fixed it — and revealed that the aggregate content F1 had looked fine all along because table pages were a small fraction of the sample.

### 7. The comment swamp that taught the model to argue

An under-configured extractor kept comment sections. On forum-heavy and news sources, this meant the corpus was full of "First!", flame wars, quote pyramids, and spam. The model picked up a distinctly combative, low-quality register for certain prompts. The extractor had a `include_comments` flag that defaulted on for the library version in use. Flipping it off and re-extracting removed a large slice of toxic, low-value text — comments are occasionally valuable, but they need to be an explicit opt-in per source, not a silent default.

### 8. The boilerplate blocklist that aged badly

A team that did extract reasonably well also maintained a hand-written blocklist of boilerplate phrases ("Accept all cookies," "Sign up for our newsletter") to scrub residual chrome. It worked at first, then quietly decayed: the web's consent-banner language drifted, new phrasings appeared, and the blocklist caught a shrinking fraction. Worse, a couple of entries were generic enough to delete legitimate sentences. The lesson was to treat extraction as a *model* of content-vs-boilerplate, not a *blocklist* of known-bad strings — heuristics on structure generalize; string blocklists rot.

## When to reach for a content extractor (and when WET is fine)

Reach for a real content extractor (trafilatura or resiliparse) when:

- **You are building a corpus you will actually train a shipped model on.** Any production pretraining or serious fine-tuning corpus should re-extract from WARC. This is not optional anymore; the entire field moved here.
- **Your corpus has any technical content** — code, tables, math, documentation. Structure preservation is the whole game, and only real extractors do it.
- **You run dedup downstream.** Good extraction is the cheapest way to make dedup tractable, because it removes the boilerplate that manufactures duplicates.
- **You care about token efficiency.** Every boilerplate token is GPU-hours spent learning navigation. At scale this is a meaningful fraction of your compute.
- **You are extracting at a scale where throughput matters** — then specifically reach for resiliparse and accept the small F1 cost for the 10-100x speed, the way DCLM did.

WET (or a quick generic strip) is acceptable when:

- **You are prototyping a pipeline** and want a baseline corpus fast, with full knowledge you will replace it before shipping.
- **You are doing analysis, not training** — measuring language distribution, URL coverage, or crawl statistics, where boilerplate does not hurt the measurement much.
- **The downstream use is robust to noise** and the cost of better extraction genuinely is not justified — a rare case, and one you should confirm with an ablation rather than assume.

The decision rule that captures all of it: **the cheapest quality in your whole pipeline is the quality you buy at extraction time.** A point of content precision is nearly free to get from a better extractor and brutally expensive to claw back with downstream filtering. Spend the hour building a gold set, run the benchmark on your own pages, ablate WET-vs-extraction the way FineWeb did, and then you can treat extraction as the load-bearing decision it actually is — instead of the plumbing it pretends to be.
