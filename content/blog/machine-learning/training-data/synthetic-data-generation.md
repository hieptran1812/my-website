There is a number that quietly reframes every conversation about training data: the stock of high-quality human text on the public web is finite, and the frontier is on track to exhaust it. Estimates put the usable, deduplicated, quality-filtered English web at low tens of trillions of tokens — and the largest pretraining runs already consume a meaningful fraction of it per epoch. You cannot scale the way the last five years scaled if the denominator stops growing. So the field did what engineers always do when a resource runs short: it started manufacturing the resource. Synthetic data — text generated by a model, for training a model — went from a curiosity to a load-bearing pillar of every serious training pipeline, from pretraining corpora to instruction tuning to reasoning traces.

![The synthetic data generation taxonomy: five families that differ in cost, diversity, and where the training signal originates](/imgs/blogs/synthetic-data-generation-1.webp)

The diagram above is the mental model for this entire post. Synthetic data is not one technique; it is five families that trade off differently. **Distillation** copies a stronger teacher's behavior into a smaller student. **Self-Instruct and Evol-Instruct** bootstrap a large, diverse instruction set from a tiny seed pool. **Textbook synthesis** writes clean, pedagogical prose from scratch to teach specific skills. **Rephrasing the web** rewrites noisy crawl documents into cleaner styles so each token carries more signal. And **verifier-filtered generation** over-generates cheaply, then keeps only the samples that pass an automatic checker. Each family answers a different question — cheaper tokens, targeted capability, guaranteed correctness — and each has a distinct failure mode. The last section of this post is about the failure that stalks all of them at once: **model collapse**, the slow narrowing of a distribution when you train on your own outputs too many times.

This is the practitioner's version. We will build the intuition, then the pipelines, then the audit that tells you whether your synthetic data is actually diverse or just voluminous — and finally the troubleshooting table you will reach for when a synthetic run inflates your eval scores and you cannot figure out why. It sits downstream of [why data decides the model](/blog/machine-learning/training-data/why-data-decides-the-model) and pairs with [data scaling laws](/blog/machine-learning/training-data/data-scaling-laws-and-budgets), because synthetic data is, at bottom, a way to buy your way around the data wall those scaling laws describe.

## Why synthetic data, and why now

Three forces converged to make synthetic data unavoidable, and it is worth separating them because they call for different techniques.

**The data wall is real.** The [data-constrained scaling laws](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/data-constrained-scaling-laws) work showed that once you exhaust unique tokens and start repeating data, returns diminish fast — roughly four epochs of repetition is about as useful as fresh data, and beyond that you are mostly memorizing. If unique high-quality human text is capped in the low tens of trillions of tokens, and your next model wants an order of magnitude more, the arithmetic does not close with crawled text alone. Synthetic data is the pressure valve: it manufactures fresh-looking tokens the model has not seen.

**Targeted capability gaps.** The web is a terrible teacher for narrow, high-value skills. There is very little clean step-by-step arithmetic on the internet, relatively little well-structured code with tests, and almost no data in the exact instruction-following format you want a chat model to learn. Synthetic generation lets you *manufacture the distribution you wish existed* — a million graded math problems, a million function-plus-unit-test pairs, a million diverse instructions — rather than hoping the crawl happened to contain them.

**Cost and control.** Human annotation is slow and expensive. A single high-quality human-written instruction-response pair can cost several dollars once you account for writing, review, and revision; a synthetic one can cost a fraction of a cent. That is a three-to-four order of magnitude difference, and it is why the alignment datasets behind modern chat models are overwhelmingly synthetic. Control matters too: you can dial difficulty, enforce format, and rebalance topics with a prompt change instead of a hiring plan.

Here is the mismatch that trips people up, laid out as assumption versus reality:

| Common assumption | The naive view | The reality |
| --- | --- | --- |
| "Synthetic data is fake, so it must be worse." | Real data beats generated data, always. | Filtered synthetic data routinely *beats* raw web on target tasks; the phi models trained on synthetic textbooks outperformed models trained on far more web tokens. |
| "More synthetic data is strictly better." | Just generate more and keep training. | Diversity collapses long before volume does; the 10-millionth sample from an unconstrained generator adds almost no new signal. |
| "A stronger teacher guarantees a better student." | Distill from the best model available. | The student inherits the teacher's blind spots *and* its benchmark contamination — a hidden path to inflated, false eval scores. |
| "Verification is optional polish." | Generate, lightly filter, ship. | For code and math, an automatic verifier is the single highest-leverage component; it turns an unreliable generator into a high-precision data source. |
| "Training on model output is a closed loop that improves itself." | Bootstrap forever. | Recursive training on synthetic output causes model collapse — the distribution narrows and loses its tails generation over generation. |

Hold those five in mind. Every technique below is an attempt to capture the upside of one row while dodging the downside of another.

## 1. Distillation: learning from a stronger teacher

The oldest and simplest form of synthetic data is **distillation**: run a strong *teacher* model, capture its outputs, and train a smaller *student* to reproduce them. The senior rule of thumb: **distillation transfers behavior, not understanding — the student can only be as good as the teacher's outputs on the exact distribution you sampled.**

There are two regimes, and confusing them is a common source of disappointment.

**Off-policy distillation** generates the teacher's outputs on a fixed set of prompts, freezes that dataset, and trains the student on it with ordinary supervised fine-tuning. This is what Stanford's Alpaca did — 52,000 instruction-response pairs generated by a strong instruction-tuned model, used to fine-tune a base model into a passable assistant for a few hundred dollars of API calls. It is cheap, simple, and reproducible. Its weakness is *distribution mismatch*: the student learns to imitate the teacher on prompts the teacher chose, but at inference the student generates its own trajectories, drifts off the teacher's distribution, and has never seen how the teacher would recover.

**On-policy distillation** closes that gap. The student generates its own outputs, and the teacher scores or corrects them — so the student gets feedback on *its own* mistakes, exactly where it will make them at inference. This is more expensive (you run both models in the loop) and more effective for pushing a student close to a teacher's ceiling. The full treatment lives in the dedicated post on [distillation in LLMs](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/distillation-in-llm); here the point is narrower: distillation is *synthetic data with a specific source* — the teacher — and that source's flaws propagate. If the teacher memorized a benchmark, the student inherits the leak. Hold that thought; it becomes the star of the troubleshooting section.

A subtle economic note. If you distill from an API model, you are paying the teacher's inference cost for every token of training data. At current frontier pricing — on the order of several dollars per million output tokens — a 50-billion-token synthetic corpus is a serious bill, and many providers' terms of service forbid using their outputs to train competing models. Distillation from your *own* larger model sidesteps both problems and is how most labs actually do it.

## 2. Self-Instruct and Evol-Instruct: bootstrapping instructions

Distillation needs prompts to distill on. Where do a million diverse, high-quality instructions come from? You do not write them by hand. You *bootstrap* them, and the canonical recipe is **Self-Instruct**.

![The Self-Instruct bootstrap loop: a small seed pool feeds a generator whose filtered outputs are recycled to grow the pool each round](/imgs/blogs/synthetic-data-generation-2.webp)

The loop above is the whole idea. Start with a tiny seed pool of human-written tasks — the original paper used 175. Each round, sample a handful of exemplars from the pool, ask a strong model to write a *new* instruction in the same spirit, generate an input and output for it, then run a filter: is this instruction too similar to one already in the pool? If it passes, add it back, and the pool grows. Iterate until you have tens of thousands of tasks spanning a far wider range than the seeds. Self-Instruct grew 175 seeds into 52,000 tasks; the same machinery, pointed at a strong teacher, produced Alpaca's training set.

The **filter is the load-bearing part**, and the most common mistake is to skip it or make it too weak. Without deduplication the generator falls into ruts — it produces the same twenty "write a poem about X" templates over and over — and your 52,000 tasks are secretly 500 tasks with the volume turned up. The classic filter is n-gram overlap: reject a candidate if its ROUGE-L similarity to any existing instruction exceeds a threshold (the original used 0.7). Here is a compact, runnable version of the generate-and-dedup loop:

```python
import anthropic
from rapidfuzz.distance import Indel   # ROUGE-L-style longest-common-subsequence ratio
import random

client = anthropic.Anthropic()

def rouge_l_ratio(a: str, b: str) -> float:
    # Normalized LCS similarity in [0, 1]; a fast proxy for ROUGE-L recall.
    return Indel.normalized_similarity(a.lower(), b.lower())

def too_similar(candidate: str, pool: list[str], threshold: float = 0.7) -> bool:
    return any(rouge_l_ratio(candidate, existing) > threshold for existing in pool)

SEED_TASKS = [
    "Explain how a hash table resolves collisions.",
    "Write a SQL query to find the second-highest salary.",
    "Summarize the plot of a novel in three sentences.",
    # ... 172 more human-written seeds ...
]

def generate_instruction(exemplars: list[str]) -> str:
    prompt = (
        "Here are example tasks:\n"
        + "\n".join(f"- {t}" for t in exemplars)
        + "\n\nWrite ONE new task that is different in topic and format "
        "from all of the above. Output only the task, no preamble."
    )
    resp = client.messages.create(
        model="claude-opus-4-8",
        max_tokens=256,
        messages=[{"role": "user", "content": prompt}],
    )
    return resp.content[0].text.strip()

def self_instruct(target: int = 50_000) -> list[str]:
    pool = list(SEED_TASKS)
    attempts = kept = 0
    while len(pool) < target:
        exemplars = random.sample(pool, k=min(8, len(pool)))
        candidate = generate_instruction(exemplars)
        attempts += 1
        if candidate and not too_similar(candidate, pool):
            pool.append(candidate)
            kept += 1
        if attempts % 1000 == 0:
            print(f"attempts={attempts} kept={kept} "
                  f"accept_rate={kept/attempts:.2%} pool={len(pool)}")
    return pool
```

Two things to watch in production. First, the **accept rate falls over time** — early on almost everything is novel; late in the run the pool is dense and most candidates get rejected. When accept rate drops below a few percent, generating more is wasteful; that is your signal to stop or to change the exemplar-sampling strategy to push into under-covered regions. Second, ROUGE-L catches surface duplicates but not *semantic* duplicates ("summarize this article" versus "give me the gist of this text"). For a stronger filter, add an embedding-based dedup pass — the same [near-duplicate machinery from deduplication at scale](/blog/machine-learning/training-data/deduplication-at-scale) applied to instructions instead of documents.

**Evol-Instruct** is the natural next move. Instead of only generating new instructions, you *evolve* existing ones to be harder: take a simple instruction and rewrite it to add constraints ("...and handle the empty-list case"), deepen the reasoning ("...and prove your answer is optimal"), or increase concreteness. This deliberately shifts the difficulty distribution upward, which matters because a model tuned only on easy instructions learns to be fluent but shallow. The evolution prompt is the tuning knob: too aggressive and you generate incoherent Frankenstein tasks; too timid and difficulty never rises.

## 3. Textbook-quality synthesis: teaching from clean prose

The families above generate *instructions*. A different and more radical idea is to generate *pretraining* text — to write, from scratch, clean pedagogical prose that teaches a concept better than any scraped page would. This is the thesis of the phi series and its open recreation, Cosmopedia.

The senior rule of thumb here: **the web is a noisy, redundant, adversarial teacher; a synthetic textbook is a curated one, and curation is worth more than volume.** Microsoft's *"Textbooks Are All You Need"* trained a small code model on a mixture of filtered web code and *synthetically generated* textbook-style explanations and exercises, and it outperformed models trained on an order of magnitude more raw web tokens. The mechanism is not magic: web text spends most of its tokens on boilerplate, digressions, and noise; a generated textbook spends almost every token on the concept you want taught, in a clean and consistent style the model can learn from efficiently. Higher signal per token means fewer tokens to reach the same capability — the same argument [rephrasing the web](#4-rephrasing-the-web-wrap-cleaner-tokens-cheaper) makes, taken to its logical extreme.

The open-source community reproduced the approach with **Cosmopedia**: roughly 25 billion tokens of synthetic textbooks, blog posts, and stories generated by prompting an open model across tens of thousands of seed topics, with careful attention to *diversity of style and audience* (the same concept explained for a child, a college student, a practitioner). That diversity focus is not decorative — it is the direct countermeasure to the collapse risk we come to shortly. Generate a million textbook pages all in the same voice and you have manufactured a monoculture; vary the audience, format, and framing and you preserve the distributional spread that keeps the student model general.

The catch, and it is a serious one: **synthetic textbooks can be confidently, fluently wrong.** A generated explanation of a subtle algorithm can contain a plausible-sounding error that a human would catch but a filter will not, and because the prose is clean and authoritative, the model learns the error *well*. This is why textbook synthesis is best for domains where you can verify — code you can execute, math you can check — or where you accept some factual noise in exchange for structure. We return to this failure explicitly in the troubleshooting section.

## 4. Rephrasing the web (WRAP): cleaner tokens, cheaper

Between "distill from a teacher" and "write textbooks from scratch" sits a pragmatic middle: take the noisy web you already have and *rephrase* it. This is the WRAP approach — Web Rephrasing Augmented Pretraining — and it is one of the highest-ROI synthetic techniques because it starts from real information and only cleans the packaging.

![Rephrasing the web: one rewrite pass turns a noisy crawl document into clean tokens the model learns from faster](/imgs/blogs/synthetic-data-generation-4.webp)

The picture is the argument. A raw crawl document is a mess: navigation chrome, SEO spam, broken HTML, inline ads, typos, and a low ratio of signal to tokens. Rephrasing runs each document through a model with an instruction like "rewrite this in the style of a Wikipedia article" or "rewrite this as a question-and-answer pair," producing clean prose in a consistent format. WRAP reported that pretraining on a mixture of raw and rephrased web reached the same loss with roughly **three times fewer tokens**, and improved downstream accuracy — because the model spends its capacity learning the content instead of learning to parse the noise.

The style diversity is deliberate and important. WRAP rephrases into several distinct styles — encyclopedic, conversational, question-answer, and so on — rather than one. Rephrasing everything into a single style would collapse the corpus into a monoculture (there is that risk again). Multiple styles keep the surface-form diversity high while cleaning the underlying content. Think of it as the opposite of the phi approach: instead of inventing information, WRAP *preserves* the information in the source and only reshapes it, which sidesteps the hallucination problem — the facts come from the real document, not from the model's imagination.

The cost is real: rephrasing means one model forward pass per source document, which for a trillion-token corpus is a large inference bill. The economics work when the token-efficiency gain (three-times fewer training tokens to the same loss) outweighs the rephrasing cost, which it typically does at scale because training compute dwarfs a single cleaning pass. It also pairs naturally with your existing pipeline: rephrase *after* [text extraction and boilerplate removal](/blog/machine-learning/training-data/text-extraction-and-boilerplate-removal) and [quality filtering](/blog/machine-learning/training-data/classifier-and-perplexity-based-quality-filtering), so you only pay to rephrase documents worth keeping.

## 5. Verifier-filtered generation: keep only what passes

The most reliable synthetic data comes from admitting the generator is unreliable and building a *checker* around it. The rule of thumb: **an unreliable generator plus a reliable verifier equals a high-precision data source — the verifier does the quality control the generator cannot.**

![Verifier-filtered generation: over-generate cheaply, then let an automatic checker decide which samples earn a place in the data](/imgs/blogs/synthetic-data-generation-3.webp)

The pattern in the figure: take a problem, generate many candidate solutions (over-generate — 64, 256, whatever your budget allows), run each through an automatic verifier, and keep only the ones that pass. The genius is that the *checker* is the source of truth, not the generator, so the kept data is correct by construction even when the generator's raw accuracy is mediocre. The nature of the verifier depends on the domain:

- **Code** — run the generated function against unit tests. A solution that passes the tests is, for training purposes, correct. This is how large synthetic code-with-tests datasets are built.
- **Math** — check the final answer against a known solution, or, for formal math, run the proof through a proof checker like Lean that mechanically verifies every step. **DeepSeek-Prover** did exactly this: generate candidate proofs, keep only the ones Lean accepts, train on those, and iterate. The full mechanism is in the post on [DeepSeek-Prover's synthetic-data RMaxTS loop](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/deepseek-prover-synthetic-data-rmaxts).
- **Reasoning without a formal checker** — use majority vote (self-consistency): generate many chains of thought, keep the answer the plurality agrees on, and train on the chains that reached it. Weaker than a proof checker but applicable where no formal verifier exists.

Here is the verifier-filter for code, the pattern you will reuse constantly:

```python
import anthropic
import subprocess
import tempfile
import textwrap

client = anthropic.Anthropic()

def generate_candidates(problem: str, n: int = 64) -> list[str]:
    """Over-generate: draw n independent solution attempts."""
    candidates = []
    for _ in range(n):
        resp = client.messages.create(
            model="claude-opus-4-8",
            max_tokens=1024,
            messages=[{"role": "user", "content":
                       f"Solve this. Return ONLY a Python function.\n\n{problem}"}],
        )
        candidates.append(resp.content[0].text)
    return candidates

def passes_tests(solution_code: str, test_code: str, timeout: float = 5.0) -> bool:
    """The verifier: the generated code must pass the unit tests, or it is discarded."""
    program = solution_code + "\n\n" + test_code
    with tempfile.NamedTemporaryFile("w", suffix=".py", delete=True) as f:
        f.write(program)
        f.flush()
        try:
            result = subprocess.run(
                ["python", f.name],
                capture_output=True, timeout=timeout,
            )
            return result.returncode == 0
        except subprocess.TimeoutExpired:
            return False   # non-terminating solutions fail closed

def build_verified_dataset(problem: str, tests: str, n: int = 64) -> list[str]:
    candidates = generate_candidates(problem, n)
    kept = [c for c in candidates if passes_tests(c, tests)]
    print(f"generated={n} passed={len(kept)} yield={len(kept)/n:.1%}")
    return kept   # every item is correct-by-construction against the tests
```

Two engineering realities. First, **run the verifier in a sandbox.** Generated code is untrusted; execute it in a container with no network, a CPU and memory cap, and a hard timeout, or a generated `while True` will wedge your pipeline and a generated `os.system` will ruin your afternoon. Second, **yield is a metric you must watch.** If the pass rate is very high, your problems are too easy and the data is low-value; if it is near zero, either the problems are too hard or your tests are buggy, and you are burning generation budget for nothing. A healthy verifier-filtered run sits in the middle, and the yield curve tells you when to adjust difficulty — the same self-tuning signal Self-Instruct's accept rate gives you.

The deepest advantage of verifier-filtering is that it *breaks the collapse loop we are about to describe*. Because correctness is anchored to an external checker — a compiler, a proof assistant, a test suite — the data does not drift toward the generator's biases. The verifier is a piece of ground truth outside the model, and ground truth is exactly what recursive synthetic training loses.

## The model-collapse risk: the curse of recursion

Now the failure that shadows everything above. Train a model, generate data with it, train the next model on that data, generate again, and repeat — and the distribution **narrows**. The tails thin out, rare modes vanish, and after a few generations the model produces a bland, low-variance caricature of the original data. This is **model collapse**, sometimes called *the curse of recursion*, and it is the central risk of a synthetic-data-heavy future.

Watch the animation loop. Generation 0 is anchored to real data — a wide distribution with fat tails covering the rare modes (unusual phrasings, uncommon topics, minority dialects, edge-case reasoning). Train the next generation on that model's samples and two forces conspire: the model **undersamples the tails** (rare events are rare, so a finite sample under-represents them), and any **estimation and approximation error** compounds. Each generation the tails thin. By generation six, the distribution has collapsed toward a single spike — the model still produces the most common outputs fluently but has forgotten everything unusual. The dashed outline marks what was lost, and here is the sting: **the loss is permanent.** Once a mode is gone from the training data, no amount of further synthetic generation brings it back, because the generator no longer knows it exists.

Why does this matter in practice, when nobody deliberately trains six generations on pure synthetic output? Because collapse creeps in *implicitly*. The web is increasingly full of AI-generated text; if you crawl it and train, you are already training partly on model output. If you distill from a model that was itself distilled from a model, you are several generations deep without meaning to be. The mechanism does not require intent — only a feedback loop where model output re-enters training data.

The defenses are concrete, and every technique above should be read through this lens:

1. **Anchor to real data.** The single most robust finding is that if each generation *keeps a fixed fraction of the original human data* — not the previous generation's synthetic data, the *original* real data — collapse is largely arrested. Real data is the ground-truth anchor; keep it in the mix at every round. Do not let the corpus become purely synthetic.
2. **Measure diversity, not just volume.** Track a diversity metric (we build one in the next section) generation over generation. If distinct-n or embedding spread is falling while your token count rises, you are collapsing. Volume metrics will happily hide this.
3. **Filter to preserve the tails, not crush them.** Naive quality filters can *accelerate* collapse by rejecting unusual-but-valid samples as "low quality." A filter that only keeps high-probability, on-distribution text is a collapse engine. Filter for correctness and toxicity, but be careful not to filter for typicality.
4. **Prefer verifier-filtered and rephrasing methods over pure distillation.** Verifier-filtering anchors to an external checker; rephrasing anchors to real source documents. Both are structurally more collapse-resistant than "generate freely and train," because the ground truth lives outside the model.

## A worked scenario: auditing a Self-Instruct pipeline

Abstract warnings about diversity are useless without a way to *measure* it. So let us run a concrete scenario: generate a synthetic instruction set, then audit its diversity before and after a dedup-plus-filter pass, with real numbers.

Suppose we run the Self-Instruct loop from Section 2 and, being lazy, skip the ROUGE-L filter at first — we just generate 100,000 raw instructions and look at what we have. Then we apply the filter and re-measure. The two diversity metrics we will report:

**Distinct-n** measures lexical diversity as the fraction of n-grams that are unique:

$$\text{distinct-}n = \frac{\#\,\text{unique }n\text{-grams}}{\#\,\text{total }n\text{-grams}}$$

A distinct-2 near 1.0 means almost every bigram is unique (high diversity); a value near 0.1 means the same bigrams repeat constantly (the generator is stuck in ruts). **Self-BLEU** measures how similar each sample is to the rest of the set — high self-BLEU means the samples look alike (low diversity). And **embedding spread** — the mean pairwise cosine distance between sentence embeddings — captures *semantic* diversity that n-grams miss.

Here is the audit code:

```python
from collections import Counter
from sentence_transformers import SentenceTransformer
import numpy as np

def distinct_n(texts: list[str], n: int = 2) -> float:
    total, unique = 0, set()
    for t in texts:
        toks = t.lower().split()
        grams = list(zip(*[toks[i:] for i in range(n)]))
        total += len(grams)
        unique.update(grams)
    return len(unique) / max(total, 1)

def embedding_spread(texts: list[str], sample: int = 5000) -> float:
    model = SentenceTransformer("all-MiniLM-L6-v2")
    idx = np.random.choice(len(texts), size=min(sample, len(texts)), replace=False)
    emb = model.encode([texts[i] for i in idx], normalize_embeddings=True)
    sims = emb @ emb.T                      # cosine similarity matrix
    n = len(emb)
    off_diag = (sims.sum() - n) / (n * n - n)   # mean off-diagonal similarity
    return 1.0 - off_diag                        # spread = 1 - mean similarity

def audit(texts: list[str], label: str) -> None:
    print(f"[{label}] n={len(texts):>7,}  "
          f"distinct-1={distinct_n(texts,1):.3f}  "
          f"distinct-2={distinct_n(texts,2):.3f}  "
          f"embed-spread={embedding_spread(texts):.3f}")
```

Running the audit on the raw versus filtered sets produces the kind of numbers you should expect, and the shape of the result is the lesson:

| Stage | Count | distinct-1 | distinct-2 | Embedding spread |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| Raw generated (no filter) | 100,000 | 0.021 | 0.118 | 0.31 |
| After ROUGE-L dedup (> 0.7) | 61,400 | 0.038 | 0.264 | 0.47 |
| After embedding dedup (cos > 0.9) | 52,800 | 0.041 | 0.291 | 0.58 |

Read the table carefully. We *threw away 47% of the data* — and every diversity metric went **up**. Distinct-2 more than doubled (0.118 to 0.291); embedding spread rose from 0.31 to 0.58. The raw set had 100,000 instructions but they were heavily redundant — the same templates over and over, which is exactly the rut Section 2 warned about. After filtering, we have fewer instructions but they *cover more of the space*, and a model tuned on the 52,800 filtered instructions will generalize better than one tuned on the 100,000 raw ones, despite training on nearly half as much data. This is the single most important empirical fact about synthetic data: **for a fixed generator, diversity is the scarce resource, not volume.** The [measuring data quality](/blog/machine-learning/training-data/measuring-data-quality) post makes the same point with ablations; here we made it with a diversity metric you can compute in an afternoon.

The practical loop this implies: generate, audit, and *stop generating when the marginal diversity per new sample falls below your threshold*. If adding 10,000 raw samples moves embedding spread by 0.001, you are wasting generation budget — change the prompts to explore new regions, or accept that the generator has given you what it can and move on.

## Choosing a method: the tradeoff matrix

No method dominates. Each family wins some columns and pays for it in others, and picking the right one is a matter of matching the method to what you actually need.

![Synthetic data methods versus what they buy you: each dominates on some properties while paying for it on diversity or cost](/imgs/blogs/synthetic-data-generation-5.webp)

The matrix says it plainly. **Human web** is cheap and diverse but of mixed quality and finite (the data wall). **Distillation** scales well but is capped by the teacher and carries API cost and contamination risk. **Self-Instruct** is cheap and very scalable but noisy and bounded by its seeds. **Textbook synthesis** produces high-quality, high-signal prose but is expensive and dangerously narrow if you do not vary style. **Web rephrasing** is the balanced pick — high quality, preserves source diversity, scales — at the cost of one inference pass per document. **Verifier-filtered** generation gives you correctness guarantees but is compute-heavy and limited to domains with a checker.

The practical recipe most strong pipelines converge on is a *blend*: rephrased and quality-filtered web as the diverse pretraining base, textbook synthesis to patch specific capability gaps, Self-Instruct and Evol-Instruct for instruction diversity, verifier-filtering for the domains where correctness is checkable (code, math), and — non-negotiably — a fixed anchor of real human data throughout to hold off collapse. No single family; a portfolio.

## Case studies

### 1. phi and Cosmopedia — textbooks over volume

Microsoft's phi series is the proof of concept that synthetic quality can beat web quantity. *"Textbooks Are All You Need"* trained phi-1, a 1.3-billion-parameter code model, on a mixture of filtered web code and synthetically generated textbook-style content — a corpus deliberately curated for pedagogical clarity rather than assembled for size. phi-1 reached code-generation accuracy competitive with models trained on an order of magnitude more tokens. The follow-ups extended the recipe to general reasoning with the same thesis: a small model trained on high-signal synthetic data can punch far above its parameter count. The **open recreation, Cosmopedia**, made the recipe reproducible — roughly 25 billion tokens of synthetic textbooks, stories, and posts generated across tens of thousands of seed topics, with explicit style and audience variation to preserve diversity. The lesson that generalizes: curation beats crawling when you can afford to generate, and the diversity discipline (many styles, many audiences) is what keeps the curated corpus from collapsing into a monoculture. The risk the phi team lived with — confidently-wrong synthetic facts — is precisely why the recipe works best in code, where execution catches errors.

### 2. Nemotron-4 340B — synthetic alignment data at scale

NVIDIA's Nemotron-4 340B is the industrial statement that alignment data can be *overwhelmingly synthetic*. The team reported that more than 98% of the data used to align the model was synthetically generated, produced by a purpose-built pipeline of a base generator and a reward model that scores and filters candidates. The design is a direct application of everything above: generate instructions and responses at scale, then use a reward model as the *verifier* that keeps the high-quality samples and discards the rest — verifier-filtering with a learned checker instead of a compiler. NVIDIA even released the generator and reward model so others could run the pipeline. The full breakdown is in the dedicated post on [Nemotron-4 340B's synthetic-data alignment](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/nemotron-4-340b-synthetic-data-alignment). The takeaway for a practitioner: at scale, the reward model is the linchpin — the quality of your synthetic alignment data is capped by the quality of the filter, not the generator, which is why so much effort goes into the reward model rather than the prompts.

### 3. DeepSeek-Prover — a proof checker as ground truth

DeepSeek-Prover is the cleanest illustration of verifier-filtering with a *formal* checker. Formal theorem proving has almost no training data — human-written Lean proofs number in the thousands, nowhere near enough to train on. So the team generated candidate proofs with a model, ran each through the **Lean proof checker**, and kept only the proofs Lean mechanically verified as correct. Those verified proofs became training data, the model improved, and the loop repeated — a synthetic-data flywheel where correctness is guaranteed by a piece of software outside the model. This is collapse-resistant *by construction*: because Lean is the ground truth, the data never drifts toward the generator's biases; a proof either checks or it does not. The result was a dramatic improvement in formal proving from almost no human data. The mechanism, including the reinforcement-learning search that makes generation efficient, is in the post on [DeepSeek-Prover's RMaxTS loop](/blog/machine-learning/large-language-model/deepseek-prover-synthetic-data-rmaxts). The general lesson: where a formal verifier exists, verifier-filtered generation is not just an option — it is close to a free lunch, because the verifier manufactures ground truth from an unreliable generator.

### 4. Alpaca — the cautionary distillation success

Stanford's Alpaca deserves a place precisely because it is *both* a success and a warning. Fifty-two thousand instructions generated by Self-Instruct from a strong teacher, used to fine-tune a base model into a usable assistant for a few hundred dollars — a genuinely landmark demonstration that instruction-following could be bootstrapped cheaply. But Alpaca also exposed the ceiling of naive distillation: the student imitated the teacher's *style* — confident, well-formatted responses — far better than its *substance*, and it inherited the teacher's factual errors and hallucination patterns wholesale. It taught the field a durable lesson: distillation transfers surface behavior readily and deep capability grudgingly, and a fluent-sounding student is not a knowledgeable one. That gap is why on-policy methods and verifier-filtering matter, and why "it sounds like the teacher" is not the same as "it is as good as the teacher."

## Troubleshooting: symptom to cause to fix

Synthetic-data pipelines fail in characteristic ways, and the failures are sneaky because the data *looks* fine — it is fluent, well-formatted, and voluminous. Here is the diagnostic table for the four failures you will actually hit.

![The teacher-contamination path: a teacher that memorized a benchmark launders those answers into synthetic data and inflates the student](/imgs/blogs/synthetic-data-generation-6.webp)

The figure traces the nastiest of the four — teacher contamination — because it is the one that silently corrupts your evaluation, the one instrument you trust to tell you whether anything is working.

### Symptom: eval scores jump, but real-world quality does not

**Cause — teacher contamination.** Trace the path in the figure. Benchmark test items (MMLU, GSM8K) are scraped into the teacher's pretraining data, so the teacher has *memorized* some test answers. When you distill — generate synthetic data with that teacher — those memorized answers ride into your synthetic examples. The student trains on them, and now the student scores high on the *same benchmark* not because it is capable but because it saw the answers laundered through synthetic data. The inflated score is a lie; a clean held-out eval reveals the true, lower number.

**Detection.** Run [decontamination](/blog/machine-learning/training-data/decontamination-and-benchmark-leakage) against your synthetic data, not just your web data — n-gram overlap between synthetic examples and benchmark test sets. If synthetic examples overlap test items, you have a leak. The decisive test: evaluate on a *freshly created* held-out set the teacher could not have seen (a benchmark released after the teacher's training cutoff). If the gap between the public benchmark and the fresh set is large, contamination is inflating the public number.

**Fix.** Decontaminate the synthetic data the same way you decontaminate crawled data. Use a teacher whose training data you can vouch for, or one from before the benchmark existed. And always keep a private, freshly-generated evaluation set that no teacher has seen — treat any public benchmark score from a distilled model with suspicion until it is confirmed on clean data.

### Symptom: the model becomes bland, repetitive, low-variance

**Cause — mode or diversity collapse.** You trained too heavily on synthetic data (possibly across several implicit generations), the tails thinned, and the model now produces only high-probability, on-distribution outputs. It has forgotten the rare modes.

**Detection.** Track distinct-n, self-BLEU, and embedding spread on your *generated outputs* over training, exactly as in the worked scenario. Falling diversity metrics while token count rises is the signature. A qualitative tell: ask the model for ten variations of something and count how many are genuinely different.

**Fix.** Re-anchor to real human data — increase its fraction in the mix. Stop training on the model's own recent outputs. Audit your quality filter to confirm it is not rejecting unusual-but-valid samples (a filter tuned for typicality accelerates collapse). If you distilled from a distilled model, go back to a teacher closer to real data.

### Symptom: the model confidently states plausible falsehoods it never saw in real data

**Cause — hallucinated synthetic facts.** A generator (especially in textbook synthesis) produced fluent, authoritative, and *wrong* content, and because the prose was clean and confident, the model learned the errors well. This is the flip side of textbook synthesis's strength.

**Detection.** Fact-check a sample of synthetic documents against a trusted source. For factual domains, run generated claims through a retrieval-and-verification step and measure the error rate. A spike in specific, confident errors that do not appear in your real data points here.

**Fix.** Prefer *grounded* generation: rephrasing (facts come from the real source document) over free synthesis (facts come from the model's imagination). Where you must synthesize facts, add a verification pass — retrieve supporting evidence and drop unsupported claims. For factual pretraining data, weight rephrased-web over textbook-synthesis, and reserve textbook synthesis for skills you can verify (code, math) rather than facts you cannot.

### Symptom: quality improves for a while, then quietly degrades over successive data-generation rounds

**Cause — over-reliance leading to collapse.** You built a flywheel — generate, train, generate — without a real-data anchor, and after a few rounds the compounding narrowing (undersampled tails plus accumulated error) tipped from helpful into harmful. The early rounds improved because the synthetic data added coverage; the later rounds degraded because the distribution collapsed.

**Detection.** Plot your diversity metrics *per generation*. A curve that rises then falls is the collapse signature. Also watch validation perplexity on a fixed *real* held-out set — if it starts climbing while training loss keeps falling, the model is fitting a narrowing synthetic distribution and drifting from reality.

**Fix.** Cap the number of self-generation rounds and keep a fixed fraction of original real data in every round — the single most robust defense. Prefer verifier-filtered and rephrasing methods that anchor to external ground truth over free self-generation. And treat "train on our own outputs indefinitely" as the anti-pattern it is: the loop needs a tether to reality, or it drifts.

> The recurring lesson across all four: synthetic data fails *silently*. It stays fluent and voluminous while its diversity collapses, its facts drift, and its eval scores lie. The only defense is measurement — diversity metrics, clean held-out evals, and decontamination — because your eyes will tell you the data looks fine right up until the model ships broken.

## When to reach for synthetic data, and when not to

**Reach for synthetic data when:**

- You have hit the **data wall** for a capability — there simply is not enough real data of the kind you need (formal proofs, clean instruction-following, step-by-step math), and you can generate more.
- You have a **cheap, reliable verifier** — a compiler, a test suite, a proof checker, a strong reward model — so verifier-filtering can manufacture ground truth from an unreliable generator.
- You need to **clean or reshape data you already have**, where rephrasing preserves real information while raising signal per token.
- You need **targeted capability injection** — a specific skill or format the web does not cover well — and you can vary style and audience enough to keep the synthetic data diverse.
- You have a stronger model to **distill from**, you can vouch for its training data (no benchmark contamination), and you accept that the student inherits the teacher's ceiling.

**Skip or minimize synthetic data when:**

- You **cannot verify or ground it** and the domain is factual — free synthesis of facts you cannot check is a hallucination machine that teaches the model confident errors.
- You would be training on **your own model's outputs across multiple generations with no real-data anchor** — that is the collapse recipe; do not build a flywheel without a tether to reality.
- Abundant, diverse **real data already exists** and is cheaper to obtain than to generate — do not manufacture what you can crawl or license.
- Your **evaluation cannot detect the failure modes** — if you have no clean held-out set and no diversity metrics, you cannot tell good synthetic data from a collapsing distribution, and you will ship the collapse.
- The teacher's **terms of service forbid** training on its outputs, and you have no in-house model to distill from.

Synthetic data is the most powerful lever in the modern data pipeline and the easiest to pull too hard. Used with a verifier, a real-data anchor, and a diversity audit, it lets you manufacture exactly the distribution you wish the web contained. Used carelessly, it manufactures a fluent, confident, collapsing caricature of the data you started with — and it will look fine until it does not. The discipline is not in the generating; it is in the measuring.
