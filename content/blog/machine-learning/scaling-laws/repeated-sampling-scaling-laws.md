There is a moment in every scaling-laws discussion where someone says "we should just train a bigger model," and for years that was the only lever anyone reached for. Pre-training scaling laws - [Kaplan](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/kaplan-scaling-laws-language-models), [Chinchilla](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/chinchilla-compute-optimal-scaling), [the inference-aware correction](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/inference-aware-scaling-laws) - all answer the same question: where do I spend training compute to get the lowest loss or the lowest serving cost? But there is a second lever, and it lives entirely at inference time. Take a model you already have. Ask it the same question not once but a thousand times, with temperature turned up so the answers differ. Most of those thousand attempts will be wrong. The bet of repeated sampling is that *at least one* of them is right - and that if you can tell which one, you have a far stronger system than the single-attempt number on the model card would suggest. The diagram below is the mental model for the whole post: one cheap model, fanned out into many independent attempts, filtered by a verifier into rising coverage.

![A flow diagram showing one cheap model fanning out into many samples that a verifier filters into rising coverage while a no-verifier path collapses into a selection plateau](/imgs/blogs/repeated-sampling-scaling-laws-1.png)

This is the argument from Brown et al. 2024, "Large Language Monkeys: Scaling Inference Compute with Repeated Sampling" (arXiv:2407.21787), out of Stanford, Oxford, and Google DeepMind. The title is a wink at the infinite-monkey theorem - given enough random attempts, even a weak generator stumbles onto the right answer - but the paper makes the analogy precise and useful. It defines a single metric, **coverage**, equal to the fraction of problems for which at least one of $k$ samples is correct, which is exactly $\text{pass@}k$. It then shows that coverage grows with $k$ as a clean, near-straight line on a log-log plot, over roughly four orders of magnitude of samples, across model families, sizes, and benchmarks. And it draws the one conclusion that turns this from a curiosity into a scaling law: in any domain where you can *automatically verify* a candidate answer - code with unit tests, math with an exact-match grader, formal proofs with a checker - repeated sampling from a small, cheap model is a real, cost-effective axis of compute scaling, often beating a single call to a much larger model at the same total cost.

> [!important] The one number to remember: 15.9% -> 56%
> - **Coverage is pass@k**: the fraction of problems where at least one of $k$ independent samples is correct. It rises with $k$ as an *exponentiated power law*: $\log(\text{coverage}) \approx a \cdot k^{-b}$, equivalently $\text{coverage} \approx \exp(a \cdot k^{-b})$, which plots as a near-straight line over about four orders of magnitude of $k$.
> - **The headline result**: on SWE-bench Lite, DeepSeek-Coder-V2-Instruct goes from **15.9% with a single sample to 56% with 250 samples**, blowing past the 43% single-sample state of the art at the time. Same model, no retraining, just more attempts plus the test suite as the verifier.
> - **It works for tiny models too**: Llama-3-8B-Instruct on GSM8K climbs from **13.3% pass@1 to 95.3% pass@10,000**; on MATH from 26.6% to 79.8%. Even Pythia-160M on MATH goes from **0.27% to roughly 57%** over 10,000 samples.
> - **FLOPs-matched, small beats big**: spend the same inference compute on many Llama-3-8B samples and you beat a single Llama-3-70B pass on MiniF2F, GSM8K, and MATH. The cheap-model-plus-sampling system is the better use of a fixed FLOP budget.
> - **Cheaper in dollars too**: on SWE-bench Lite, five samples from DeepSeek-Coder-V2 cost about **\$0.04 per issue at 29.6% solved**, versus a single GPT-4o call at about **\$0.13 per issue at 24% solved** - roughly 3.25x more cost-effective.
> - **The wall**: coverage only converts into *solved* problems when you have a verifier. Without one, majority voting and reward-model selection **plateau after a few hundred samples**. The bottleneck shifts from generation to verification, and in open-ended domains that wall is where the scaling stops.

## Why this is a different kind of scaling law

The cleanest way to feel why repeated sampling deserves to sit alongside the pre-training laws is to notice that it measures a different quantity than everything before it. Pre-training scaling laws measure *loss* - the negative log-likelihood the model assigns to held-out text - as a function of parameters, data, and compute. Loss is a property of a single forward pass: feed in the context, read out the distribution, score the next token. Repeated sampling measures *coverage* - whether the correct answer appears anywhere in a set of $k$ attempts - which is a property of a *population* of forward passes. Those are not the same axis, and they do not have to obey the same law.

| Question | Pre-training laws | Repeated-sampling law |
|---|---|---|
| What is measured? | Loss (nats/token) on held-out text | Coverage = pass@k = fraction solved by >=1 of k tries |
| What is the free variable? | Parameters $N$, tokens $D$, compute $C$ | Number of samples $k$ at fixed model |
| When is the compute spent? | Once, at training time | Every time you serve a query |
| What shape on log-log axes? | Straight line: $L \propto C^{-\alpha}$ | Near-straight: $\log(\text{coverage}) \approx a k^{-b}$ |
| What does "better" require? | A better-trained model | A verifier to pick the winning sample |
| Who pays? | The training cluster | The inference fleet, per request |

Look at the last two rows, because they are where this law earns its asterisk. A pre-training law improves the model itself: a lower-loss model is better for everyone, on every query, forever, with no extra work at serving time. The repeated-sampling law does not improve the model at all. It improves the *system* you build around the model, and only if that system can recognize a correct answer when it sees one. Coverage is a statement about what the model *could* do across many tries; turning that potential into a delivered result needs a selection step. When the selection step is a verifier that is always right - a compiler, a test harness, a proof checker - the full coverage curve is yours. When the selection step is fallible - a reward model, a majority vote - you get only part of it, and the part you get stops growing long before coverage does.

> If you take one thing from this post: repeated sampling does not make the model smarter. It makes the *model-plus-verifier system* dramatically stronger, and the verifier is load-bearing. Pull it out and the law collapses to a plateau.

This is why the paper's framing as a "scaling law" is both fair and carefully hedged. It is a genuine power law - the coverage curve is as clean and extrapolable as any loss curve in the [foundations literature](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/scaling-laws-predictability-foundations), which means you can fit it on cheap small-$k$ runs and forecast the payoff of a large sampling budget before you spend it. But unlike a loss law, its practical value is gated by a property of the *domain* (is there a verifier?) rather than a property of the *model*. That gating is the single most important caveat in the entire test-time-compute literature, and we will keep returning to it.

There is a second, subtler way this law differs from the pre-training laws, and it concerns *who controls the exponent*. In pre-training, the exponents $\alpha$ and $\beta$ on parameters and data are roughly architecture-invariant - they are properties of the data distribution and the learning problem, and changing the model mostly shifts the offset, not the slope, as the [foundations work](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/scaling-laws-predictability-foundations) established. In repeated sampling, the exponent $b$ on $k$ is a property of the *interaction* between a specific model and a specific benchmark - it encodes how the model's per-problem success probabilities $p_i$ are distributed across that benchmark. A different model or a different benchmark gives a different $b$. This means you cannot borrow a coverage exponent from one setting and apply it to another the way you might reuse a pre-training exponent; you must fit $a$ and $b$ for each model-benchmark pair you care about. The good news is that fitting is cheap (a few dozen samples on a validation set), and once fitted, the extrapolation is reliable - so the workflow is "fit per setting, then extrapolate freely within that setting."

### Where this sits in the test-time-compute family

Repeated sampling is the simplest member of a family of techniques that all spend compute at inference rather than training. It is worth placing it precisely. The crudest version - draw $k$ independent samples and check each - is what this paper studies and what scales as the coverage power law. More elaborate cousins add structure: best-of-$N$ with a learned verifier picks the single best sample by score; beam search and lookahead search use a process reward model to prune partial solutions; sequential revision trains the model to edit its own answer over several rounds. Those richer methods are the subject of [compute-optimal test-time scaling](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/test-time-compute-scaling), and the long-chain-of-thought reasoning models that learn to spend test-time compute through reinforcement learning are covered in [reasoning models and RL test-time scaling](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/reasoning-models-rl-test-time-scaling). Repeated sampling is the floor of that hierarchy: no search, no revision, no learned selection - just independent draws plus a yes/no verifier. Its value is that the floor is already remarkably high, and that its scaling behavior is the cleanest of the bunch, which makes it the right place to build intuition before the more complicated methods.

## 1. Coverage, pass@k, and the exponentiated power law

**Senior rule of thumb: coverage is what the model can do across many tries; accuracy is what it does on one. The whole field of test-time scaling lives in the gap between them.**

Let us define the metric carefully, because the rest of the post hangs on it. Fix a problem $i$ and draw $k$ independent samples from the model at some temperature $T > 0$. Let $c_i$ be the number of those $k$ samples that are correct (according to the verifier). The per-problem pass@k is the probability that at least one of the $k$ is correct. If $p_i$ is the model's per-sample success probability on problem $i$, then assuming independence,

$$\text{pass@}k_i = 1 - (1 - p_i)^k.$$

Coverage over a benchmark is the average of this over all problems: $\text{coverage}(k) = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{D}|}\sum_i \big[1 - (1-p_i)^k\big]$. (In practice you estimate it from a larger pool of $n \geq k$ samples using the unbiased estimator $1 - \binom{n - c_i}{k} / \binom{n}{k}$, which is the form the Codex/HumanEval paper introduced and that everyone now uses; sampling $k$ fresh draws for every $k$ would be wasteful.) The single-problem formula already tells you the qualitative story: for any problem the model can solve with nonzero probability, pass@k climbs toward 1 as $k$ grows. A problem with $p_i = 0.001$ - one success in a thousand tries - still reaches about 63% pass@k by $k = 1000$ and about 99.99% by $k = 10{,}000$. That is the monkey theorem made quantitative: tiny per-sample odds, compounded over enough samples, become near-certainty.

The figure below is the centerpiece of the paper and of this post: coverage plotted against the number of samples $k$, with $k$ on a log axis spanning $1$ to $10{,}000$, and the curve climbing as a smooth, near-straight line.

![A log-log plot of coverage against number of samples k showing a near-straight rising line from pass-at-1 of 13.3 percent to pass-at-10k of 95.3 percent with the exponentiated power law annotated](/imgs/blogs/repeated-sampling-scaling-laws-2.png)

The empirical finding is that coverage as a function of $k$ is well described by an **exponentiated power law**:

$$\log(\text{coverage}) \approx a \cdot k^{-b}, \qquad \text{equivalently} \qquad \text{coverage} \approx \exp\!\big(a \cdot k^{-b}\big),$$

with $a < 0$ and $b > 0$ fitted per model-benchmark pair. Read it as two nested transformations. First, taking the log of coverage and the log of $k$ turns the relationship into a straight line over a wide range - the defining property of any power law, and the reason log-log axes are the universal language of scaling. Second, the *exponentiated* part says the quantity that behaves as a power law is not coverage itself but its logarithm: coverage saturates at 1, so a plain power law (which would shoot past 1) cannot be right, and exponentiating a negative power keeps the curve bounded below 1 while preserving the straight-line behavior across orders of magnitude of $k$. The practical upshot is the same as for any scaling law: fit $a$ and $b$ on cheap runs - say $k$ up to 100 - and you can extrapolate the coverage you would get at $k = 10{,}000$ without paying for it first.

It is worth dwelling on what "four orders of magnitude" buys you, because it is what separates this from a marketing curve. The relationship holds from $k = 1$ to $k = 10{,}000$, which is $10^4$. Across that range the curve does not bend, break, or flatten prematurely; it keeps climbing along the fitted line. That is the empirical content of the claim that this is a *law* and not just a trend: the same two constants describe the model's behavior whether you draw ten samples or ten thousand. A trend that held for one decade of $k$ and then collapsed would be useless for budgeting; one that holds for four decades lets you make real decisions about how much to spend.

### Why this functional form and not another

It is reasonable to ask why coverage follows *this* form - exponentiated power law - rather than a plain power law, a logistic, or a simple logarithm. The constraint that pins it down is that coverage is a probability bounded in $[0, 1]$, and it approaches its ceiling from below as $k$ grows. A plain power law $\text{coverage} = a k^{-b}$ is unbounded and decreasing, which is backwards. A logarithm $\text{coverage} = a + b \log k$ is unbounded above and would eventually exceed 1. The exponentiated power law $\text{coverage} = \exp(a k^{-b})$ with $a < 0$ has exactly the right shape: at $k = 1$ it equals $\exp(a)$, a value strictly between 0 and 1; as $k \to \infty$ the exponent $a k^{-b} \to 0$ and coverage $\to \exp(0) = 1$ (or toward the relevant ceiling once you account for $p_i = 0$ problems). And critically, $\log(\text{coverage}) = a k^{-b}$ is itself a power law in $k$, so plotting $\log(\text{coverage})$ against $k$ on log-log axes gives the straight line. The form is not arbitrary - it is the simplest bounded function whose log is a power law, which is what the data demands. This is the same kind of reasoning that justifies the [Chinchilla parametric loss form](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/chinchilla-compute-optimal-scaling): you pick the functional family that respects the quantity's bounds and asymptotes, then fit its constants, rather than fitting an unconstrained polynomial that violates the physics at the extremes.

### Why independence (mostly) holds, and where it breaks

The clean power law leans on the samples being roughly independent, which deserves scrutiny. The samples are drawn from the *same* model on the *same* prompt, so they are not independent in the strong sense - they share a prior, and a model with a confidently wrong belief will repeat that wrong belief across many draws. The independence that matters here is conditional: given that the model has *some* probability mass on a correct trajectory, the draws explore that mass roughly independently, so the chance that none of $k$ draws lands on a correct answer falls off like $(1 - p_i)^k$. Where this breaks is the problems with $p_i = 0$ exactly - problems the model cannot solve at any temperature, because it has zero probability mass on any correct answer. Those problems never get solved no matter how large $k$ is, and they set the asymptotic ceiling of the coverage curve below 100%. The shape of the curve, then, is governed by the *distribution* of per-problem $p_i$ across the benchmark: a few easy problems ($p_i$ near 1) are solved immediately, a long tail of hard-but-nonzero problems ($p_i$ tiny) get picked off slowly as $k$ grows, and the truly impossible problems ($p_i = 0$) are never solved and cap the ceiling. This is also why benchmarks with a sharper difficulty spread produce cleaner power laws: the gradual harvesting of the long tail is exactly what makes log-log linear.

### Temperature is a real knob

Because the whole method depends on the samples *differing*, temperature is not a detail. At $T = 0$ (greedy decoding) every sample is identical, so pass@k equals pass@1 for all $k$ - the curve is flat and repeated sampling buys nothing. As you raise $T$, the samples diversify, the effective per-sample $p_i$ spreads, and coverage at large $k$ rises because you are exploring more of the model's distribution. Push $T$ too high and the samples become incoherent, $p_i$ collapses toward zero, and even large $k$ cannot recover. The paper finds, sensibly, that the optimal temperature for *coverage* is higher than the optimal temperature for single-sample accuracy: when you only get one shot you want the mode, but when you get ten thousand shots you want diversity. This is a recurring theme - the settings that maximize pass@1 are not the settings that maximize pass@k, and tuning for the wrong one leaves coverage on the table.

There is a clean way to see why the optimal temperature shifts upward. Single-sample accuracy is maximized when the probability of a correct answer on one draw, $p_i$, is highest, which usually means decoding near the mode (low temperature) so the model commits to its single most-confident trajectory. Coverage over $k$ draws is governed instead by $1 - (1 - p_i)^k$, and for large $k$ this is dominated not by the height of $p_i$ but by whether $p_i$ is *nonzero at all* and how much *distinct* probability mass the sampler can reach. A higher temperature trades a lower per-draw $p_i$ for a wider exploration of the distribution, which raises the chance that some draw lands on a correct trajectory that the low-temperature mode would never visit. The crossover point - where higher temperature stops helping coverage and starts hurting it by producing incoherent samples - depends on $k$: the larger your sampling budget, the higher the temperature you can afford, because you have more draws to absorb the lower hit rate per draw. In practice this means temperature should be a function of your sample budget, not a fixed hyperparameter copied from the pass@1 config.

### A worked single-problem trace

To cement the mechanics, trace one problem end to end. Suppose the model's per-sample success probability on some MATH problem is $p_i = 0.01$ - one correct solution per hundred attempts. Then pass@1 = 0.01, pass@10 = $1 - 0.99^{10} = 0.096$, pass@100 = $1 - 0.99^{100} = 0.634$, pass@1000 = $1 - 0.99^{1000} = 0.99996$. The progression is the whole story in miniature: a problem that looks 1% solved on a single sample is essentially always solved by a thousand samples, *provided a verifier can pick the one correct attempt out of the roughly ten correct ones among the thousand*. Now suppose a second problem has $p_i = 0$ - the model has no probability mass on any correct trajectory. Its pass@k is $0$ for every $k$; no amount of sampling touches it. Average these two problems and you already have the shape of a coverage curve: a piece that climbs steeply with $k$ (the solvable-but-unreliable problem) and a piece that stays flat at zero (the impossible one), summing to a curve that rises and then asymptotes below 100%. Scale this up to thousands of problems with a spread of $p_i$ values and you get the smooth, log-log-linear coverage curve the paper reports - it is the superposition of thousands of individual $1 - (1-p_i)^k$ saturation curves, each climbing on its own schedule set by its $p_i$.

## 2. The headline results: from single digits to near-certainty

**Senior rule of thumb: a model's pass@1 tells you what it knows reliably; its pass@10k tells you what it knows at all. The two can differ by 50 percentage points.**

The numbers are the argument, so let us put them side by side. The table below is the core empirical claim of the paper: across a 160-million-parameter model and an 8-billion-parameter model, across grade-school math, competition math, and real software engineering, repeated sampling turns single-digit or low-double-digit single-sample accuracy into majority-or-better coverage.

![A matrix table of coverage gains across four model and benchmark pairs showing pass-at-1, the number of samples, coverage at k, and the lift factor for each](/imgs/blogs/repeated-sampling-scaling-laws-4.png)

Walk the rows. **Llama-3-8B-Instruct on GSM8K** - the grade-school math benchmark - starts at 13.3% pass@1 and reaches 95.3% pass@10,000. That is a 7.2x lift, and it means that for over 95% of grade-school math problems, an 8-billion-parameter model has at least one correct solution somewhere in ten thousand attempts. The model "knows" how to solve almost all of these problems in the sense that a correct trajectory exists in its distribution; it just does not reliably produce one on the first try. **Llama-3-8B-Instruct on MATH** - the harder competition-math benchmark - goes from 26.6% to 79.8% pass@10,000, a 3.0x lift; the smaller multiple reflects that MATH has more truly-hard problems where $p_i$ is either tiny or zero. **Pythia-160M on MATH** is the most striking entry: a 160-million-parameter model, two orders of magnitude smaller than the Llamas and not even instruction-tuned in the modern sense, has a pass@1 of 0.27% - essentially zero, one success in 370 tries - yet reaches roughly 57% coverage over 10,000 samples. A model that looks useless on a single-sample benchmark turns out to have nonzero probability mass on correct solutions for more than half the problems. And **DeepSeek-Coder-V2-Instruct on SWE-bench** goes from 15.9% to 56% over just 250 samples, which we will dwell on next because it is the result that made the field pay attention.

> The Pythia-160M number is the one that should reframe how you read a model card. A 0.27% pass@1 looks like a model that cannot do math. A 57% pass@10k says it can do half of competition math - it simply needs ten thousand tries and a grader. The single-sample number was measuring reliability, not capability.

### SWE-bench Lite: the result that mattered

SWE-bench is the benchmark that turned repeated sampling from an academic observation into an engineering strategy, because it is *real*. Each problem is an actual GitHub issue from a popular Python repository, and a candidate solution is a patch; the patch is "correct" if and only if it makes the repository's hidden test suite pass. There is no partial credit, no LLM judge, no human grading - the test suite is a perfect, automatic verifier. That is the ideal habitat for repeated sampling, and the figure below shows what happens.

![A before and after comparison showing DeepSeek-Coder-V2 at 15.9 percent with a single sample on the left and 56 percent with 250 samples on the right, beating the 43 percent single-shot state of the art](/imgs/blogs/repeated-sampling-scaling-laws-3.png)

With a single sample, DeepSeek-Coder-V2-Instruct solves 15.9% of SWE-bench Lite issues. That is not a competitive number; the best single-attempt systems at the time scored around 43% by combining strong models with elaborate agent scaffolding. But draw 250 samples per issue, run each candidate patch against the test suite, and keep any issue for which at least one patch passes, and coverage rises to **56%** - thirteen points above the 43% single-shot state of the art, achieved with a weaker base model and no agent framework, just more attempts and the test harness doing the selection. The verifier here is doing something profound: it converts the model's ability to *eventually* produce a working patch (across 250 tries) into a delivered solution, because it can recognize a working patch the instant it sees one. The model never got better. The system got better, because the system can now exploit the long tail of the model's distribution.

This is the result that should change your mental model of "what a model can do on SWE-bench." The single-sample 15.9% measures how often the model nails it first try. The 56% coverage measures how often a correct patch exists within a budget you can actually afford. For a domain with a perfect verifier, the second number is the one that matters for what you can ship, and it is wildly higher than the first.

### The asymptote is below 100%, and that is fine

A fair reading of these numbers notices that none of them reach 100%, even at 10,000 samples. GSM8K tops out around 95%, MATH around 80%, Pythia around 57%. Those ceilings are the $p_i = 0$ problems - the ones where the model has no probability mass on any correct trajectory and never will, no matter how many samples you draw. Repeated sampling cannot create capability that is not there; it can only harvest capability that exists but is unreliable. The gap between the coverage ceiling and 100% is therefore a real, model-level limitation: those are the problems where you genuinely need a better model, more knowledge, or a different approach. This is the honest boundary of the technique. It does not say "any model can solve anything with enough samples." It says "any problem the model can solve with nonzero probability gets solved with enough samples," and the fraction of such problems is the coverage ceiling.

### What the gains tell you about model size

There is a pattern across the four model-benchmark pairs worth naming, because it guides which model to reach for. The *lift factor* - the ratio of pass@k to pass@1 - is largest for the weakest starting points. Pythia-160M on MATH has a roughly 210x lift (0.27% to 57%); Llama-3-8B on GSM8K has a 7.2x lift (13.3% to 95.3%); Llama-3-8B on MATH has a 3.0x lift (26.6% to 79.8%). The weaker the single-sample accuracy, the more headroom repeated sampling unlocks in *relative* terms, because a model with a low but nonzero $p_i$ on many problems has an enormous reservoir of solvable-but-unreliable problems to harvest. But notice that the weaker model's *absolute* ceiling is also lower - Pythia tops out at 57% while Llama-8B reaches 95% on the easier benchmark - because the weaker model has more $p_i = 0$ problems. So the right reading is two-sided: a smaller model benefits more from sampling in relative terms but reaches a lower ceiling in absolute terms, and the model you choose should be the smallest one whose coverage ceiling clears your target. That is a very different selection criterion than "pick the model with the best pass@1," and it is the criterion that makes repeated sampling economical.

### Why GSM8K climbs higher than MATH

The contrast between Llama-3-8B's 95.3% on GSM8K and 79.8% on MATH is not an accident of fitting; it reflects the difficulty distribution of the two benchmarks. GSM8K is grade-school word problems - almost all of them are within an 8B model's reach if it gets enough tries, so the $p_i = 0$ set is small and the ceiling is high. MATH is competition mathematics, with a substantial fraction of problems requiring techniques or insights the 8B model simply does not have probability mass on, so the $p_i = 0$ set is larger and the ceiling is lower. The coverage curve's *ceiling* is a property of the benchmark's difficulty relative to the model, while the coverage curve's *slope* (the rate at which it climbs with $\log k$) is a property of how the solvable-but-unreliable mass is distributed. Two benchmarks can have the same slope but different ceilings, or the same ceiling but different slopes; reading both off the fitted curve tells you whether to spend on more samples (steep slope, far from ceiling) or on a better model (near the ceiling already).

## 3. FLOPs-matched: a small sampling model beats a big single-shot one

**Senior rule of thumb: when you can verify, the right question is not "how big a model" but "how should I split a fixed inference budget between model size and number of samples."**

The results so far show that repeated sampling raises coverage, but raising coverage by spending more compute is not interesting unless the spend is *efficient*. The sharp claim is that it is - that at a fixed inference-compute budget, you are better off drawing many samples from a small model than one sample from a large one. This is the inference-time analogue of the [Beyond-Chinchilla](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/inference-aware-scaling-laws) argument: there, accounting for inference cost pushed the optimum toward a smaller model trained on more tokens; here, accounting for the option to sample repeatedly pushes the optimum toward a smaller model queried more times. The figure below shows the crossover.

![Two coverage curves against total inference FLOPs on a log axis showing a small model with repeated sampling overtaking a larger model run once at equal compute](/imgs/blogs/repeated-sampling-scaling-laws-5.png)

The accounting is straightforward. Generating one token costs about $2N$ FLOPs, where $N$ is the parameter count (the same $2N$ that the [inference-aware post](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/inference-aware-scaling-laws) derives from one forward pass per token). So $k$ samples from an $N$-parameter model that each generate about $L$ tokens cost roughly $2 N k L$ FLOPs. A single sample from a model that is, say, 8.75x larger - the Llama-3-70B to Llama-3-8B ratio - costs $2 (8.75 N) L$ for one sample. At equal FLOPs, the small model gets about 8.75x as many samples as the big model gets... well, the big model gets one, so the small model gets roughly nine samples for the same price as the big model's single shot, and far more if you are willing to spend more. The paper's finding is that across MiniF2F (formal math), GSM8K, and MATH, the small-model-with-many-samples curve crosses above the big-model-single-sample point at equal FLOPs. Llama-3-8B with repeated sampling beats Llama-3-70B run once, FLOP for FLOP, in these verifiable domains.

The intuition for why this works is worth making explicit, right alongside the figure: a bigger model raises every problem's per-sample success probability $p_i$ by some amount, which helps, but it helps multiplicatively and uniformly. Repeated sampling, by contrast, attacks the *long tail* directly - every additional decade of $k$ harvests the next layer of low-$p_i$ problems - and for problems where $p_i$ is small but nonzero, throwing samples at them is a more compute-efficient way to push pass@k toward 1 than paying for a uniformly-better model on every query. The bigger model wins on the problems it newly makes solvable (turns $p_i = 0$ into $p_i > 0$); the sampling strategy wins on the much larger set of problems that are already solvable-but-unreliable. In verifiable domains, the second set dominates, and so sampling wins the FLOP comparison.

### A worked FLOP comparison

Put numbers on it. Take Llama-3-8B ($N = 8 \times 10^9$) and Llama-3-70B ($N = 70 \times 10^9$), a parameter ratio of 8.75. Each sample generates about $L = 1000$ tokens, and a forward pass costs $2N$ FLOPs per token. One 70B sample costs $2 \times 70\times 10^9 \times 1000 = 1.4 \times 10^{14}$ FLOPs. One 8B sample costs $2 \times 8 \times 10^9 \times 1000 = 1.6 \times 10^{13}$ FLOPs. So at the FLOP budget of a single 70B sample you can draw $1.4 \times 10^{14} / 1.6 \times 10^{13} \approx 8.75$ samples from the 8B model - the same as the parameter ratio, which is no coincidence: at fixed token length, samples-per-FLOP is inversely proportional to parameter count. The question is then purely empirical: does pass@9 of the 8B model exceed pass@1 of the 70B model in the verifiable domain? The paper's answer on MiniF2F, GSM8K, and MATH is yes - the eight or nine extra 8B samples harvest enough of the long tail to clear the 70B single-shot number. And the comparison only improves as you spend more: at ten times the budget you get ninety 8B samples versus ten 70B samples, and the coverage gap widens because the 8B model's curve is still climbing while the 70B model's pass@10 has its own (lower) ceiling problems.

The deeper point is that this comparison is a *choice of axis under a budget constraint*, exactly like the pre-training compute-allocation problem. There, you split a training budget between parameters and tokens. Here, you split an inference budget between parameters (model size) and samples (number of draws). The pre-training laws found the optimal split is roughly equal scaling; the repeated-sampling result finds that in verifiable domains the inference-budget split tilts hard toward *more samples of a smaller model*, because samples buy coverage of the existing distribution more cheaply than parameters buy a uniformly better distribution. This is the inference-time mirror of [Beyond-Chinchilla](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/inference-aware-scaling-laws): both push toward smaller models, one by counting training-plus-inference cost, the other by counting the option to sample.

### Where the crossover does not happen

Honesty requires the boundary. The FLOPs-matched win is not universal; it holds in the regime the paper measured, and it has known edges. First, it requires a verifier - without one, the small model's extra samples produce coverage you cannot cash in, and the comparison reverses. Second, it favors problems where the small model already has nonzero $p_i$; on the hardest tail, where only the big model has any probability mass at all, sampling the small model is futile and the big model wins. Third, the comparison is in *FLOPs*, and FLOPs are not the only cost - memory bandwidth, latency, and the wall-clock cost of running a verifier 250 times all matter in production, and we return to them. The [compute-optimal test-time scaling](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/test-time-compute-scaling) work makes this boundary sharp: test-time compute substitutes for parameters in the easy-to-medium regime and at small budgets, but pre-training a bigger model still wins the hardest problems and the largest budgets. Repeated sampling is the easy-regime, verifiable-domain corner of that larger tradeoff surface - a corner that happens to be enormous and economically important, but a corner nonetheless.

## 4. The dollars: cheaper, not just FLOP-efficient

**Senior rule of thumb: FLOPs are the physicist's cost; dollars are the engineer's. They diverge, and you ship on dollars.**

A FLOP-matched comparison is satisfying but slightly academic, because nobody pays for FLOPs directly - you pay for API calls or GPU-hours, and the price per FLOP depends on hardware utilization, batching, and how the provider prices its tiers. The repeated-sampling paper makes the dollar case directly on SWE-bench Lite, and it is the most persuasive number for a practitioner.

Five samples from DeepSeek-Coder-V2-Instruct, with the test suite as the verifier, cost about **\$0.04 per issue and solve 29.6%** of SWE-bench Lite. A single call to GPT-4o - a far larger, far more expensive model - costs about **\$0.13 per issue and solves 24%**. So the cheap model with five samples is both *cheaper* (about a third of the cost per issue) and *more accurate* (29.6% versus 24%) than one call to the premium model. Combining the cost and the accuracy, the repeated-sampling system is roughly **3.25x more cost-effective**: you pay less and you solve more. That is not a marginal optimization; it is a different operating point, and it exists only because SWE-bench has a verifier that lets the five cheap samples be filtered down to the one that works.

| Strategy | Model | Samples | Cost / issue | Solved | Cost-effectiveness |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Single premium call | GPT-4o | 1 | ~\$0.13 | 24% | baseline |
| Repeated cheap sampling | DeepSeek-Coder-V2 | 5 | ~\$0.04 | 29.6% | ~3.25x better |
| Repeated cheap sampling | DeepSeek-Coder-V2 | 250 | higher | 56% | coverage ceiling for this budget |

The reason the dollar story is even stronger than the FLOP story is the same divergence that haunts the [inference-aware scaling law](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/inference-aware-scaling-laws): inference runs at far lower hardware utilization than training, and small models batch far more efficiently than large ones. When you draw 250 samples from one prompt, every sample shares the same prefill - the prompt is encoded once and the KV cache is reused across all 250 continuations - so the marginal cost of the $251$st sample is just its decode tokens, not a fresh prefill. Large models, by contrast, are memory-bandwidth-bound during decode and harder to batch, so their per-token dollar cost is disproportionately high. The economics compound in favor of "small model, shared prefill, many cheap continuations, one verifier pass each."

### The verifier has a cost too

The dollar accounting above quietly assumes the verifier is cheap, which is true for SWE-bench (running a test suite costs CPU-seconds, not GPU-dollars) but is not always true. If your verifier is itself an expensive model call - a strong LLM judge, say - then the cost of verifying 250 samples can dwarf the cost of generating them, and the whole economic argument inverts. The clean wins come when the verifier is cheap and reliable: a compiler, a unit-test harness, a formal proof checker, an exact-match grader against a known answer. Part of the engineering discipline of repeated sampling is keeping the verifier on the cheap-and-reliable side of that line, because the moment verification becomes the dominant cost or starts making mistakes, the curve you are climbing is no longer the coverage curve - it is the much shorter selection curve, which is the subject of the next section.

## 5. The wall: no verifier, no scaling

**Senior rule of thumb: coverage is free; selection is not. The entire value of repeated sampling is realized at the selection step, and the selection step is where open-ended domains hit a wall.**

Everything to this point assumed a verifier that is always right. That assumption is doing enormous work, and the moment you remove it, the scaling law changes character entirely. The figure below is the most important caveat in the paper, and arguably in the whole test-time-compute story: coverage keeps climbing as a power law, but the *fraction you can actually deliver* - using majority voting or a reward model to pick one answer - plateaus after a few hundred samples.

![Two curves against number of samples on a log axis where coverage with a verifier keeps rising while majority vote and reward-model selection flatten into a plateau](/imgs/blogs/repeated-sampling-scaling-laws-6.png)

Here is the mechanism, stated plainly. Coverage measures whether a correct answer *exists* in the sample set. To turn that into a delivered answer you must *select* one sample to return, and you do not get to peek at the ground truth. With a perfect verifier, selection is trivial: try each candidate, return the first one the verifier accepts, and you realize the full coverage. Without a verifier you must guess, and the two standard guessing strategies both saturate:

- **Majority voting** returns the most common answer among the $k$ samples. It works while the correct answer is the plurality, but as $k$ grows you do not keep finding *new* correct answers in a way that shifts the vote - you keep resampling from the same distribution, so the vote stabilizes. Once the model's most-frequent answer is fixed (whether right or wrong), more samples do not change it. Majority voting therefore plateaus at the accuracy of the model's modal answer, typically after a few hundred samples, far below the coverage ceiling.
- **Reward-model selection** scores each sample with a learned reward or verifier model and returns the highest-scoring one. It can do better than majority voting because it can recognize a correct answer that is not the plurality. But the reward model is imperfect: it has false positives (confidently scoring a wrong answer high) and false negatives, and as $k$ grows the chance that *some* wrong answer gets an undeservedly high score grows too. This is reward hacking by sampling - draw enough candidates and you will eventually find one that fools the reward model. So reward-model selection also plateaus, and at large $k$ can even *degrade*, because the adversarial pressure of many samples finds the reward model's blind spots.

The gap between the rising coverage curve and the flat selection curve is, in the paper's framing, the right answers you cannot pick out. The model produced a correct solution; your selection mechanism could not identify it. That gap is wasted compute, and it grows with $k$: every additional sample widens the coverage you cannot cash in.

> The verifier is not a convenience; it is the load-bearing assumption. In code and formal math the verifier is free and perfect, so repeated sampling is a clean scaling law. In open-ended generation - summaries, essays, advice, most chatbot turns - there is no cheap perfect verifier, so the curve you actually climb is the short, flat selection curve, and "draw more samples" stops helping after a few hundred.

### Why this reframes the entire research agenda

The selection plateau is why so much of the recent test-time-compute literature is really about *verification*, not generation. If coverage scales beautifully but selection is the bottleneck, then the highest-leverage research is on better verifiers and better selection: process reward models that score reasoning steps rather than final answers, learned verifiers trained specifically to be hard to fool, self-consistency methods that exploit structure in the answer space, and - the big one - reinforcement learning that trains the model to produce verifiable, self-checkable reasoning in the first place. The [reasoning-models post](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/reasoning-models-rl-test-time-scaling) tells that story: o1 and DeepSeek-R1 do not just sample more; they are trained, via RL on verifiable rewards, to generate long chains of thought that effectively verify themselves, which is one way to push the selection curve up toward the coverage curve. Repeated sampling defines the ceiling (coverage); the rest of the field is trying to reach it (selection). Seeing that decomposition cleanly is the main payoff of understanding this paper.

## 6. How to apply repeated sampling in practice

**Senior rule of thumb: check for a verifier first, fit the coverage curve on cheap samples second, and set your sample budget where marginal coverage equals marginal cost third.**

The theory converts into a short, concrete playbook. The pipeline below is the decision procedure I would follow to deploy repeated sampling on a real problem.

![A pipeline showing the steps to deploy repeated sampling check the verifier pick a cheap model fit the coverage law set the sample budget and ship while monitoring cost](/imgs/blogs/repeated-sampling-scaling-laws-7.png)

**Step 1: confirm you have a verifier.** This is the gate, not a step you can skip. Ask: can I automatically and cheaply decide whether a candidate answer is correct, with near-zero false positives? For code, yes - run the tests. For math with known answers, yes - exact-match the final result. For theorem proving, yes - run the proof checker. For SQL against a known schema, often yes - execute and compare. For "write me a good product description," no - and you should stop here and use a single call to the strongest model you can afford, because repeated sampling will hit the selection plateau. If your verifier is a strong-but-fallible model (an LLM judge), proceed with caution and expect a plateau lower than the coverage ceiling.

**Step 2: pick a cheap model.** The whole economic argument depends on the per-sample cost being low. Reach for the smallest model that has nonzero $p_i$ on your problems - often an 8B-class open model. You are not choosing the model that is best on one try; you are choosing the model with the best coverage-per-dollar, which is usually much smaller than your instinct says.

**Step 3: fit the coverage law on cheap samples.** Draw a modest number of samples - say $k$ up to 100 - on a validation set, compute coverage at several values of $k$, and fit $\log(\text{coverage}) \approx a k^{-b}$. This costs little and gives you the two constants that let you extrapolate. Now you can answer the budgeting question quantitatively: how much coverage do I get at $k = 1000$? At $k = 10{,}000$? The fit tells you before you spend.

**Step 4: set the sample budget at the economic margin.** With the fitted curve and your per-sample cost, find the $k$ where the marginal coverage gain from one more sample no longer justifies its cost. Because coverage is concave in $\log k$, there is always a point of diminishing returns; the fit locates it. For a high-value problem (a critical bug fix) you push $k$ high; for a cheap-to-be-wrong problem you stop early. This is the same marginal-analysis logic as any compute-allocation decision, applied to samples.

**Step 5: ship and monitor.** Deploy with shared-prefill batching so the 250 samples for one prompt reuse the encoded context. Monitor the realized coverage against your fit - if it drifts, refit. Monitor cost per solved problem, which is the number that justifies the whole approach (recall the ~3.25x SWE-bench win). And monitor verifier behavior: a verifier that starts producing false positives silently converts your coverage curve into a degrading selection curve.

### A worked budgeting example

Make it concrete with numbers. Suppose you fit Llama-3-8B on your code benchmark and find pass@1 = 0.20, and the coverage curve extrapolates to pass@100 = 0.55 and pass@1000 = 0.72. Each sample averages 800 generated tokens; at an 8B model and roughly \$0.20 per million output tokens on a cheap provider, one sample costs about $800 \times 0.20 / 10^6 \approx \$0.00016$, call it \$0.0002 with prefill amortized over the batch. Then:

- 100 samples/issue: ~\$0.02/issue, 55% coverage. Cost per *solved* issue: $0.02 / 0.55 \approx \$0.036$.
- 1000 samples/issue: ~\$0.20/issue, 72% coverage. Cost per solved issue: $0.20 / 0.72 \approx \$0.28$.

Going from 100 to 1000 samples raises coverage by 17 points but raises cost per solved issue by nearly 8x, because you are spending ten times the samples to harvest the increasingly rare long tail. Unless each additional solved issue is worth a great deal, the economic optimum is closer to 100 than 1000. That is the marginal analysis the fit enables, and it is why "just crank $k$ to the maximum" is usually wrong - the coverage curve keeps rising, but the *cost per delivered solution* turns sharply upward as you chase the tail.

```python
import math

# Fitted exponentiated power law: coverage(k) = exp(a * k**(-b))
# Fit a, b from cheap runs (k <= 100). Example values below are illustrative.
a, b = -1.61, 0.22          # log(coverage) = a * k^(-b),  a < 0, b > 0
cost_per_sample = 0.0002     # dollars, prefill amortized over the batch

def coverage(k: int) -> float:
    return math.exp(a * k ** (-b))

def cost_per_solved(k: int) -> float:
    cov = coverage(k)
    return (k * cost_per_sample) / cov if cov > 0 else float("inf")

for k in (1, 10, 100, 1000, 10000):
    print(f"k={k:>6}  coverage={coverage(k):6.1%}  "
          f"cost/issue=${k*cost_per_sample:7.4f}  "
          f"cost/solved=${cost_per_solved(k):7.4f}")

# Find the k that minimizes cost per *delivered* solution (the economic optimum,
# which is well below the k that maximizes coverage).
best_k = min(range(1, 10001), key=cost_per_solved)
print(f"economic optimum: k={best_k}, cost/solved=${cost_per_solved(best_k):.4f}")
```

The script makes the point mechanical: the $k$ that maximizes coverage is always 10,000 (more samples never lower coverage), but the $k$ that minimizes *cost per delivered solution* is much smaller, because the denominator (coverage) saturates while the numerator (cost) grows linearly. Budget on cost-per-solved, not on coverage.

## 7. When repeated sampling wins and when it stalls

**Senior rule of thumb: the deciding question is always "is there a verifier," and the second question is "is the problem already in the model's distribution." Get both yes and sampling is the cheapest scaling axis you have.**

The two-by-two below is the decision grid I keep in my head. The columns are whether a verifier exists; the rows are whether you sample many times from a cheap model or call one big model once.

![A decision grid crossing verifier presence against sampling strategy showing repeated sampling wins with a verifier and stalls without one](/imgs/blogs/repeated-sampling-scaling-laws-8.png)

The top-left cell is the home run: a verifier exists and you sample many times from a cheap model. This is the SWE-bench operating point - \$0.04 per issue at 29.6%, ~3.25x more cost-effective than a single premium call. Whenever you land here, repeated sampling is almost certainly the right strategy, and the only question is how high to set $k$.

The top-right cell - many samples, no verifier - is the trap. Coverage rises beautifully and you cannot cash any of it in, so you spend compute climbing a curve whose payoff plateaus after a few hundred samples. People fall into this cell by reading the coverage results without internalizing the verifier caveat: they see "pass@10k = 95%" and deploy heavy sampling on an open-ended task, then discover that majority voting tops out far below 95% and the extra samples were waste.

The bottom-left cell - one big model, verifier available - is merely wasteful. You have a verifier and you are not exploiting it; you are leaving the cheap scaling axis on the table. This is GPT-4o at \$0.13 and 24% when DeepSeek-at-five-samples would have done better for less.

The bottom-right cell - one big model, no verifier - is the default for genuinely open-ended generation, and it is the correct default there. With no verifier there is no sampling lever to pull, so your quality is bounded by a single pass, and you should spend your budget on the strongest model rather than on samples you cannot select among.

### The substitution boundary in one sentence

Across all four cells, the unifying statement is this: **repeated sampling substitutes inference compute for model quality, but only through a verifier, and only on problems already in the model's distribution.** Take away the verifier and there is nothing to substitute *into* - coverage you cannot select is not quality. Take away the in-distribution assumption (the $p_i = 0$ problems) and there is nothing to substitute *for* - samples cannot create capability that is not there. Both conditions hold for vast, valuable domains - code, math, proofs, structured generation with checkable constraints - which is why this is a major scaling axis and not a niche trick. But neither condition is universal, which is why it is an axis with a hard boundary rather than a free lunch.

## 8. Connections, history, and second-order consequences

**Senior rule of thumb: every "new" inference-scaling idea is a rediscovery that the model knows more than its greedy decode reveals, plus a new way to extract it.**

Repeated sampling did not appear from nowhere, and placing it in its lineage sharpens what is genuinely new about it. The pass@k metric itself comes from the Codex/HumanEval work, which used pass@k to evaluate code models precisely because code has a verifier (the tests) and because a single greedy sample undersells a model's ability to *eventually* write working code. Self-consistency - sampling several chains of thought and taking the majority answer - was an early, verifier-free version that already showed sampling-plus-aggregation beats greedy decoding, and it is exactly the majority-voting curve that we now know plateaus. Best-of-$N$ with a learned verifier, studied heavily in the RLHF and math-reasoning literature, is the reward-model-selection curve. What "Large Language Monkeys" added was the clean *scaling-law* framing - coverage as an exponentiated power law over four orders of magnitude, with the small-model-beats-big FLOP comparison and the explicit cost analysis - and the sharp articulation that the verifier is the gate.

### Second-order consequence: it changes what "model capability" means

Once you internalize coverage, the single-sample benchmark number on a model card stops being the measure of capability and becomes a measure of *reliability*. A model with 13% pass@1 and 95% pass@10k on GSM8K is not a model that "can't do math"; it is a model that can do almost all of this math but does so unreliably. That reframing has teeth: it means the right way to compare models in verifiable domains is by their coverage curves, not their pass@1, and a model that is worse on one try might be better across a budget if its distribution covers more problems. It also means that "improving a model" can mean two very different things - raising $p_i$ uniformly (a better base model) or sharpening the distribution so the right answer is easier to select (better post-training, better reasoning). The reasoning-model paradigm is largely the second: not necessarily higher coverage, but a distribution where the correct trajectory is the one the model actually commits to.

### Second-order consequence: it pressures the verifier ecosystem

If repeated sampling is the cheapest way to scale in verifiable domains, then *making domains verifiable* becomes strategically valuable. This is already visible: the surge of interest in formal proof languages (Lean, Coq) as targets for LLM math, the construction of executable benchmarks like SWE-bench, the investment in process reward models and learned verifiers, and the RLVR (reinforcement learning from verifiable rewards) training recipe that powers reasoning models all flow from the realization that a good verifier turns a cheap model into a strong system. The bottleneck the paper identifies - verification, not generation - is now where a large fraction of the field's effort goes, because that is where the marginal returns are.

### Second-order consequence: it interacts with over-training

The [inference-aware](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/inference-aware-scaling-laws) story said: if you will serve a lot, train a smaller model on more tokens, because inference is cheap per token and the small model is cheaper to run. Repeated sampling amplifies this: if you will *also* sample that model many times per query in a verifiable domain, the case for a small, heavily-over-trained model gets even stronger, because you are going to run it 250 times and every parameter you shed saves on all 250 passes. The two laws compose. A small over-trained model is the ideal substrate for repeated sampling: cheap per token, strong enough to have nonzero $p_i$ on most in-distribution problems, and inexpensive enough that drawing hundreds of samples is economical. This composition - small over-trained model plus heavy verifiable test-time sampling - is one of the central syntheses of the whole inference-scaling era.

## Case studies from the literature and from practice

The patterns are clearest in concrete incidents. Each of the following is drawn from the paper's results or from the well-trodden practice around them; the lesson in each is the same theme viewed from a different angle.

### 1. The SWE-bench coverage jump

The symptom: DeepSeek-Coder-V2-Instruct scores a mediocre 15.9% on SWE-bench Lite with a single sample, well behind the 43% single-shot state of the art. The wrong first hypothesis: the model is just weaker and needs to be replaced with a bigger one. The actual situation: the model has a correct patch in its distribution for far more than 15.9% of issues; it simply does not produce one reliably on the first try. The fix: draw 250 samples per issue and let the hidden test suite select. Coverage rises to 56%, thirteen points past the single-shot SOTA. The lesson: in a domain with a perfect verifier, the single-sample number understates the achievable result by tens of points, and the cheapest path to state of the art can be more attempts rather than a better model.

### 2. The 160-million-parameter mathematician

The symptom: Pythia-160M scores 0.27% pass@1 on MATH - one correct answer in roughly 370 tries - which reads as "this model cannot do competition math." The wrong hypothesis: a 160M model is simply too small to have any mathematical ability worth extracting. The actual situation: the model has nonzero probability mass on correct solutions for more than half of MATH problems; that mass is just spread thin. The fix: 10,000 samples per problem with an exact-match grader. Coverage reaches roughly 57%. The lesson: capability and reliability are different axes, and a model that looks useless on pass@1 can be more than half-capable on pass@10k. Never confuse "rarely produces the answer" with "cannot produce the answer."

### 3. The FLOPs-matched upset

The symptom: a team assumes that to improve formal-math (MiniF2F) results they must move from Llama-3-8B to Llama-3-70B, an 8.75x compute increase per call. The wrong hypothesis: more parameters per sample is the only way to spend more compute productively. The actual situation: at equal total FLOPs, drawing many 8B samples covers more problems than one 70B sample, because the extra samples harvest the solvable-but-unreliable tail more efficiently than uniformly raising $p_i$ does. The fix: keep the 8B model and spend the FLOP budget on samples. The lesson: at a fixed inference budget in a verifiable domain, "how many samples" can be a better axis than "how big a model," and the FLOP-optimal choice is often the smaller model.

### 4. The dollar inversion

The symptom: a product uses single GPT-4o calls for an issue-resolution feature at \$0.13 per issue and 24% solved, and the bill is uncomfortable. The wrong hypothesis: the premium model is necessary for quality, so cost is the price of accuracy. The actual situation: five DeepSeek-Coder-V2 samples filtered by the test suite cost \$0.04 and solve 29.6% - cheaper *and* more accurate. The fix: switch to repeated cheap sampling with the verifier doing selection, a ~3.25x cost-effectiveness gain. The lesson: in verifiable domains, the premium-single-call operating point is frequently dominated on both axes by cheap-model repeated sampling; FLOP efficiency understates the dollar win because small models batch and share prefill far better.

### 5. The majority-vote plateau

The symptom: a team applies repeated sampling to an open-ended generation task, sees coverage rise toward 90% in offline analysis, deploys with majority voting, and observes delivered accuracy stuck near 40% no matter how many samples they draw. The wrong hypothesis: a bug in the sampling or aggregation code. The actual situation: there is no verifier, so coverage is unrealizable; majority voting plateaus at the accuracy of the model's modal answer after a few hundred samples, and more samples cannot move a fixed mode. The fix: there is no cheap fix - either find a verifier or accept the plateau and stop spending on samples. The lesson: coverage without a verifier is a mirage; the curve you actually climb is the flat selection curve, and you must confirm the verifier *before* you build the sampling pipeline.

### 6. The reward-model degradation

The symptom: a team replaces majority voting with a learned reward model to select among samples, sees improvement at $k = 64$, then watches delivered accuracy *drop* as they push to $k = 1024$. The wrong hypothesis: the reward model needs more training data. The actual situation: with enough samples, the draw eventually finds a wrong answer that the reward model scores highly - the many-sample regime is adversarial pressure that finds the reward model's blind spots, so beyond some $k$ the selected answer gets worse. The fix: cap $k$ at the point where selection quality peaks, or invest in a verifier that cannot be fooled (an actual checker rather than a learned score). The lesson: fallible verifiers do not just plateau, they can degrade under heavy sampling, because more candidates means more chances to exploit the verifier's errors.

### 7. The temperature trap

The symptom: a team ports a pass@1-tuned configuration (low temperature, tuned for the single best answer) directly into a repeated-sampling pipeline and finds coverage barely improves with $k$. The wrong hypothesis: repeated sampling does not work for their task. The actual situation: at low temperature the samples are nearly identical, so pass@k stays close to pass@1 - there is no diversity to harvest. The fix: raise the temperature for the sampling stage, accepting lower single-sample accuracy in exchange for the diversity that makes large-$k$ coverage climb. The lesson: the settings that maximize pass@1 actively suppress pass@k; repeated sampling needs its own, higher-temperature configuration, and reusing the single-sample config silently kills the benefit.

### 8. The verifier-cost surprise

The symptom: a team builds repeated sampling on top of an expensive LLM-judge verifier, draws 250 samples per query, and finds the verifier bill is 10x the generation bill, erasing the cost advantage. The wrong hypothesis: generation is the dominant cost, as it is in the SWE-bench example. The actual situation: when the verifier is itself a large model call, verifying 250 candidates costs far more than generating them, and the economics invert. The fix: replace the LLM judge with a cheap, reliable verifier (a checker, a test suite, an exact-match grader) wherever possible, or reduce $k$ and verify only the most promising candidates. The lesson: the clean economics of repeated sampling assume a cheap verifier; an expensive verifier turns the strategy from a 3x win into a loss, so verifier cost belongs in the budget from the start.

### 9. The shared-prefill optimization

The symptom: a first implementation draws 250 samples by issuing 250 independent API calls, each re-encoding the full prompt, and the cost is far higher than the FLOP analysis predicted. The wrong hypothesis: the FLOP analysis was wrong. The actual situation: each independent call paid for a fresh prefill of the (long) prompt, so the 250 calls did 250x the prefill work instead of sharing it. The fix: batch the 250 continuations off a single shared prefill, so the prompt is encoded once and the KV cache is reused across all samples; the marginal cost of each extra sample collapses to its decode tokens. The lesson: the cost model of repeated sampling assumes shared prefill; implement it naively with independent calls and you pay a large, avoidable tax that can swamp the strategy's advantage.

### 10. The coverage-ceiling reality check

The symptom: a team extrapolates the coverage curve and promises stakeholders "near-100% solve rate with enough samples," then plateaus at 80% on MATH no matter how high $k$ goes. The wrong hypothesis: they did not sample enough. The actual situation: the remaining 20% are $p_i = 0$ problems - the model has no probability mass on any correct solution, so no number of samples will find one; they set the coverage ceiling. The fix: recognize the ceiling as a model-level limit, and improve the *model* (better base, better post-training, more knowledge) for the residual, rather than throwing more samples at problems that are out of distribution. The lesson: repeated sampling harvests solvable-but-unreliable problems; it cannot solve out-of-distribution ones, and the gap between the coverage ceiling and 100% is exactly the set where you still need a better model.

### 11. The extrapolation that held

The symptom: a team needs to forecast the SWE-bench coverage they would get at 250 samples but can only afford to run 32 samples in their evaluation loop. The wrong hypothesis: they must run the full 250 to know the answer, which is eight times the budget they have. The actual situation: coverage follows the exponentiated power law, so fitting $\log(\text{coverage}) \approx a k^{-b}$ on the cheap $k \leq 32$ runs gives a curve that extrapolates accurately to $k = 250$. The fix: fit on the cheap runs, extrapolate, and validate with a single confirmation run at the target $k$. The forecast lands within a couple of points. The lesson: the value of a *law* over a *trend* is exactly this - the same two constants describe the model at $k = 32$ and $k = 250$, so you can budget for the expensive regime by measuring only the cheap one, the same way pre-training scaling laws let you forecast a large run from small ones.

### 12. The agent-loop confusion

The symptom: a team conflates "repeated sampling" with "agentic retry loops" and builds a system that feeds each failed attempt's error back into the next attempt, expecting the coverage curve from the paper. The wrong hypothesis: independent sampling and sequential retry are the same scaling axis. The actual situation: the paper's clean coverage power law is for *independent* samples drawn in parallel from the same prompt; sequential revision where each attempt conditions on the previous one is a different mechanism (closer to the revision axis of compute-optimal test-time scaling) with different - sometimes better, sometimes worse - scaling behavior, and it does not parallelize the way independent sampling does. The fix: be explicit about which axis you are on. If you want the paper's power law and its batching economics, draw independent samples in parallel; if you want to exploit error feedback, you are doing sequential revision and should measure its own curve. The lesson: "more attempts" is not one technique - independent parallel sampling and sequential conditioned revision are distinct axes with distinct laws, costs, and parallelism, and mixing them up leads to budgets that do not match reality.

## What this means in practice

Repeated sampling is the cleanest, cheapest inference-scaling axis we have, and it is also the one with the sharpest boundary, so the practical guidance is correspondingly crisp.

**Reach for repeated sampling when:**
- You have a **cheap, reliable, automatic verifier** - unit tests, a compiler, a proof checker, an exact-match grader, an executable SQL comparison. This is the gate; everything else is secondary.
- The problems are **mostly in the model's distribution** (nonzero per-sample success probability), so that more samples harvest a real long tail rather than chasing $p_i = 0$ problems.
- You can use a **small, cheap model** and afford to query it many times per problem, with **shared-prefill batching** so the marginal sample cost is just decode tokens.
- The value of a solved problem justifies climbing the coverage curve to the $k$ where marginal coverage still beats marginal cost - and you budget on **cost per delivered solution**, not on coverage.
- You want **state-of-the-art results at low cost** in code, math, or formal reasoning, where the ~3.25x cost-effectiveness and small-beats-big FLOP results apply directly.

**Skip repeated sampling when:**
- There is **no cheap, reliable verifier** - open-ended generation, summaries, advice, most chat. Majority voting and reward-model selection plateau after a few hundred samples, so the coverage you generate is unrealizable.
- Your only verifier is **expensive or fallible** (a large LLM judge): the verifier cost can swamp generation, and a fallible verifier can degrade under heavy sampling as the draws find its blind spots.
- The problems are **out of the model's distribution** ($p_i \approx 0$): you need a better model, not more samples, and you should spend the budget on model quality.
- You need **low latency on a single query**: drawing and verifying hundreds of samples is throughput-friendly but latency-unfriendly, so a single strong call may be the right operating point for interactive use.

The deepest takeaway is the decomposition. Repeated sampling cleanly separates two things the field used to conflate: how much a model *can* do (coverage, which scales as a beautiful power law over four orders of magnitude) and how much you can *get out* of it (selection, which is gated by verification). In verifiable domains the two coincide and you get a major, cheap scaling axis. In open-ended domains they diverge and you hit the selection wall. Everything downstream - process reward models, learned verifiers, RL on verifiable rewards, the entire reasoning-model program - is the field's attempt to push the selection curve up toward the coverage curve. Understand that gap, confirm your verifier before you build, and budget on cost-per-solved, and repeated sampling is one of the highest-leverage tools in the inference-scaling toolbox.

There is also a strategic reading worth carrying out of this post. For most of the deep-learning era, "get a better result" meant "train a bigger model," and the scaling laws were maps of how to spend training compute. Repeated sampling is the clearest early demonstration that there is a whole second continent of compute to spend - at inference, per query - and that in the right domains it is *cheaper per unit of capability* than the training continent. That reframing is what connects this post to the rest of the inference-scaling track: the [inference-aware law](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/inference-aware-scaling-laws) right-sizes the model for a serving budget, [compute-optimal test-time scaling](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/test-time-compute-scaling) decides how to spend the inference budget between search and revision, and [reasoning models](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/reasoning-models-rl-test-time-scaling) train the model to spend that inference budget productively through RL. Repeated sampling is the foundation they all build on - the proof that a small model plus a verifier plus many tries is a scaling axis in its own right - and the verifier caveat it surfaces is the constraint that shapes every technique above it. Where you can verify, spend at inference. Where you cannot, the wall is real, and no amount of sampling will get you past it.
