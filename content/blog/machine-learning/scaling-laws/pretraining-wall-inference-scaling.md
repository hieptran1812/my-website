For four years the recipe for a better language model was boring and reliable: make it bigger, feed it more of the internet, and the loss came down on a straight line you could draw on a log-log plot before you even started the run. That was the whole promise of the scaling-laws literature this series has been walking through — [Kaplan](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/kaplan-scaling-laws-language-models), [Chinchilla](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/chinchilla-compute-optimal-scaling), the [data-constrained](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/data-constrained-scaling-laws) and [data-quality](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/data-quality-scaling-laws) refinements — all of it said the same comforting thing: spend more compute on pre-training, get a predictably better model. Then, in late 2024, the story changed. The head of one of the labs that wrote those laws went on stage and said pre-training as we know it is ending. Trade reporters wrote that the next flagship model was a smaller leap than the last. And a competing camp of analysts said the wall was a mirage. The diagram below is the mental model for this entire post: the frontier of capability is moving from one scaling axis to two, and the second axis — how long the model thinks at inference time — has a catch that the first one never did.

![A flow diagram showing the pre-training axis leading to a reported slowdown, which leads to a test-time compute axis that passes through a verifier gate splitting into verifiable domains that scale cleanly and open-ended domains that plateau](/imgs/blogs/pretraining-wall-inference-scaling-1.png)

This is the closer for the test-time-scaling track of the series, so I am going to be deliberately careful about what we actually know versus what we have only heard. A lot of the "pre-training hit a wall" narrative is executive commentary and trade-press reporting about private training runs, not a published empirical law you can falsify. A lot of the rebuttal is equally a matter of how you read a log-scale chart. So the first half of this post is a fair fight between the two readings, with the evidence labeled by what kind of evidence it is. The second half is the part I am confident about, because it rests on the peer-reviewed scaling laws we covered in the last several posts: a small, heavily over-trained model in the [Beyond-Chinchilla](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/inference-aware-scaling-laws) sense, combined with heavy test-time compute from [repeated sampling](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/repeated-sampling-scaling-laws), search, or the long chain-of-thought of [reasoning models](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/reasoning-models-rl-test-time-scaling), can match or beat a large compute-optimal model at lower total serving cost. With one enormous asterisk: it only works cleanly where you can *verify* the answer.

> [!important] The one thing to remember: test-time compute is a real scaling axis, but it is gated by verification
> - **The "pre-training wall" is mostly reporting and commentary, not a published law.** Ilya Sutskever (Reuters, Nov 2024; NeurIPS 2024) said results from scaling pre-training have plateaued and that "we have but one internet" — the data wall. The Information (Nov 2024) reported OpenAI's Orion (the model that became GPT-4.5) showed a smaller jump than GPT-3 to GPT-4. Both are credible, neither is a falsifiable loss-versus-compute curve.
> - **The counter-camp disputes that the wall is real.** Epoch AI and LessWrong analysts argue the published scaling curves show no clean break, that a "smaller jump" is exactly what a power law on a log axis predicts, and that synthetic and RL-generated data extend the supply beyond the human internet.
> - **Reasoning models themselves show diminishing returns as inference grows.** o1's accuracy improves log-linearly with test-time compute, which means each additional unit of accuracy costs exponentially more samples. Test-time scaling is not a free lunch; it is its own power law with its own knee.
> - **The synthesis that ties the series together:** a small over-trained model (cheap at ~2N FLOPs per token) plus heavy test-time compute can match a large compute-optimal model at lower serving cost — *provided a verifier exists*. Repeated sampling on SWE-bench got DeepSeek-Coder-V2 from 15.9% at one sample to 56% at 250 samples, beating the single-sample state of the art of 43%.
> - **Verification is the gate.** In verifiable domains (code with tests, math with checkers, formal proofs) coverage converts to solved problems and scales over four orders of magnitude of samples. In open-ended domains, majority voting and reward-model selection plateau after a few hundred samples — the bottleneck moves from generation to selection.
> - **The practical lesson:** do not bet the roadmap on one axis. Size a right-sized base for your inference demand, then spend test-time compute where a verifier turns extra samples into extra correct answers. Where no verifier exists, more inference compute mostly burns money.

## Why the frame is different now

The cleanest way to understand the shift is to notice that for most of the deep-learning era there was effectively only one knob worth turning, and now there are two. The single-knob world is the world of every post in this series so far. You had a training budget, you used the scaling laws to split it between model size and data, you trained once, and the model you got was the model you shipped. The quality of the answer at inference time was a fixed property of the weights. You could not buy a better answer at serving time by spending more; a forward pass cost what it cost, and that was that.

| Assumption from the pre-training era | The naive read in 2024 | The reality we are converging on |
|---|---|---|
| More compute always means a bigger pre-trained model | Pre-training stalled, so progress is over | Pre-training is one axis of several; test-time compute is a second |
| Answer quality is fixed once weights are frozen | A weak model is permanently weak | A weak model can be made strong per-query by thinking longer, if you can check the result |
| The data supply is the human internet | We are out of data, full stop | The internet is the cheap data; synthetic, RL, and self-generated data extend it at a cost |
| Capability scales smoothly with training FLOPs | A smaller jump proves a wall | A smaller jump is what a power law predicts; you need the curve, not the vibe |
| One model serves every query the same way | Pick the biggest model you can afford | Route per query: cheap base for easy ones, heavy test-time compute for hard ones |

The second knob — test-time compute — breaks the old assumption that answer quality is frozen at training time. You can now spend more at inference to get a better answer: sample the model many times and keep the best, run a search over reasoning steps, or train the model to produce a long chain-of-thought that explores and backtracks. That is genuinely new, and it is why the "pre-training is dead, AI is over" framing is wrong even if the pre-training-plateau claim turns out to be partly true. A plateau on one axis is not a ceiling on capability when a second axis just opened.

But — and this is the load-bearing caveat for the whole post — the second knob is not a clean substitute for the first. The pre-training axis gave you better answers on *every* query, in every domain, for free at inference. The test-time axis only pays off when you can tell a good answer from a bad one cheaply enough to select it, and it costs real compute every single time you query. Those two differences are the entire reason this post exists, and they are why the synthesis at the end is precise about *where* it applies.

> The pre-training axis sells you a better model. The test-time axis sells you a better answer, one query at a time, and only if you can grade it. Confusing the two is the most expensive mistake in the current roadmap debate.

## The debate: did pre-training hit a wall?

Let me lay out the actual claims, because the public conversation has compressed a careful disagreement into a yes/no shouting match. The figure below puts the two cases side by side, and the most important thing about it is the color: the case *for* a wall is built almost entirely from executive commentary and trade reporting, while the case *against* leans on published scaling curves and the absence of any falsifying data.

![A before-and-after comparison contrasting the case for a pre-training wall built from reporting and commentary against the case against a wall built from published analysis and the absence of a falsifying curve](/imgs/blogs/pretraining-wall-inference-scaling-2.png)

### The case for a wall

The most prominent voice is Ilya Sutskever, who co-authored some of the foundational work this field rests on. In a Reuters interview in November 2024 and in his NeurIPS 2024 talk, he said that results from scaling pre-training have plateaued, and he gave the reason a memorable name: the data wall. His exact framing was that "pre-training as we know it will unquestionably end," because "we have but one internet" — there is a finite supply of high-quality human text, and we are running out of it. He has elsewhere called data "the fossil fuel of AI": a one-time deposit that we are burning through, not a renewable resource. The argument is not that scaling laws are wrong; it is that one of the inputs to the law, the data term $D$, has a hard ceiling set by how much the human race has written down.

The second pillar is reporting from The Information in November 2024, which described OpenAI's Orion model — the model that was subsequently released as GPT-4.5 — as showing a smaller quality jump over GPT-4 than GPT-4 had shown over GPT-3. The reporting framed this as gains not keeping pace with the cost of the training run, and it described an internal strategy shift toward post-training and test-time techniques as a result. Around the same time, similar reporting touched Google and Anthropic, suggesting the slowdown was felt across labs, not just at one.

The economic version of the argument is the one that actually moves budgets. Even if the next model is meaningfully better, if it costs several times more to train and serve for a marginal benchmark gain, the return on the marginal pre-training dollar has collapsed. That is a real claim about ROI, and it is the version that a CFO understands. It does not require the loss curve to literally flatten; it only requires the slope to fall below the point where the next doubling of compute pays for itself.

It is worth putting rough arithmetic on the data-wall claim, because the constraint is quantitative and the numbers are sobering. Estimates of the total stock of reasonably high-quality public human text land somewhere in the low hundreds of trillions of tokens — the often-cited figure is on the order of $3 \times 10^{14}$ to $10^{15}$ tokens of usable text on the open web, with another large but lower-quality tail in scanned books, code, and transcribed audio. Frontier models in 2024 were already training on 15T tokens or more. A single model trained on 15T tokens has consumed a few percent of the entire high-quality stock; a handful of frontier labs each doing multiple such runs per year are collectively drawing down a meaningful fraction of the deposit annually. Chinchilla scaling says that to keep loss falling you need to grow $D$ roughly in proportion to $N$, so a model ten times larger wants ten times the tokens — and there is no ten-times-the-internet to feed it. That is the arithmetic behind "we have but one internet." It is a real constraint on the $D$ term, and it is the most defensible part of the entire wall thesis.

### Why none of that is a scaling law

Here is the discipline I want to hold throughout. Every item in the previous section is either a qualitative statement from an executive or a description of private, unpublished training runs by trade reporters. None of it is a published loss-versus-compute curve with error bars that you or I could attempt to falsify. The Sutskever data-wall claim is about an *input* to the scaling law (the finite supply of $D$), which is a genuine and important constraint, but it is a claim about resource exhaustion, not about the functional form $L(N, D) = E + A/N^{\alpha} + B/D^{\beta}$ breaking down. The Orion reporting is about a single model family seen through the lens of journalists with partial access. These are signals worth taking seriously — the people making them have skin in the game and information we do not — but treating them as if they were the Chinchilla paper is a category error.

This matters because the field has a bad habit, which the entire [Kaplan-versus-Chinchilla saga](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/kaplan-vs-chinchilla-reconciliation) was a lesson in: it tends to canonize a vivid claim and then discover years later that the bookkeeping was off. Kaplan's compute-optimal exponent of 0.73 was treated as gospel for two years and then turned out to be largely an artifact of counting non-embedding parameters at small scale. The "pre-training wall" deserves at least that much skepticism, because the evidence for it is far softer than the evidence for Kaplan ever was.

### The case against a wall

The counter-camp — concentrated at Epoch AI and in a series of careful LessWrong posts — does not argue that scaling is infinite. It argues something more specific and more deflationary: that nothing in the public record shows a clean break from the established power laws, and that the appearance of a wall is mostly an artifact of how humans read log-scale charts.

The strongest version of the rebuttal goes like this. A power law says that capability improves by a constant multiplicative factor for each multiplicative increase in compute. On a log-log plot that is a straight line, and a straight line has a constant slope forever. But humans do not perceive constant slopes; they perceive *absolute* jumps. GPT-3 to GPT-4 felt like a bigger leap than GPT-4 to GPT-4.5 not necessarily because the slope changed, but because the same slope on a curve that is already high produces a smaller perceived jump — and because benchmark scores saturate near 100%, compressing the apparent gain at the top. A "smaller jump" is exactly what an unbroken power law predicts once you are far enough along it. To claim a wall, you need to show that the *slope itself* fell, and that requires the loss-versus-compute curve, which the labs have not published.

The data-wall argument also has a softer floor than it first appears. The human internet is the cheapest source of training tokens, but it is not the only one. The [data-constrained scaling work](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/data-constrained-scaling-laws) showed that you can repeat data for several epochs at near-zero quality cost, stretching a fixed unique-token budget — up to about four epochs is nearly free, and gains continue with diminishing returns out to roughly sixteen. That alone multiplies the effective stock by a factor of several before any new data is collected. Synthetic data — model-generated text, filtered and verified — extends the supply further, and reinforcement learning generates fresh training signal from the model's own rollouts rather than from a fixed corpus. The reasoning-model results in particular show that you can manufacture high-value training data in verifiable domains essentially without limit, because a math solver or a code test can label the synthetic data for free. The internet may be one deposit, but it is not the only place to mine.

There is also a subtler statistical point that the Epoch-style analyses press hard. The headline benchmarks the public uses to judge "the jump" — MMLU, common multiple-choice suites, popular coding evals — are bounded above by 100% and increasingly saturated near the top by frontier models. When a model is at 88% on a benchmark, the most it can gain is twelve points, and most of those last points are mislabeled or genuinely ambiguous questions that no model will ever get. So the *measured* jump shrinks as scores approach the ceiling even if the underlying capability is improving at a constant rate. This is not a scaling-law effect at all; it is a measurement-ceiling artifact, and it systematically biases the public toward perceiving a slowdown. The proper instrument for detecting a wall is the loss-versus-compute curve, which has no ceiling and is exactly the thing the labs do not publish for their frontier runs. Judging the slope of a power law by eyeballing saturated benchmark deltas is, the critics argue, close to the worst available method.

Finally, the counter-camp points out that "the wall" conflates two very different claims that ought to be kept separate. One is that the *return on the marginal training dollar* has fallen — which can be true purely because compute got more expensive or because the data wall raised the cost of the $D$ term, with the loss law itself perfectly intact. The other is that the *loss law has broken* — that the functional relationship between compute and loss has changed shape. Almost all the public evidence bears only on the first claim, which is an economics claim, while the dramatic framing implies the second, which is a science claim. Keeping those apart is most of the work of thinking clearly about this debate.

### Weighing it honestly

The honest summary is that the wall is unproven in either direction from public data, and the disagreement is partly about evidence standards. The figure below scores the main pieces of evidence by what kind of evidence each one is, whether it is falsifiable, and whether it actually supports the wall once you account for its caveat.

![A matrix scoring five pieces of evidence about the pre-training wall by source type, whether each is falsifiable, whether it supports the wall, and the caveat that weakens it](/imgs/blogs/pretraining-wall-inference-scaling-4.png)

Read down the "Falsifiable?" column and the picture is stark. The three rows that most strongly support the wall — Sutskever's commentary, the Orion reporting, the cost-versus-gain argument — are all non-falsifiable from where we sit, because they rest on private runs or qualitative judgment. The two rows that weaken the wall — the public loss-versus-compute fits and the existence of synthetic and RL data — are the only ones backed by measurable, reproducible evidence, and both cut against a clean break. That asymmetry is the single most important thing to take from the debate. It does not prove there is no wall. It proves that the loudest evidence for a wall is the least falsifiable, which is exactly the situation in which a careful engineer reserves judgment.

My own read, stated as opinion rather than fact: the pure-pre-training-scaling slope has very likely fallen for the labs operating at the frontier, mostly because they are running into the data wall on high-quality human text and because benchmark saturation makes the remaining gains hard to see and hard to monetize. But "the slope fell" is a long way from "we hit a wall," and it says nothing about the ceiling on capability, because the second axis was opening at exactly the moment the first one slowed. Which is the more interesting story.

## The second axis: how test-time compute changed the game

**Senior rule of thumb: when one input saturates, the move is to find an input that has not, not to declare the output finished.** The pre-training axis spends compute once, at training time, to produce a better model. The test-time axis spends compute every query, at inference time, to produce a better answer from a fixed model. The figure below is the schematic of the divergence: as you pour more compute into pre-training, the capability gain flattens; as you pour compute into test-time methods, the gain is still climbing steeply, because we started exploiting that axis seriously only in 2024.

![A schematic chart with two curves over compute, a solid pre-training curve that flattens at a knee labeled as the data wall and a dashed test-time-compute curve that rises steeply as a second axis](/imgs/blogs/pretraining-wall-inference-scaling-3.png)

I want to be precise that this figure is a schematic, not fitted data — the shapes are the claim, not the exact numbers. The solid pre-training curve flattens because of everything in the debate above: finite $D$, benchmark saturation, the constant-slope-looks-smaller effect. The dashed test-time curve rises steeply because we are early on it, and early on a fresh power law the absolute gains per dollar are large. Both curves will eventually bend — there is no axis that scales forever, and we will see test-time compute's own knee in a moment — but right now they are at very different points on their respective curves, and that gap is the entire reason the industry pivoted.

There are three concrete forms of the test-time axis, each covered in detail in an earlier post of this track, and it is worth restating them compactly because the synthesis depends on all three.

### Form one: repeated sampling

The simplest form is to ask the same model the same question many times and keep the best answer. The [Large Language Monkeys work](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/repeated-sampling-scaling-laws) (Brown et al. 2024) showed this is a scaling law in its own right. The metric is coverage — the fraction of problems solved by at least one of $k$ samples, which is pass@k — and it follows an exponentiated power law in $k$:

$$\log(\text{coverage}) \approx a \cdot k^{-b} \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \text{coverage} \approx \exp\!\big(a \cdot k^{-b}\big)$$

which means log-coverage is roughly linear in log-$k$ over about four orders of magnitude of samples. The headline numbers are striking. On SWE-bench Lite, DeepSeek-Coder-V2-Instruct went from 15.9% with a single sample to 56% with 250 samples, beating the single-sample state of the art of 43%. Llama-3-8B-Instruct went from 13.3% pass@1 to 95.3% pass@10k on GSM8K, and from 26.6% to 79.8% on MATH. Even a tiny Pythia-160M model went from 0.27% to roughly 57% on MATH over 10,000 samples. And it is cost-effective: five samples from DeepSeek-V2 cost about \$0.04 per SWE-bench issue at 29.6% solve rate, versus \$0.13 per issue for a single GPT-4o sample at 24% — roughly 3.25 times more cost-effective.

The shape of the law deserves a sentence of intuition, because it is what makes the strategy work and also what limits it. The exponentiated power law means that each problem has some per-sample success probability $p$, and over $k$ independent samples the chance of getting at least one right is $1 - (1-p)^k$ — which rises fast at first and then saturates toward 1 as $k$ grows. A problem with $p = 0.01$ — one percent per try — has about a 63% chance of being solved at least once in 100 samples and 99.99% in 1,000. That is why a model with a single-sample accuracy that looks hopeless can have very high coverage: as long as $p$ is not exactly zero, enough samples eventually hit. The flip side is the hard limit that motivates the whole verification discussion: if $p$ is exactly zero for a problem — the model simply cannot represent the solution — then $1 - (1-0)^k = 0$ for every $k$, and no amount of sampling helps. Coverage scaling amplifies non-zero capability; it cannot create capability from nothing.

#### Second-order effect: temperature and diversity

A non-obvious operational detail is that repeated sampling only works if the samples are genuinely diverse. If you sample at temperature zero you get the same answer every time and coverage never moves off pass@1. The whole strategy depends on injecting enough randomness — typically a temperature around 0.6 to 1.0 — that the $k$ samples explore different solution paths, while not so much randomness that the outputs become incoherent. There is a sweet spot, and it shifts with $k$: at small $k$ you want lower temperature for reliability, and at very large $k$ you can afford higher temperature because you are betting on the tail of the distribution containing a correct answer that the verifier will find. This interacts with the verifier directly — high temperature is only safe when a verifier can reject the garbage it produces.

### Form two: search and revision

A more structured form is to spend the test-time compute on a guided search rather than naive resampling. The [compute-optimal test-time work](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/reasoning-models-rl-test-time-scaling) (Snell et al. 2024) distinguishes two axes within test-time compute: verifier-guided search (best-of-N, beam search, lookahead, scored by a process reward model) and proposal revision (training the model to iteratively fix its own answer in sequence). Their central finding is that the optimal allocation is prompt-difficulty-dependent: easy problems benefit from sequential revision, hard problems from parallel search. Allocating adaptively per prompt — what they call compute-optimal — is more than four times more efficient than a fixed best-of-N baseline. On problems where a small base model already has non-trivial success, this lets it outperform a model fourteen times larger at matched FLOPs — though that advantage breaks down on the hardest problems and the largest budgets, where a bigger pre-trained model is still better.

The two sub-axes are worth distinguishing carefully because they fail in different ways. Parallel search — generate many candidates, score them, keep the best — is robust to a weak generator as long as the scorer is good, because it only needs one good candidate to appear in the pile and the scorer to find it. It is bottlenecked by the scorer's quality, which is exactly the verification problem again, dressed as a process reward model. Sequential revision — generate, critique, regenerate, repeat — is the opposite: it leans on the model's ability to *recognize and fix* its own mistakes, which is a capability the base model either has or does not. Revision wins on easy problems because the fixes are local and the model can see them; search wins on hard problems because the answer requires exploring a branch the model would not revise its way into. The compute-optimal recipe is to estimate difficulty and route: revise the easy ones, search the hard ones. The four-times efficiency gain comes entirely from not wasting search compute on problems a revision pass would have fixed, and not wasting revision passes on problems that need a search.

### Form three: long chain-of-thought from RL

The most powerful form trains the model so that a single long generation explores, backtracks, and self-corrects. This is the reasoning-model paradigm: OpenAI's o1 and the open reproduction DeepSeek-R1. o1's performance improves log-linearly with both train-time RL compute and test-time compute — both plots are straight lines on a log x-axis, though OpenAI never published a slope. On AIME 2024 a single o1 sample scored 74%, majority vote of 64 samples reached 83%, and a learned reranker over 1,000 samples reached 93%, against a GPT-4o baseline of 12%. DeepSeek-R1-Zero reproduced the effect with pure reinforcement learning — no supervised fine-tuning — on the DeepSeek-V3 base using GRPO with rule-based rewards, and the test-time scaling *emerged from the RL alone*: the model spontaneously grew its response length and exhibited a self-reflective "aha moment." R1-Zero went from 15.6% to 71.0% pass@1 on AIME 2024, and to 86.7% with consensus over 64 samples, matching OpenAI-o1-0912.

All three forms share the same shape: spend more compute at inference, get a better answer, along a curve you can characterize. And all three share the same gate, which we come to next.

## The gate: verification decides everything

**Senior rule of thumb: generation is cheap and getting cheaper; the scarce resource is knowing which generation is correct.** This is the single most important idea in the test-time-scaling track, and it is the reason the synthesis at the end of this post is so carefully hedged. The figure below shows what happens to the two regimes as you scale samples: with a verifier, the fraction of problems you actually solve keeps climbing; without one, it plateaus early, while the cost keeps growing linearly in the number of samples.

![A schematic chart with a solid curve for verified coverage that keeps rising with samples and a dashed curve for unverified selection that flattens early, with a note that cost grows linearly so unverified scaling burns compute](/imgs/blogs/pretraining-wall-inference-scaling-5.png)

The mechanism is simple once you separate two quantities that sound the same but are not. *Coverage* is the fraction of problems for which at least one of your $k$ samples is correct — it measures whether the right answer is somewhere in the pile. *Solve rate* is the fraction of problems for which you actually output the correct answer — it measures whether you can find the right answer in the pile and return it. Coverage is a property of the generator. Solve rate is coverage filtered through your selection mechanism. When the two are equal, you have a perfect verifier; when they diverge, the gap is the tax that selection imposes.

In verifiable domains the verifier is essentially free and essentially perfect. A unit test either passes or it does not. A formal proof checker either accepts the proof or it rejects it. A math problem with a known numeric answer is a string comparison. In these domains, coverage *is* solve rate: if any of your $k$ samples is correct, you can identify it with certainty and return it. That is why the Large Language Monkeys numbers are real — SWE-bench has tests, MiniF2F has a proof checker, GSM8K and MATH have ground-truth answers. The exponentiated-power-law coverage curve translates directly into solved problems, and it keeps translating over four orders of magnitude of $k$.

In open-ended domains there is no free verifier, and the selection problem becomes the wall. If the task is "write a good essay" or "give helpful medical advice" or "summarize this document well," you cannot check correctness with a string comparison. You have to *select* the best of your $k$ samples using something imperfect: majority voting (which assumes the most common answer is the best, often false for open-ended generation) or a learned reward model (which is itself a fallible model with its own errors and exploits). Both of these saturate. The Large Language Monkeys paper is explicit about this: without a verifier, majority voting and reward-model selection plateau after a few hundred samples, and the bottleneck shifts from generation to verification and selection. You can keep sampling, but you stop solving more problems, while your bill keeps climbing linearly in $k$.

| Property | Verifiable domain | Open-ended domain |
|---|---|---|
| Example tasks | code with tests, math, formal proofs | essays, advice, summaries, dialogue |
| Verifier | unit test, checker, answer key — free and exact | reward model or human — costly and fallible |
| Does coverage equal solve rate? | Yes, essentially | No — selection tax grows with $k$ |
| Scaling behavior of solve rate | rises over ~4 orders of magnitude of $k$ | plateaus after a few hundred samples |
| What test-time compute buys | more solved problems, cheaply | mostly a higher bill past the knee |
| RL training signal available? | Yes — outcome reward is free | Only via a learned, gameable reward model |

This table is the heart of the matter, and it explains a great deal of the current landscape that otherwise looks like coincidence. Why did reasoning models arrive first and most convincingly in math and code? Because those are the domains with free verifiers, so the RL that teaches long chain-of-thought has a clean reward signal and the test-time sampling has a clean selection mechanism. Why is "agentic coding" the killer app of the reasoning-model era? Because code execution is a verifier you can run thousands of times for pennies. And why is open-ended chat still served mostly by single-sample generation from large models rather than heavy test-time search? Because in chat there is no verifier, so the second axis mostly does not pay.

### The diminishing-returns caveat, applied to the second axis

There is a symmetry here that the test-time-scaling enthusiasts sometimes skip, and honesty requires stating it. The test-time axis is itself a power law, which means it has its own diminishing returns. o1's accuracy is log-linear in test-time compute: each additional fixed increment of accuracy costs an *exponentially* growing number of samples. Going from 74% to 83% on AIME took moving from one sample to a majority vote of 64; squeezing out the next ten points to 93% took a learned reranker over 1,000 samples. That is more than a tenfold increase in compute for the second batch of points. The same exponentiated-power-law form that makes repeated sampling work also guarantees that the marginal sample is worth exponentially less than the one before it.

So the test-time axis is not the escape from diminishing returns that the breathless framing suggests. It is a *second* curve with diminishing returns, which is valuable precisely because we are early on it and the early gains are cheap — exactly as the pre-training axis was cheap in 2018 and expensive by 2024. The reason to be excited about the second axis is not that it is exempt from the laws; it is that it is a fresh axis we had not exploited, sitting next to one that had saturated. Both will bend. The skill is knowing which axis is currently cheaper per marginal point of capability for your specific problem.

There is a deeper symmetry between the two axes that makes the wall debate look almost quaint in hindsight. The pre-training axis and the test-time axis are *both* ways of spending FLOPs to lower the expected loss on the next token, and both are governed by power laws with finite exponents. Pre-training spends the FLOPs ahead of time and bakes the result into the weights; test-time spending defers the FLOPs to the moment of the query and applies them to that specific input. Neither is magic, and neither is infinite. When people ask "did pre-training hit a wall," the more precise question is "has the pre-training axis become more expensive per marginal point of capability than the test-time axis, in the domains we care about?" — and the answer to *that* is clearly yes for verifiable domains, regardless of whether the pre-training loss law literally broke. The pivot to inference scaling is rational even under the optimistic reading of the wall debate, because the test-time axis was simply the cheaper place to spend the next dollar. That is the reframing that dissolves the argument: you do not need to resolve whether pre-training hit a wall to explain why the field pivoted; you only need to observe that a fresh, cheaper axis opened next to it.

Where this gets genuinely hard is forecasting *when* the test-time axis bends sharply enough that the pre-training axis becomes the better buy again. Nobody has a published cross-over law for this, because it depends on the domain, the verifier quality, and the base model's coverage profile — all of which vary wildly. The honest engineering posture is to treat both axes as live, measure the marginal return on each for your actual workload, and reallocate as the curves move. Betting the entire roadmap on the test-time axis would be repeating, in the opposite direction, exactly the mistake the pure-pre-training maximalists are accused of.

> Test-time compute is not a loophole in scaling laws. It is a second scaling law, with its own exponent and its own knee. We pivoted to it because it was the unexploited axis, not because it is infinite.

## The synthesis: small over-trained model plus test-time compute

This is the punchline that ties the whole series together, and it is the part I am most confident about because it rests entirely on peer-reviewed results rather than on reporting. The figure below is the synthesis in one picture: take a small, heavily over-trained base model — the [Beyond-Chinchilla](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/inference-aware-scaling-laws) recommendation, cheap at roughly $2N$ FLOPs per token — and spend the savings on test-time compute. If a verifier exists, that combination can match a large compute-optimal model at lower total serving cost. If no verifier exists, it plateaus.

![A flow diagram showing a small over-trained Beyond-Chinchilla base combined with test-time compute, passing through a verifier check that either matches a large model at lower cost or plateaus when no verifier is available](/imgs/blogs/pretraining-wall-inference-scaling-7.png)

Let me assemble the argument piece by piece, because each piece is a result from an earlier post and the synthesis is the product of stacking them.

### Piece one: the base should be small and over-trained

[Beyond-Chinchilla](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/inference-aware-scaling-laws) (Sardana and Frankle et al. 2023) showed that once you account for lifetime inference cost, the optimal model is smaller than Chinchilla and trained on far more tokens. The objective is to minimize $6 N D_{tr} + 2 N D_{inf}$ subject to hitting a target loss, where $D_{inf}$ is the lifetime inference token count. Because model size $N$ multiplies *both* the training and inference terms, shrinking $N$ saves on every inference forward pass forever, paid for by more training tokens to hold quality. For a 30B-Chinchilla-quality target at $10^{13}$ inference tokens, the cost-optimal model is about 13.6B parameters trained on roughly 2.84 times Chinchilla data, cutting total FLOPs by about 28%. In practice the field has gone much further: LLaMA-3 8B was trained on 15T tokens, about 1,875 tokens per parameter, roughly 94 times the Chinchilla ratio. The result is a small model that is unusually good for its size and very cheap to run.

### Piece two: a cheap base makes test-time compute affordable

Here is where the pieces lock together. The whole reason test-time compute is economically interesting is that you are spending many forward passes per query — five, fifty, a thousand samples. The cost of that is linear in the per-token inference cost, which is $2N$. So a small over-trained model is not just cheaper to serve once; it is the *enabling condition* for heavy test-time compute, because a thousand samples from an 8B model can cost less than a single sample from a 175B model. The Beyond-Chinchilla recommendation and the repeated-sampling recommendation are not two separate ideas — they are the same idea read from two directions. Size the model small so each sample is cheap, then take many samples to recover and exceed the quality you gave up by shrinking it.

The Large Language Monkeys paper makes this concrete with FLOPs-matched comparisons: Llama-3-8B with repeated sampling beats Llama-3-70B at fixed total FLOPs on MiniF2F, GSM8K, and MATH. That is the synthesis in a single experiment. The small model, given the same compute budget spent on many samples instead of on more parameters, wins — in verifiable domains. The compute-optimal test-time work shows the same thing from the search angle: on problems where a small base has non-trivial success, test-time compute lets it outperform a model fourteen times larger at matched FLOPs.

The arithmetic of why the budgets line up is worth spelling out, because it is the quantitative core of the synthesis. Per-token inference cost is $2N$ FLOPs. For an 8B model that is $1.6 \times 10^{10}$ FLOPs per token; for a 70B model it is $1.4 \times 10^{11}$ FLOPs per token — a ratio of roughly 8.75 to 1. So for the cost of generating one token from the 70B model you can generate close to nine tokens from the 8B model, and for the cost of one full 70B response you can afford on the order of nine 8B responses of the same length. Nine samples is already deep into the steep part of the coverage curve for a model whose pass@1 is in the 25-30% range: the coverage of nine independent tries at $p = 0.27$ is $1 - (1 - 0.27)^9 \approx 94\%$ on the problems where $p = 0.27$, and even averaged across a difficulty mix the verified solve rate climbs well past the single-sample number. The 70B model gets one shot; the 8B model gets nine, and a verifier to pick the winner. That is the entire mechanism, and it scales: at fixed budget, every factor by which you shrink $N$ is a factor more samples you can afford, and the coverage law turns those samples into solved problems as long as the verifier holds.

### Piece three: the verifier is the precondition, not a detail

Everything above carries the asterisk we spent the whole previous section on. "Llama-3-8B with repeated sampling beats Llama-3-70B" is true on MiniF2F and GSM8K and MATH *because those have verifiers*. The pass@k coverage of the small model is high, and the verifier converts that coverage into solve rate. Take away the verifier and the comparison inverts: the 70B model's single sample is better than the 8B model's best-of-many, because without a verifier the 8B model cannot reliably pick its own best sample, and majority voting over a weak model's samples just amplifies its most common mistake.

This is why I keep insisting the synthesis is gated, not universal. The honest one-sentence version is: *in verifiable domains, a small over-trained model plus test-time compute beats a large compute-optimal model at lower cost; in open-ended domains, you are back to needing the bigger pre-trained model, because there is no verifier to make the cheap-samples strategy pay.* The frontier is genuinely moving from "make the pre-trained model bigger" to "make a right-sized model think longer" — but only as fast as we can build verifiers for the domains we care about.

### Piece four: where the bigger model still wins

For balance, I want to be explicit about the regime where pre-training still beats test-time compute, because the synthesis is a tradeoff surface, not a slogan. The compute-optimal test-time work found that the small-model-plus-test-time-compute advantage breaks down on the hardest problems and the largest budgets — there, pre-training a bigger model is still better. The intuition is that test-time compute amplifies capability the base model already has latent; if the base model has essentially zero chance of producing a correct sample, no amount of resampling helps, because coverage starts and stays at zero. A bigger pre-trained model raises the floor of what is reachable at all, and on the hardest tail that floor is what matters. So the right picture is not "test-time compute replaces pre-training." It is "test-time compute is the cheaper axis in the easy-to-medium regime and the small-budget regime; pre-training is still the axis that wins the hardest tail and the largest budgets."

| Regime | Cheaper axis | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Easy/medium problems, verifier available | test-time compute on a small over-trained model | coverage is high, verifier converts it cheaply |
| Hardest-tail problems | pre-training a bigger model | base coverage is ~0; resampling cannot help |
| Open-ended, no verifier | bigger pre-trained model, single sample | selection plateaus, cheap samples do not pay |
| Largest budgets | pre-training | test-time returns diminish faster at scale |
| High-traffic, verifiable deployment | small base + adaptive test-time compute | minimizes lifetime serving cost |

## Worked example: matching a large model with a small one

Let me make the synthesis concrete with numbers, using the constants and results from the earlier posts so nothing here is invented. Suppose you need to solve competition-math-style problems and you have a verifier — a checker that confirms a final numeric answer. You are choosing between two deployments at a fixed inference budget.

Deployment A is a single large model, call it a 70B-class model, run once per problem. Its cost per problem is one forward pass at $2N = 140$ GFLOPs per token, times the answer length. Call that one unit of cost per problem, and say it solves some baseline fraction of problems — on MATH, a strong large model single-sample might land in the 40-50% range.

Deployment B is a small over-trained 8B-class model run many times per problem with the verifier picking any correct sample. Its cost per sample is $2N = 16$ GFLOPs per token — about one-ninth the per-token cost of the 70B model. So at the same total budget you can afford roughly nine samples from the 8B model for every one sample from the 70B model. From the Large Language Monkeys curves, Llama-3-8B on MATH goes from 26.6% pass@1 toward 79.8% pass@10k; the coverage climbs steeply over the first few hundred samples. At nine to a few dozen samples — well within the budget that one 70B sample buys — the 8B model's verified solve rate crosses the 70B model's single-sample rate. That is the FLOPs-matched crossover the paper reports on MiniF2F, GSM8K, and MATH, restated as a budget decision.

Now change one assumption and watch the whole thing collapse. Remove the verifier — say the task is open-ended explanation rather than a checkable answer. Deployment B can still generate nine samples, but now it must *select* one without a checker, using majority voting or a reward model. Its effective solve rate stops tracking coverage and plateaus; the nine samples no longer convert to nine chances at a correct answer, because the model cannot reliably identify which sample is correct. Deployment A's single large-model sample, which never depended on selection, is now the better choice. Same models, same budget, opposite conclusion — the verifier is the entire difference.

The lesson from the worked example is the one to carry into your own roadmap: the FLOPs-matched advantage of the small-plus-sampling strategy is real and large, but it is *contingent* on a verifier converting coverage into solved problems. Audit your domain for a verifier before you bet the architecture on test-time compute.

It is also worth carrying the dollar version of the comparison, because FLOPs and dollars diverge in a way that *strengthens* the small-model case. As the [inference-aware scaling post](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/inference-aware-scaling-laws) showed, inference runs at far lower hardware utilization than training — the Beyond-Chinchilla analysis uses roughly a 50-times model-FLOPs-utilization gap — and small models tend to be easier to serve at high utilization than very large ones, which spill across more devices and pay more communication overhead. So the real serving-cost ratio between the 8B and 70B models is often larger than the clean $2N$ FLOP ratio suggests, which means you can usually afford *more* than nine samples per 70B-equivalent dollar, not fewer. The dollar economics tilt the synthesis further toward the small-plus-sampling strategy than the FLOP arithmetic alone, in exactly the verifiable, high-traffic regime where the strategy already wins. The same gap is why a Chinchilla-70B can cost about 36% more than the cost-optimal model at around 2T inference tokens despite being only about 1.3% off compute-optimal — dollars punish large $N$ harder than FLOPs do.

## The 2024-2025 pivot, in sequence

It helps to see the pivot as a sequence of events rather than a single moment, because the wall debate and the test-time-scaling breakthroughs were happening in parallel and feeding each other. The timeline below lays out the twelve months in which the field's center of gravity moved from pre-training scale to test-time reasoning compute.

![A timeline of six events from March 2022 to January 2025 showing Chinchilla, Beyond-Chinchilla, Large Language Monkeys, OpenAI o1, the pre-training wall reports, and DeepSeek-R1](/imgs/blogs/pretraining-wall-inference-scaling-6.png)

The ordering is the story. [Chinchilla](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/chinchilla-compute-optimal-scaling) in March 2022 set the compute-optimal baseline of about 20 tokens per parameter. [Beyond-Chinchilla](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/inference-aware-scaling-laws) in January 2024 said to over-train and shrink for inference cost — the first crack in the "bigger pre-trained model" reflex. [Large Language Monkeys](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/repeated-sampling-scaling-laws) in July 2024 turned repeated sampling into a scaling law. OpenAI's o1 in September 2024 showed RL could teach a model to use test-time compute productively, with log-linear curves on both training and inference compute. The wall reports landed in November 2024, in the middle of all this — which is exactly why they did not read as "AI is over" to people paying attention: the second axis was visibly taking off at the same moment. And DeepSeek-R1 in January 2025 closed the loop by reproducing the o1 paradigm in the open with pure RL, proving the recipe was general and not a single lab's secret.

Notice that four of the six events are about the test-time axis or sizing for it, and only one is about the wall. The narrative that "pre-training hit a wall" got the headlines, but the substance of the year was the opening of the second axis. The wall, real or not, was the backdrop; the pivot to inference scaling was the event.

### Why the open reproduction mattered so much

It is worth dwelling on why DeepSeek-R1 was such a load-bearing moment for this synthesis. o1 was a closed model with a blog post and no paper; you could observe that test-time scaling worked but you could not inspect *how*, and a skeptic could reasonably wonder whether it was a genuine paradigm or a one-lab trick with a lot of hand-tuning behind the curtain. R1-Zero answered that decisively: pure reinforcement learning on a base model, with rule-based rewards and no supervised fine-tuning, *spontaneously* grew the reasoning length and produced the self-correction behavior. The test-time scaling emerged from the RL alone. That is the strongest possible evidence that the second axis is a real property of the training-plus-inference setup and not an artifact — it showed up unbidden, in an open reproduction, the moment you applied RL with a verifiable reward. The verifiable reward is, again, the precondition: R1-Zero's clean signal came from math and code, the domains with free verifiers.

## Case studies: where the two axes met reality

Abstract arguments about scaling axes become much clearer when you trace specific episodes where one axis or the other proved decisive. Each of the following is grounded in a result from this series or in the public record of the pivot; together they map the terrain of where small-plus-test-time wins, where pre-training still rules, and where the wall debate gets its evidence.

### 1. SWE-bench: the verifier that made sampling pay

The cleanest demonstration of the synthesis is the Large Language Monkeys result on SWE-bench Lite. DeepSeek-Coder-V2-Instruct, a model that was not the largest available, solved 15.9% of issues with a single sample. That is unremarkable. With 250 samples and the repository's own test suite as the verifier, it solved 56% — beating the 43% single-sample state of the art held by a much more expensive model. The lesson is that the verifier did the heavy lifting: SWE-bench ships with executable tests, so every one of the 250 candidate patches could be checked exactly and for pennies, and coverage converted directly to solve rate. The wrong first hypothesis here is "the bigger model is smarter, so it wins." The actual root cause of the win is that code execution is a free, exact verifier, which turns a mediocre single-sample model into a strong system when you let it try many times. Remove the test suite and the result evaporates.

### 2. The Pythia-160M existence proof

The most provocative single data point in the repeated-sampling work is Pythia-160M on MATH: a tiny model, two orders of magnitude smaller than a frontier model, going from 0.27% single-sample accuracy to roughly 57% over 10,000 samples. Taken naively this looks like magic — a model that is almost useless becoming half-decent purely by trying more. The mechanism is the coverage law: even at $p \approx 0.003$ per sample, 10,000 tries gives a meaningful chance of hitting the answer on some problems, and the answer key verifies which try was right. The lesson is double-edged. On one hand it proves that verified test-time compute can extract latent capability from astonishingly small models. On the other hand it is a warning: this only works because MATH has an answer key, and it tells you nothing about whether a 160M model could write a good essay, because there is no answer key for essays. The existence proof is real and the caveat is total.

### 3. Orion / GPT-4.5: the smaller jump heard around the world

The model that became GPT-4.5 is the single most-cited piece of wall evidence, and it is instructive precisely because of how thin the public record is. What we know from The Information's reporting is qualitative: the jump over GPT-4 was smaller than GPT-4's jump over GPT-3, and the gains did not justify the cost. What we do not know — because it was never published — is the loss-versus-compute curve, the exact compute multiple, or whether the smaller jump reflects a broken law or simply the saturation-plus-log-axis effect the critics describe. The wrong first hypothesis is to treat the reporting as a measurement. The honest reading is that a credible source with partial access reported a disappointing run, which is a meaningful signal about ROI and a near-useless signal about the loss law. The episode is best understood as the moment the industry's *strategy* shifted toward post-training and test-time methods, not as proof that pre-training scaling broke.

### 4. o1 and the log-linear test-time curve

OpenAI's o1 was the proof of concept that test-time compute is a scaling axis you can drive with training. Its accuracy on AIME climbed from 74% at a single sample to 83% with a majority vote of 64 and 93% with a learned reranker over 1,000 samples, against a GPT-4o baseline of 12% — and both the train-time-RL and test-time-compute curves were log-linear. The case study within the case study is the *cost* of those last ten points: going from 83% to 93% required moving from 64 samples to over 1,000, more than a tenfold increase in compute for the final stretch. That is the diminishing-returns caveat made concrete. o1 is simultaneously the best evidence that the second axis is real and the best evidence that it obeys its own power law. AIME, of course, has answer keys — the reranker over 1,000 samples is a verifier in disguise.

### 5. DeepSeek-R1-Zero: emergence from pure RL

R1-Zero is the case study that turned a proprietary result into a reproducible paradigm. Starting from the DeepSeek-V3 base, with no supervised fine-tuning and only rule-based outcome rewards under GRPO, the model spontaneously grew its response length during RL and developed a self-reflective "aha moment" where it revisited and corrected its own reasoning. AIME pass@1 went from 15.6% to 71.0%, and consensus over 64 samples reached 86.7%, matching OpenAI-o1-0912. The lesson is mechanistic: test-time scaling is not something you bolt on at inference; it can *emerge* from training, specifically from RL with a verifiable reward. The reason it emerged in math and code and not in open-ended chat is the same reason it always is — those domains have free, exact rewards to optimize against. R1-Zero is the synthesis's proof that the second axis is general, plus its proof that verification is the gate, in a single experiment.

### 6. FLOPs-matched: 8B-plus-sampling beats 70B

The experiment that most directly states the synthesis is the FLOPs-matched comparison in the repeated-sampling work: Llama-3-8B with repeated sampling beats Llama-3-70B at fixed total FLOPs on MiniF2F, GSM8K, and MATH. This is the whole argument in one row of a table. Given a fixed compute budget, you can spend it on more parameters (the 70B model, one sample) or on more samples from a smaller model (the 8B model, many samples), and in verifiable domains the second allocation wins. The wrong intuition is that parameters are always the better place to spend FLOPs. The actual result is that, behind a verifier, samples from a cheap model are a better buy than parameters — until the coverage curve's knee, and until you hit problems the 8B base cannot represent at all, at which point the 70B floor matters again.

### 7. LLaMA-3 8B: over-training as quiet consensus

The clearest sign that the field already internalized the Beyond-Chinchilla half of the synthesis is LLaMA-3 8B, trained on 15T tokens — about 1,875 tokens per parameter, roughly 94 times the Chinchilla ratio of 20. Nobody called this a test-time-scaling decision at the time, but it is the enabling condition for one: an 8B model this thoroughly over-trained is unusually capable for its size and extremely cheap to run, which is exactly the base you want if you intend to sample it many times. The case study is that the two halves of the synthesis arrived independently — Beyond-Chinchilla as a sizing argument, repeated sampling as an inference argument — and only in hindsight are they obviously the same strategy. The industry built the cheap base before it fully understood why it would want one.

### 8. The open-ended plateau: where the strategy fails

The negative case study is as important as the positive ones. Take the same repeated-sampling machinery and point it at an open-ended task — drafting a product description, giving general advice, writing prose — and the curve that climbed over four orders of magnitude on MATH flattens after a few hundred samples. The reason is mechanical: with no verifier you must select with majority voting or a reward model, and both saturate. Majority voting over open-ended generations often selects the blandest common answer rather than the best one; reward models are themselves fallible and exploitable, and past a few hundred samples you start selecting their errors rather than your model's successes. The cost, meanwhile, keeps climbing linearly in the number of samples. This is the case that disciplines all the others: it shows that the synthesis is not a general law of inference compute but a specific property of verifiable domains.

### 9. The data wall as a binding constraint

The final case study is the one piece of the wall thesis that survives scrutiny intact. Frontier models training on 15T tokens are consuming a few percent of the entire high-quality public text stock per run, and Chinchilla scaling says larger models want proportionally more data. There is no second internet. This is a genuine, quantitative constraint on the $D$ term, and it is the strongest reason to expect pure pre-training scaling to get more expensive over time. The case study's lesson is the bridge to the synthesis: the escape from the data wall, in the domains that have it, is exactly the verifiable-reward machinery that powers test-time scaling. RL in math and code manufactures and labels fresh data for free, which is why the data wall bites hardest on the open human internet and barely at all where a verifier can generate training signal on demand.

## What this means in practice

**Senior rule of thumb: do not bet the roadmap on a single scaling axis, and never spend test-time compute where you cannot grade the output.** The figure below is the decision flow I would actually run when sizing a system today, and it folds together every result in this series.

![A pipeline showing the decision flow to forecast inference demand, size a small over-trained base, check whether a verifier exists, scale test-time compute where it pays, and route per query by difficulty](/imgs/blogs/pretraining-wall-inference-scaling-8.png)

The first step is to forecast lifetime inference demand and set a quality target, because that is what determines how far to over-train the base — the Beyond-Chinchilla calculation. The second step is to size a small, heavily over-trained base model so each inference token is cheap, which is the enabling condition for everything downstream. The third step is the gate: does a verifier exist for this domain? If yes, scale test-time compute — repeated sampling, search, or RL-trained long chain-of-thought — until the marginal sample stops paying, which it will, because the coverage curve has a knee. If no verifier exists, do not pour compute into sampling; you will hit the selection plateau and burn money. The last step is to route per query: easy problems get sequential revision, hard problems get parallel search, and the hardest tail — where the small base has near-zero coverage — falls back to a bigger model, because no amount of test-time compute rescues a base that cannot produce a correct sample at all.

Here is how I would translate that into concrete engineering decisions.

### Reach for the small-plus-test-time strategy when

- **Your domain has a cheap, exact verifier.** Code with a test suite, math with an answer key, formal proofs with a checker, structured extraction you can validate against a schema. This is the precondition; everything else is secondary to it.
- **You serve high traffic.** The Beyond-Chinchilla math only swings toward the small over-trained model when $D_{inf}$ is large. For a high-traffic deployment the lifetime inference term dwarfs training, so a small cheap base plus many samples is dramatically cheaper than a large model.
- **The problems are easy-to-medium for your base model.** Test-time compute amplifies latent capability; it does best where the base already has non-trivial coverage and the verifier just needs to find the right sample in the pile.
- **You can route adaptively.** If you can classify prompt difficulty cheaply, you can spend test-time compute where it pays (hard-but-reachable problems) and skip it where it does not (trivial problems that one sample solves, impossible problems that no sampling solves).

### Skip it — and lean on a bigger pre-trained model — when

- **You cannot verify the output.** Open-ended generation, subjective quality, advice without ground truth. Without a verifier, selection plateaus and the second axis mostly buys you a larger bill. Serve these with a strong single-sample model.
- **The hardest tail is what matters.** If your value is concentrated in the problems a small base cannot touch at all, raise the floor by pre-training bigger; resampling a model with zero coverage yields zero solves.
- **Traffic is low and latency is tight.** Test-time compute trades latency and per-query cost for quality. A low-traffic, latency-sensitive service may be better served by a single forward pass from a sufficiently large model.
- **You are tempted to treat one axis as the whole answer.** The deepest practical lesson of this series is that there is no single scaling axis that dominates. Pre-training, data quality, over-training for inference, repeated sampling, search, and RL-trained reasoning are all axes with their own laws and their own diminishing returns. The engineering skill is matching the axis to the regime, not picking a favorite.
- **Your verifier is a learned reward model you have not stress-tested.** A fallible verifier is worse than an honest single sample, because heavy sampling against a gameable reward model will find and exploit its blind spots. If the only verifier you have is itself a model, validate its precision before you scale samples against it, and cap the sample count well short of where reward-hacking dominates.

There is a meta-lesson hiding in all of this that I want to state plainly, because it is the through-line of the whole series. Every time the field found a scaling axis, it overshot — it treated that axis as the whole game until the diminishing returns became undeniable, then pivoted to the next axis and overshot again. Kaplan said spend on parameters; Chinchilla said no, balance with data; Beyond-Chinchilla said no, over-train for inference; the reasoning-model wave said no, spend at test time. Each correction was right, and each was then over-applied. The mature posture is not to pick the current favorite axis but to hold all of them as instruments and measure the marginal return on each for the specific workload in front of you. The labs that win the next phase will be the ones that route compute across axes adaptively rather than the ones that bet everything on whichever axis is fashionable this year.

### A note on the data wall as a real constraint

I have argued that the "pre-training wall" is overstated as a claim about scaling laws breaking, but I do not want to leave the impression that the data wall is fake. It is the realest part of the whole debate. High-quality human text is genuinely finite, repetition helps only up to a few epochs before returns collapse, and you cannot conjure more Shakespeare or more Stack Overflow by spending more GPUs. What the test-time-scaling track adds is a partial escape: in verifiable domains, you can *generate* training data and *verify* it for free, which is exactly what RL with verifiable rewards does. The model produces rollouts, the verifier labels them, and the good ones become training signal. That is a renewable data source in the domains that have verifiers — which is, once again, why math and code led the reasoning-model wave. The data wall is real for the open human internet; it is much softer wherever a verifier can manufacture and grade fresh data.

The asymmetry between the two kinds of data is the quiet engine of the whole pivot. Human internet text is a *stock* — a fixed deposit you draw down — while verifiable rollouts are a *flow* you can generate on demand, bounded only by compute and the quality of your verifier. A field that has exhausted the cheap stock will naturally route its next investments toward the flow, and that is precisely what the reasoning-model wave is: a redirection of compute from consuming a finite human corpus toward generating and grading an effectively unbounded synthetic one, in the domains where grading is cheap. This reframes the data wall from a dead end into a forcing function. It pushed the field to invent the verifiable-reward machinery that, not coincidentally, also powers the test-time-scaling axis. The constraint and the escape are two faces of the same coin, which is why the timeline puts the wall reports and the reasoning-model breakthroughs in the same twelve months.

A sober caveat keeps this from becoming its own piece of hype: synthetic and self-generated data are renewable only in proportion to the verifier's reach. Where the verifier is exact and cheap — unit tests, proof checkers, answer keys — the flow is genuinely abundant. Where the verifier is a learned reward model, the flow is contaminated by the reward model's own errors, and training on it risks reinforcing those errors rather than correcting them. So the renewable-data escape from the data wall inherits exactly the same gate as test-time scaling: it is clean where verification is clean and murky everywhere else. There is no general escape from the data wall, only a domain-specific one, and the boundary of that domain is drawn by the verifier.

### Closing the loop on the whole series

Step back and the arc of this series is a single argument with a twist at the end. The early posts established that loss is predictable from compute — [Hestness](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/scaling-laws-predictability-foundations), Kaplan, Chinchilla — and that the right way to spend a training budget is roughly equal scaling of model and data at about 20 tokens per parameter. The middle posts complicated that with the realities of [finite data](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/data-constrained-scaling-laws), [data quality](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/data-quality-scaling-laws), and the discovery that if you are going to *serve* the model you should over-train a smaller one. The late posts opened an entirely new axis — spending compute at inference — and showed it is a scaling law in its own right.

This post is the synthesis: the pre-training axis may have slowed (the evidence is real but soft and non-falsifiable), a second axis opened at the same moment (the evidence is published and strong), and the two combine into a strategy that is cheaper and at least as capable as the old one — small over-trained base plus heavy test-time compute — gated entirely by whether you can verify the answer. The frontier did not hit a wall. It turned a corner. And the thing that decides which side of the corner your problem lands on is not the size of your model. It is whether you can grade its work.
