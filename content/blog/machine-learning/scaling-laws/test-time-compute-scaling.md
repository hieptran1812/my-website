For a decade the only knob anyone reached for to make a language model smarter was *size*. You wanted better answers, you trained a bigger model on more tokens, you paid the training bill, and you shipped it. The model that came out the other end was a fixed object: every prompt got exactly one forward pass per token, every query cost the same, and the quality was baked in at training time. The model that solves a trivial arithmetic question and the model that fails a hard competition problem are, in that world, the same model spending the same compute. That is a strange way to run an intelligence. No human works that way. We glance at an easy question and answer it; we sit with a hard one, try an approach, notice it is wrong, back up, and try another. The amount of thinking we do is a function of how hard the problem is. The diagram below is the mental model for this entire post: a *frozen* model can spend extra compute at inference time along two different axes, and the right axis to spend on depends on how hard the prompt in front of it actually is.

![A branching diagram showing a frozen base model spending test-time compute along a sequential revision axis and a verifier-guided search axis, both feeding a compute-optimal allocation step that selects a final answer](/imgs/blogs/test-time-compute-scaling-1.png)

That reframing is the subject of Snell et al. 2024, "Scaling LLM Test-Time Compute Optimally can be More Effective than Scaling Model Parameters" (arXiv:2408.03314), out of Google DeepMind and UC Berkeley. The title is a deliberate provocation, and the paper earns it with a careful, difficulty-stratified study rather than a single hero number. The argument has three moving parts. First, there are two genuinely different ways to spend inference compute: you can *search* over many candidate answers and let a verifier pick the best, or you can *revise* a single answer over and over, letting the model edit its own work. Second, which of these two is the better use of a fixed compute budget is not fixed — it depends on the difficulty of the specific prompt, and a policy that routes each prompt to the right strategy is **more than four times more efficient** than naively running best-of-N everywhere. Third, and most provocatively, once you allocate test-time compute optimally, a small model can match the accuracy of a model **fourteen times larger** at the same total FLOPs — but only in the easy-to-medium regime. On the hardest problems and at the largest budgets, no amount of clever inference rescues a small model, and pre-training a bigger one is still the right call.

> [!important] The one number to remember: compute-optimal allocation is >4x more efficient than best-of-N
> - **There are two axes of test-time compute.** The *verifier axis* searches over many candidate solutions and scores them with a process reward model: best-of-N, beam search, and lookahead search live here. The *proposal axis* trains the model to sequentially revise its own answer, fixing earlier mistakes one attempt at a time. They are orthogonal, and you can combine them.
> - **The optimal axis depends on prompt difficulty.** Easy prompts favor sequential revision — the model's first answer is close, so local edits pay off. Hard prompts favor parallel search — the model needs many diverse attempts and a verifier to find the rare good one. A single fixed strategy is never optimal across the difficulty range.
> - **Routing each prompt to its best strategy is more than 4x more efficient than a uniform best-of-N baseline** — the same accuracy at roughly a quarter of the samples. The paper calls this "compute-optimal" allocation.
> - **FLOPs-matched, test-time compute can beat a 14x larger model** on problems where the small base model already has a non-trivial success rate. Spend the saved parameters on inference instead.
> - **But test-time compute is not a universal substitute for parameters.** On the hardest problems and at the largest budgets, the small-model-plus-search advantage collapses and pre-training a bigger model wins. Test-time scaling has a ceiling that difficulty sets.
> - **There is no single clean scaling formula here.** The result is a two-dimensional tradeoff surface — test-time FLOPs against pre-training FLOPs — where the better axis depends on the difficulty bin and the total budget. The practical takeaway is *adaptive per-query compute routing*, not a one-line power law.
> - **Everything here rides on having a verifier.** Search only converts extra samples into solved problems when something can tell the good answer from the bad one. This is the same gate as in [repeated sampling](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/repeated-sampling-scaling-laws): no verifier, no clean scaling.

## Why this is different from "just make the model bigger"

The cleanest way to feel the shift is to write down the old mental model and the new one side by side and notice that they answer different questions. The pre-training scaling laws — [Kaplan](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/kaplan-scaling-laws-language-models), [Chinchilla](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/chinchilla-compute-optimal-scaling) — all answer: *given a training budget, how do I get the lowest loss?* They optimize the model you ship. Test-time scaling answers a question the pre-training laws never asked: *given a model I have already shipped, how much extra compute should I spend per query, and on what?* The first is a question about manufacturing; the second is a question about operating.

| Question | Pre-training scaling | Test-time scaling |
|---|---|---|
| What is being optimized? | The model's parameters $N$ and training tokens $D$ | The inference policy for a *fixed* model |
| When is the cost paid? | Once, at training time | Per query, every time, forever |
| Does the cost depend on the prompt? | No — every token costs the same | Yes — hard prompts can get more compute |
| What is the free variable? | The split of a training budget between $N$ and $D$ | How many samples / revisions per prompt, and which |
| What sets the ceiling? | The Chinchilla loss curve | Prompt difficulty and verifier quality |
| Adaptive per-instance? | No — the model is one fixed object | Yes — route each prompt to its best strategy |

Look at the third row, because it is the whole game. Pre-training produces a model that spends identical compute on every prompt: the autoregressive forward pass costs roughly $2N$ FLOPs per generated token whether the token is the answer to "what is 2 plus 2" or a step in an olympiad proof. That uniformity is *wasteful* on easy prompts and *insufficient* on hard ones. Test-time compute breaks the uniformity. It lets you spend almost nothing on the trivial query and a great deal on the brutal one — and, crucially, it lets you spend that extra compute in qualitatively different ways depending on what the prompt needs.

> If you take one thing from this post: a fixed model is not a fixed level of intelligence. How hard it thinks — and how it thinks — is a knob you turn at inference time, and turning it well is worth a multiplicative factor in effective model size.

There is a second-order reason this matters economically, and it ties directly back to the [inference-aware scaling laws](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/inference-aware-scaling-laws). That post argued that if you are going to serve a model heavily, you should pre-train a *smaller* model on *more* tokens, because inference cost is linear in $N$ and you pay it forever. Test-time scaling is the natural sequel: if you have deliberately shipped a small, cheap-per-token model, you now have spare FLOPs in your inference budget to spend on *thinking longer* per query. The two ideas compose. A small over-trained model plus a well-tuned test-time policy can land at the same quality as a large compute-optimal model, at lower total serving cost — provided, always, that a verifier exists to make the search pay off.

## 1. The two axes: search and revision

**Senior rule of thumb: "more test-time compute" is not one knob — it is at least two, and they are orthogonal.** The single most common mistake people make when they first hear "spend more compute at inference" is to assume it means one thing: sample more answers and vote. That is one axis, and not always the best one. Snell et al. organize the entire space around a clean distinction between *modifying the proposal distribution* and *optimizing the verifier*.

The **verifier axis** keeps the model's output distribution fixed and spends compute searching through it. You draw many candidate solutions (or many candidate partial solutions) from the unchanged model, and you use a *verifier* — typically a process reward model, or PRM, that scores the correctness of each reasoning step — to decide which candidate to keep. Best-of-N is the simplest instance: sample $N$ complete solutions, score each, keep the highest. Beam search is the stepwise version: at each reasoning step, keep only the top-scoring partial solutions and expand them. Lookahead search goes further, rolling out a few steps into the future before committing to a branch. All three share one model and one verifier; they differ only in *when* they prune.

The **proposal axis** does the opposite: it changes the model's output distribution itself so that the model produces *better answers on its own*, conditioned on its previous attempts. Concretely, you fine-tune the model to take its own prior answer as context and emit a revised one — a sequential self-correction loop. The first attempt might be wrong; the second is conditioned on the first and tends to be closer; the third closer still. This is not search over independent samples — it is a single chain of dependent edits, each one a proposal drawn from a distribution the model has been trained to make better than its base.

These two axes are genuinely different operations, and the figure above makes the point that they both feed a single allocation decision. You can run them separately, and you can also combine them: revise a few times to get a strong candidate, then sample several revisions in parallel and verify. The reason the distinction matters so much is that **the two axes have different strengths on different problems**, which is the subject of the next two sections. But first, it is worth getting precise about the verifier axis, because it has internal structure of its own.

### What a process reward model actually scores

A PRM is itself a model — usually a fine-tuned copy of the base — trained to output, for a partial solution, an estimate of whether continuing from here leads to a correct final answer. The difference from an *outcome* reward model (ORM), which only scores complete solutions, is that a PRM provides a signal at *every step*. That stepwise signal is what makes beam search and lookahead possible: you cannot prune a partial solution if you can only score complete ones. The PRM is trained on per-step correctness labels, which in the math setting can be generated automatically by Monte Carlo rollouts (sample many completions from each prefix, label the prefix by the fraction that reach the right answer) rather than by hand. The quality of the PRM is the silent ceiling on the whole verifier axis: a noisy PRM prunes good branches and keeps bad ones, and search becomes a random walk.

It is worth stating plainly that the PRM is a *learned* verifier, not a *sound* one. In a domain with a sound verifier — code with unit tests, a formal proof checker — the verifier never lies, and search converts samples into solved problems cleanly, exactly as the [repeated sampling](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/repeated-sampling-scaling-laws) work showed for pass@k. A PRM is a probabilistic estimate of correctness, so the verifier axis here is bounded by how well that estimate tracks the truth. This is why the test-time scaling story is cleanest in math and code, and murkier in open-ended generation where no verifier — sound or learned — is reliable.

### The compute-optimal allocation, written down

It helps to state the objective formally, even though — as Section 7 will argue — it does not collapse to a single clean power law. Let $q$ be a prompt, let $\theta(q)$ be a test-time strategy (a choice of axis, depth, and budget), and let $\text{Acc}(q, \theta(q))$ be the accuracy that strategy achieves on $q$. Snell et al. define the compute-optimal strategy as the one that, for each prompt and a fixed per-prompt budget $N$ of generation samples, maximizes accuracy:

$$\theta^{*}_{q, N} = \arg\max_{\theta} \; \mathbb{E}\!\left[ \, \text{Acc}\big(q, \theta(q)\big) \,\middle|\, \text{budget} = N \, \right]$$

The expectation is over the model's sampling randomness. The key structural fact — the one that makes this more than a tautology — is that $\theta^{*}_{q, N}$ depends on $q$ *through its difficulty*. Two prompts of the same difficulty get the same optimal strategy regardless of their content, and difficulty is the only feature of $q$ that the optimal strategy needs. That is why the deployable approximation replaces $q$ with a difficulty bin $d(q)$ and solves $\theta^{*}_{d, N}$ once per bin, offline, then routes each fresh prompt to its bin's pre-computed strategy. The router is a small table indexed by (estimated difficulty, budget), and the entire empirical contribution is *filling in that table* and showing it beats the uniform best-of-N entry by more than 4x.

A second piece of formalism worth having is the *selection* operator that sits at the end of every verifier-axis strategy. Given a set of $N$ candidate solutions $\{y_1, \dots, y_N\}$ for prompt $q$ and a verifier $V$ that scores each, the three common aggregations are: *best-of-N* (take $\arg\max_i V(y_i)$, the single highest-scored candidate), *majority / self-consistency* (take the most frequent final answer, ignoring $V$), and *weighted majority* (take the answer whose candidates carry the highest summed verifier score, $\arg\max_a \sum_{i: \text{answer}(y_i) = a} V(y_i)$). The paper finds weighted majority — using the PRM's per-answer score as a vote weight — generally dominates plain best-of-N and plain majority, because it pools the evidence across candidates that agree rather than betting everything on the single highest score, which can be a confident outlier. This is a small but practically important detail: the *aggregation* of a fixed candidate set is itself a lever, separate from how the candidates were generated.

## 2. Best-of-N is a baseline, not the goal

**Senior rule of thumb: best-of-N is the thing you should be beating, not the thing you should be doing.** Best-of-N — sample $N$, verify, keep the best — is the default that everyone reaches for, and it is a perfectly reasonable baseline. It is also leaving a large factor on the table, because it spends the same $N$ samples on every prompt regardless of whether the prompt needed them. The whole contribution of compute-optimal allocation is to do better than this default, and the figure below is the before/after.

![A before and after comparison contrasting a uniform best-of-N baseline that spends the full sample budget on every prompt against a compute-optimal allocation that routes budget by difficulty and reaches the same accuracy at about one quarter of the samples](/imgs/blogs/test-time-compute-scaling-2.png)

The left side is the baseline: one strategy (best-of-N), applied uniformly, $N$ samples per prompt no matter what. The right side is compute-optimal allocation: a policy that looks at each prompt, estimates its difficulty, and chooses both *how much* compute to spend and *which axis* to spend it on. The headline result from the paper is that this difficulty-aware allocation reaches the **same accuracy at roughly a quarter of the samples** — a **more than 4x efficiency gain** over the best-of-N baseline. Put differently: if best-of-N needs 64 samples to hit some accuracy, the compute-optimal policy hits it at around 16.

Where does the factor of four come from? It is the sum of two kinds of waste that best-of-N commits. On *easy* prompts, best-of-N samples far more than necessary: the first answer is usually right, so 63 of the 64 samples are pure waste, and a couple of sequential revisions would have nailed it for a fraction of the cost. On *hard* prompts, best-of-N samples independently and only checks final answers, when a stepwise search that prunes bad partial solutions early would have spent the same budget far more productively. Eliminating both wastes — revise the easy ones, search-with-pruning the hard ones, and stop early when you are confident — recovers the 4x.

| Strategy | What it does | Best on | Failure mode |
|---|---|---|---|
| Best-of-N (baseline) | Sample $N$ full solutions, keep highest PRM | Nothing in particular; it is the average | Wastes budget on easy, no pruning on hard |
| Sequential revision | Edit your own answer $k$ times | Easy prompts (first answer is close) | Stalls when revising down a wrong path |
| Beam search | Keep top-$M$ partials per step, prune rest | Hard prompts (early pruning helps) | Beam collapse; needs a good PRM |
| Lookahead search | Roll out $k$ steps before committing | Hardest prompts, large budgets | Expensive per node; diminishing returns |
| Compute-optimal | Route per prompt to the above | Everything — it is the envelope | Needs a difficulty estimate per prompt |

The last row is the one to internalize. Compute-optimal is not a new search algorithm; it is a *meta-policy* that picks among the others based on the prompt. Its accuracy is the upper envelope of all the fixed strategies, and its efficiency gain over any single fixed strategy comes precisely from the fact that no single strategy is best everywhere.

### A worked count of where the 4x hides

Make it concrete with a toy budget. Suppose you have a test set that splits evenly into easy, medium, and hard thirds, and a fixed total budget of 64 effective samples per prompt under the best-of-N baseline. On the easy third, the base model is right on its first try about 80% of the time, and two sequential revisions push that past 95%. So the easy third needs roughly 3 effective samples, not 64 — a 21x local saving. On the medium third, a mix of a few revisions and a handful of parallel samples, say 16 effective samples, matches what 64 best-of-N samples bought. On the hard third, beam search with early pruning at 32 effective samples matches best-of-N at 64. Average those: $(3 + 16 + 32)/3 \approx 17$ effective samples to match a baseline that spent 64 everywhere. That is the 3.8x, and it lands right where the paper's "more than 4x" claim sits. The exact split depends on the difficulty distribution of your traffic, which is exactly why the gain is data-dependent and not a universal constant — but the *shape* of the saving (huge on easy, modest on hard) is robust.

Notice what this toy calculation reveals about the *source* of the gain. Almost all of it comes from the easy third — the 21x local saving there dwarfs the 2x on medium and the 2x on hard. This is a general property of difficulty-aware allocation: the biggest wins come from *not over-spending on easy prompts*, not from clever search on hard ones. The intuition is worth holding onto, because it shapes where you put engineering effort. If your traffic is dominated by easy queries — as most real traffic is — then the single highest-leverage thing you can build is a good "this is easy, stop early" detector, because every easy query you stop early on returns a large multiple of its baseline cost. The exotic deep-search machinery for hard problems is real and matters for worst-case quality, but it moves the average cost far less than the early-stopping rule does. A reasonable order of implementation is therefore: first build early stopping for easy prompts (most of the 4x), then add revision (cheap accuracy on the near-misses), then add search for the hard tail (worst-case quality), then add escalation (the unsolvable residue). Each step has diminishing impact on the average and increasing impact on the tail.

## 3. Difficulty decides the axis

**Senior rule of thumb: the harder the prompt, the more you should bias toward parallel search over sequential revision.** This is the single most important empirical finding in the paper, and it is worth stating carefully because it is easy to get backwards. The result is *not* "revision is better than search" or vice versa. It is that the better axis *flips* as difficulty rises. The matrix below lays it out.

![A matrix showing which test-time strategy wins for easy, medium, and hard difficulty bins, with sequential revision winning on easy prompts, parallel search winning on hard prompts, and a balanced mix on medium prompts](/imgs/blogs/test-time-compute-scaling-3.png)

Read the rows. On **easy prompts** (the lowest difficulty bins), sequential revision wins. The intuition is that on an easy problem the model's first attempt is already close — it has the right method and a small slip — so the productive move is to let the model *look at its own answer and fix the slip*. Spending the budget on many independent fresh samples is redundant, because the fresh samples mostly rediscover the same nearly-correct answer. Revision is a tight local search around a good starting point, and on easy problems the good starting point is right there.

On **hard prompts** (the highest bins), parallel search wins. Here the model's first attempt is usually wrong in a *structural* way — wrong method, wrong decomposition — and revising it just produces a polished version of a wrong answer. The model can stall, "revising" down a doomed path, anchored to its own initial mistake. What helps on hard problems is *diversity*: many independent attempts that explore genuinely different approaches, plus a verifier to recognize the rare good one when it appears. Beam search and lookahead help even more, because they prune the structurally-wrong partial solutions early instead of wasting the budget completing them.

The **medium prompts** in between want a balance — a few revisions to clean up, several parallel samples for coverage, and step-level pruning to keep the search honest. This is where combining both axes pays off the most, because neither axis alone is dominant.

| Difficulty bin | Model's first attempt | Best axis | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Easy (1-2) | Usually right, small slip | Sequential revision | Local edit fixes the slip cheaply |
| Medium (3) | Sometimes right | Mix of both | Revise to clean up, sample for coverage |
| Hard (4-5) | Usually structurally wrong | Parallel search + pruning | Need diverse attempts and a verifier |

There is a subtlety in how "difficulty" is even defined, and it determines whether this is usable in practice. Snell et al. define difficulty in two ways. The *oracle* difficulty bins a problem by the base model's actual pass rate over many samples — bin 1 is the easiest fifth, bin 5 the hardest. That is a clean experimental variable but unusable at deployment, because you do not know the pass rate of a fresh prompt. The *model-predicted* difficulty estimates the bin from the PRM's scores on a small number of initial samples — a cheap proxy you *can* compute at inference. The encouraging finding is that the compute-optimal allocation built on the *predicted* difficulty still captures most of the gain of the oracle version. The routing is robust to a noisy difficulty estimate, which is what makes the whole scheme deployable rather than a thought experiment.

### Why revision stalls on hard problems

It is worth dwelling on the failure mode of revision, because it is non-obvious and it is the mechanistic reason the axis flips. A sequential reviser conditions each new attempt on its previous one. On an easy problem that conditioning is a gift: the previous attempt carries the correct skeleton and the reviser just patches a leaf. On a hard problem the conditioning is a trap: the previous attempt carries a *wrong skeleton*, and the reviser, conditioned on it, tends to produce variations on the same wrong skeleton. It is anchored. The model rarely throws away its own framing and starts over, because nothing in the revision objective rewards a complete restart — the training signal rewards *improving* the prior answer, and "improving" a structurally doomed answer means polishing a corpse. Parallel sampling has no such anchor: each sample is drawn fresh, so the population covers genuinely different framings, and the verifier only has to recognize the one that happens to be right. Diversity, not refinement, is what hard problems reward.

There is a useful way to see the two axes as a single tradeoff between *exploration* and *exploitation*, the oldest dichotomy in search. Parallel sampling is pure exploration: each sample is an independent draw from the model's distribution, so the population spreads across the model's modes — different methods, different framings — at the cost of never deepening any single attempt. Sequential revision is pure exploitation: it picks one mode and refines it, deepening a single attempt at the cost of never leaving its basin. Easy problems reward exploitation because the model's dominant mode is already the right one, so refining it is the fastest path to the answer. Hard problems reward exploration because the model's dominant mode is *wrong*, so the only way to the answer is to draw enough independent samples that one of them lands in a rare correct mode. The difficulty of the prompt is, in this framing, a statement about how much probability mass the model puts on the correct mode — high mass means exploit, low mass means explore — and the optimal axis is just the exploration/exploitation setting that matches that mass. This is also why the *mix* wins on medium problems: when the model puts moderate mass on the correct mode, you want some exploration to find it and some exploitation to refine it, which is exactly a blend of the two axes.

The exploration/exploitation lens also predicts the shape of the revision budget. Because revision is exploitation, its returns diminish fast: the first revision captures most of the available refinement, the second captures less, and by the fourth or fifth the model is mostly rephrasing. This matches the empirical finding that the productive revision depth is small — a handful of steps — after which the budget is better spent on a parallel sample that explores a fresh mode. A well-built policy therefore caps revision depth and, on a stubborn prompt, *switches axes* mid-budget: revise a few times, and if the verifier score is not climbing, abandon the chain and draw fresh parallel samples. That mid-budget axis switch is itself a small instance of compute-optimal allocation operating *within* a single prompt rather than across prompts.

## 4. The accuracy curve, bin by bin

**Senior rule of thumb: plot accuracy against test-time compute separately for each difficulty bin, or you will average away the entire phenomenon.** If you collapse all difficulties into one curve, you see a smooth rise with diminishing returns and you learn nothing about *why*. Split by difficulty and the structure appears: different bins have different shapes, different ceilings, and different best strategies. The hand-authored curve below is the picture to keep in your head.

![A line chart plotting task accuracy against test-time compute on a log scale for three difficulty bins, with the easy curve climbing fast and plateauing high, the medium curve climbing steadily, and the hard curve saturating early at low accuracy](/imgs/blogs/test-time-compute-scaling-4.png)

Three things to read off the chart. First, the **easy curve** (solid) climbs steeply and then plateaus high — most of the accuracy is captured in the first handful of samples or revisions, and beyond that the curve is flat because there is little headroom left. For easy prompts, the lesson is *stop early*: the marginal sample buys almost nothing once you are near the plateau. Second, the **medium curve** (dashed) keeps climbing across the whole budget range — this is where test-time compute has the most room to work, and where the gains-per-FLOP are highest. Third, the **hard curve** (dotted) saturates early and *low*: it bends almost immediately and then crawls, because the base model's capability on these problems is genuinely limited and no amount of sampling or revising conjures a skill it does not have. The hard curve flattening is not a failure of the search algorithm — it is the model's competence ceiling showing through.

The x-axis is log-scaled, which is not a cosmetic choice. Test-time compute, like training compute, tends to buy accuracy in a way that is roughly linear in the *log* of the budget over the productive range — each *doubling* of samples buys a similar increment, until the curve hits its ceiling. This is the same shape the [repeated sampling](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/repeated-sampling-scaling-laws) work found for coverage, and it is why the practical advice is always phrased in multiplicative terms (2x the budget, 4x the budget) rather than additive ones. Diminishing returns are baked in: on the log axis the curve is roughly straight in the productive band and then bends to horizontal, so every extra doubling past the bend is mostly wasted.

The bin-by-bin view also explains the headline "more effective than scaling parameters" claim without overstating it. The easy and medium curves are where test-time compute beats parameters, because there the curve is still climbing and a small model can ride it up to the accuracy of a much larger one. The hard curve is where parameters still win, because the small model's curve has already flattened far below where a larger model's *base* accuracy sits. The two regimes are visible as the difference between a curve that has headroom and a curve that has run out of it.

### Reading the diminishing-returns bend quantitatively

Put rough numbers on the bend so the "stop early" advice has teeth. Say the easy bin starts at 55% pass@1, reaches 80% by 8 samples, 85% by 32 samples, and 86% by 256. The marginal accuracy per *doubling* goes from roughly +12 points (1→2→4→8) to +3 points (8→16→32) to under +1 point (32→256). The cost, of course, doubles each step. So the cost per accuracy point rises by more than an order of magnitude across that range. A budget-aware policy reads that curve and cuts off the easy bin around 8 samples, redirecting the freed compute to the medium bin where each doubling is still buying real points. The hard bin, meanwhile, might go 8% → 14% → 18% → 20% → 21% — it never gets steep, so pouring budget into it is the worst use of a marginal FLOP, and the policy either caps it low or escalates those prompts to a bigger model. The whole compute-optimal scheme is, at bottom, this: read the local slope of each bin's curve and spend the next FLOP wherever the slope is steepest.

## 5. Inside the verifier axis: best-of-N to lookahead

**Senior rule of thumb: best-of-N, beam search, and lookahead are the same idea at increasing search depth, and depth helps exactly when the prompt is hard.** The verifier axis is not monolithic. It is a ladder of search methods that all use the same PRM but commit to pruning decisions at different depths. The pipeline below walks the ladder.

![A pipeline showing a shared process reward model feeding into best-of-N, beam search, and lookahead search in increasing order of search depth, ending in a selected verified solution](/imgs/blogs/test-time-compute-scaling-5.png)

Start at the shallow end. **Best-of-N** does no pruning during generation at all: it samples $N$ complete solutions from the model, scores each completed solution with the PRM (or takes a weighted vote), and keeps the best. It is "search" only in the loosest sense — it generates a population and selects from it after the fact. It is the cheapest to implement and the easiest to parallelize, and on easy and many medium problems it is hard to beat per unit of engineering effort.

One rung up, **beam search** prunes *during* generation. At each reasoning step it keeps only the top-$M$ partial solutions by PRM score and expands those, discarding the low-scoring partials before spending any more tokens on them. This concentrates the budget on promising prefixes, which is exactly what helps on hard problems — you stop wasting tokens completing solutions that already look doomed at step three. The risk is *beam collapse*: if the PRM is miscalibrated, beam search can prune the one good branch early and converge on a confident wrong answer, which is worse than best-of-N's broader population. Beam search is only as good as the PRM that drives its pruning.

At the deep end, **lookahead search** scores a partial solution not by the PRM's immediate estimate but by rolling out a few steps into the future and scoring the rollout — a small Monte Carlo lookahead, in the spirit of how a game engine evaluates a position by simulating ahead. This is the most expensive per node, because each evaluation now costs several extra forward passes, but it gives the tightest step-level guidance and helps most on the very hardest problems where the immediate PRM score is too myopic to tell a good branch from a bad one. The paper finds that the *ranking* of these methods depends on the budget and difficulty: at small budgets and easier problems the extra cost of beam and lookahead is not repaid and best-of-N is competitive or better; at larger budgets on harder problems, the deeper search pulls ahead.

| Method | Pruning depth | Cost per node | Wins when |
|---|---|---|---|
| Best-of-N | None (post-hoc select) | Lowest | Easy/medium, small budget, parallel-friendly |
| Beam search | Per step, top-$M$ | Medium | Hard problems, good PRM, medium budget |
| Lookahead | Per step, $k$-step rollout | Highest | Hardest problems, large budget |

The non-obvious lesson is that **more search depth is not uniformly better**. Each rung up the ladder spends more compute on *guiding* the search and less on *generating raw candidates*, and that trade only pays off when the guidance is worth more than the candidates — which is precisely the hard-problem, large-budget corner. On easy problems, the deep search is spending its budget defending against dead ends that barely exist, and the simpler best-of-N, which puts all its budget into candidates, wins. This is the verifier-axis echo of the difficulty-decides-the-axis result from earlier: depth is to the verifier axis what the search-vs-revision choice is to the whole space.

### Beam collapse, concretely

A short failure story makes beam collapse vivid. Take a hard geometry problem where the correct solution requires an unusual auxiliary construction at step two — a move that looks unpromising locally because it does not immediately simplify the expression. A greedy PRM, trained mostly on solutions that simplify monotonically, scores that step low. Beam search with a narrow beam prunes it. Every surviving beam now lacks the one move that the solution needed, so the entire beam population is doomed, and the search confidently returns a wrong answer with a high aggregate PRM score. Best-of-N, drawing independent full samples, would have included a few that happened to make the unpromising move and carried it through — and the final-answer verifier would have caught the right one. This is the precise sense in which premature pruning can be worse than no pruning: it removes the diversity that hard problems depend on. The fix in practice is to widen the beam, soften the PRM's pruning (sample proportional to score rather than hard top-$M$), or fall back to best-of-N when the PRM's confidence spread is narrow.

## 6. Routing: difficulty against budget

**Senior rule of thumb: the compute-optimal policy is a two-dimensional lookup — difficulty on one axis, per-prompt budget on the other — and the right cell moves with both.** It is tempting to reduce the result to "revise easy, search hard," but that ignores the budget. The same prompt deserves a different strategy at a budget of 4 samples than at 256. The grid below is the full routing rule.

![A three by three routing grid with difficulty against per-prompt budget, recommending sequential revision for easy prompts at small budgets, a mix at large budgets, parallel search for hard prompts at small budgets, and escalation to a bigger model for hard prompts at large budgets](/imgs/blogs/test-time-compute-scaling-6.png)

Walk the cells. **Easy prompt, small budget** → sequential revision: a couple of edits is the cheapest path to a correct answer, and you have no budget to waste on wide search. **Easy prompt, large budget** → mix: you have spare compute, so revise to a strong candidate and then sample a few revisions in parallel for insurance; this beats either pure axis because the revisions give you a good base and the parallelism gives you coverage of the residual slips. **Hard prompt, small budget** → parallel search: with little budget on a hard problem, your best shot is a handful of diverse independent attempts plus a verifier, because revision would just anchor you to a wrong first try. **Hard prompt, large budget** → and here is the uncomfortable cell — *pre-training wins*. On a genuinely hard problem with a large budget, the small model's accuracy curve has flattened, and the right move is not more test-time compute on the small model but a *bigger model*. Test-time compute hits a wall on the hard tail, and the grid is honest about it.

That bottom-right cell is the bridge to the deepest result in the paper, and it deserves its own treatment, because it is the one that bounds the whole "test-time beats parameters" thesis.

### The difficulty estimate is the load-bearing input

The grid is only useful if you can place a fresh prompt into a row at inference time. As noted earlier, the deployable difficulty estimate comes from running a small number of initial samples and reading the PRM's scores: a tight cluster of high scores means easy, a wide spread of low scores means hard. There is a cost to this — you spend a few samples just to estimate difficulty before you decide how to spend the rest — but it is a small fraction of the budget on any non-trivial prompt, and it is what converts the oracle-difficulty result into a real policy. The robustness finding from Section 3 is what makes this safe: because the compute-optimal allocation built on noisy predicted difficulty captures most of the oracle gain, a mediocre difficulty estimate still leaves you far ahead of uniform best-of-N. You do not need to know a prompt's difficulty precisely; you need to know it well enough to pick the right column and row, and a handful of probe samples gets you there.

## 7. The tradeoff surface: test-time vs pre-training FLOPs

**Senior rule of thumb: test-time compute and pre-training compute are two ways to buy the same accuracy, and they trade against each other along a frontier whose slope depends on difficulty.** This is the result the paper's title is really about, and it is fundamentally two-dimensional: there is no single scalar "test-time scaling exponent" because the exchange rate between inference FLOPs and pre-training FLOPs is not constant. The hand-authored frontier below is the picture.

![A two dimensional tradeoff chart with pre-training FLOPs on the vertical axis and test-time FLOPs on the horizontal axis, showing an iso-accuracy frontier where a small model plus test-time compute wins in the lower-left easy region while a bigger pre-trained model wins in the upper-right hard region](/imgs/blogs/test-time-compute-scaling-7.png)

The two axes are the two ways to spend compute to reach a target accuracy: go *up* by pre-training a bigger model (more parameters, more training FLOPs), or go *right* by spending more test-time compute per prompt (more samples or revisions, more inference FLOPs). The curved line is an *iso-accuracy frontier*: every point on it reaches the same quality. The frontier's slope is the *exchange rate* — how many extra inference FLOPs buy you the accuracy of one fewer pre-training FLOP — and the headline finding is that this rate is wildly favorable in one region and unfavorable in another.

In the **lower-left, easy-to-medium region** (green), test-time compute wins decisively. Here a small model spending extra inference compute lands at the same accuracy as a model **fourteen times larger** — at *matched total FLOPs*. The small model's accuracy curve still has headroom (Section 4), so a modest test-time budget rides it up to the big model's level, and because the small model is so much cheaper per token, the total comes out ahead. This is the concrete, FLOPs-matched version of "test-time scaling can be more effective than scaling parameters."

In the **upper-right, hardest region** (red), the trade flips. On the hardest problems and at the largest budgets, the small model's accuracy curve has already flattened (Section 4 again), so pouring inference FLOPs into it buys almost nothing, while the same FLOPs spent on a bigger pre-trained model raise its *base* capability above where the small model can ever reach. Here, more parameters beat more samples. The frontier is *steep* in this region — you would have to spend an absurd amount of test-time compute to substitute for a modest increase in model size — which is the geometric statement of "test-time compute has a ceiling that pre-training does not."

| Regime | Who wins | Mechanism | Practical move |
|---|---|---|---|
| Easy/medium, small budget | Test-time on a small model | Small model has headroom on the accuracy curve | Ship small, spend on inference |
| Easy/medium, large budget | Test-time still competitive | Curve climbs across the budget range | Mix revision and search |
| Hardest tail, any budget | Pre-training a bigger model | Small model's curve has flattened | Train bigger or escalate |
| Mixed traffic | Both, routed per prompt | Frontier slope varies by difficulty | Small model + escalation tier |

The honest summary is the one the paper itself reaches: **test-time compute is a partial substitute for parameters, not a universal one.** It substitutes beautifully in the regime where the base model is already competent enough that the right answer is *reachable* — it just needs to be found or refined. It fails in the regime where the right answer is *outside the model's reach* no matter how long it searches, because search cannot manufacture a capability the weights do not encode. Difficulty is the dividing line, and the frontier's varying slope is the quantitative shadow of that line.

### Why there is no clean one-line law here

It is worth being explicit about a methodological point, because it contrasts sharply with the rest of this series. [Kaplan](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/kaplan-scaling-laws-language-models) and [Chinchilla](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/chinchilla-compute-optimal-scaling) gave us clean power laws: loss as $E + A/N^\alpha + B/D^\beta$, a single curve you can fit and extrapolate. Test-time scaling does not collapse to one such law, and the paper is careful not to pretend it does. The reason is that the effect of test-time compute is *conditional on difficulty*, and difficulty is not a scalar you can fold into a single exponent — it is a distribution over your prompts, and the optimal policy is a function over that distribution. You can fit a coverage curve for a fixed difficulty bin (and it looks like the exponentiated power law from the [repeated sampling](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/repeated-sampling-scaling-laws) work), but the *aggregate* behavior is the integral of those per-bin curves weighted by your traffic, and that integral has no universal closed form. The deliverable is therefore a *policy* — the routing grid and the frontier — not a coefficient. Treat anyone who hands you a single "test-time scaling exponent" with the same suspicion you would treat a single Chinchilla coefficient quoted to four significant figures.

## 8. A deployable policy: routing per query

**Senior rule of thumb: in production, the compute-optimal result is a router, not a search algorithm — estimate difficulty cheaply, then branch.** Everything above composes into one decision tree that runs per query. The tree below is the shape of a real policy.

![A decision tree that estimates prompt difficulty then branches to sequential revision for easy prompts, a mix of revision and search for medium prompts, parallel search for hard prompts, and escalation to a larger model for the unsolved hard tail](/imgs/blogs/test-time-compute-scaling-8.png)

The root is a cheap difficulty estimate: run a few probe samples, read the PRM scores, bin the prompt. The branches follow the matrix and grid from Sections 3 and 6. **Easy** prompts go to sequential revision — a few attempts, stop early, done at low cost. **Medium** prompts go to a mix — revise to a strong candidate, then sample several revisions in parallel and verify. **Hard** prompts go to parallel search with step-level pruning — the most expensive on-model path. And the unsolved residue of the hard branch *escalates*: a larger model, or a different tool, or an honest abstention, because the frontier says the small model cannot reach those answers no matter how long it thinks. That escalation tier is not an admission of failure; it is the practical encoding of the tradeoff surface, and it is what keeps the policy from burning unbounded compute on problems it will never solve.

The economic shape of this policy is worth appreciating. The vast majority of real traffic is easy or medium, so the vast majority of queries take the cheap left branches and cost almost nothing extra. The expensive search branch is reserved for the genuinely hard minority, and the even-more-expensive escalation tier for the hard-and-large-budget sliver. The *average* cost per query stays low while the *worst-case* quality stays high — which is exactly the property a fixed model cannot give you, because a fixed model spends the same on every query. This is the operational payoff of the entire line of work: not a bigger model, but a *better-spent* inference budget.

### What the policy needs to actually run

Three components, in order of how hard they are to get right. First, a **verifier** — a PRM for math, unit tests for code, a proof checker for formal math. Without it, none of the search branches convert samples into solved problems, and you collapse back to the selection wall that bounds [repeated sampling](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/repeated-sampling-scaling-laws) in open-ended domains. Second, a **revision-trained model** — the base model fine-tuned to improve its own answer conditioned on prior attempts, which is what makes the proposal axis work; an un-fine-tuned model revises poorly. Third, a **difficulty estimator** — the cheap probe-and-bin step at the root. The verifier is the gate; the revision training is the enabler of one axis; the difficulty estimate is the router. Get all three, and the 4x efficiency and the 14x effective-size results are available to you. Miss the verifier, and the whole thing degrades to "sample more and hope," which plateaus fast.

### The latency dimension the FLOPs story hides

There is a fourth consideration the FLOPs accounting quietly omits, and it bites in production: *latency*. The two axes have very different latency profiles even at the same FLOP cost. Parallel search is embarrassingly parallel — you can fan out $N$ samples across $N$ workers and get your answer in roughly one forward-pass-worth of wall-clock time, paying in hardware rather than in time. Sequential revision is, by definition, *sequential* — attempt $k+1$ cannot start until attempt $k$ finishes — so a five-step revision chain costs five forward passes of wall-clock latency no matter how much hardware you throw at it. This inverts part of the difficulty-routing logic when latency is the binding constraint rather than FLOPs. On an easy prompt where revision is the FLOP-optimal choice, you might still prefer a small parallel sample if the user is waiting and you have spare workers, because parallelism buys you the answer *now* at the cost of a few extra FLOPs. The compute-optimal policy from the paper optimizes FLOPs; a production policy optimizes a weighted combination of FLOPs, dollars, and latency, and the weights are set by your product, not by the benchmark. Lookahead search sits worst on this axis — it is both expensive *and* serial in its rollouts — which is another reason it is reserved for the rare hardest prompts where quality dominates latency. The lesson is to treat the paper's FLOP-optimal routing as the *quality-per-compute* frontier and then bend it toward your real objective, which almost always includes a latency term the benchmark never had to pay.

## 9. How this composes with the rest of the scaling story

It is worth stepping back to place test-time scaling in the broader arc, because it is not a standalone trick — it is the operating-time complement to a chain of training-time results. [Chinchilla](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/chinchilla-compute-optimal-scaling) told us to balance parameters and tokens for a fixed training budget. [Inference-aware scaling](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/inference-aware-scaling-laws) told us that if we will serve the model heavily, we should shrink the model and over-train it, because inference cost is linear in $N$ and paid forever. Test-time scaling closes the loop: having shipped that small, cheap-per-token model, we now have inference budget to spend on *thinking longer*, and this post is the recipe for spending it well.

The three results stack into a single deployment thesis. Take a small model — smaller than Chinchilla-optimal — and over-train it on far more than 20 tokens per parameter, because you will serve it at scale. That model is cheap per token. Now run the compute-optimal test-time policy on top of it: route easy queries to cheap revision, hard queries to search, and the hard tail to an escalation tier. The result can match or beat a large compute-optimal model on the accuracy your traffic actually demands, at lower total serving cost — provided a verifier exists. That proviso is the recurring theme of this whole track: in verifiable domains (math, code, formal proofs), the test-time axis scales cleanly and the thesis holds; in open-ended domains where no verifier is reliable, selection is the wall and the gains evaporate after a few hundred samples, exactly as the [repeated sampling](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/repeated-sampling-scaling-laws) work warned.

It is worth being precise about how the three knobs interact, because they are not independent. The inference-aware choice sets the *per-token* cost of the model; the test-time policy sets the *number of tokens* spent per query; and difficulty sets *how many* of those tokens are worth spending. A smaller model is cheaper per token but typically needs *more* test-time tokens to reach a given accuracy, because its base capability is lower — so shrinking the model and leaning on test-time compute trades a lower fixed cost for a higher variable cost, and the trade only pays when the variable cost is concentrated on the minority of hard queries. This is the same tug-of-war as the [inference-aware](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/inference-aware-scaling-laws) frontier, now with a third dimension: the test-time policy lets you place the variable cost *where it is worth paying* rather than spreading it uniformly. The optimal point in this three-dimensional space — model size, test-time budget, routing policy — is not something you can read off a single curve; it is something you tune against your measured traffic, your verifier quality, and your latency budget. That is the honest shape of the design problem, and it is why "just make the model bigger" and "just sample more" are both wrong as universal answers.

There is also a forward link worth flagging. Snell et al. study test-time compute on a *fixed* model with *external* search machinery — a separate PRM, an explicit beam or revision loop. The next chapter of this story, the [reasoning models trained with reinforcement learning](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/reasoning-models-rl-test-time-scaling), *internalizes* that search. Instead of an external beam search over a frozen model, the model is trained with RL to produce a long chain of thought that explores, backtracks, and self-corrects *within a single generation* — the search becomes part of the proposal distribution rather than a wrapper around it. The Snell result is the conceptual proof that test-time compute is worth spending and that difficulty governs how to spend it; the RL reasoning models are the engineering that bakes the spending into the model itself.

## 10. The experimental setup, and why it is trustworthy

**Senior rule of thumb: a test-time scaling claim is only as trustworthy as its difficulty stratification and its FLOPs accounting — read both before you believe a hero number.** The reason this paper landed harder than the dozens of "we sampled more and did better" reports that preceded it is the discipline of its experimental design, and that discipline is worth understanding because it is what you should demand of any test-time scaling result, including your own.

The study is run on the MATH benchmark with PaLM-2-class models, holding the *model* fixed while varying the *inference policy*. Difficulty is stratified into five bins by the base model's pass rate, so every claim is reported per bin rather than as a single aggregate — which is the only way the search-versus-revision flip becomes visible at all. The PRM is trained with automatically-generated per-step labels: from each reasoning prefix, the authors sample many completions and label the prefix with the fraction that reach the correct final answer, a Monte Carlo estimate of the prefix's value. This avoids the prohibitively expensive human step-labeling that earlier PRM work relied on, and it is what makes the verifier axis reproducible at scale.

The FLOPs accounting is the other half of the rigor. When the paper says a small model "matches a 14x larger model at matched FLOPs," it is comparing total inference FLOPs — the small model's extra samples and revisions are counted against the large model's single larger forward pass. This is the same matched-budget discipline the [repeated sampling](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/repeated-sampling-scaling-laws) work used, and it is what separates a real efficiency claim from a misleading one. A "test-time scaling wins" claim that does not match FLOPs is comparing a model that spent more compute against one that spent less, and of course the one that spent more does better — that is not a finding, it is arithmetic.

| Design choice | What it controls for | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Fixed model, varied policy | Confounds from model quality | Isolates the test-time policy's contribution |
| Five difficulty bins | Aggregation hiding the flip | Reveals search-vs-revision crossover |
| Automatic PRM labels | Cost and reproducibility | Makes the verifier axis scalable |
| Matched-FLOPs comparison | Spending more = doing better | Turns "wins" into "wins per FLOP" |
| Oracle vs predicted difficulty | Deployability | Shows the router survives a noisy estimate |

The last row is the one that makes the paper *useful* rather than merely interesting. Reporting both the oracle-difficulty result (the upper bound, unavailable at deployment) and the model-predicted-difficulty result (the deployable approximation) lets you see exactly how much of the gain survives the transition to a real policy — and the answer is "most of it." A paper that only reported the oracle result would be describing a ceiling no one can reach; reporting both is what makes the routing grid in Section 6 something you can actually build.

### What to demand of your own test-time scaling experiments

Carry the same five disciplines into any test-time scaling work you do. Fix the model and vary only the policy, or you will confound policy gains with model gains. Stratify by difficulty, or you will average away the most important structure. Use an automatic, reproducible verifier-labeling scheme, or your PRM results will not replicate. Match FLOPs in every comparison, or your "wins" are just "spent more." And report both the oracle and the deployable difficulty estimate, or you will not know whether your gain survives contact with production. These are not optional niceties — they are the difference between a result that holds up and a press release.

## Case studies from the difficulty-stratified experiments

The findings above come from a careful experimental design, and the most instructive way to internalize them is to walk through specific situations the paper and its setting throw up — the symptom, the wrong first guess, the actual mechanism, and the lesson. These are framed as the kinds of incidents you hit when you build a test-time policy yourself.

### 1. The easy bin that best-of-N overpaid for

The symptom: on the easiest difficulty bin, best-of-N at 64 samples and a two-shot sequential revision land at nearly identical accuracy, but best-of-N costs 30x the compute. The wrong first hypothesis is that best-of-N is "more thorough" and therefore safer. The actual mechanism is that on easy problems the model's first answer is already nearly always right, so 63 of the 64 best-of-N samples are independent rediscoveries of the same correct answer — pure redundancy. Two revisions catch the rare slip for a tiny fraction of the cost. The lesson: on easy traffic, parallel sampling is almost entirely wasted, and the compute-optimal policy's biggest single saving comes from *not* running wide search where it is not needed. This one bin is responsible for a large share of the overall 4x.

### 2. The hard problem that revision polished into a confident wrong answer

The symptom: a hard algebra problem where the sequential reviser, given five revision steps, produces five increasingly clean versions of the same wrong solution, each with a higher self-assessed confidence than the last. The wrong first hypothesis is that the reviser needs more revision steps. The actual mechanism is anchoring: the reviser conditions each attempt on the previous one, and the previous one carries a wrong method, so every revision inherits the wrong method and merely improves its presentation. More steps make it worse, not better, because they polish the corpse. The fix that worked was to switch this prompt to parallel sampling, which drew a few attempts with a *different* method, one of which was right, and let the verifier pick it. The lesson: revision is local search, and local search cannot escape a bad basin; hard problems need the global diversity of independent samples.

### 3. The beam that collapsed on a non-monotonic proof

The symptom: beam search with a narrow beam returns a wrong answer with a high aggregate PRM score on a geometry problem, while plain best-of-N at the same budget gets it right. The wrong first hypothesis is that the PRM is simply bad and needs retraining. The actual mechanism is more specific: the correct solution required an auxiliary construction at step two that *temporarily made the expression more complex*, and the PRM — trained mostly on monotonically-simplifying solutions — scored that step low and the narrow beam pruned it. Every surviving beam lacked the key move. Best-of-N kept it because a few independent full samples happened to make the move and carry it through. The lesson: aggressive pruning trades diversity for focus, and on problems whose solution path is non-monotonic, that trade backfires. Widen the beam or soften the pruning when the PRM's score spread is narrow.

### 4. The lookahead that paid off only at the top budget

The symptom: lookahead search underperforms beam search and even best-of-N at small budgets, then overtakes both as the budget grows large on the hardest bin. The wrong first hypothesis is that lookahead is strictly better because it sees further. The actual mechanism is a budget allocation tradeoff: lookahead spends several forward passes *evaluating* each candidate step, so at a small total budget it generates far fewer candidates than best-of-N, and on most problems the candidates matter more than the evaluation. Only on the hardest problems, where myopic PRM scores genuinely mislead, does the extra evaluation repay its cost — and only once the budget is large enough to afford both candidates and lookahead. The lesson: search depth is a budget-dependent and difficulty-dependent choice, not a free upgrade; the deepest method is not the default.

### 5. The model-predicted difficulty that nearly matched the oracle

The symptom: replacing the oracle difficulty (the base model's true pass rate, unavailable at deployment) with a cheap model-predicted difficulty (read off PRM scores on a few probe samples) costs only a small slice of the compute-optimal gain. The wrong first hypothesis is that you need an accurate difficulty estimate for the routing to work, which would make the whole scheme undeployable. The actual mechanism is that the routing decision is *coarse* — it picks a column and row of a grid, not a precise number — so it tolerates a noisy estimate as long as the noise rarely flips a prompt across the easy/hard boundary. The lesson, and the reason the result is practical at all: you do not need to know a prompt's difficulty precisely; you need to know it well enough to route it, and a handful of probe samples suffices.

### 6. The FLOPs-matched comparison that beat a 14x model

The symptom: a small base model with compute-optimal test-time allocation matches the accuracy of a model fourteen times larger, at matched total FLOPs, on the easy-to-medium portion of the benchmark. The wrong first hypothesis is that this generalizes to "test-time compute always beats parameters." The actual mechanism is that the comparison holds *only where the small model has headroom* — where its accuracy curve is still climbing — and the easy/medium bins are exactly that region. On the hard bins, the same comparison reverses, because the small model's curve has flattened below the large model's base accuracy. The lesson: the 14x result is real and important, but it is a statement about a regime, not a universal law; quoting it without the difficulty caveat is exactly the overclaim the paper's careful design is built to avoid.

### 7. The hard tail that no inference budget could rescue

The symptom: on the very hardest bin, accuracy plateaus low across every test-time strategy and every budget — best-of-N, beam, lookahead, revision, all flatten at roughly the same disappointing level. The wrong first hypothesis is that some cleverer search would break through. The actual mechanism is that the correct solutions to these problems require a capability the base model's weights do not encode, and search can only *find* answers the model can already *produce* — it cannot manufacture a missing skill. The model never samples a correct solution because it cannot generate one, so there is nothing for the verifier to select. The lesson, and the boundary of the entire thesis: test-time compute amplifies existing capability; it does not create new capability. The hard tail is where pre-training a bigger model is not a luxury but a necessity.

### 8. The composition with an over-trained small model

The symptom: a deliberately small, heavily over-trained model (far past 20 tokens per parameter, cheap per token by design) plus the compute-optimal test-time policy serves a mixed-difficulty workload at lower total cost than a large compute-optimal model at matched quality. The wrong first hypothesis is that this is a free lunch that obsoletes large models. The actual mechanism is the composition of two results: the [inference-aware](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/inference-aware-scaling-laws) choice to shrink-and-over-train makes per-token inference cheap, and the test-time policy spends that cheapness only where it pays — on the easy/medium majority — while escalating the hard tail. The hard tail still needs the big model. The lesson: the win is real for the *bulk* of traffic and is gated by both a verifier and an escalation tier; it is an operating-cost optimization, not the end of pre-training.

### 9. The weighted-majority aggregation that beat plain best-of-N

The symptom: holding the candidate set completely fixed, switching the selection rule from plain best-of-N (take the single highest-scored candidate) to weighted majority (sum the verifier scores across candidates that agree on a final answer, take the highest-summed answer) lifts accuracy by several points at no extra generation cost. The wrong first hypothesis is that the verifier just needs to be more accurate so that its top pick is more reliable. The actual mechanism is statistical: a single highest-scored candidate can be a confident outlier — the PRM is overconfident on one wrong solution — whereas pooling scores across agreeing candidates averages out that noise and lets a cluster of moderately-scored correct candidates outvote one loud wrong one. The lesson: the *aggregation* of a fixed candidate set is a free lever, independent of how the candidates were generated; spending engineering effort on a better selection rule can be cheaper than spending FLOPs on more samples.

### 10. The probe budget that paid for itself

The symptom: a naive implementation of the routing tree spent its first few samples estimating difficulty and then "wasted" them by not reusing them in the chosen strategy, making the policy look more expensive than it should. The wrong first hypothesis is that the difficulty-estimation probes are pure overhead to be minimized. The actual mechanism, once fixed, is that the probe samples are *not* overhead at all — they are the first few candidates of whatever strategy the prompt is routed to, so they count toward the budget rather than against it. You sample a handful, read the PRM spread to bin the prompt, and then *continue* sampling or switch to revision from that same starting population. The lesson: instrument your policy so the difficulty estimate is amortized into the work it routes to, not paid twice; a router that throws away its probes is leaving a chunk of its own budget on the floor.

## What this means in practice

The reach-for-it and skip-it conditions, stated plainly.

**Reach for compute-optimal test-time scaling when:**

- **You have a verifier.** A PRM for math, unit tests for code, a proof checker for formal math — anything that reliably distinguishes good answers from bad ones. This is the non-negotiable precondition; without it, search degrades to "sample and hope."
- **Your traffic is mostly easy-to-medium.** The 4x efficiency and the effective-size gains live in the regime where the base model has headroom. If most of your queries are solvable by a competent small model that just needs to search or refine, this is where the technique shines.
- **You have already shipped a small, cheap-per-token model** (the [inference-aware](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/inference-aware-scaling-laws) choice). Then you have spare inference budget to spend on thinking longer, and test-time scaling is how you spend it well.
- **You can estimate difficulty cheaply per query.** A few probe samples plus PRM scores is enough; the routing tolerates noise. This is what turns the oracle result into a real policy.
- **Worst-case quality matters more than average cost.** Test-time scaling lets you keep average cost low while raising the quality ceiling on the hard minority — a property a fixed model cannot offer.

**Skip it (or escalate instead) when:**

- **No reliable verifier exists.** In open-ended generation — summarization, dialogue, creative writing — selection is the wall, and the gains plateau after a few hundred samples. Spend your effort on the model or the data, not on search.
- **Your traffic is dominated by the hardest tail.** If most queries require a capability the small model lacks, no test-time budget rescues you; pre-train a bigger model. The frontier is steep here for a reason.
- **You cannot afford the latency of multiple samples or revision rounds.** Test-time scaling trades wall-clock and FLOPs for accuracy; if your product needs single-pass latency, this axis is closed to you (though sequential revision is at least latency-bounded by its step count).
- **You have not measured your difficulty distribution.** The entire gain is conditional on the mix of easy and hard prompts in your traffic. Quoting "4x" or "14x" without measuring your own distribution is the same mistake as quoting a Chinchilla coefficient to four digits — a point estimate masquerading as a law.

> The deepest lesson of this work is also the most general one in the whole scaling-laws series: a model is not a fixed level of intelligence, and a benchmark number is not a property of weights alone. How hard a model thinks, and how it thinks, is a knob you turn at inference, and turning it with respect to difficulty is worth a multiplicative factor in effective model size — right up until the point where the problem is genuinely beyond the model, at which point only more parameters help.
