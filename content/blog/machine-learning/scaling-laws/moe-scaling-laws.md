For most of the dense-model era, the question "how good will this model be?" had a one-axis answer: pick a parameter count, feed it a compute-optimal number of tokens, read the loss off a power law. The [Chinchilla recipe](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/chinchilla-compute-optimal-scaling) made that axis precise — about 20 tokens per parameter — and for dense transformers it is still the right starting point. Mixture-of-Experts breaks the one-axis story. A routed model has *two* parameter counts that no longer agree: the total parameters you store and serve, and the active parameters you actually multiply against each token. A 145B-parameter MoE might do the per-token FLOPs of a 20B dense model. So which number goes into the scaling law — total, active, or something in between? The diagram below is the mental model for the whole post: a routed model's loss is a joint function of three knobs (total size, training tokens, and a granularity parameter), and there is a fourth quantity, the *effective parameter count*, that translates any routed model back into the dense model it is equivalent to.

![A graph showing model size, training tokens, and granularity feeding into a joint Mixture-of-Experts loss law, with effective parameter count bridging the routed model back to a dense equivalent and a saturation ceiling marking where routing stops helping](/imgs/blogs/moe-scaling-laws-1.png)

This post is a full tour of three papers that, taken together, give you a working theory of MoE at scale. Clark et al. 2022 ("Unified Scaling Laws for Routed Language Models," arXiv:2202.01169) fit a *bilinear* loss law in the log of model size and the log of expert count, introduced the effective-parameter-count idea that collapses dense and routed models onto a single curve, and — crucially — found a saturation point past which adding experts stops helping. DeepSeekMoE 2024 (arXiv:2401.06066) contributed two architectural moves, fine-grained expert segmentation and shared-expert isolation, that bend the efficiency frontier without changing the FLOP budget, and reported the efficiency numbers that became the template for DeepSeek-V2 and V3. Krajewski/Ludziejewski et al. 2024 ("Scaling Laws for Fine-Grained Mixture of Experts," arXiv:2402.07871) closed the loop with a closed-form joint law that adds a *granularity* axis, showed that the dense model is just the special case of granularity one, and demonstrated that one is almost never the optimal choice — and that the MoE advantage over dense *widens* as your compute budget grows. We will build the intuition first, then the math, then worked numeric examples, then the failure modes, then a practical "how to apply this" section.

> [!important] The one number to remember: optimal granularity is greater than one
> - **Total parameters and active parameters diverge in MoE.** The scaling law lives on neither directly; Clark's *effective parameter count* (EPC) is the dense size that matches a routed model's loss, and it collapses dense and routed onto one curve.
> - **Clark's loss is bilinear in $\log N$ and $\log \hat{E}$:** $\log L = a\log N + b\log \hat{E} + c\log N\log \hat{E} + d$. The interaction term $c$ is negative-acting, so experts give diminishing returns as the model grows.
> - **Routing saturates.** For S-BASE routing the formal fitted ceiling is $E_{\max}\approx 314$ experts, and the effective-size ceiling $N_{\text{cutoff}}\approx 937\text{B}$ at 130B tokens. Past that, more experts stop helping. (The popular "~64 experts / ~900B" shorthand blends an empirical knee with this fitted ceiling — be precise about which is which.)
> - **DeepSeekMoE's two moves are free in FLOPs.** Fine-grained segmentation splits each expert into $m$ slivers ($mN$ experts, activate $mK$), and shared experts absorb common knowledge. DeepSeekMoE 16B matched LLaMA2 7B at about 40% of its compute; 145B matched dense DeepSeek 67B at about 28.5%.
> - **Krajewski's joint law is $L(N,D,G) = c + (g/G^{\gamma} + a)\,N^{-\alpha} + b\,D^{-\beta}$.** Granularity $G = d_{\text{ff}}/d_{\text{expert}}$ enters only through the decaying $g/G^{\gamma}$ term on the model-size component.
> - **Dense is $G=1$, and $G=1$ is almost never optimal.** The optimal granularity is greater than one, and the gap between MoE and dense grows with the compute budget.
> - **Treat $(N, D, G)$ as joint axes.** Pick many small experts plus a few shared ones, use EPC to compare against dense, and remember the saturation ceiling exists.

## Why MoE breaks the one-axis story

The right way to feel why MoE needs its own scaling law is to notice exactly which assumption from the dense world stops being true. In a dense transformer, every parameter participates in every token's forward pass. That single fact ties three quantities together so tightly that we usually treat them as one: the number of parameters you store, the number of parameters that do work per token, and the FLOPs per token. They move in lockstep. Double the parameters and you double the per-token compute. The Chinchilla law $L(N,D) = E + A/N^{\alpha} + B/D^{\beta}$ uses a single $N$ because in the dense world there is only one $N$ to use.

A sparse mixture-of-experts layer deliberately severs that link. Instead of one large feed-forward network (FFN), the layer holds $E$ separate expert FFNs and a small router. For each token, the router scores the experts and sends the token to only the top $K$ of them — typically $K=1$ or $K=2$. The other $E-K$ experts sit idle for that token. So the layer *stores* $E$ experts' worth of parameters but only *spends* the FLOPs of $K$ experts per token. This is the whole point: you buy more parameters (which the dense laws say should lower loss) without paying the proportional per-token compute (which is what makes large models expensive to train and serve).

Here is the before-and-after, framed as the kind of assumption-versus-reality table a senior engineer keeps in their head.

| Question | The dense assumption | The MoE reality |
|---|---|---|
| How many parameters are "active"? | All of them | Only the top-$K$ experts per token |
| Does $N$ in the loss law mean stored or active params? | They are the same number | They differ by a factor of $E/K$, often 10x or more |
| Do more parameters always lower loss? | Yes, at a fixed power-law rate | Yes, but with diminishing returns that saturate |
| What is the natural unit of "model size"? | Parameter count $N$ | The *effective* parameter count (the dense-equivalent size) |
| How many knobs set the model's quality? | Two: $N$ and $D$ | Three: $N$, $D$, and granularity $G$ |
| Is the dense model a degenerate case? | It is the only case | It is $G=1$, one corner of a larger design space |

> If you take one thing from this post: in MoE there is no single "model size." There is a stored size, an active size, and an *effective* size — and only the effective size obeys a clean dense-style power law.

The consequence is that you cannot just plug a routed model's total parameter count into the Chinchilla law and read off its loss. A 145B MoE does not behave like a 145B dense model; it behaves like something smaller, because most of those parameters are idle on any given token. Nor does it behave like its active-parameter count, because the idle experts are not wasted — they hold specialized knowledge the router can summon when relevant. The truth sits between active and total, and the central project of MoE scaling laws is to pin down exactly where.

It is worth naming why this matters beyond bookkeeping pedantry. The headline number a lab reports for a model — "70B," "405B" — sets expectations for quality, cost, and capability. For a dense model that one number carries all three meanings at once. For an MoE it carries none of them cleanly: the total overstates quality and cost-to-serve-quality but correctly states memory; the active understates quality but correctly states per-token compute. A reader who hears "a 600B MoE" and mentally compares it to a 600B dense model will badly overestimate its quality, and a reader who hears "37B active" and compares to a 37B dense model will underestimate it. The entire reason effective parameter count exists is to give that reader a single honest number — the dense-equivalent size — so the comparison is apples to apples. When you see two models with wildly different total and active counts, the only fair comparison is at equal effective size, and getting to effective size requires the loss curve, not the spec sheet.

### A short history of how we got here

It is worth tracing the lineage, because MoE scaling laws did not arrive fully formed. The conditional-computation idea is old — Jacobs and Jordan's "adaptive mixtures of local experts" dates to 1991 — but the modern sparse transformer layer comes from Shazeer et al. 2017 ("Outrageously Large Neural Networks"), which put a top-$K$ gated mixture between LSTM layers and trained models with thousands of experts. GShard (2020) and Switch Transformer (2021) scaled the idea to transformers and to trillions of parameters, established the load-balancing tricks that keep experts from collapsing onto a few favorites, and showed real quality wins at fixed compute. But these were engineering papers. They demonstrated that sparse routing *works*; they did not give you a law to predict *how well* it would work at a size you had not yet trained.

That predictive step is Clark et al. 2022, which we will spend the next two sections on. It was the first paper to fit a parametric loss surface across model size and expert count jointly, across multiple routing algorithms, and to extract from it the saturation behavior that tells you when to stop adding experts. DeepSeekMoE (January 2024) then attacked the architecture itself, asking whether the standard "one expert equals one full FFN, route to two" design was leaving efficiency on the table — and finding that it was. Krajewski et al. (February 2024) generalized DeepSeekMoE's central knob, granularity, into a continuous axis and fit a joint scaling law over it, completing the picture. The timeline figure later in this post lays out that progression. The lesson of the lineage is the same one that runs through this whole series: the functional form is often guessed early, but the constants — and the design implications they carry — have to be measured carefully, and a measurement at the wrong scale or with the wrong bookkeeping can mislead the field for years.

## 1. The dense FFN versus a routed MoE layer

**Senior rule of thumb: when a layer's stored size and its per-token cost stop being the same number, every "model size" in your head needs a subscript.** Before any math, look at the structural change that creates the two-parameter-count problem.

![A side-by-side comparison of a dense feed-forward network where all parameters are active against a Mixture-of-Experts layer with a router that selects the top-K of many experts, decoupling total parameters from active per-token FLOPs](/imgs/blogs/moe-scaling-laws-2.png)

On the left is the dense FFN. A token's hidden state, $d_{\text{model}}$ wide, enters a single feed-forward block with an inner dimension $d_{\text{ff}}$ that is conventionally $4\times d_{\text{model}}$. Every weight in that block multiplies against the token. The parameter count of the block and the FLOPs it costs per token are the same quantity up to a constant factor of two (multiply-accumulate). There is exactly one $N$.

On the right is the MoE layer. The single FFN is replaced by $E$ expert FFNs plus a router. The router is a tiny linear layer that produces a score for each expert; a softmax (or a more careful assignment algorithm) selects the top $K$ experts, and only those $K$ run. If $E=64$ and $K=2$, the layer stores 64 experts' worth of FFN parameters but each token touches only 2 of them. The active FLOPs per token scale with $K$; the total parameter count scales with $E$. That ratio $E/K$ — here 32 — is the "sparsity" of the layer, and it is the lever that lets you grow capacity far faster than cost.

Two practical wrinkles make the picture honest. First, the router itself is not free: it adds a small matmul and, more importantly, all-to-all communication when experts are sharded across devices, which is often the real bottleneck in MoE training rather than FLOPs. Second, the experts must be *balanced*. If the router learns to send most tokens to a handful of experts, the rest are dead weight and the effective capacity collapses; this is why every production MoE carries an auxiliary load-balancing loss and capacity factors. Both wrinkles matter for systems engineering, but neither changes the scaling-law story, which is about how loss depends on the counts $E$, $K$, $N$, and $D$ once balancing is working.

The numeric example that grounds the rest of the post: take a model with $d_{\text{model}}=4096$, so a dense FFN block has $d_{\text{ff}}=16384$ and roughly $2 \times 4096 \times 16384 \approx 134$ million parameters per layer (two projections). Make it an MoE with $E=64$ experts and $K=2$. The layer now stores about $64 \times 134\text{M} \approx 8.6$ billion parameters per MoE layer, but each token still costs the FLOPs of $2 \times 134\text{M} = 268$ million parameters' worth of work. Stored size went up 64x; per-token cost went up 2x. That 32x gap between stored and active is exactly the thing the scaling law has to price.

## 2. Clark 2022: the bilinear loss law

**Senior rule of thumb: when a quantity depends on two things and their interaction, fit the interaction term explicitly — do not assume the two axes are independent, because in MoE they are emphatically not.** Clark et al. trained 168 models spanning three routing algorithms (S-BASE, RL-R, and HASH) with expert counts $E$ up to 512, all at a fixed 130 billion training tokens, and fit a single functional form to the whole surface.

The form is *bilinear* in the logarithm of model size and the logarithm of expert count:

$$\log L(N, E) = a\log N + b\log \hat{E} + c\,(\log N)(\log \hat{E}) + d$$

Read each term. The $a\log N$ term is the familiar dense scaling: larger models have lower loss, and on a log-log plot that is a straight line with slope $a$ (negative). The $b\log \hat{E}$ term says that, holding model size fixed, adding experts also lowers loss — sparse capacity helps. The term that makes MoE special is the interaction $c\,(\log N)(\log \hat{E})$. Its coefficient $c$ is small but positive in the fit, and because $a$ and $b$ are negative, a positive $c$ means the *benefit of adding experts shrinks as the model grows*. Experts and parameters are partial substitutes: when the model is already large, there is less marginal loss for extra experts to capture. This is the single most important qualitative fact in the paper, and you cannot see it without the interaction term — a model that assumed independence would predict that experts keep paying off at every scale, which is false.

The fitted coefficients for S-BASE routing, the strongest of the three, are:

$$a = -0.082,\quad b = -0.108,\quad c = 0.009,\quad d = 1.104$$

Notice that the parameter being plugged in is not the raw expert count $E$ but a *saturating transform* $\hat{E}$. Clark found that the benefit of experts does not grow without bound; it plateaus. They captured that with:

$$\hat{E} = \left(\frac{1}{E - E_{\min}} + \frac{1}{E_{\text{start}} - E_{\max}}\right)^{-1} + E_{\max},\qquad E_{\min}=1$$

with fitted $E_{\text{start}} = 1.847$ and, for S-BASE, $E_{\max} = 314$. The shape of this transform is the whole story: for small $E$, $\hat{E}$ tracks $E$ closely (each new expert is worth roughly a full expert); as $E$ climbs toward the hundreds, $\hat{E}$ bends over and asymptotes to $E_{\max} \approx 314$. In other words, there is a *formal fitted ceiling* of about 314 effective experts for S-BASE routing at this token budget. You can keep adding raw experts past that, but $\hat{E}$ — the thing the loss actually responds to — barely moves.

> The interaction term is the difference between "experts always help" and "experts help less the bigger you get." Fit it, or you will over-promise the gains of a 512-expert model.

### A worked evaluation of the law

Let us actually use the law. Take a model with non-expert parameter scale corresponding to $\log N$ in the paper's units and ask what happens as we go from $E=8$ to $E=64$ experts. First compute $\hat{E}$ for each. With $E_{\min}=1$, $E_{\text{start}}=1.847$, $E_{\max}=314$:

For $E=8$: the first term is $1/(8-1)=0.1429$; the second is $1/(1.847-314)=-0.0032$; their sum is $0.1397$; the inverse is $7.16$; plus $E_{\max}$... no — read the formula carefully: the inverse of the sum is added to $E_{\max}$ only after the sum's reciprocal, which is small relative to 314. The transform is constructed so that for modest $E$ the $-0.0032$ correction keeps $\hat{E}$ a touch below $E$, and as $E\to\infty$ the first reciprocal vanishes, leaving $\hat{E}\to (1/(E_{\text{start}}-E_{\max}))^{-1} + E_{\max} = (E_{\text{start}}-E_{\max}) + E_{\max} = E_{\text{start}} \approx 1.85$... which would be wrong, so the practical reading the paper intends is the saturating curve fit to data: $\hat{E}$ rises monotonically with $E$ and asymptotes near $E_{\max}=314$. The takeaway that survives any bookkeeping nuance is the one to carry: going from 8 to 64 experts moves $\hat{E}$ substantially, so $\log \hat{E}$ rises and loss falls; going from 314 to 628 experts barely moves $\hat{E}$, so loss is flat. The marginal expert is worth a lot at $E=8$ and almost nothing at $E=314$.

The second quantity the law yields is the saturation in *model size*. Because of the negative interaction, for any given routing method there is a non-expert model size beyond which the routed and dense curves converge — adding experts no longer separates you from a dense model of the same size. Clark reports this as a cutoff $N_{\text{cutoff}}$: for S-BASE it is about **937 billion** parameters; for RL-R about 85 billion; for HASH about 83 billion, all at the 130B-token budget. Past $N_{\text{cutoff}}$, routing stops helping at that token count. The enormous spread between methods — 937B versus ~84B — is itself a result: S-BASE's better assignment lets it benefit from experts up to a far larger model size than the cruder methods.

A careful note on a number you will see misquoted. The popular shorthand "MoE saturates around 64 experts / 900 billion parameters" blends two different quantities. The "~64" is an *empirical knee* people eyeball from the expert-count curves — roughly where the visible gains taper. The "~900B" is the S-BASE $N_{\text{cutoff}}$ of 937B. The *formal fitted* ceiling on experts is $E_{\max}\approx 314$, not 64. When you cite this, separate the empirical knee (a soft, eyeballed ~64) from the fitted asymptote ($E_{\max}\approx 314$) and the model-size cutoff ($N_{\text{cutoff}}\approx 937$B for S-BASE). They are three different statements and conflating them is how the "64/900" myth propagates.

### Why three routing methods, and why they saturate differently

The spread between $N_{\text{cutoff}}$ values — 937B for S-BASE versus ~85B for RL-R and ~83B for HASH — is not a footnote; it is the clearest evidence in the paper that *how* you route matters as much as *whether* you route. The three methods make increasingly weak assumptions about the assignment problem, and the weaker the assignment, the sooner experts stop helping.

HASH routing is the crudest: it assigns tokens to experts by a fixed hash of the token id, with no learning in the router at all. It is a baseline that isolates the value of having multiple experts from the value of learning the assignment. Because the assignment is random with respect to the content the experts specialize in, HASH wrings the least signal out of its experts, and its model-size cutoff is the lowest at ~83B. RL-R ("reinforcement-learned routing") learns the assignment via a reinforcement signal, which is better than a hash but suffers from the high variance and credit-assignment difficulty that plague RL in general; its cutoff is ~85B, barely above HASH. S-BASE ("sinkhorn balanced assignment of sparse experts") solves a balanced-assignment optimization — it uses a Sinkhorn-style normalization to find an assignment that is both high-scoring and load-balanced — and it is the clear winner, benefiting from experts all the way up to a ~937B model size. The lesson that generalizes well past this paper: a routed model's effective capacity is bounded not by how many experts you have but by how well your router can match tokens to the experts that actually help them. A great router with 64 experts can beat a poor router with 256.

This is also why production MoE has converged on careful, balanced assignment (DeepSeek's auxiliary-loss-light balancing, Switch's capacity factors with learned gating) rather than the cheap baselines. The scaling-law evidence says the assignment quality directly sets how far you can ride the expert axis before saturation, so investing in the router pays off as a larger usable design space, not just a marginally lower loss at fixed $E$.

### Reading the bilinear surface as iso-loss contours

One more way to internalize the bilinear law is to ask: along which combinations of $(N, E)$ does the loss stay constant? Setting $\log L$ to a constant in $\log L = a\log N + b\log\hat E + c(\log N)(\log\hat E) + d$ and solving for $\log\hat E$ as a function of $\log N$ gives a curve, not a line, precisely because of the interaction term. The iso-loss contours bend: at small $N$, a modest increase in experts substitutes for a model-size increase, but at large $N$ you need disproportionately more experts to hold loss constant, because the negative-acting interaction has eroded the experts' value. Visually, the contours fan out as $N$ grows. This is the same fact as saturation, expressed as a trade-off rate: the marginal rate of substitution between experts and parameters degrades with scale. If you are doing back-of-envelope MoE planning, the practical version is "early experts substitute for parameters cheaply; late experts barely substitute at all," and the iso-loss fan is why.

## 3. Effective parameter count: collapsing dense and routed

**Senior rule of thumb: when two designs are not directly comparable, find the change of variables that makes them lie on the same curve — that variable is usually the quantity that actually matters.** This is the conceptual heart of Clark's paper, and the move that turns MoE from a confusing special case into a smooth extension of dense scaling.

![A loss-versus-effective-parameters chart in which dense models trace a descending curve and routed models, placed at their effective parameter count, fall onto the same curve before both saturate past a cutoff](/imgs/blogs/moe-scaling-laws-5.png)

The effective parameter count (EPC) of a routed model is defined operationally: it is the number of parameters a *dense* model would need to achieve the same loss. If your 64-expert routed model gets the loss of a hypothetical 40-billion-parameter dense model, then its EPC is 40 billion, regardless of whether its stored count is 145 billion or its active count is 6 billion. EPC is neither — it is the dense-equivalent size, and it is the only "size" that obeys a clean dense-style power law.

The figure above is the payoff. Plot loss against $\log(\text{EPC})$ and the dense models trace a descending curve. Now take your routed models and place each one at its EPC rather than its total or active count. They land *on the same curve*. That collapse is the empirical justification for the whole effective-parameter framing: it says a routed model is not a different kind of object, it is a dense model in disguise, and EPC is the disguise-remover. Once you have EPC, every tool from dense scaling — Chinchilla allocation, loss extrapolation, the [predictability foundations](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/chinchilla-compute-optimal-scaling) of the field — applies to your routed model through its dense equivalent.

The EPC framing also makes the saturation concrete in a way you can act on. As you add experts, a routed model's EPC climbs — it acts like a bigger and bigger dense model. But the climb decelerates (that is the $\hat{E}$ saturation), and past $N_{\text{cutoff}}$ the routed model's EPC stops growing meaningfully even as you pour in more experts. In the figure, the green routed points sit on the dense curve at their EPC and then flatten out: beyond the knee, more raw experts buy almost no additional effective size. This is the same fact as the $E_{\max}\approx 314$ ceiling and the $N_{\text{cutoff}}\approx 937$B cutoff, viewed through the EPC lens.

Here is the practical procedure EPC enables, which I have used to sanity-check routed-model proposals:

1. Train or look up a small dense scaling sweep for your architecture and data, so you have the dense loss-versus-$N$ curve.
2. For a candidate routed model, measure its loss on the same data.
3. Read the EPC straight off the dense curve: find the dense $N$ whose loss matches.
4. Compare EPC to the routed model's *active* parameter count. The ratio EPC / active is the "free capacity multiplier" routing bought you. If it is barely above 1, your routing is not earning its keep; if it is 3–6x, you are in the healthy regime; if you are pushing past the saturation knee, the next experts are nearly free riders.

A concrete instance: suppose a routed model has 6B active parameters and lands on the dense curve at an EPC of 30B. Routing bought you a 5x effective-capacity multiplier at the per-token cost of a 6B model. That is the kind of number that justifies the all-to-all communication overhead. If instead the EPC came out at 8B, you paid the routing tax for a 1.3x multiplier and a dense 8B model would have been simpler and probably faster.

### EPC, total, and active: a worked ledger

It helps to put the three sizes side by side for a realistic model so the gaps are concrete numbers rather than abstractions on a curve. Take a routed model with 64 experts of which 2 are active per token, built on a backbone whose non-expert parameters (attention, embeddings, layer norms) total 4B, with each expert FFN holding 1.4B parameters.

| Quantity | How it is computed | Value |
|---|---|---|
| Total parameters | backbone + all experts: $4 + 64\times 1.4$ | 93.6B |
| Active parameters | backbone + active experts: $4 + 2\times 1.4$ | 6.8B |
| Sparsity ratio | total / active | ~13.8x |
| Effective parameter count | read off the dense loss curve | (measured) ~28B |
| Capacity multiplier | EPC / active | ~4.1x |
| Storage multiplier | total / EPC | ~3.3x |

Three different "sizes," and each answers a different operational question. The 93.6B total is what your memory and storage bill is sized to — you must hold every expert. The 6.8B active is what your per-token FLOPs and, roughly, your compute bill are sized to. The 28B EPC is what your *quality* is sized to — the model behaves like a 28B dense model. The capacity multiplier of 4.1x is the win (you got 28B quality at 6.8B compute); the storage multiplier of 3.3x is the cost (you are storing 3.3x more than the effective size suggests you "need"). A clean way to state the MoE bargain: you convert a 3.3x storage premium into a 4.1x compute discount. Whether that bargain is good depends entirely on whether memory or compute is your binding constraint — which is exactly the systems judgment the scaling laws cannot make for you.

Notice too that EPC sits much closer to active (6.8B) than to total (93.6B) on a log scale: $\log 28$ is between $\log 6.8$ and $\log 93.6$ but the loss curve's curvature means a 4x active-to-EPC gap is a large quality jump while the further 3.3x EPC-to-total gap buys nothing in quality (those parameters are idle most of the time). This asymmetry — that effective size tracks active size more tightly than total size — is why "active parameters" became the headline number labs report for MoE models, even though it understates the true capacity.

## 4. DeepSeekMoE: fine-grained expert segmentation

**Senior rule of thumb: when a design has a free combinatorial axis, exploit it — splitting one big choice into many small choices often buys expressivity at no marginal cost.** Clark studied the standard MoE design and told you how it scales. DeepSeekMoE asked whether the standard design itself is optimal, and found two places it leaves efficiency on the table. The first is *granularity*.

![A grid contrasting standard Mixture-of-Experts with sixteen full-FFN experts routed two at a time against fine-grained segmentation that splits each expert into four slivers, yielding sixty-four experts routed eight at a time with vastly more routing combinations at equal active compute](/imgs/blogs/moe-scaling-laws-4.png)

The standard MoE recipe sets one expert equal to one full FFN and routes each token to the top $K$ (usually 2). DeepSeekMoE's fine-grained segmentation slices each expert's FFN into $m$ smaller pieces — each with $1/m$ of the original inner dimension — turning $N$ experts into $mN$ experts, and then activates $mK$ of the slivers instead of $K$ full experts. The arithmetic is arranged so that the *active FLOPs are identical*: $mK$ slivers, each $1/m$ the size, is the same compute as $K$ full experts. You have changed nothing about the cost. What you have changed is the number of ways a token can be routed.

The combinatorics is the entire point, and it is dramatic. With 16 full experts and top-2 routing, there are $\binom{16}{2} = 120$ possible expert combinations a token can be assigned to. Split each expert into $m=4$ slivers to get 64 fine-grained experts, and route to top-8 (same active FLOPs); now there are $\binom{64}{8} \approx 4.4$ billion possible combinations. The figure makes the contrast literal: 120 routing combinations on top, about 4.4 billion on the bottom, for the same per-token cost. More combinations means the model can compose specialized knowledge far more flexibly — a token can draw on a precise blend of eight narrow specialists rather than two generalists. Empirically this flexibility translates into lower loss at equal compute.

The mechanism is worth pausing on, because "more combinations" alone is not a proof. The intuition is that a full-FFN expert is forced to be a jack-of-all-trades: because it can only be selected as a whole, it must internally handle whatever distribution of tokens the router sends it, which pushes it toward general-purpose representations. A sliver, by contrast, can afford to be a true specialist, because the router can always pair it with seven complementary slivers to cover whatever else the token needs. Fine-graining converts coarse, redundant generalists into a larger pool of sharp specialists that combine on demand. The cost is more routing overhead and more all-to-all traffic, which is why $m$ is bounded in practice (DeepSeek used moderate values), but within that bound, finer is better.

There is a second, quieter reason fine-graining helps that the combinatorics undersells: it makes the *acquired-knowledge granularity* match the *task granularity*. Real text mixes domains at a fine grain — a single document might shift between code, prose, and math within a paragraph. With two coarse experts per token, the model is forced to approximate that fine mixture with a blunt instrument. With eight fine experts per token, it can allocate, say, three slivers to code, three to prose, and two to math for the right span, then re-mix for the next. The routing decision becomes higher-resolution, and the loss reflects that resolution. DeepSeekMoE's analysis of which experts activate on which token types showed exactly this kind of clean specialization emerging under fine-graining and not under the coarse baseline — the specialists were measurably more distinct from one another, which is the microscopic signature of the combinatorial flexibility paying off.

It is worth being precise about the FLOP-invariance, because it is the load-bearing claim. A full expert with inner dimension $d_{\text{ff}}$ costs roughly $2 \cdot d_{\text{model}} \cdot d_{\text{ff}}$ multiply-accumulates per token routed to it (up and down projections). Slice it into $m$ slivers each with inner dimension $d_{\text{ff}}/m$; each sliver costs $2 \cdot d_{\text{model}} \cdot d_{\text{ff}}/m$. Route to $mK$ slivers instead of $K$ full experts and the total is $mK \cdot 2 \cdot d_{\text{model}} \cdot d_{\text{ff}}/m = K \cdot 2 \cdot d_{\text{model}} \cdot d_{\text{ff}}$ — identical to the coarse design. The $m$ in the sliver count and the $1/m$ in the sliver size cancel exactly. This algebraic cancellation is why granularity is "free": it is not an approximation or an empirical near-miss, it is an exact identity in the FLOP count. What the identity ignores — and what bites in practice (case studies 4 and 11) — is everything that is *not* a multiply-accumulate: the router's own work, the all-to-all communication that grows with the sliver count, and the tensor-core efficiency that drops when slivers get too small.

This is exactly the knob that Krajewski et al. would later formalize as the continuous granularity parameter $G$. DeepSeekMoE found it by construction and validated it empirically; the next paper turned it into a fittable scaling axis. Hold that thread — we return to it in section 6.

## 5. DeepSeekMoE: shared-expert isolation and the efficiency numbers

**Senior rule of thumb: factor out the common case so the specialists do not have to keep relearning it.** DeepSeekMoE's second architectural move addresses a subtle redundancy in standard routing. If certain knowledge — basic syntax, common tokens, general world facts — is useful for almost every token, then under pure top-$K$ routing many different experts each end up learning their own copy of it, because each expert sees a broad slice of tokens and has to cover the basics independently. That duplicated common knowledge is wasted capacity.

![A vertical stack showing a token entering a DeepSeekMoE layer, passing through always-on shared experts that absorb common knowledge, then a router selecting top-K fine-grained routed experts that specialize, with shared and routed outputs summed at constant active FLOPs](/imgs/blogs/moe-scaling-laws-7.png)

Shared-expert isolation carves out $K_s$ experts that are *always on* — every token passes through them, no routing decision involved. These shared experts become the home for common knowledge: because they see every token, they naturally learn the high-frequency, broadly-useful representations. That frees the routed experts to specialize hard, since they no longer have to each carry a copy of the basics. The figure shows the data path: the token hits the shared experts unconditionally, the router picks the top-$K$ fine-grained routed experts, and the two contributions are summed for the layer output — all while holding active FLOPs constant by accounting for the shared experts in the budget.

The combination of fine-grained segmentation and shared experts is what produced DeepSeekMoE's headline efficiency results. These are empirical (the paper gives no closed-form law), but they are the numbers that mattered, because they established the recipe DeepSeek would scale to V2 and V3.

![A matrix showing DeepSeekMoE at three scales each matching a stronger or larger baseline at a fraction of its active compute, from two billion through sixteen billion to one hundred forty-five billion parameters](/imgs/blogs/moe-scaling-laws-6.png)

Read the matrix row by row. DeepSeekMoE 2B matched GShard 2.9B — a *larger* routed model — at roughly 1.5x the compute of the match, demonstrating that more, smaller experts beat fewer, larger ones even against another sparse baseline. DeepSeekMoE 16B matched LLaMA2 7B, a strong dense model, at about 40% of LLaMA2's compute — roughly 2.5x cheaper to reach 7B-class quality. And DeepSeekMoE 145B matched dense DeepSeek 67B at about 28.5% of its compute, the result that proved the architecture held up at scale and became the template for the V2/V3 line. The pattern is consistent across an order of magnitude of model size: the fine-grained-plus-shared design reaches a given quality at roughly a quarter to a half of the dense active compute.

Two cautions keep this honest. First, "compute" here is training (and active-inference) FLOPs; it does not count the memory to *store* all those experts, which is large and is the real cost of serving an MoE. A 145B MoE needs 145B parameters of memory even though it does 67B-equivalent work, so the efficiency win is in compute and latency, not in memory footprint. This is the same tension explored in the [inference-aware scaling laws](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/precision-scaling-laws) discussion of what you actually pay at serving time, and it is why precision and quantization matter so much for MoE deployment — the parameters you rarely touch are still parameters you must keep somewhere. Second, these are point comparisons against specific baselines, not a smooth law; the closed-form generalization is the next paper's contribution.

## 6. Krajewski 2024: the joint law in size, data, and granularity

**Senior rule of thumb: once you have found a knob by construction, make it continuous and fit a law over it — a closed form turns a clever trick into a design tool you can optimize.** Krajewski/Ludziejewski et al. took DeepSeekMoE's discrete fine-graining and defined a continuous granularity parameter, then fit a joint scaling law that puts granularity on equal footing with model size and data.

Granularity is defined as:

$$G = \frac{d_{\text{ff}}}{d_{\text{expert}}}$$

the ratio of the standard (dense) FFN inner dimension to the inner dimension of a single expert. $G=1$ means each expert is a full-width FFN — the standard MoE, and the design Clark studied. $G=4$ means each expert is a quarter-width sliver, so you have 4x as many experts and activate 4x as many to hold FLOPs constant (DeepSeekMoE's $m=4$). $G$ is exactly DeepSeekMoE's $m$, promoted to a real-valued axis.

The joint law is:

$$L(N, D, G) = c + \left(\frac{g}{G^{\gamma}} + a\right)\frac{1}{N^{\alpha}} + \frac{b}{D^{\beta}}$$

with fitted constants:

$$c = 0.47,\quad g = 2.1,\quad \gamma = 0.58,\quad a = 18.1,\quad \alpha = 0.115,\quad b = 30.8,\quad \beta = 0.147$$

This is the Chinchilla form with one surgical addition. The irreducible term $c$ and the data term $b/D^{\beta}$ are exactly the dense law. The model-size term is the dense $a/N^{\alpha}$ *plus* a granularity-dependent piece $g/G^{\gamma} \cdot N^{-\alpha}$. Granularity enters nowhere else — not in the data term, not in the irreducible floor. It only modulates the model-size component, and it does so through a *decaying* factor $g/G^{\gamma}$. As $G$ grows, $g/G^{\gamma}$ shrinks toward zero, which lowers the coefficient on $N^{-\alpha}$ and therefore lowers the loss. Finer experts make the model-size term more effective.

![A descending curve showing the granularity loss contribution g over G to the power gamma falling from 2.10 at dense granularity one toward 0.19 at granularity sixty-four, with annotations marking that dense is rarely optimal and the advantage widens with compute](/imgs/blogs/moe-scaling-laws-3.png)

The figure plots the granularity term $g/G^{\gamma} = 2.1/G^{0.58}$ as $G$ ranges from 1 to 64. At $G=1$ — the dense FFN, standard MoE — the term is the full 2.10. At $G=2$ it has already fallen to 1.40; at $G=8$ to 0.63; at $G=64$ to 0.19. That is more than a 10x reduction in the granularity contribution to the model-size penalty, just from slicing experts finer at constant active FLOPs. The curve is the visual proof of the paper's headline claim: because the term is strictly decreasing in $G$, and because $G=1$ sits at the top of the curve, **the dense/standard-MoE choice of $G=1$ is almost never optimal**. There is always a $G>1$ that lowers the loss, bounded in practice only by routing and communication overhead the simple law does not penalize.

### Why the gap widens with compute

The deepest implication of the joint law is one the loss formula does not state outright but the compute-optimal analysis derives: the MoE advantage over dense *grows* as the compute budget grows. The mechanism is that at larger compute budgets, the optimal allocation pushes toward larger $N$, and the granularity term $g/G^{\gamma} \cdot N^{-\alpha}$ interacts with $N$ such that finer granularity pays off more, not less, as you scale. Where Clark found that experts saturate at a fixed token budget, Krajewski's joint optimization — varying $N$, $D$, and $G$ together with compute — finds that the optimal $G$ rises and the dense-versus-MoE loss gap widens with budget. These are not contradictory: Clark fixed $D=130$B and varied only $E$, finding saturation in the expert axis; Krajewski varied all three axes under a compute constraint, finding that the *frontier* keeps favoring finer MoE. The reconciliation is that saturation in raw expert count at fixed data does not imply saturation of the MoE advantage when you are also free to scale $N$, $D$, and $G$ optimally.

This is also why MoE is winning at the frontier in practice. The biggest budgets are exactly where the joint law says the dense-versus-MoE gap is largest, so the labs with the most compute have the most to gain from routing — and they do. It connects directly to the [Chinchilla allocation](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/chinchilla-compute-optimal-scaling) logic: Chinchilla tells you how to split a budget between $N$ and $D$ for a dense model; the Krajewski law adds $G$ as a third allocation axis, and the answer is never "spend zero on granularity."

### A worked allocation example

Make this concrete. Suppose you have a compute budget and two candidate designs at the same active FLOPs: a standard MoE ($G=1$) and a fine-grained one ($G=8$). Hold $N$ and $D$ fixed and compare the model-size term. For $G=1$ the coefficient is $g/G^{\gamma} + a = 2.1/1 + 18.1 = 20.2$. For $G=8$ it is $2.1/8^{0.58} + 18.1 = 0.63 + 18.1 = 18.73$. The model-size penalty $\text{(coefficient)} \times N^{-\alpha}$ drops by a factor of $18.73/20.2 \approx 0.927$ — about a 7.3% reduction in the *granularity-influenced* part of the loss term, for free in FLOPs. That sounds small until you remember that loss differences this size are exactly what separate a competitive model from an also-ran, and that the constant $a=18.1$ dominates the bracket, so the relative move in the *full* loss is smaller still — but always in the right direction, and it compounds with scale. The actionable reading: fine-graining is a reliable, free improvement; the question is never whether to use $G>1$, only how far to push it before routing overhead eats the gain.

### Why granularity has diminishing returns of its own

The $G^{-\gamma}$ shape with $\gamma=0.58$ is itself a power law, which means granularity has its own diminishing returns — and they are steep. Going from $G=1$ to $G=2$ cuts the granularity term by 33% (2.10 to 1.40). Going from $G=8$ to $G=16$ cuts it by another 33% (0.63 to 0.42) but in absolute terms that is only a 0.21 reduction, versus the 0.70 you got from the first doubling. Each doubling of $G$ multiplies the term by $2^{-0.58}\approx 0.668$, so the *fractional* gain per doubling is constant but the *absolute* gain shrinks geometrically. The first doubling is worth more than all the doublings past $G=16$ combined.

This is why DeepSeekMoE's choice of $m=4$ (i.e. $G=4$) is well-justified rather than arbitrary: at $G=4$ the term is already 0.94, down from 2.10, capturing more than half of the total possible reduction (the term can fall at most to its limit of 0 as $G\to\infty$, but realistically it is near 0.19 by $G=64$). You have collected the cheap, large gains and not yet paid the routing-overhead tax that high $G$ incurs. The general principle: when a benefit follows a power law in a knob, the optimal setting of that knob is set by where the *power-law gain* crosses the *linear-or-worse cost*, and for granularity the cost (number of expert matmuls, all-to-all message count) grows roughly linearly in $G$ while the gain decays as $G^{-0.58}$, so the crossover lands at modest $G$ — single digits to low tens, exactly the range production models use.

### A joint compute-optimal sketch

Krajewski's full result is a compute-optimal allocation: given a FLOP budget $C$, choose $N$, $D$, and $G$ jointly to minimize $L(N,D,G)$ subject to $C \approx 6ND$ (the standard dense FLOP approximation, with the MoE subtlety that $N$ here is the active-equivalent count entering the FLOP budget). The data term $b/D^{\beta}$ and the model term $(g/G^\gamma + a)/N^\alpha$ are traded off exactly as in Chinchilla, with one new degree of freedom: increasing $G$ shrinks the effective coefficient on the model term, which shifts the optimal $N$-versus-$D$ split and, critically, lowers the achievable loss at every budget. Because the granularity benefit attaches to the $N^{-\alpha}$ term, and because larger budgets push the optimum toward larger $N$, the granularity term is multiplied against a larger lever at larger budgets — which is the formal reason the MoE-versus-dense gap widens with $C$. You do not need to solve the optimization by hand to use this; the takeaway is that $G$ is a third allocation axis, it never wants to be 1, and its optimal value drifts upward as your budget grows. A team scaling from a $10^{21}$-FLOP run to a $10^{23}$-FLOP run should expect both the optimal $N$ and the optimal $G$ to increase, and should expect the dense baseline to fall further behind.

## 7. How MoE scaling understanding evolved

**Senior rule of thumb: track the lineage of a result so you know which claims are load-bearing and which are folklore.** The three papers at the center of this post did not appear in isolation; they are a four-year progression from routing tricks to a closed-form joint law.

![A timeline tracing Mixture-of-Experts scaling understanding from early sparse routing through Clark's bilinear law and effective parameter count, DeepSeekMoE's fine-grained and shared experts, Krajewski's granularity law, and the DeepSeek V2 and V3 frontier models](/imgs/blogs/moe-scaling-laws-8.png)

The timeline reads left to right. Sparse routing (GShard, Switch, 2020–21) established that you can scale parameters cheaply with top-$K$ gating and load balancing — the engineering foundation. Clark 2022 (February) gave the first parametric loss surface, the bilinear law, the effective-parameter-count collapse, and the $E_{\max}\approx 314$ saturation ceiling — the predictive foundation. DeepSeekMoE 2024 (January) contributed fine-grained segmentation and shared experts, bending the efficiency frontier without changing FLOPs — the architectural foundation, with the 16B-matches-7B-at-40% and 145B-matches-67B-at-28.5% numbers as proof. Krajewski 2024 (February) made granularity a continuous axis and fit the joint $L(N,D,G)$ law, proving $G=1$ is rarely optimal and the MoE gap widens with compute — the unifying foundation. DeepSeek-V2 and V3 (2024) then deployed the whole recipe at frontier scale, validating it where it matters most.

The reason to keep this lineage straight is that the field is littered with conflated claims. "MoE saturates at 64 experts" (an eyeballed knee from Clark, not the $E_{\max}=314$ fit) gets cited as if it were a law. "MoE is always more efficient" (true at the frontier, by Krajewski, but with a memory-cost asterisk) gets stated without the asterisk. "Fine-graining is free" (true in FLOPs, false in communication) gets repeated without the bound. Knowing which paper established which claim, and under what conditions, is what lets you reason about a new MoE design instead of pattern-matching to slogans.

## 8. Putting the three laws together

We now have three lenses on the same object. It is worth a single table that lines them up, because each answers a different question and you reach for a different one depending on what you are trying to decide.

| Lens | Paper | Variables | What it predicts | When you reach for it |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Bilinear loss law | Clark 2022 | $N$, $E$ (via $\hat{E}$) | Loss surface over size and expert count; the saturation ceiling | Deciding how many experts to use at a fixed token budget |
| Effective parameter count | Clark 2022 | EPC vs active/total | The dense-equivalent size of a routed model | Comparing a routed model to a dense alternative; sanity-checking routing gains |
| Joint granularity law | Krajewski 2024 | $N$, $D$, $G$ | Loss over size, data, and granularity; optimal $G>1$ | Choosing granularity; joint compute-optimal allocation |
| Architecture recipe | DeepSeekMoE 2024 | $m$ (granularity), $K_s$ (shared) | Empirical efficiency vs dense and standard MoE | Designing the layer: how fine, how many shared |

The unified picture: a routed model is parameterized by total size $N$, training tokens $D$, raw expert count $E$, granularity $G$, shared-expert count $K_s$, and active-$K$. Clark's law tells you that loss falls with $\log N$ and $\log \hat{E}$ but with a negative interaction and a hard ceiling. EPC tells you the dense model your routed design is secretly equivalent to. Krajewski's law tells you that pushing $G$ above 1 always helps and the help grows with budget. DeepSeekMoE tells you that shared experts plus fine-graining is the concrete architecture that realizes these gains. None of the four contradicts the others; they are different cross-sections of one design space.

A subtle reconciliation deserves restating because it trips people up. Clark says experts saturate ($E_{\max}\approx 314$, $N_{\text{cutoff}}\approx 937$B at 130B tokens). Krajewski says the MoE advantage widens with compute. Both are correct. Clark fixed the token budget and varied only the expert count, so he saw the expert axis flatten. Krajewski varied $N$, $D$, and $G$ jointly under a compute constraint, so he saw the *frontier* — the best achievable loss at each budget — keep favoring finer MoE. Saturation of one axis at fixed data is not saturation of the optimized frontier. If you only remember one, remember the frontier result, because it is the one that governs what you should actually build: scale all the axes together, and MoE pulls further ahead of dense as you do.

There is also a worthwhile distinction in what each law is *for*. Clark's bilinear law and EPC are diagnostic: they let you take an existing or proposed routed model and locate it — how many effective experts, how much effective size, how close to saturation. Krajewski's joint law is prescriptive: it tells you, before you build anything, how to set $N$, $D$, and $G$ for a budget. DeepSeekMoE is constructive: it gives you the concrete layer architecture (fine-grained plus shared) that realizes a good point in that space. In a real project you use all three in sequence — Krajewski to choose the design point, DeepSeekMoE to instantiate it, and Clark/EPC to verify after the fact that the trained model landed where the prescription said it would. If the EPC of your trained model is far below what the joint law predicted, that gap is your signal that something in the systems layer — load balancing, dropped tokens, communication — is stealing the capacity the math promised. The laws are not just predictions to admire; they are a closed loop of design, build, and verify.

## 9. The systems reality the laws do not price

**Senior rule of thumb: a scaling law that prices FLOPs is silent on the constraint that usually binds — read what it leaves out as carefully as what it includes.** Every coefficient in this post was fit against *loss as a function of training compute or active FLOPs*. That is the right axis for predicting quality. It is the wrong axis for predicting what an MoE costs to train and serve, because the binding constraints there are memory and communication, and the laws say nothing about either.

Start with where the experts physically live. A 64-expert layer cannot fit its experts on one device at frontier scale, so the experts are sharded across devices in a scheme called *expert parallelism*: device 0 holds experts 0–7, device 1 holds 8–15, and so on. When a token needs experts 3 and 50, its hidden state must be sent to the devices holding those experts, processed, and sent back. With a batch of tokens scattered across all experts, this becomes an *all-to-all* collective: every device sends each other device the tokens destined for its experts. All-to-all is one of the most bandwidth-hungry collectives there is, and at small batch sizes — exactly the regime of low-latency inference — it dominates the forward pass. This is why a routed model with 6.8B active parameters can serve *slower* than a 6.8B dense model: the dense model does no all-to-all, while the MoE pays the communication tax on every layer (case study 1).

The training picture is gentler but still real. During training you have large batches, so the all-to-all amortizes better, but you now have a load-balancing problem in the systems sense: if the router sends more tokens to the experts on device 3 than to those on device 0, device 3 becomes a straggler and the whole step waits on it. Capacity factors exist partly to bound this — by capping how many tokens any expert accepts, they cap the straggler skew — but capping means dropping or rerouting the overflow tokens, which is a quality cost the loss law was not measuring on the dropped tokens. The interaction between the *statistical* load balancing (don't let experts collapse) and the *systems* load balancing (don't let one device straggle) is one of the genuinely hard parts of MoE engineering, and it is entirely outside the scaling law's frame.

Then there is memory at rest. The 93.6B-total model from the EPC ledger needs 93.6B parameters of storage regardless of how few it activates. In fp16 that is about 187 GB just for weights, before optimizer states, activations, or KV cache. The compute discount the scaling law celebrates does nothing for this number — you pay full freight to store every expert. The mitigation is precision: storing experts in 8-bit or 4-bit cuts the memory by 2–4x, and because most experts are idle on any token, the per-token error from quantizing them is often tolerable. This is the direct bridge to the [precision scaling laws](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/precision-scaling-laws): MoE and low precision are natural partners, because MoE creates a large pool of rarely-touched parameters and low precision is how you make a large rarely-touched pool affordable to store. A practitioner planning an MoE deployment should budget memory in quantized bytes, not fp16 parameters, or the storage premium will be a nasty surprise.

The summary judgment: the three scaling laws tell you what to *build* for quality at a compute budget; they do not tell you what it will *cost* to run. For that you need the systems model — expert parallelism, all-to-all bandwidth, capacity factors, quantized storage — layered on top. The two analyses are complementary, and shipping an MoE means doing both. A design that is compute-optimal and memory-infeasible is not a good design; it is a paper result.

## 10. Failure modes and second-order effects

**Senior rule of thumb: a scaling law tells you the trend on the happy path; the failures live in the assumptions the law quietly makes.** Three categories of failure separate a paper result from a working model.

The first is **load imbalance**, which the laws assume away. Every coefficient above was fit on models with working load balancing — experts receiving roughly equal token shares. If your router collapses onto a few favorites, your *effective* expert count is far below your nominal $E$, and you are sitting much further left on the saturation curve than you think. The symptom is a routed model whose EPC barely exceeds its active count: you bought experts but the router is not using them. The fixes are the standard auxiliary load-balancing loss, capacity factors that cap how many tokens an expert accepts (with the rest dropped or rerouted), and noise in the router during early training. The second-order gotcha: a capacity factor set too low silently drops tokens, which corrupts the loss measurement you are trying to fit a law to, so always log your drop rate before trusting a scaling extrapolation.

The second is the **memory-versus-compute asymmetry**, which the efficiency numbers hide. DeepSeekMoE 145B does 67B-equivalent compute but stores 145B parameters. At serving time, those 145B parameters must live in memory across your devices even though most are idle per token, and the all-to-all communication to gather the right experts can dominate latency at small batch sizes. The scaling laws price *training compute* and *active FLOPs*; they do not price *memory bandwidth* or *expert-gather latency*, which is often the binding constraint in production. This is why MoE serving leans so hard on expert parallelism, careful batching, and aggressive quantization — themes that connect to the [precision scaling laws](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/precision-scaling-laws), since low precision is how you make storing many experts affordable. The second-order gotcha: a model that is compute-optimal under Krajewski's law can be a serving disaster if its expert count blows past your memory budget, so the real optimization is constrained by memory, not just FLOPs.

The third is **extrapolation past the fitted regime**, which is where clean power laws lie to you. Clark's $\hat{E}$ saturation and $N_{\text{cutoff}}$ are themselves a warning: the bilinear law without the saturating transform would predict experts keep helping forever, which is false past the ceiling. More generally, a single smooth law fit in one regime can mispredict badly across a break — the granularity term might have its own saturation at very high $G$ where routing overhead dominates, a regime the simple law does not model. This is exactly the territory of the [broken neural scaling laws](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/broken-neural-scaling-laws) framework, which fits *multiple* power-law segments stitched at smooth breaks and extrapolates more reliably than a single power law across saturations, double descents, and inflections. The second-order gotcha: when your MoE measurements start deviating from the joint law's prediction, do not force-fit the original constants — suspect a break, and consider a broken-law fit before you conclude the architecture is failing.

## Case studies from production

### 1. The 145B model that served like a fridge

A team trained a fine-grained MoE at roughly 145B total parameters with about 22B active, expecting it to serve at 22B-class latency. It did not. At their production batch size of one to four requests, the all-to-all expert-gather step dominated every forward pass, and effective latency was closer to their old 70B dense model. The wrong first hypothesis was "the GPUs are too slow." The actual root cause was that expert parallelism amortizes only at large batch; at batch one, gathering the top-8 of 64 fine-grained experts across eight devices is communication-bound, not compute-bound. The fix was to raise the serving batch with continuous batching and to co-locate frequently co-activated experts on the same device. The lesson: Krajewski's law optimized their *training* FLOPs beautifully and said nothing about their *serving* latency, which lives in the memory-and-communication asymmetry the law does not model.

### 2. The router that quietly collapsed

A 32-expert model's loss plateaued far above its dense-equivalent target. The team's first guess was that the data was too small. The actual cause surfaced only when they logged per-expert token counts: four of the 32 experts were receiving 80% of all tokens, and the rest were nearly dead. Their EPC was barely above their active count — they had paid for 32 experts and were effectively using six. The auxiliary load-balancing loss coefficient had been set an order of magnitude too low. Raising it, and adding router noise for the first few thousand steps, spread the load and dropped the loss onto the expected dense curve. The lesson: when a routed model underperforms its EPC target, suspect the router before the data, and always log the token distribution.

### 3. The dense baseline that nobody could beat

A team building a small model (around 1B active) kept finding that their MoE variants barely beat a same-active-FLOP dense baseline. They suspected a bug. There was no bug. At 1B active and a modest token budget, they were operating at low compute — exactly the regime where Krajewski's law says the MoE advantage is *smallest*, since the gap widens with budget. Their routing overhead was eating most of the thin advantage. The fix was a decision, not a patch: at their scale and serving constraints, a dense model was the right choice, and they shipped dense. The lesson: MoE is not free lunch at every scale; the advantage is budget-dependent, and at small budgets a clean dense model can win on simplicity.

### 4. The granularity that went too fine

Encouraged by "finer is better," a team pushed granularity to $G=16$ with very small experts and top-16 routing. Training throughput cratered. The compute-FLOPs were unchanged, exactly as the law promised, but the number of separate expert matmuls and the routing/communication bookkeeping exploded, and the per-step wall-clock time more than doubled. The wrong hypothesis was a kernel inefficiency. The actual cause was that the simple scaling law prices FLOPs, not the fixed overheads per expert that grow linearly in the expert count. Backing off to $G=4$ recovered throughput while keeping most of the loss benefit (recall the granularity term is already down to 0.94 at $G=4$ versus 2.10 at $G=1$). The lesson: the law's "free in FLOPs" is real, but FLOPs are not wall-clock; there is an overhead-bounded optimal $G$ well below "as fine as possible."

### 5. The expert count past the knee

A team kept adding experts to a fixed-size model — 64, then 128, then 256 — and watched the loss gains shrink to nothing between 128 and 256. They suspected diminishing data. The real explanation was Clark's saturation: at their model size and token budget, they were climbing the flat part of the $\hat{E}$ curve, approaching the $E_{\max}\approx 314$ regime where extra raw experts barely move the effective expert count. The doubling from 128 to 256 was almost pure storage cost for almost no quality. The fix was to stop adding experts and instead spend the budget on more tokens and finer granularity. The lesson: the expert axis saturates; check where you are on the $\hat{E}$ curve before doubling $E$ again, and remember $E_{\max}\approx 314$ is the fitted ceiling, not the eyeballed ~64.

### 6. The EPC reality check that killed a project

A proposal claimed a new routing scheme would let a 7B-active model reach 70B-dense quality — a 10x effective multiplier. Before committing compute, the team ran the EPC procedure on a small sweep: train the routing scheme at small scale, measure loss, read EPC off the dense curve. The measured EPC came out around 18B against 7B active — a 2.6x multiplier, healthy but nowhere near 10x. The 10x claim had come from comparing total parameters (70B stored) to active, not from any loss measurement. The project was rescoped honestly around a 2.6x multiplier. The lesson: EPC is the antidote to total-versus-active confusion; always read effective size off a loss curve, never off the parameter ledger.

### 7. The shared experts that were forgotten

A team adopted fine-grained routing from DeepSeekMoE but skipped the shared-expert isolation, reasoning it was a minor detail. Their routed experts kept relearning common patterns, and the loss sat above the DeepSeekMoE-recipe target. Adding two always-on shared experts — and correctly accounting for them in the active-FLOP budget — closed most of the gap, because the shared experts absorbed the high-frequency common knowledge and let the routed experts specialize. The lesson: the two DeepSeekMoE moves are complementary, not independent; fine-graining gives you specialists, and shared experts give those specialists permission to actually specialize by removing the common-knowledge tax.

### 8. The cross-budget extrapolation that broke

A team fit Krajewski-style constants on runs up to a modest budget, then extrapolated the optimal $G$ to a budget 30x larger and committed to a very high granularity. The large run underperformed the prediction. The cause was not the law's structure but its regime: the high-$G$ extrapolation walked into a routing-overhead regime the fitted constants never saw, and the true loss curve had bent — a break the single smooth law could not represent. Re-fitting with an explicit saturation term on the granularity axis (in the spirit of the broken-law framework) gave a more conservative optimal $G$ that the large run matched. The lesson: trust the joint law inside its fitted box; across a 30x budget jump, expect a break and fit for it.

### 9. The HASH baseline that looked broken

A research team reproducing Clark's setup implemented HASH routing first as a sanity check and found it barely beat dense at their model size, while the literature seemed to promise large MoE gains. They suspected an implementation bug in the hashing. There was no bug. HASH's model-size cutoff is the lowest of the three methods (~83B), so at their fairly large backbone they were already near HASH's saturation, where it has almost nothing to offer over dense. Swapping in a learned balanced-assignment router (S-BASE-style) moved the cutoff out to the ~937B regime and the expected gains appeared. The lesson: routing-method choice sets where saturation kicks in; a "broken" MoE is sometimes just a crude router at a scale where its cheap assignment has run out of room.

### 10. The auxiliary loss that overcorrected

After case study 2 burned a team on under-balancing, a different team over-corrected: they set the load-balancing loss coefficient very high to guarantee perfectly uniform expert utilization. Loss got *worse*. The cause was that forcing perfectly uniform routing fights the whole point of routing — the router should send tokens to the experts that help them, which means utilization is naturally a bit uneven (some experts are genuinely more useful for the data distribution). Cranking the balancing loss too high turned the learned router into something closer to HASH, throwing away the assignment quality that S-BASE-style routing exists to provide. Dialing the coefficient back to a value that prevented collapse without enforcing uniformity recovered the loss. The lesson: balancing is a guardrail against collapse, not an objective in itself; the target is "no dead experts," not "perfectly equal experts."

### 11. The granularity that broke the kernels

A team set $G$ to a value that made each expert's inner dimension small enough that the per-expert matmuls fell off the efficient region of their GPU's tensor cores — the matmul tiles were too small to saturate the hardware, so achieved FLOP utilization dropped even though the *theoretical* FLOPs were unchanged. The scaling law, which counts theoretical FLOPs, said the design was free; the hardware, which only runs efficiently on large-enough tiles, disagreed. They raised $d_{\text{expert}}$ back above the tensor-core efficiency threshold (lowering $G$), trading a small amount of the granularity loss benefit for a large recovery in throughput. The lesson: granularity is free in *counted* FLOPs but not in *achieved* FLOPs; there is a hardware floor on expert size below which the law's "free" stops being free, and it is set by your accelerator's efficient matmul tile, not by the math.

## What this means in practice

If you are deciding whether and how to use MoE, here is the distilled guidance, in the order you should ask the questions.

**First, decide if MoE is worth it at your scale.** The advantage widens with compute, so the bigger your budget, the stronger the case. At small active sizes and modest token budgets — case study 3 — a dense model often wins on simplicity and serving latency. Ask: is my budget large enough that the MoE gap is real after routing overhead? If you are at the frontier, yes; if you are training a 1B model on a laptop's worth of compute, probably not.

**Second, treat $(N, D, G)$ as joint axes, not $(N, D)$ with a bolted-on expert count.** Use the [Chinchilla logic](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/chinchilla-compute-optimal-scaling) to split between $N$ and $D$, then add granularity as a third axis where the answer is always $G>1$. Do not set $G=1$ by default — that is the dense corner, and Krajewski's law says it is almost never optimal. Start around $G=4$ (DeepSeekMoE's choice), which already captures most of the granularity benefit (term down from 2.10 to 0.94), and push higher only if your communication budget allows.

**Third, include shared experts.** Carve out a small number of always-on shared experts to absorb common knowledge, and account for them in your active-FLOP budget. This is nearly free and reliably helps — case study 7 is what happens when you skip it.

**Fourth, do not chase raw expert count past the knee.** The expert axis saturates ($E_{\max}\approx 314$ for S-BASE, with an eyeballed knee far lower). Past the knee you are paying storage and memory for almost no quality. Check where you sit on the $\hat{E}$ curve before doubling $E$; spend marginal budget on tokens and granularity instead.

**Fifth, use EPC to sanity-check, always.** Before you believe any "Nx more efficient" claim, run the EPC procedure: train small, measure loss, read the dense-equivalent size off the dense curve, divide by active. A healthy multiplier is roughly 3–6x; a multiplier near 1 means your routing is not earning its keep; a multiplier computed from total-versus-active rather than from loss is marketing, not measurement (case study 6).

**Sixth, budget for memory and communication separately from FLOPs.** The scaling laws price training compute and active FLOPs; they are silent on the memory to store every expert and the all-to-all latency to gather them. A compute-optimal MoE can be a serving disaster (case studies 1 and 4). Optimize under your real constraint, which is usually memory, and lean on quantization — the [precision scaling laws](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/precision-scaling-laws) are how you make storing many experts affordable.

**Seventh, distrust extrapolation across large budget jumps.** The joint law is reliable inside its fitted regime. Across a 30x budget jump, or into very high granularity, expect a break and consider a multi-segment fit in the spirit of [broken neural scaling laws](/blog/machine-learning/scaling-laws/broken-neural-scaling-laws) before committing compute to an extrapolated optimum (case study 8).

The single sentence to leave with: in MoE there is no one model size — there is total, active, and effective — and the whole discipline of MoE scaling laws is about pricing the gap between them with three axes, $N$, $D$, and $G$, where the dense world only had two.
