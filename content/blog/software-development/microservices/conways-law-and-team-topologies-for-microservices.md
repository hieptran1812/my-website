The cleanest microservices design review I ever sat in produced the worst microservices system I ever operated, and the reason had nothing to do with the architecture diagram. The diagram was lovely. We had drawn boundaries by domain, given each service its own database, sketched the events that would flow between them, and argued productively about whether the order service should call payment synchronously or emit an event. We left the room proud. Eighteen months later that same system shipped on a release train, six services deployed together every other Thursday behind a frozen change window, and the thing that had quietly strangled it was not on the whiteboard at all: there were four teams, and one of the services — a service called `common` that held "shared" entities and "shared" utilities — was edited by three of them. No team owned it. Every team needed it. So every meaningful change required three teams to agree, and the agreement meeting was the bottleneck the whole platform throttled through. We had designed the services. We had forgotten to design the *organization*, and the organization designed the architecture right back, the way it always does.

That is the realization this post is about, and it is the realization that separates a senior engineer from a staff or principal one: **microservices are an organizational design problem at least as much as a technical one, and your architecture will mirror your org whether you plan it or not.** You can spend a month on bounded contexts and aggregate roots and still end up with a distributed monolith if four teams share one service and three teams co-edit a god library, because the *seams of the system land at the boundaries of how people communicate*, not at the boundaries you drew on the diagram. This is not a soft "people matter too" platitude. It is a structural force with a name — Conway's Law — and the figure below is the whole post in one picture: the same product, built by two differently-shaped organizations, producing two structurally different systems.

![A side by side comparison of a misaligned organization where four teams share a common service producing lock-step deploys and seams that cut through teams versus an aligned organization where one team owns one service producing independent deploys and seams at team boundaries](/imgs/blogs/conways-law-and-team-topologies-for-microservices-1.webp)

On the left, an org where ownership overlaps: four teams share a `common` service, no service has a single clear owner, and the seams of the system fall *through* the teams rather than between them. The architecture that org produces deploys in lock-step (the co-deploy ratio sat at 3.1 services per release) because a change anywhere requires a coordination meeting somewhere. On the right, the same product built by an org where one team owns one service, the seams fall cleanly at team boundaries, and the co-deploy ratio drops to 0.2 — each service ships on its own. Same number of boxes, same domain, opposite operability. The difference is not the architecture. The difference is the org chart that *produced* the architecture. By the end of this post you will be able to read your own architecture as a reflection of your communication structure, deliberately reshape teams to produce the architecture you want (the inverse-Conway maneuver), use the four Team Topologies team types and three interaction modes to organize a microservices fleet, size a team's responsibilities by cognitive load rather than headcount, and diagnose the specific failure mode — a service owned by no one — that turns clean diagrams into release trains.

This is a Track 9 post, the organizational track, and it sits at the meeting point of everything earlier in the series. The boundaries you draw with [domain-driven design](/blog/software-development/microservices/service-boundaries-with-domain-driven-design) only hold if the team boundaries hold them; the [distributed monolith](/blog/software-development/microservices/shared-data-anti-patterns-and-the-distributed-monolith) is as often an org failure as a data failure; and [independent deployability](/blog/software-development/microservices/ci-cd-and-independent-deployability) — the litmus test of whether you have microservices at all — is determined more by who has to agree before you ship than by your CI pipeline. We will use the same running system, **ShopFast**, that appears across this series.

## 1. Conway's Law, stated precisely

Let me start with the law itself, because it is constantly paraphrased and almost as constantly misquoted. In 1968, Melvin Conway published a paper called "How Do Committees Invent?" with a thesis he stated in one sentence: *"Any organization that designs a system (defined broadly) will produce a design whose structure is a copy of the organization's communication structure."* Read the words carefully, because the precision matters. It does not say the architecture copies the *org chart*. It says it copies the *communication structure* — who actually talks to whom to get work done, which is sometimes the org chart and often is not. And it does not say "might" or "tends to." Conway's claim is that this is a near-inevitability, because of a simple mechanism: for two software modules to interface correctly, the two people or groups building them must communicate and agree on that interface. Where communication is easy and frequent — inside a team — interfaces are rich, chatty, and tightly coupled, because there is no friction to discourage coupling. Where communication is hard and infrequent — across a team boundary, especially across a department or a time zone — interfaces are thin, formal, and few, because every interaction costs a meeting. So the *coupling structure* of the software comes to match the *communication structure* of the people, edge for edge.

For microservices, this has a blunt corollary that is worth tattooing somewhere visible: **if four teams build a system, you will get four-ish services, and the seams will land at the team boundaries.** Not because anyone decreed it, but because within each team people will couple freely (in-process calls, shared models, the same database) and between teams they will couple reluctantly (an API, an event, a contract). The places where coupling is cheap stay inside a service; the places where coupling is expensive become the service boundaries. The architecture is a fossil of the communication structure that produced it. This is why, when I review a system I have never seen, I ask for the org chart *before* I ask for the architecture diagram — the org chart predicts the architecture better than anyone's stated intentions do, and where the two disagree, the org chart is telling the truth.

The corollary that does the real damage is the *shared service* case. Suppose three teams all need to edit one service — say a `common` service, or a shared authentication library, or a central "platform" service that grew tendrils into everything. Now the communication structure for changing that service is "three teams must agree." Conway's Law says the software's coupling will mirror that: every change to that service couples three teams' release schedules, three teams' review queues, three teams' priorities. The service becomes a *coordination bottleneck*, and the throughput of the whole platform is capped by the slowest of the three teams' availability to meet. The service didn't fail technically. The communication structure around it was a bottleneck, and the software faithfully reproduced it.

There is a subtle but important nuance that separates people who have read about Conway's Law from people who have been bitten by it. The law is symmetric in a way that the casual reading misses: it is not just that a bad org produces a bad architecture, it is that the architecture, once it exists, *reinforces* the communication structure that produced it. A shared service that three teams edit forces those three teams to keep talking, keep meeting, keep coordinating — which keeps the service shared, which keeps them meeting. It is a feedback loop, a homeostasis, and it is why distributed monoliths are so stubborn: the architecture and the org are each holding the other in place. You cannot fix one without fixing the other, and most failed migrations fail because they try to fix only the code.

It helps to walk through the *scenarios* Conway's Law produces, because once you can name them you start seeing them everywhere. Picture four common org shapes and the architecture each one mirrors into existence. An org split by **technology layer** — a front-end team, a back-end team, a database team — produces an architecture split by layer: a thin UI tier calling a fat services tier calling a database tier maintained by a third group, with a hand-off at every boundary and no single team able to ship a feature end to end. An org split by **business capability** — a team for ordering, a team for catalog, a team for fulfilment — produces services aligned to those capabilities, which is exactly what you want, because each team can ship its slice without a hand-off. An org with a **central shared-services team** that everyone depends on produces an architecture with a central shared service that everything routes through, recreating the bottleneck at the code level. And an org with **offshore or split-timezone teams** produces an architecture with thick, formal, asynchronous interfaces at exactly the timezone seams — because that is where communication is most expensive — and rich coupling within each timezone's services. None of these are accidents. Each architecture is the fingerprint of the org that built it, and if you do not like the architecture, the durable fix is upstream, in the org.

The reason this matters for a microservices practitioner specifically is that microservices *amplify* Conway's Law rather than escaping it. In a monolith, the coupling between two badly-aligned teams shows up as messy code in a shared codebase — annoying, but the compiler and the single deploy contain the damage. In a microservices system, the same misalignment shows up as a *distributed* coupling: a synchronous call chain across team boundaries, a shared database two teams both write, a god library vendored into five services. Now the coupling has a network in the middle of it, and the blast radius is production rather than a code review. So the cost of getting the org wrong scales *up* when you go to microservices, which is the deep reason this is a Track 9 post and not a footnote: the more distributed your architecture, the more your org structure determines whether it works.

## 2. ShopFast: the org that built the architecture

Let me ground all of this in the running example. **ShopFast** is the e-commerce platform that appears throughout this series. Today it has six engineering teams and around a dozen services, and its history is a textbook Conway story. Two years ago ShopFast was a Rails monolith owned by one team of nine. As it grew, leadership did what most companies do: they hired, split into teams roughly by feature area, and told the teams to "break the monolith into microservices." Nobody designed the *teams* to produce a target architecture. The teams formed first — by who happened to be hiring, by who sat near whom, by which features were hot that quarter — and then the architecture grew out of the teams, exactly as Conway predicts.

The result was a mess that looked fine on a slide. Let me describe ShopFast's *intended* end-state first, because it is also the shape we will spend the rest of this post justifying. A healthy ShopFast org is mostly **stream-aligned** teams — teams that each own a slice of the business end to end — supported by a thin set of specialist teams. The figure below is that target org as a taxonomy.

![A tree diagram of the ShopFast engineering organization showing six teams split into three stream-aligned teams that own business slices and three supporting teams a platform team an enabling team and a complicated-subsystem team for the pricing engine](/imgs/blogs/conways-law-and-team-topologies-for-microservices-2.webp)

There are six teams. Three of them are stream-aligned: a Checkout team (owning the order, payment, and cart services), a Catalog team (owning product, search, and recommendations), and a Fulfilment team (owning inventory, shipping, and warehouse integration). Three of them are supporting teams of different types: a Platform team that owns the paved road — the Kubernetes substrate, the CI/CD pipelines, the observability stack — so the stream-aligned teams do not each have to; an Enabling team that coaches the stream-aligned teams on practices like testing and tracing without owning anything in production; and a complicated-subsystem team that owns the pricing and promotions engine, a genuinely hard piece of math that benefits from concentrated expertise. We will define all four of these team types precisely in §4. For now the shape is what matters: **mostly stream-aligned, thinly supported.** Hold that picture; the rest of the post is an argument for why it works and how ShopFast got there from the mess it started in.

What ShopFast actually had, before the re-org, was different in one decisive way: a service called `common`. It held the shared `Customer` and `Order` entity classes, the shared ORM mappings, a pile of utility code, and — over time — a creeping amount of business logic that "didn't fit anywhere else." Three teams edited it. And that single service, owned by no one and needed by everyone, is the thread we will pull on, because it is the most common way Conway's Law turns a clean design into a release train.

## 3. The shared service that nobody owned

Here is the mechanism in detail, because it is the heart of the org-architecture failure mode and you will see it everywhere once you can name it. A shared service edited by multiple teams violates the rule that gives microservices their entire value proposition: independent change. When the Checkout team needs to add a field to the `Order` entity, that entity lives in `common`, so the change is a pull request against `common`. But `common` is also imported by Catalog and Fulfilment, so the change has to be reviewed by people on those teams, scheduled around their release cadence, and tested against their usage. A change that should have been one team's one-line commit becomes a three-team negotiation. Multiply that across every feature, and the platform's velocity collapses to the speed of cross-team coordination.

#### Worked example: the cost of a service edited by three teams

Let me put numbers on it, because "it slows us down" does not survive a planning meeting but a number does. At ShopFast, the `common` service was edited by three teams. Over a representative month, the git history showed 38 pull requests against `common`, spread across the three teams. Of those, 14 hit merge conflicts because two teams were editing overlapping regions of the same files — a conflict rate of 37%, where a single-owner service in the same org ran a conflict rate near 2%. Each conflict cost, conservatively, an hour of someone's time to resolve plus a re-review, so call it 14 hours/month of pure conflict tax.

The lead-time cost was worse than the conflict cost. Because every `common` change needed sign-off from at least one engineer on each of the other two teams, and those engineers were busy with their own work, the median time from "PR opened" to "PR merged" for a `common` change was **9 calendar days**, versus a median of **6 hours** for a change to a service with a single owning team. That 9-day lead time was not engineering time — it was almost entirely *waiting for other teams to have capacity to look*. When ShopFast measured how many features in a quarter touched `common` (because they needed a shared entity change), it was 60% of them. So 60% of the roadmap was running through a 9-day coordination gate. The architecture diagram had nothing to do with it. The communication structure — "three teams must agree to change one service" — was the bottleneck, and the software faithfully reproduced it as a co-deploy ratio of 3.1 and a release train.

![A before and after comparison showing a common service edited by three teams with 14 merge conflicts per month and a 9 day lead time versus the same service owned by one platform team with 1 merge conflict per month and a 2 day lead time](/imgs/blogs/conways-law-and-team-topologies-for-microservices-7.webp)

The fix, shown on the right of that figure, was not technical in the first instance — it was an *ownership* decision. ShopFast assigned the `common` service to a single owning team (the Platform team), turned its grab-bag of "shared utilities" into a properly versioned, published library with a stable contract, and pushed the business logic that had crept into it back out to the stream-aligned teams that owned those concepts. After the re-org, `common` saw about 1 merge conflict per month (the Platform team controlled all writes), and the lead time for the rare genuinely-shared change dropped to 2 days. But more importantly, *most changes stopped going through `common` at all*, because the stream-aligned teams now owned their own copies of the entities they cared about, behind their own service boundaries. The coordination gate that throttled 60% of the roadmap was gone — not because the code got faster, but because the communication structure that produced the coupling was redesigned.

The general principle, and it is the single most important rule in this post: **every service should have exactly one owning team.** Not "a primary owner with helpers." One team. The team that owns a service is the team that can change it, deploy it, and is woken up when it breaks, without needing anyone else's permission. A service owned by two teams is owned by neither; a service owned by a committee is the seed of a distributed monolith. We will return to this rule from several angles, but it is the foundation everything else rests on.

## 4. Team Topologies: the four team types

If "one team per service" is the rule, the natural question is: what *kinds* of teams should exist, and how should they relate? The most useful answer I have found is the framework Matthew Skelton and Manuel Pais lay out in their book *Team Topologies* (2019). It is the rare management book that is concrete enough for engineers to act on, and it maps cleanly onto a microservices fleet. The core claim is that almost every effective software organization can be built from just **four fundamental team types**, and that you should be deliberate about which type each team is, because the type determines what the team owns and how it should interact with others.

![A matrix comparing the four team types stream-aligned platform enabling and complicated-subsystem across their purpose what they own their default interaction mode and when to create one](/imgs/blogs/conways-law-and-team-topologies-for-microservices-4.webp)

Let me define each precisely, because the names are evocative but the distinctions are what you act on.

A **stream-aligned team** is aligned to a single valuable stream of work — typically a slice of the business or product, end to end. This is the *default* team type, and a healthy org is mostly stream-aligned teams. At ShopFast, Checkout, Catalog, and Fulfilment are stream-aligned: each owns its piece of the customer journey from the database to the API to the on-call pager. The defining trait is end-to-end ownership of a flow of business value, with as few hand-offs to other teams as possible. The reason this is the default is Conway's Law working *for* you: if you want services that are aligned to business capabilities (which is what [domain-driven boundaries](/blog/software-development/microservices/service-boundaries-with-domain-driven-design) give you), you want teams aligned to business capabilities, because the teams will produce the services. Stream-aligned teams are the streams; everything else exists to support them.

A **platform team** provides an internal product — a "platform," or what is often called the "paved road" or "golden path" — that reduces the cognitive load on stream-aligned teams. The key word is *product*: a good platform team treats its internal users (the stream-aligned teams) as customers, ships self-service tooling, writes docs, and is judged by adoption, not by mandate. At ShopFast, the Platform team owns the Kubernetes clusters, the CI/CD pipelines, the deployment tooling, the observability and tracing stack, and the service templates that scaffold a new service with logging, metrics, health checks, and an mTLS sidecar already wired in. The point is that the Checkout team should not have to become Kubernetes experts to ship a service — they should `platform deploy` and get a production-grade runtime for free. We will spend §5 on why this matters so much (cognitive load) and §6 on how the platform team should interact with its users (X-as-a-Service).

To make the platform team's value concrete: at ShopFast, before the Platform team existed, standing up a new service took a stream-aligned team about two weeks of elapsed time — provisioning a database, writing a Dockerfile, wiring up a CI pipeline, adding logging and metrics and health checks, configuring the mesh sidecar and TLS, and getting it into the deploy rotation. That two weeks was almost entirely *extraneous* load: none of it was the team's domain, and every team was independently solving the same problems slightly differently, so the estate became inconsistent and hard to operate. The Platform team turned that into a *golden path*: a `platform new-service` command that scaffolds a repository with the Dockerfile, the CI pipeline, the [Kubernetes manifests](/blog/software-development/microservices/kubernetes-for-microservices-the-essentials), the logging and tracing and health-check wiring, and a provisioned database already in place, conformant to the org's standards by construction. New-service time dropped from two weeks to under an hour. That is not a convenience; it is cognitive load returned to the stream-aligned teams, and consistency imposed on the estate as a *byproduct* of the paved road being the easy path. The platform team did not mandate the standards; it made the standard way the path of least resistance, which is the only kind of standardization that survives contact with deadlines.

An **enabling team** helps other teams acquire missing capabilities — it coaches, it pairs, it runs internal workshops, it researches and recommends practices. Crucially, an enabling team owns *nothing in production*. Its output is uplift in other teams, not running services. At ShopFast, the Enabling team spent a quarter embedded with each stream-aligned team in turn, getting them fluent in [distributed tracing](/blog/software-development/microservices/distributed-tracing-and-observability-with-opentelemetry) and [SLO-based alerting](/blog/software-development/microservices/slos-golden-signals-and-alerting-for-microservices), then moved on. The interaction is deliberately *temporary*: an enabling team that becomes permanently attached to a stream-aligned team has failed at its job, which is to make itself unnecessary.

A **complicated-subsystem team** owns a part of the system that requires deep, specialized expertise that it would be wasteful or impractical to spread across every stream-aligned team. The canonical examples are a real-time pricing engine, a video transcoding pipeline, a fraud-scoring model, a routing/optimization solver. At ShopFast, the pricing-and-promotions engine is a complicated subsystem: it involves combinatorial promotion stacking, currency and tax rules, and latency-sensitive computation, and concentrating that expertise in one team is genuinely better than asking every stream-aligned team to half-understand it. The danger to watch is that "complicated-subsystem team" is *not* a license to carve up the org by technical layer. It is justified only when the subsystem is genuinely, intrinsically complicated — not merely "a thing some team didn't want to own." Most attempts to create one are really just a stream-aligned team in disguise, and you should resist the reflex.

The proportions matter as much as the types. Skelton and Pais are explicit that stream-aligned teams should be the overwhelming majority, with platform, enabling, and complicated-subsystem teams kept thin and justified. The failure mode is an org that is *all* supporting teams and few streams — a "platform team," an "infrastructure team," a "shared services team," a "DevOps team," a "data team" — where the actual delivery of business value has to be assembled across five hand-offs. That org will produce an architecture with five hand-offs in every flow. Mostly streams, thinly supported.

## 5. Cognitive load: the constraint that sizes a team

Here is the question Team Topologies answers that almost nothing else in the microservices literature does: *how many services should a team own?* The naive answers are all wrong. "One per engineer" is arbitrary. "As many as fit in the repo" ignores the human. The right constraint, and the one that makes the whole framework click, is **cognitive load**: a team can only hold so much in its collective head, and you should size a team's responsibilities to fit *under* that ceiling, not pack it to the brim.

Cognitive load, in this framing, is the total mental effort a team must expend to do its job well: understanding the domain it serves, the services it operates, the technologies in its stack, the dependencies it integrates with, and the operational reality of being on call for all of it. When a team's cognitive load exceeds its capacity, quality degrades in predictable ways — things get understood shallowly, corners get cut, the on-call rotation becomes a game of pattern-matching alerts to runbooks nobody fully understands, and change-failure rate climbs because nobody can hold the whole picture. The platform team exists precisely to *reduce* this load: by owning the undifferentiated heavy lifting (the Kubernetes, the CI, the observability plumbing), it lets the stream-aligned teams spend their limited cognitive budget on the domain that is actually their job.

Team Topologies borrows a useful decomposition from cognitive science: load comes in three flavors, and you treat each differently. **Intrinsic** load is the irreducible difficulty of the problem itself — understanding the ordering domain, the payment rules, the inventory invariants. You cannot remove intrinsic load; you can only make sure the team has the skills to carry it (which is where an enabling team helps) and that the domain a team owns is coherent enough that its intrinsic load is *one* domain, not three unrelated ones. **Extraneous** load is the accidental difficulty of the environment — how to deploy, how to wire up logging, how to configure the mesh, how to get a database provisioned. Extraneous load is *pure waste*: it is mental effort spent on things that are not the team's domain, and it is exactly what a platform team's paved road exists to eliminate. Every piece of extraneous load you can move onto the platform is cognitive budget returned to the stream-aligned team for its actual job. **Germane** load is the effort that produces learning and value — designing the domain model well, thinking through the failure modes, improving the architecture. This is the load you *want* a team spending its budget on. So the design goal sharpens: minimize extraneous load (platform's job), keep intrinsic load coherent and bounded (boundary design's job), and protect the team's budget for germane load (the actual engineering). A team drowning in extraneous load — fighting YAML, debugging the pipeline, reinventing observability — has no budget left for germane work, and its architecture quality suffers for a reason that has nothing to do with its talent.

![A layered stack diagram showing stream-aligned teams on top resting on a self-service platform API then CI and deploy then observability and SLOs then Kubernetes and service mesh then base cloud and network infrastructure](/imgs/blogs/conways-law-and-team-topologies-for-microservices-5.webp)

The figure shows the relationship: the platform sits *underneath* the stream-aligned teams, absorbing the layers of complexity — the runtime, the CI/CD, the observability — so that the team on top only has to think about its slice of the business plus a thin, self-service platform API. Every layer the platform owns is a layer the stream-aligned team does not have to hold in its head. This is why a platform team is not overhead; it is a cognitive-load *amplifier* for the teams it serves. A platform team that does its job well lets each stream-aligned team own more business value with the same headcount, because the team is spending its mental budget on the domain rather than on YAML.

#### Worked example: a team owning 12 services versus 3

Let me make the cognitive-load constraint concrete with numbers, because this is where it stops being a slogan. Early in ShopFast's life, the Fulfilment team — six engineers — owned 12 services. They had inherited them through a series of "you touched it last, you own it now" hand-offs, and on paper it looked efficient: 12 services, 6 people, two services each. In practice it was a disaster, and the metrics showed exactly why.

With 12 services, the team carried 12 sets of dashboards, 12 deployment pipelines, 12 dependency graphs, 12 on-call runbooks. Their **change-failure rate** — the fraction of deploys that caused an incident or required a rollback — sat at 23%, because no one engineer understood more than three or four of the services deeply, so changes to the other eight were made on shaky understanding. Their **on-call burden** was brutal: with 12 services paging into one rotation, the on-call engineer was woken a median of 4 nights out of a 7-night shift, and the mean-time-to-recovery was high because the person paged often had never touched the service that fired the alert. Two engineers quit in six months, and exit interviews named on-call.

ShopFast restructured. They split Fulfilment's 12 services across the org so that the Fulfilment team kept the 3 services that were genuinely its domain (inventory, shipping, warehouse), moved 4 to a newly-formed stream-aligned team, folded 3 into the Catalog team's domain where they actually belonged, and *retired* 2 that turned out to be barely used. Now the Fulfilment team — same six people — owned 3 services. The change-failure rate dropped to 7%, because each engineer could hold all three services in their head. On-call woke the engineer a median of 1 night in 7. The team's feature throughput went *up*, not down, despite owning a quarter as many services, because they were no longer drowning. The lesson is the one Team Topologies hammers: **the number of services a team owns should be bounded by the team's cognitive capacity, and when you exceed it, you do not work harder — you split ownership.** Fewer services, deeply understood, beats more services, shallowly held, on every metric that matters.

A rough heuristic I use, which is not in any book but has held up across several orgs: a stream-aligned team of 5–8 engineers can own and operate **2–4 services well**, *if* a platform team is carrying the runtime load. Without a platform team — if the stream-aligned team also has to own its own Kubernetes, its own CI, its own observability — that number drops to 1–2, because the cognitive budget is being eaten by infrastructure. The ratio is not a law; it is a reminder that the answer to "how many services?" is "how much can this team hold?", and that the platform's job is to raise that ceiling.

## 6. The three interaction modes

Defining team types is half the framework; the other half is defining how teams *interact*, because Conway's Law operates on the interactions. Team Topologies names exactly three interaction modes, and the senior skill is choosing the right one for each pair of teams and being deliberate about how long they stay in it.

![A matrix comparing the three interaction modes collaboration X-as-a-Service and facilitating across their coupling cost what they are best for their expected duration and the failure mode if overused](/imgs/blogs/conways-law-and-team-topologies-for-microservices-6.webp)

**Collaboration** is two teams working closely together for a defined period, sharing responsibility for an outcome with high-bandwidth communication. It is the *highest-coupling, highest-cost* mode, and it is appropriate during discovery — when two teams are figuring out a new interface, exploring an unknown problem, or building something whose shape isn't clear yet. The critical constraint is that collaboration is meant to be *temporary*: weeks, not quarters. Two teams in permanent collaboration are, by Conway's Law, going to produce tightly-coupled software with a blurred ownership boundary — which is exactly the shared-service failure mode from §3. When the discovery is done, the teams should transition to a clearer, lower-coupling mode.

**X-as-a-Service** is one team consuming something another team provides "as a service," with a clear, minimal, well-documented interface and no ongoing coordination needed. This is the *low-coupling, steady-state* mode, and it should be the default for most team relationships in a mature org. The Checkout team consumes the Platform team's deployment pipeline as a service: they do not collaborate with the Platform team on every deploy, they just use the self-service tooling. They consume the Pricing team's engine as a service: a stable API, a versioned contract, no meeting required. X-as-a-Service is what you are aiming for, because it is the interaction mode that lets teams move independently — which is the whole point of microservices. The only failure mode is when the "service" interface is too thin or too rigid and the consuming team is constantly blocked waiting for new capabilities; the fix there is a better-designed, more self-service interface, not a regression to collaboration.

**Facilitating** is one team (usually an enabling team) helping another team learn or adopt a capability, then stepping away. It is time-boxed coaching. The ShopFast Enabling team facilitated the Checkout team's adoption of distributed tracing — paired with them for three weeks, left them fluent, moved on. The failure mode is the enabling team becoming a permanent crutch, which both creates a dependency and signals that the enabling team has stopped enabling and started doing.

The senior insight in this section is about *defaults and transitions*. Most pairs of teams should default to X-as-a-Service. Collaboration is a temporary, expensive state you enter on purpose for discovery and exit on purpose when the shape is clear. Facilitating is a time-boxed uplift. When you find two teams stuck in permanent collaboration, that is almost always a sign that the *boundary between them is in the wrong place* — they are collaborating constantly because the work they share should really be inside one team, or because the interface between them is badly designed. The interaction mode is a diagnostic: permanent high-bandwidth collaboration is a symptom of a Conway's-Law misalignment, and the fix is usually to redraw the team boundary, not to schedule more meetings.

## 7. You build it, you run it

There is an ownership philosophy that ties cognitive load, team types, and on-call together, and it comes from Amazon by way of a famous Werner Vogels interview: **"you build it, you run it."** The team that writes a service is the team that operates it — they are on the pager for it, they own its [SLOs](/blog/software-development/microservices/slos-golden-signals-and-alerting-for-microservices), they feel the pain of their own operational decisions directly. There is no separate operations team that catches the service when it falls over. The people who decided to skip the retry logic are the same people woken at 3am when its absence cascades.

![A graph showing the Checkout team owning a service through build and test then deploy then on-call all feeding into a stable team API that the Catalog team consumes](/imgs/blogs/conways-law-and-team-topologies-for-microservices-9.webp)

The figure shows the model: one owning team (Checkout) carries the service through build and test, through self-service deploy, through on-call and SLO ownership — and exposes to the rest of the org only a stable *team API*, the published contract that consumers like the Catalog team integrate against. This is a profound design force, not just an org-chart preference, and it is worth being precise about *why* it produces better systems. When the team that builds a service also runs it, the feedback loop between design decisions and operational consequences closes inside one team. The architect who is paged for the thing they designed will, by the second 3am page, design it to page less. They will add the [circuit breaker](/blog/software-development/microservices/resilience-patterns-timeouts-retries-circuit-breakers-bulkheads), the timeout, the graceful degradation, the health check — not because a standards document told them to, but because they personally do not want to be woken up. Separating build from run breaks this loop: the team that builds optimizes for shipping features (because they never feel the operational pain), and the team that runs is left holding a service they did not design and cannot change. "You build it, you run it" is Conway's Law applied to operability: align the team that feels the pain with the team that can fix the cause.

The notion of a **team API** is the second half of this. A team's API is everything the rest of the org needs to interact with that team and its services *without* needing high-bandwidth communication. It is more than the service's HTTP/gRPC endpoints. It includes: the service interfaces and their versioned contracts; the events the service publishes; the documentation, the runbooks, the SLOs it commits to; the channel to reach the team and the way to request changes. A well-defined team API is what makes X-as-a-Service possible — the Catalog team can consume Checkout's order events without a meeting, because the contract, the schema, and the SLO are all published. Designing your team API deliberately is the practical, day-to-day expression of "one team per service": the service has one owner, and the owner publishes a stable interface that everyone else builds against. This is also why [consumer-driven contract testing](/blog/software-development/microservices/api-versioning-and-consumer-driven-contract-testing) matters organizationally — the contract is the team boundary made executable.

## 8. The ownership manifest: making "one team per service" concrete

Up to here this has been conceptual; let me make it operational, because the senior move is to encode the org-architecture mapping in artifacts that the tooling enforces, not in a wiki page nobody reads. There are three artifacts I always put in place.

The first is a `CODEOWNERS` file, which most git hosts (GitHub, GitLab) read natively to require review from the owning team. This is the cheapest, most enforceable expression of "one team per service" — a change to a service's code physically cannot merge without the owning team's review.

```
# CODEOWNERS — every path maps to exactly one owning team.
# A change cannot merge without that team's review. No path is owned by two teams.

/services/order/          @shopfast/checkout-team
/services/payment/        @shopfast/checkout-team
/services/cart/           @shopfast/checkout-team

/services/product/        @shopfast/catalog-team
/services/search/         @shopfast/catalog-team
/services/recommendations/ @shopfast/catalog-team

/services/inventory/      @shopfast/fulfilment-team
/services/shipping/       @shopfast/fulfilment-team
/services/warehouse/      @shopfast/fulfilment-team

/services/pricing-engine/ @shopfast/pricing-team

# Platform-owned paved road: the shared library is now versioned and
# single-owner, not a free-for-all common service.
/platform/                @shopfast/platform-team
/libs/shopfast-contracts/ @shopfast/platform-team
```

Notice what is *not* there: no path is owned by two teams. The old `common` service is gone; what remains shared (`shopfast-contracts`) is owned solely by the Platform team and published as a versioned library, so consumers depend on a release, not on a co-edited directory. The `CODEOWNERS` file is the org chart and the architecture, reconciled, and enforced by CI.

The second artifact is a **service catalog** entry — one per service, declaring its single owning team, its SLOs, its on-call rotation, and its dependencies. Backstage (the open-source developer portal Spotify built) popularized this format, but the idea works in any YAML you can lint in CI.

```yaml
# catalog/order.yaml — one entry per service, machine-readable ownership.
apiVersion: backstage.io/v1alpha1
kind: Component
metadata:
  name: order-service
  description: "Owns the order aggregate: create, amend, cancel."
  annotations:
    pagerduty.com/service-id: "PXYZ123"   # on-call rotation: who gets woken
spec:
  type: service
  lifecycle: production
  owner: checkout-team                      # EXACTLY ONE owning team
  system: checkout
  providesApis:
    - order-api-v2                           # the published team API
  consumesApis:
    - payment-api-v1                         # explicit cross-team dependency
    - pricing-api-v3
  dependsOn:
    - resource:order-postgres                # db-per-service: its own datastore
```

The `owner` field is mandatory and single-valued; a CI check rejects any catalog entry where `owner` is missing, multi-valued, or points at a non-existent team. The `consumesApis` field makes every cross-team dependency *explicit and countable* — which, as we will see in §10, is exactly the number the inverse-Conway maneuver drives down. A service catalog turns "who owns this and what does it depend on?" from a Slack archaeology project into a query.

The third artifact is the **team API document** itself — a short, living markdown file each team maintains, declaring how the rest of the org interacts with it. It does not need tooling; it needs to exist and be honest.

```markdown
# Checkout Team API

- **Services we own:** order, payment, cart
- **APIs we provide:** order-api (v2, gRPC), payment-api (v1, REST)
- **Events we publish:** OrderPlaced, OrderCancelled, PaymentCaptured (Avro, schema registry)
- **SLOs we commit to:** order-api p99 < 120ms, 99.9% availability
- **On-call:** PagerDuty "checkout-primary", 24/7
- **How to request a change:** open an issue in checkout/roadmap; we triage weekly
- **Default interaction mode:** X-as-a-Service. Collaboration only during a named discovery.
```

These three artifacts together make the org-architecture mapping *legible* and *enforceable*. The mapping itself — team boundary to service boundary — is the thing you are designing, and the figure below shows it for ShopFast after the re-org: each service has exactly one owning team, with the Platform team's paved road underpinning several of them as a service rather than a co-ownership.

![A graph showing the Checkout team owning the order and payment services and the Catalog team owning the product and search services with each service owned by exactly one team and the Platform team providing a paved road to several services](/imgs/blogs/conways-law-and-team-topologies-for-microservices-3.webp)

Read the edges: every service has exactly one *owning* edge from a stream-aligned team, and the Platform team's edges are a different relationship — it provides the paved road (the runtime, the pipeline) *to* those services without owning their code or their domain. That distinction — owning versus underpinning — is the whole architecture in one picture, and it is only possible because the org was shaped to produce it.

A practical note on how to *audit* this mapping, because it is the kind of thing that rots silently. Once a quarter I run a simple reconciliation: take the `CODEOWNERS` file and the service catalog, and check three invariants. First, **every service has exactly one owner** — no service appears under two teams, and no service has no owner. Second, **every owner is a real, current team** — ownership does not point at a team that was dissolved or renamed, which is the most common way a service becomes secretly unowned. Third, **the `consumesApis` graph has no surprises** — every cross-team dependency in the catalog corresponds to a real, intended X-as-a-Service relationship, not an accidental coupling someone added under deadline. The reconciliation takes an hour and catches drift before it hardens. Ownership is not a one-time decision; it is a property you maintain, the same way you maintain test coverage or dependency hygiene, because the org changes — people leave, teams reorganize, services get spun up — and without an audit the mapping silently decays back toward "owned by whoever touched it last," which is the road to the unowned-service failure mode in §10.

## 9. The inverse-Conway maneuver

So far Conway's Law has mostly been a warning: your architecture will mirror your org whether you like it or not. The senior turn is to weaponize it. If the org produces the architecture, then to get the architecture you want, *design the org first*. This is the **inverse-Conway maneuver**: deliberately shape teams and their communication structure to produce the target architecture, rather than drawing the architecture and hoping the org cooperates.

The reasoning is direct. Suppose you want an architecture of cleanly-bounded, independently-deployable services aligned to business capabilities. Conway's Law says you will get that architecture if and only if you have teams that are cleanly-bounded, independently-working, and aligned to business capabilities. So instead of mandating the architecture and fighting the org's natural pull toward shared services and coordination, you mandate the *org*: you create stream-aligned teams around the business capabilities you want as service boundaries, give each one clear single ownership, set up a platform team to carry the shared load, and let Conway's Law produce the architecture as a *consequence*. You are not fighting the law; you are aiming it.

The maneuver is not a one-day re-org; it is a deliberate sequence. Here is how ShopFast did it, and it took a quarter.

![A timeline of an inverse-Conway re-org over sixteen weeks mapping dependencies finding eleven cross-team dependencies then defining the target architecture splitting the common service forming a platform team stream-aligning the teams and ending with three cross-team dependencies](/imgs/blogs/conways-law-and-team-topologies-for-microservices-8.webp)

The sequence in the figure is worth walking through because the *order* matters. First (Week 0), map the current cross-team dependencies — every place where one team has to wait on, coordinate with, or get sign-off from another. ShopFast counted 11. Second (Week 2), define the target architecture and, critically, the target *team* structure that would produce it — the six-team shape from §2. Third (Week 4), the highest-leverage move: dismantle the shared `common` service, assigning each piece to a single owner. Fourth (Week 8), stand up the Platform team and have it take over the runtime and CI that the stream-aligned teams had been each maintaining. Fifth (Week 12), complete the stream-alignment of Checkout and Catalog around their business capabilities. By Week 16, the cross-team dependency count had dropped from 11 to 3 — and the three that remained were genuine, named, X-as-a-Service relationships (Checkout consumes Pricing; everyone consumes Platform), not accidental coupling.

#### Worked example: reducing cross-team dependencies from 11 to 3

The dependency count is the metric the whole maneuver optimizes, so let me show the arithmetic that makes it a real win and not a vanity number. Before the re-org, ShopFast had 11 cross-team dependencies. Each one was a coordination cost: empirically, each cross-team dependency that a feature touched added a median of **3 days** of lead time (waiting for the other team's review, scheduling, or release window). The average feature touched 2.4 of those 11 dependencies, so the average feature paid roughly 2.4 × 3 = **7.2 days** of pure coordination latency on top of its actual engineering time.

After the maneuver, 3 cross-team dependencies remained, and the average feature touched 0.5 of them — because the re-alignment had pulled most of what a feature needed *inside* a single team's ownership. So the average feature now paid 0.5 × 3 = **1.5 days** of coordination latency. That is a reduction of 5.7 days per feature, and at ShopFast's throughput of roughly 40 features per quarter, it freed up on the order of 228 engineer-days per quarter that had previously been spent *waiting*. The re-org did not add a single engineer or rewrite a single service's internals. It changed who had to talk to whom — the communication structure — and the architecture's coupling, and therefore its velocity, followed. That is the inverse-Conway maneuver paying off in the only currency that matters: the rate at which the org can ship change.

The optimization framing here is worth stating explicitly, because it is the production-grade lens on org design: **the thing you are optimizing is cross-team coupling and cognitive load, and both are measurable.** Cross-team coupling you measure as the cross-team dependency count and the co-deploy ratio (services that ship together per release — ShopFast went from 3.1 to 0.2). Cognitive load you proxy with services-per-team, change-failure rate, and on-call wake frequency. When you re-org, you measure these before and after, and you do not declare victory on vibes — you declare it when the dependency count drops, the co-deploy ratio approaches zero, the change-failure rate falls, and on-call stops driving attrition. An org change you cannot measure is an org change you cannot defend, and the senior skill is to instrument the re-org as rigorously as you would instrument a service.

## 10. Trade-offs: when org and architecture fight

No framework is free, and Team Topologies is a set of trade-offs, not a recipe. Let me name the costs honestly, because recommending an org shape without naming its price is exactly the mistake §3 punished.

The first cost is that **stream-aligned ownership requires teams to be cross-functional and full-stack enough to own a slice end to end.** If your engineers are siloed by technology — front-end here, back-end there, DBAs over there — you cannot stand up stream-aligned teams without either retraining or hiring, and Conway's Law will faithfully reproduce your technology silos as architectural layers (a "front-end service," a "back-end service," a "database team's stored procedures") with hand-offs at every layer boundary. The fix — broadening skills — is real work, and it is where an enabling team earns its keep.

The second cost is that **a platform team is a real investment that pays off only above a certain scale.** For three teams and six services, a dedicated platform team is overhead; the stream-aligned teams can share a thin paved road maintained part-time. The platform team becomes worth its headcount somewhere around 5–8 stream-aligned teams, when the duplicated effort of each team maintaining its own runtime exceeds the cost of a team to centralize it. Stand one up too early and you have created a bottleneck team that the streams wait on; stand it up too late and the streams drown in undifferentiated work. The timing is a judgment call, and getting it wrong in either direction hurts.

The third cost is the **complicated-subsystem trap** I flagged in §4: the temptation to carve teams by technical layer and call each one a "complicated subsystem." Most things that feel complicated are merely *unfamiliar*, and the right response to unfamiliar is an enabling team, not a permanent specialist silo. A genuine complicated subsystem is rare; if you have more than one or two, you are probably mis-classifying stream-aligned work as specialist work, and you will pay for it in hand-offs.

The deepest trade-off, though, is the one in this section's title: **org and architecture can actively fight, and when they do, the org wins.** If you draw a clean architecture but staff it with an org that pulls toward shared services and coordination, the architecture will erode back toward the org's shape — the shared service will reappear, the coordination meeting will return, the release train will re-form. This is why the inverse-Conway maneuver is not optional polish; it is the only durable way to get and *keep* a target architecture. The architecture you can sustain is the architecture your org structure produces. Fighting that with willpower and code review is a losing battle; the winning move is to change the org so it produces the architecture for free.

#### Stress test: a service is owned by no one

Let me stress-test the framework the way the series stress-tests everything else: what breaks under load and at the edges? Start with the most common failure: **a service is owned by no team.** This is the `common`-service disease in its purest form. When a service has no single owner, three things happen, all bad. Changes to it require ad-hoc coordination among whoever happens to depend on it, so its lead time balloons (ShopFast's 9 days). Its operational quality rots, because no one is on the pager for it — when it breaks, the incident is a hot-potato of "is that ours?" across teams, and MTTR spikes. And it accretes responsibility, because an unowned service is the path of least resistance for any code that "doesn't fit anywhere else," so it grows into a god service that couples everything. An unowned service is not a neutral state; it is an active sink that pulls the whole architecture toward a distributed monolith. The fix is non-negotiable: assign it a single owning team *today*, even if imperfectly, because any single owner beats no owner.

#### Stress test: one team owns fifteen services

Now the opposite edge: **one team owns fifteen services.** This is the cognitive-load ceiling blown clean through. At fifteen services for, say, seven engineers, no individual understands more than a fraction of the estate. On-call becomes a lottery — the paged engineer has likely never touched the service that fired — so MTTR climbs and the change-failure rate climbs with it (ShopFast saw 23% at twelve services; fifteen is worse). Worse, the team becomes a *de facto* shared-services team: because it owns so much, every other team depends on something it owns, so it turns into a coordination bottleneck for the whole org, recreating the `common`-service problem at team scale. The fix is to split ownership until each resulting team is back under its cognitive ceiling — which usually means forming new stream-aligned teams and, often, retiring services that turned out to justify nobody's attention. Owning fifteen services is not impressive; it is a smell that the org is under-staffed relative to its architecture, or that the architecture has more services than the domain needs.

#### Stress test: two teams must both change a shared service for every feature

The third edge is the subtlest: **two teams must both change a shared service for every feature.** This is permanent collaboration masquerading as normal life, and it is the clearest Conway's-Law signal that a boundary is wrong. If every feature requires both Team A and Team B to change service S, then S's boundary is not aligned to either team — the work that flows through S belongs to one capability, and that capability is split across two teams. The naive fix (more meetings, a shared Slack channel, a "sync") treats the symptom and entrenches the coupling. The real fix is to *move the boundary*: either give S a single owning team and have the other consume it as a service (X-as-a-Service), or recognize that A and B are really one stream and merge them. Permanent two-team co-change is the architecture telling you the org is mis-cut. Listen to it.

## 11. The Team Topologies decision matrix in practice

To pull the framework into something you can act on Monday morning, here is the decision logic as a table — when each team type and interaction mode is the right call, and what it costs you.

| Situation | Team type to form | Default interaction | Cost to watch |
|---|---|---|---|
| A business capability needs end-to-end ownership | Stream-aligned | X-as-a-Service to consumers | Needs full-stack, cross-functional people |
| 5+ teams each rebuild the same runtime/CI/observability | Platform | X-as-a-Service (self-service) | Becomes a bottleneck if mandated, not adopted |
| A team lacks a capability (tracing, testing, security) | Enabling | Facilitating (time-boxed) | Becomes a permanent crutch if it overstays |
| A genuinely hard, specialized subsystem (pricing, ML, codecs) | Complicated-subsystem | X-as-a-Service to consumers | Easy to over-create; most "hard" is just unfamiliar |
| Two teams discovering a new, unclear interface | (keep teams) | Collaboration (weeks only) | Blurs ownership if it becomes permanent |
| A service is shared by 2+ teams | Re-own to a single team | X-as-a-Service to the rest | The hardest political conversation; do it anyway |

The reading order for the table is top to bottom: most teams are stream-aligned; you add a platform team when duplication crosses the threshold; you use enabling teams to close capability gaps temporarily; you create complicated-subsystem teams sparingly; you use collaboration as a temporary discovery mode; and you ruthlessly re-own anything shared. Every row names the cost, because the whole discipline of this post is that there is no free org structure — only trade-offs you choose deliberately or have chosen for you.

It is also worth being explicit about how this maps onto the boundary work earlier in the series. [Domain-driven design](/blog/software-development/microservices/service-boundaries-with-domain-driven-design) gives you the *bounded contexts* — the natural seams of the business. Conway's Law tells you those seams will only become real service boundaries if they are also *team* boundaries. So the practical algorithm is: find the bounded contexts (DDD), then form one stream-aligned team per context you want as a service, then let the team produce the service. The team boundary and the service boundary and the bounded context all coincide — and when they coincide, the architecture is stable, because the org is holding it in place rather than fighting it. When they *don't* coincide — when a bounded context is split across two teams, or one team owns three unrelated contexts — that is exactly where the distributed monolith grows.

The mapping is worth stating as a checklist, because it is the thing you actually verify in a design review. For each candidate service, ask: Is there exactly one team that would own it? Does that team also own the data behind it (so we satisfy [database-per-service](/blog/software-development/microservices/service-boundaries-with-domain-driven-design) without a shared schema)? Can that team change and deploy it without coordinating with another team? Does the service correspond to a single bounded context, not a mash-up of two? If the answer to any of these is no, you have found a Conway's-Law misalignment *before* it ships, while it is still cheap to fix by moving a boundary on a whiteboard rather than re-owning a service in production. The senior habit is to run this checklist on the *org* and the *architecture* together — never one without the other — because they are two views of the same object. A design review that approves the services without looking at the teams that will own them is approving half a design, and the missing half is the half that determines whether the approved half survives contact with reality. I have watched beautiful architectures rot in a year for want of this five-minute check; the architecture was right, the org was never asked, and the org won.

## Case studies

The framework is not theoretical; the best-known microservices organizations are case studies in Conway's Law applied on purpose. Here are four, kept accurate and to the lesson each teaches.

**Amazon's two-pizza teams and "you build it, you run it."** Amazon's enduring organizational idea, popularized in the early 2000s, is the *two-pizza team*: a team small enough to be fed by two pizzas — roughly 6 to 10 people. The rule is not really about pizza; it is about communication overhead. A team of that size can coordinate internally with low friction, which (by Conway's Law) means it can own a richly-coupled service internally while exposing a thin interface externally. Pair that with Werner Vogels's "you build it, you run it" — the team that builds a service operates it, on-call included — and you have an org explicitly designed to produce small, independently-owned, independently-operated services. Amazon did not draw a microservices architecture and then staff it; they built an org of small, autonomous, full-ownership teams, and the service-oriented architecture (and eventually AWS itself) was the Conway's-Law consequence. The lesson: bound team size to bound communication overhead, and align ownership of build and run, and the architecture follows.

**Spotify's squads and tribes — and their limits.** Around 2012, Spotify published its model of *squads* (small, autonomous, stream-aligned teams), *tribes* (collections of related squads), *chapters* (people with the same skill across squads), and *guilds* (cross-cutting interest groups). It became the most-copied org diagram in tech, and the honest lesson is partly cautionary. The squad model is a clean expression of stream-aligned teams with autonomy, and the chapter/guild structure was an early attempt at the cross-cutting uplift that enabling teams formalize. But the model was a *snapshot of an aspiration*, not a finished recipe, and Spotify engineers themselves later wrote that the published "Spotify model" was never fully implemented even at Spotify, and that copying the diagram without the underlying culture of autonomy and trust produces cargo-cult org design. The lesson is double: stream-aligned autonomous teams are the right default *and* you cannot get a healthy org by copying another company's chart — you have to design for your own constraints, your own cognitive-load realities, and your own communication patterns.

**The inverse-Conway maneuver as deliberate practice.** The clearest articulation of using Conway's Law on purpose comes from ThoughtWorks, who coined the term "Inverse Conway Maneuver" and put it on their Technology Radar: deliberately evolving team and organizational structure to encourage the desired software architecture. The pattern shows up repeatedly in real migrations — an organization that wants to move from a monolith to services first reorganizes into stream-aligned teams aligned to the target service boundaries, and only then does the decomposition, so that the teams pull the architecture toward the target rather than the legacy org pulling it back. This is exactly the ShopFast sequence in §9. The lesson: a monolith-to-microservices migration that does not reshape the org first usually reverts, because the old communication structure keeps re-creating the old coupling under the new topology — which is precisely how you get a [distributed monolith](/blog/software-development/microservices/shared-data-anti-patterns-and-the-distributed-monolith).

**Team Topologies adoption and the platform-as-product shift.** Since *Team Topologies* was published in 2019, the most visible industry effect has been the maturation of the *platform team* from "the team that owns Kubernetes" into a product team whose customers are internal developers — the discipline now called platform engineering, with internal developer platforms and golden paths as its deliverables. Organizations that have adopted the framework report the same pattern: the win comes not from renaming teams but from being deliberate about *cognitive load* — measuring it, and reshaping team responsibilities to keep stream-aligned teams under their ceiling. The lesson, and it is the lesson of this whole post: the team types and interaction modes are tools, but the *constraint* they serve is cognitive load, and an org designed around that constraint produces an architecture its teams can actually operate.

## When to reach for this (and when not to)

Team Topologies and the inverse-Conway maneuver are not free, and a junior reading this might over-apply them. Here is the decisive guidance.

**Reach for explicit org design when** you have enough teams (roughly four or more) that communication structure has become a real force; when you are migrating a monolith to services and want the migration to *stick*; when you can name a shared service that multiple teams edit; when on-call attrition or a high change-failure rate signals cognitive overload; or when your architecture and your org chart visibly disagree and the architecture is losing. In all of these, the org is the lever, and pulling it is the highest-leverage thing a staff engineer can do.

**Do not over-engineer the org when** you are small. For one team and a [modular monolith](/blog/software-development/microservices/monolith-first-and-the-modular-monolith), or two or three teams and a handful of services, you do not need a platform team, an enabling team, and a complicated-subsystem team — you need the teams to own their slices clearly and a thin shared paved road. Standing up the full Team Topologies apparatus at small scale is the same mistake as [adopting microservices before you need them](/blog/software-development/microservices/what-are-microservices-and-when-not-to-use-them): you pay the coordination overhead of a structure designed for scale you do not yet have. The framework scales *up* gracefully; do not import the top of it before you are climbing.

And do not mistake the org chart for the communication structure. The whole point of Conway's Law is that the architecture mirrors *how people actually communicate*, which is frequently not the reporting lines. Two teams that report into different VPs but pair-program daily are, for Conway's purposes, one team and will produce coupled software. Design the *communication structure* — who actually talks to whom to ship — not the boxes on the HR diagram.

## Key takeaways

Leveling up from junior to senior on this topic comes down to internalizing a handful of rules that turn "we have services" into "we have an organization that produces good services."

- **Your architecture mirrors your communication structure, whether you plan it or not.** Conway's Law is not a tendency; it is a structural force. Read your org chart to predict your architecture, and where they disagree, the org chart is the truth.
- **One team per service. Always.** A service owned by two teams is owned by neither; a service owned by a committee is the seed of a distributed monolith. The single most important rule in org design for microservices.
- **Cognitive load sizes a team, not headcount.** A team can only own so many services well — roughly 2–4 with a platform team carrying the runtime, fewer without. When you exceed the ceiling, split ownership; do not work harder.
- **Stream-aligned teams are the default; platform, enabling, and complicated-subsystem teams are thin support.** An org that is mostly support teams produces an architecture that is mostly hand-offs.
- **X-as-a-Service is the steady-state interaction mode.** Collaboration is a temporary discovery state; permanent collaboration is a signal that a boundary is in the wrong place.
- **You build it, you run it.** Align the team that feels the operational pain with the team that can fix the cause, and the system gets more reliable for free, because the feedback loop closes inside one team.
- **Use the inverse-Conway maneuver: design the org for the target architecture.** To get the architecture you want, shape the teams first and let Conway's Law produce it as a consequence. Fighting the law with willpower loses.
- **Measure the org like you measure a service.** Cross-team dependency count, co-deploy ratio, services-per-team, change-failure rate, on-call wake frequency — instrument the re-org and declare victory on numbers, not vibes.
- **A service owned by no one is an active sink, not a neutral state.** It pulls the whole architecture toward a distributed monolith. Assign a single owner today, even imperfectly.

The senior point, compressed to one line: **design the team boundaries and you design the service boundaries — so manage cognitive load, give every service exactly one owning team, and shape the org to produce the architecture you want.** Everything else in this series — the boundaries, the data, the resilience, the deployment — sits on top of an org that is either holding the architecture in place or quietly pulling it apart. Once you can see that force, you cannot unsee it, and you will never again review an architecture without first asking to see the org chart that is going to produce it.

The next post in the series puts this into motion: the [strangler-fig pattern for migrating a monolith to microservices](/blog/software-development/microservices/strangler-fig-migrating-a-monolith-to-microservices) is where the inverse-Conway maneuver meets the codebase, and where reshaping the org first is the difference between a migration that sticks and one that reverts. And the post after it — [microservices anti-patterns and when to go back to a monolith](/blog/software-development/microservices/microservices-anti-patterns-and-when-to-go-back-to-monolith) — is what happens when you get the org wrong and the architecture fights you the whole way down.

## Further reading

- **Melvin Conway, "How Do Committees Invent?" (1968)** — the original paper that states the law. Short, readable, and more nuanced than the one-line version everyone quotes; worth reading in full.
- **Matthew Skelton & Manuel Pais, *Team Topologies: Organizing Business and Technology Teams for Fast Flow* (2019)** — the definitive treatment of the four team types, three interaction modes, and cognitive load. The single most actionable management book for microservices orgs.
- **Nicole Forsgren, Jez Humble & Gene Kim, *Accelerate: The Science of Lean Software and DevOps* (2018)** — the data behind why loosely-coupled architectures and teams correlate with higher delivery performance; the source of the four key metrics (lead time, deploy frequency, change-fail rate, MTTR) you use to measure a re-org.
- **Sam Newman, *Building Microservices* (2nd ed., 2021)** — the org/Conway chapter ties team structure to independent deployability, the litmus test this post keeps returning to.
- **The "Spotify model" engineering write-ups (Henrik Kniberg & Anders Ivarsson, 2012) and the later "Spotify model is a failure" reflections** — read both: the aspiration and the honest retrospective on why copying the chart doesn't work.
- **ThoughtWorks Technology Radar — "Inverse Conway Maneuver"** — the concise origin of the deliberate-org-design pattern.
