Every post in this series taught one move. How to read an ambiguous prompt. How to turn a vague ask into SLOs. How to size a system on the back of an envelope. How to choose a datastore, partition it, replicate it, cache in front of it, rate-limit the door, and survive a region going dark. This post does something different: it puts the whole loop together on one realistic, ambitious system and runs it the way a staff engineer actually runs a design review — out loud, under pushback, with numbers on the whiteboard and a skeptic in the room asking "what breaks when a tenant sends you a billion events in an afternoon?"

The system is a **multi-tenant SaaS analytics platform** — think a product-analytics service like the ones that sit behind "how many users completed onboarding last week, broken down by plan and country." Customers (tenants) embed an SDK in their apps; that SDK fires events — `page_view`, `signup`, `purchase`, each a small JSON blob with a timestamp, a user id, and a bag of properties. The platform ingests those events at a firehose rate, stores billions of them, and lets each tenant slice and dice their own data in dashboards and ad-hoc queries that need to return before the analyst gets bored. We are building the thing that powers a Mixpanel, an Amplitude, a PostHog. Figure 1 is the spine of the whole system, and we will return to it after every decision to check that the new piece still fits.

![End to end architecture showing tenant SDKs feeding a collector into a partitioned log, stream processing into a columnar store and a rollup store, and a query router serving dashboards](/imgs/blogs/capstone-designing-a-multi-tenant-saas-analytics-platform-1.webp)

This is the capstone, so the goal is not to teach a new mechanism — it is to demonstrate *judgment*. Watch how the requirements force the storage choice, how the storage choice forces the query strategy, how multi-tenancy contaminates every single layer, and how the optimizations (pre-aggregation, columnar compression, tiering, per-tenant cost attribution) all fall out of taking the constraints seriously. We will lean hard on the mechanism deep-dives already written — [choosing a datastore](/blog/software-development/system-design/choosing-a-datastore-sql-nosql-newsql), [queues and event streaming](/blog/software-development/system-design/queues-and-event-streaming-for-architects), [caching strategies](/blog/software-development/system-design/caching-strategies-and-the-pitfalls-that-bite), [rate limiting and backpressure](/blog/software-development/system-design/rate-limiting-and-backpressure) — so this post can stay where a senior lives: the decision layer, where you pick, defend, and optimize. By the end you should be able to walk into a review for any data-intensive multi-tenant system and run this loop yourself.

## 1. Clarify: what are we actually building?

The first senior move is to refuse to start designing until the problem has edges. "Design an analytics platform" is three different machines depending on the answers to a handful of questions, and the cost of guessing wrong is a redesign three months in. This is the discipline from [how seniors approach ambiguous system design problems](/blog/software-development/system-design/how-seniors-approach-ambiguous-system-design-problems): the candidate who starts drawing boxes immediately is the one who fails; the senior spends the first five minutes interrogating the prompt.

So we clarify, out loud, in priority order. **Who are the tenants?** Other companies — each a separate customer with their own users, their own data, and a hard requirement that they never see each other's data. That single answer makes multi-tenancy the central theme rather than a footnote. **What is an event?** A semi-structured record: a tenant id, a distinct user id, an event name, a millisecond timestamp, and an open-ended `properties` map (plan, country, device, whatever the customer chose to attach). The schema is *per-tenant* and *evolving* — a customer adds a new property next Tuesday and expects to query it Wednesday. **What is the read pattern?** Two kinds. Curated dashboards (the same handful of charts, refreshed constantly, by every viewer) and ad-hoc exploration (an analyst asking a question nobody anticipated, scanning whatever they want). Those two read patterns pull the design in opposite directions, and reconciling them is most of the query-layer work.

A few more questions earn their keep. **How fresh must data be?** Customers tolerate a short delay — an event that happened ten seconds ago can show up in a dashboard a few seconds later. Nobody is trading stocks on this; near-real-time is fine, hard-real-time is not required, and that single relaxation buys us the entire async ingestion pipeline. **How big is the biggest tenant versus the median?** This is the question that separates seniors from juniors on a multi-tenant system. The answer is always: *power-law*. The top 1% of tenants generate more than half the events, and one whale can be a thousand times bigger than the median. If you design for the average tenant, the whale takes you down. We design for the whale and let the long tail ride for free.

What we are *not* building also matters. We are not building a transactional system — nobody's bank balance depends on a `page_view`. We are not building a general-purpose data warehouse where customers write arbitrary SQL joins across tables; we serve a constrained query surface (counts, funnels, retention, breakdowns over time). And we are not promising every event is counted exactly once forever — analytics tolerates a tiny amount of duplication or loss far better than a payment system does, and we will spend that tolerance deliberately to buy throughput. Naming the non-goals is how you keep the scope from metastasizing.

## 2. Constrain: requirements and SLOs

Clarification produces a feature list; the senior move is to convert that list into **measurable SLOs** before drawing a single box, because an SLO is a contract you can fail, and a feature is just a wish. This is the discipline from [turning vague asks into requirements and SLOs](/blog/software-development/system-design/turning-vague-asks-into-requirements-and-slos) and [reliability, SLOs, and error budgets](/blog/software-development/system-design/reliability-slos-error-budgets-and-graceful-degradation). Figure 2 is the SLO table we will defend for the rest of the post — every later decision gets checked against it.

![Requirements matrix listing ingest availability, query latency, data freshness, tenant isolation, and cost per tenant with a target, a stress condition, and a priority for each](/imgs/blogs/capstone-designing-a-multi-tenant-saas-analytics-platform-2.webp)

**Ingest availability is the top-line SLO: 99.99%.** This one is non-negotiable and it is subtle. A dashboard being slow for ten minutes annoys an analyst; an ingest endpoint being down for ten minutes *loses events forever* — the SDK fires, gets no acknowledgment or a 500, and depending on its retry policy either drops the event or hammers you. Lost events are lost revenue analysis, broken funnels, and a customer who no longer trusts your numbers. So the ingest path must be the most available thing we build, more available than the query path, because you can always query later but you can never re-ingest an event that the customer's app already forgot. Four nines means about 52 minutes of downtime per year — and we will spend the whole reliability budget protecting ingest, not dashboards.

**Query latency: dashboards p99 under one second, ad-hoc under ten.** Dashboards are the product; if a chart takes five seconds to load, the product feels broken even though no data was lost. Ad-hoc queries get a looser budget because the analyst knows they asked something expensive. We pin these as p99, not average, because the average hides the tenant whose query scans a year of data and times out — and that tenant is the one who churns. **Data freshness: an event is queryable within 30 seconds of arriving.** Loose enough to allow async batching, tight enough that a dashboard feels live. **Tenant isolation: noisy-neighbor-safe** — no tenant's load or query can degrade another tenant's SLO, and no tenant can ever read another tenant's data. The first half is a performance property, the second a security property, and a multi-tenant system has to deliver both. **Cost per tenant: tracked and attributable** — we must be able to say what each tenant costs us to serve, because without that number we cannot price, cannot spot an unprofitable whale, and cannot bill for overages.

Notice the ranking, because rankings drive trade-offs. When ingest availability conflicts with query latency — and during a 10x spike it will — we sacrifice query latency and protect ingest, because a slow dashboard recovers and a dropped event does not. When isolation conflicts with cost — and it always does, since perfect isolation means dedicated infrastructure per tenant — we accept some cost to guarantee isolation for data (a hard security line) but negotiate on isolation for performance (a tunable knob). Stating the priority order out loud is the single most senior thing you do in the first ten minutes, and it is the thing junior designs always skip.

## 3. Estimate: events, storage, and query QPS

Now the back-of-the-envelope pass, because a design you cannot size is a design you cannot defend. The whole method is in [back-of-the-envelope estimation for system design](/blog/software-development/system-design/back-of-the-envelope-estimation-for-system-design); here we apply it end to end and — critically — keep the numbers *consistent* so that the ingest rate, the storage growth, the tiering decision, and the query cost all derive from the same starting figure. Figure 3 lays out the chain. Pick round numbers; the goal is the right order of magnitude, not four significant figures.

![Estimation matrix deriving compressed daily storage and query QPS from a peak ingest rate, showing the math and the implication for each row](/imgs/blogs/capstone-designing-a-multi-tenant-saas-analytics-platform-3.webp)

Start with the firehose. Say the platform serves a few thousand tenants and, at peak, ingests **500,000 events per second** across all of them. That is roughly 43 billion events per day (500k times 86,400 seconds). An event arrives as maybe **1 KB of raw JSON** on the wire — the name, the ids, the timestamp, and a property bag. Raw, that is 43 TB per day flowing in, which sounds terrifying until you remember two facts: we never store the raw JSON, and columnar storage compresses analytics data brutally well.

Here is where the storage choice (section 5) pays off in advance. Columnar formats store each column contiguously, and a column of timestamps, or event names, or country codes, is enormously repetitive and compresses at roughly **10x or better** — often far better for low-cardinality columns. So 1 KB of raw event becomes on the order of **100 bytes stored**. That turns 43 billion events per day into about **5 TB per day** of compressed storage, growing every day forever. Over a 30-day hot window that is **150 TB**, and over a year about 1.8 PB. Five terabytes a day is the number that forces tiering: you cannot keep years of data on fast SSD at that growth rate without your storage bill eating the company, which is exactly the [cost-as-a-design-constraint](/blog/software-development/system-design/cost-as-a-design-constraint-finops) pressure we will resolve in section 9.

Now the read side. Query QPS is far lower than ingest QPS — this asymmetry is the defining shape of an analytics system. Dashboards refresh on a timer (every 30–60 seconds), and there are far fewer dashboard-viewers than there are events. Estimate a couple of **thousand queries per second** at peak across all tenants, the overwhelming majority of which hit the *same* curated dashboards. A few thousand QPS is trivially cacheable and trivially served from pre-aggregated rollups; the danger is not the QPS, it is the *tail* — the one ad-hoc query that scans 2 billion rows. So the estimation already tells us the architecture: a write-heavy ingest path that must be massively scalable and durable, a read path that is low-QPS but latency-sensitive and tail-dangerous, and a storage layer that grows so fast it must tier. Every box in Figure 1 is now justified by a number.

#### Worked example: sizing ingest and storage

Let me size the ingest tier and the hot store concretely, the way I would on a whiteboard, so the capacity numbers in the rest of the post are grounded.

**Ingest tier.** Peak is 500k events/s. A single stateless collector instance, doing JSON validation and writing to the log, comfortably handles ~10k events/s before CPU on parsing becomes the limit. So we need ~50 collectors at peak, and we run ~70 for headroom and to survive an availability-zone loss (one AZ down should not drop us below capacity). They are stateless, so autoscaling is straightforward — the [capacity planning](/blog/software-development/system-design/capacity-planning-and-autoscaling) playbook applies directly. The durable log (Kafka) must absorb 500k events/s at ~1 KB each, so ~500 MB/s of write throughput; with a replication factor of 3 that is ~1.5 GB/s of disk and network across the cluster, which a few dozen well-provisioned brokers handle. We size partitions for the *whale* — the biggest tenant must not exceed one partition's throughput, which we will return to in section 4.

**Hot store.** 5 TB/day compressed, 30-day hot window, is 150 TB. With a replication factor of 2 for the OLAP layer that is 300 TB of fast storage. On NVMe-class nodes holding, say, 10 TB usable each, that is ~30 storage nodes for the hot tier. Query CPU, not disk, usually sets the node count higher — a full-scan query is CPU-bound on decompression and aggregation — so call it ~40 nodes once you account for query headroom. The cold tier (everything older than 30 days) lives in object storage at roughly **one-tenth the per-TB cost** of NVMe, which is the entire economic argument for tiering. Hold these numbers; they reappear in the cost section.

## 4. The ingestion pipeline: collect, log, process, store

With the shape established, design the write path, because in an analytics system the write path is where the scale lives and where the hardest reliability problems hide. The pipeline has four stages, and the decisive architectural choice is the **durable log in the middle** that decouples the spiky, untrusted outside world from the steady, batched columnar writes. Figure 4 is the pipeline; everything about ingest reliability flows from that log.

![Ingestion pipeline showing a collector appending to a durable log, stream processing consuming and batching, and bulk inserts into the columnar store](/imgs/blogs/capstone-designing-a-multi-tenant-saas-analytics-platform-4.webp)

**Stage one: the collector.** A fleet of stateless HTTP endpoints that the SDKs hit. Its job is deliberately minimal — authenticate the tenant (validate the API key), do cheap schema validation (is this even JSON, does it have a timestamp), attach server-side enrichment (received-at time, GeoIP from the source address, a server-assigned event id for dedup), and then *immediately* append the event to the durable log and acknowledge. The collector does **not** talk to the columnar store, does not aggregate, does not do anything that could be slow or fail in a correlated way. It is stateless so it autoscales, and it is fast so the SDK gets a 200 in single-digit milliseconds. The instant the event is durably in the log, we have honored the ingest-availability SLO — the event will not be lost even if every downstream component is on fire.

**Stage two: the durable log.** This is Kafka (or a Kafka-shaped log), and it is the heart of the design's reliability. The mechanism is covered in depth in [Kafka as a distributed log](/blog/software-development/database/kafka-as-a-distributed-log) and the architect's view in [queues and event streaming](/blog/software-development/system-design/queues-and-event-streaming-for-architects); here is why it earns its place. The log gives us three things we cannot live without. First, **a buffer**: when ingest spikes to 10x, the log absorbs it — producers keep appending, consumers fall behind, the backlog grows, but nothing drops, which is exactly the [backpressure](/blog/software-development/message-queue/backpressure-and-flow-control) story. Second, **replay**: if the stream processor has a bug and computes a rollup wrong, we fix the bug and replay the log from an offset to recompute — the raw events are still there. Third, **decoupling of failure**: the columnar store can be slow or briefly down and ingest is unaffected, because the collector only ever talks to the log.

How do we partition the log? **By tenant** is the obvious answer and it is mostly right — keep a tenant's events together so stream processing can maintain per-tenant state locally. But "by tenant" alone creates a hot partition for the whale, so we partition by `(tenant_id, hash(user_id))` — events for one tenant spread across several partitions, ordered within a user, which is the granularity analytics actually needs (per-user event order matters for funnels and sessions; cross-user order does not). The [partitioning and sharding](/blog/software-development/system-design/partitioning-and-sharding-without-downtime) and [message ordering](/blog/software-development/message-queue/message-ordering-and-partitioning-guarantees) deep-dives cover why this key choice is the whole game.

**Stage three: stream processing.** Consumers read from the log and do the real work: dedup (drop events whose server-assigned id we have already seen, using a windowed key store — this is [idempotency and deduplication](/blog/software-development/message-queue/idempotency-and-deduplication-making-at-least-once-safe) making at-least-once delivery safe), normalize the schema (handle the new property the customer added Tuesday), compute the rollups (incrementally update per-hour, per-dimension aggregates), and **batch** the raw events for bulk insertion into the columnar store. Batching is not optional: columnar stores hate row-at-a-time inserts (each insert creates a tiny part that must later be merged) and love big bulk loads. So the stream processor accumulates events for a few seconds or a few thousand rows, whichever comes first, then writes one batch. That batching is what makes the 30-second freshness SLO and the columnar store's write pattern compatible.

A subtlety in stage three that separates a robust pipeline from a fragile one: the stream processor maintains *state* (the dedup window, the in-flight rollup buckets), and that state must survive a processor crash without losing or double-counting events. The standard answer is to checkpoint the processor's state and its log offset together, atomically, so that on restart the processor resumes from exactly where its state reflects — replaying any events after the checkpoint, which is safe because dedup makes replays idempotent. This is the same exactly-once-processing machinery covered in [exactly-once in Kafka](/blog/software-development/database/kafka-as-a-distributed-log) at the mechanism level: it is not magic, it is "checkpoint offset and state together, and make reprocessing idempotent." For rollups specifically, we make the aggregation idempotent by computing them as *replace*-able partial aggregates keyed by `(tenant, bucket, dimensions)` rather than blind increments, so reprocessing a batch overwrites the bucket to the same value instead of double-adding. Getting this right is what lets us replay the log freely — to fix a bug, to backfill a new rollup, to recover from a processor failure — without corrupting the numbers.

**Stage four: the columnar store.** Bulk-inserted, queried, tiered. We design it in the next two sections. For now, the pipeline's contract is: an event acknowledged by the collector is durably logged within milliseconds, deduplicated and aggregated within seconds, and queryable within 30 seconds — and no spike, slow store, or processor bug can violate the first guarantee. Notice how the pipeline isolates *risk* by stage: the collector is fast and stateless so it cannot be a correlated-failure source; the log is durable so it cannot lose data; the stream processor is replayable so its bugs are recoverable; the store is batched so it is never overwhelmed by write rate. Each stage owns one guarantee and depends on the previous stage's guarantee, which is what makes the whole pipeline reason-about-able rather than a mystery when something goes wrong at 3 a.m.

```python
# Collector hot path: validate, enrich, append to log, ack. Nothing slow.
async def ingest(request, tenant):
    body = await request.json()
    # Cheap validation only — reject garbage, don't do business logic here.
    if "event" not in body or "ts" not in body:
        return Response(status=400)
    enriched = {
        "tenant_id": tenant.id,
        "event_id": server_event_id(tenant.id, body),  # deterministic -> dedup key
        "name": body["event"],
        "ts": body["ts"],
        "received_at": now_ms(),
        "user_id": body.get("user_id"),
        "geo": geoip(request.remote_addr),
        "props": body.get("properties", {}),
    }
    # Partition by (tenant, user) so one tenant spreads across partitions
    # but a single user's events stay ordered for funnel/session logic.
    key = f"{tenant.id}:{hash_user(enriched['user_id'])}"
    await kafka.produce(topic="events", key=key, value=enriched)
    return Response(status=202)  # durably logged; the rest is async
```

A word on the ingest *contract*, because it is where a careless design loses events quietly. The SDK fires events and expects an acknowledgment; the question is what acknowledgment means. If the collector acks *before* the event is durably in the log, a collector crash loses every in-flight event and the SDK never knows. So the collector acks *only after* the log's producer confirms the append is replicated to a quorum of brokers — `acks=all` in Kafka terms — which costs a few extra milliseconds of latency but is the difference between "we lost some events during a deploy" and "we never lose an acked event." This is at-least-once delivery on purpose: the SDK retries on any non-2xx, the log may therefore receive duplicates, and we make duplicates harmless downstream with the server-assigned `event_id` and windowed dedup. The alternative — at-most-once, where the SDK fires and forgets — is tempting because it is simpler and faster, but it silently drops events during any hiccup, and silent data loss in an analytics platform is the one failure customers cannot forgive because they never find out until a funnel looks wrong months later. The [delivery semantics](/blog/software-development/message-queue/delivery-semantics-at-most-at-least-exactly-once) deep-dive lays out the spectrum; for ingest we land firmly on at-least-once plus dedup, never at-most-once.

There is also a batching decision on the *SDK* side that an architect should specify rather than leave to chance. A mobile or browser SDK that POSTs one event per HTTP request at high volume will hammer the collector with connection overhead and drain the client's battery and network. So the SDK buffers events locally and flushes them in batches — say, every few seconds or every 50 events, whichever comes first — and the collector accepts a batch in one request, splitting it into individual log appends server-side. This batching also makes the client resilient to brief network failures: a buffered batch survives a dropped connection and flushes on reconnect. The cost is freshness (an event buffered for a few seconds on the client is a few seconds older when it lands) and the risk of losing the client-side buffer if the app is killed before a flush — both acceptable for analytics, both decisions the architect should make explicitly rather than inherit from a default. The senior habit is to treat the client SDK as part of the system's reliability surface, not as someone else's problem.

## 5. The storage decision: why columnar, not row

Here is the decision that defines the entire system, and the one a junior gets wrong by reaching for the database they know. The instinct is "billions of rows, use a big SQL database with good indexes" or "it's high write volume, use Cassandra." Both are wrong for this workload, and being able to say *why* is the test. The framework is [choosing a datastore](/blog/software-development/system-design/choosing-a-datastore-sql-nosql-newsql), and the answer follows from the *access pattern*, not the raw scale.

Analytics queries have a specific shape: they read **a few columns** out of dozens, across **a huge number of rows**, and **aggregate** (count, sum, group-by). "How many `purchase` events happened per day last month, broken down by country" touches three columns — `name`, `ts`, `geo.country` — and ignores the other forty properties on each event, but it scans hundreds of millions of rows. A **row store** (Postgres, MySQL, Cassandra) physically stores all of a row's columns together, so to read three columns it must drag every row's *entire* payload off disk and through the CPU cache — paying for forty columns to use three. That is catastrophic for analytics: it is I/O you will never use. Worse, a row store's indexes help point lookups ("get user 12345's profile"), not full-column aggregations, so the index that makes OLTP fast does nothing for an OLAP scan.

A **columnar store** (ClickHouse, Apache Druid, Pinot, or a warehouse like BigQuery/Snowflake) physically stores each column contiguously. To read three columns it reads exactly three columns' worth of bytes and skips the rest — an order of magnitude less I/O. And because a column is a homogeneous run of similar values (all timestamps, all country codes), it compresses spectacularly: delta-encoding for sorted timestamps, dictionary-encoding for low-cardinality strings like country or event name, run-length encoding for repeated values. That is the 10x-plus compression that turned our 1 KB raw event into 100 stored bytes — it is not a free lunch, it is the direct mechanical consequence of storing like values together. Columnar storage also vectorizes aggregation: the CPU runs the same `sum` over a tight array of values, hitting SIMD instructions and staying in cache, instead of chasing pointers through scattered rows.

The access pattern is also **time-series-shaped**: events arrive in roughly timestamp order, queries almost always filter on a time range ("last 7 days"), and old data is queried less than new data. So we partition the columnar store **by time** (daily or hourly partitions) and order within a partition by `(tenant_id, ts)`. Time partitioning gives us partition pruning — a query for "last 7 days" reads 7 partitions and skips the other 358 — and it makes tiering trivial, because aging a partition to cold storage is just moving a self-contained chunk. This is the same partition-pruning logic from [database partitioning and sharding](/blog/software-development/database/database-partitioning-and-sharding), applied to time instead of a hash key. Figure 5 will compare the *tenancy* models for this store next, but the storage engine choice is settled: columnar, time-partitioned, ordered by tenant. Below is the kind of table definition that encodes those decisions.

```sql
-- ClickHouse-style columnar table for events.
-- ORDER BY puts a tenant's events together and time-sorted within tenant,
-- so per-tenant + time-range scans hit contiguous, well-compressed blocks.
-- PARTITION BY day makes old partitions cheap to age out to object storage.
CREATE TABLE events
(
    tenant_id   UInt32,
    event_id    UInt64,
    name        LowCardinality(String),   -- dictionary-encoded: tiny
    ts          DateTime64(3),
    user_id     UInt64,
    country     LowCardinality(String),
    props       Map(String, String)       -- evolving per-tenant properties
)
ENGINE = MergeTree
PARTITION BY toYYYYMMDD(ts)
ORDER BY (tenant_id, ts, user_id)
TTL ts + INTERVAL 30 DAY TO VOLUME 'cold';  -- auto-tier after 30 days
```

One more storage decision earns a sentence: **why not just a data warehouse** (BigQuery, Snowflake, Redshift) and skip building anything? For a small platform, you should — see section 13. But at this scale, three pressures push us to a self-operated columnar store. **Latency**: warehouses are optimized for throughput on large batch queries, not for the sub-second, high-concurrency dashboard refresh pattern; serving thousands of dashboard QPS at p99 under a second is exactly what ClickHouse/Druid/Pinot are built for and what warehouses struggle with. **Cost at scale**: warehouse per-query or per-slot pricing is wonderful until you are scanning petabytes thousands of times a minute, at which point the bill exceeds a self-operated cluster by a large multiple. **Real-time ingest**: warehouses ingest in micro-batches with minutes of latency, which violates our 30-second freshness SLO. The trade is explicit: the warehouse buys you zero operational burden and infinite elasticity; the self-operated columnar store buys you sub-second latency, real-time freshness, and a lower bill at scale, at the cost of running the cluster yourself. A senior names that trade and picks based on where the platform is on its growth curve — warehouse early, self-operated when the constraints bind.

Here is the consolidated storage trade-off, the kind of table you put on the board in the review so nobody can claim you ignored an option:

| Store family | Wins on | Pays in | Pick when |
|---|---|---|---|
| Row store (Postgres/MySQL) | Point lookups, transactions | Full-scan I/O, no column pruning | OLTP, not analytics |
| Wide-column (Cassandra) | Write volume, availability | No aggregation, no ad-hoc scans | Known-key time-series writes |
| Columnar OLAP (ClickHouse/Druid) | Scan + aggregate, compression, latency | Operational burden, no transactions | High-volume event analytics |
| Cloud warehouse (BigQuery/Snowflake) | Zero ops, elastic, SQL | Latency, cost at scale, batch freshness | Smaller scale, batch reporting |

The columnar OLAP row is the one we pick, and the table makes the *why* defensible: it is the only family that wins on scan-plus-aggregate at low latency, and the things it pays in (no transactions, operational burden) are things analytics does not need and can afford.

#### Worked example: row store vs columnar on the same query

Take the query "count `purchase` events per day for the last 30 days for tenant 42," over a tenant with 2 billion events. In a **row store**, even with an index on `(tenant_id, ts)`, the engine must visit every matching row and read its full payload to extract `name` and `ts` — call it 2 billion rows times ~1 KB = ~2 TB of I/O if uncached, and the index only narrows the rows, not the per-row bytes. Realistically this query times out or runs for minutes. In a **columnar store**, the same query reads only the `name` and `ts` columns for that tenant's partitions. Those two columns, compressed, are maybe ~6 GB total (2B values times ~3 bytes each across two columns), partition-pruned to the 30 relevant days, vectorized over SIMD. It returns in **single-digit seconds on raw data, and in tens of milliseconds once we pre-aggregate it** (section 6). Same data, same question, two to three orders of magnitude difference — entirely because of physical layout. That gap is why "use the database I know" is the wrong reflex and "match the store to the access pattern" is the senior one.

## 6. The query layer: rollups vs raw scan

Columnar storage makes a full scan *survivable*, but "survivable" is not "p99 under a second." The dashboard SLO demands that the same curated charts, refreshed thousands of times per minute by every viewer, return in tens of milliseconds — and you do not get there by re-scanning 2 billion rows for every refresh, even columnar. The answer is **pre-aggregation**: compute the answers to the known questions ahead of time, store the tiny results, and serve dashboards from those. This is the central optimization of any analytics platform, and it is a textbook [caching](/blog/software-development/system-design/caching-strategies-and-the-pitfalls-that-bite) and materialized-view problem. Figure 6 shows the win.

![Before and after comparison of a raw scan reading two billion rows at eight second p99 versus an hourly rollup reading seven hundred rows at forty millisecond p99 and two hundred times cheaper](/imgs/blogs/capstone-designing-a-multi-tenant-saas-analytics-platform-6.webp)

A **rollup** is a pre-computed aggregate at a coarser grain than the raw events. Instead of 43 billion raw rows per day, we maintain, per tenant, an hourly summary: for each (event name, hour, country, plan) combination, the count of events and the count of distinct users. The stream processor updates these incrementally as events flow through — every event bumps the right rollup bucket — so the rollup is always at most a few seconds behind raw. A query like "purchases per day last month by country" now reads from the rollup: 30 days times 24 hours times a handful of countries is a few thousand rows, not 2 billion. It returns in **tens of milliseconds** because it is reading kilobytes, not gigabytes. That is the 200x cost reduction in Figure 6, and it is what makes the dashboard SLO achievable at all.

But — and this is the trade-off a senior names without being asked — **pre-aggregation buys latency by spending flexibility.** A rollup only answers the questions you anticipated. The moment an analyst asks something the rollup did not pre-compute — "show me purchases broken down by a property we don't roll up on," or "filter to users who did X then Y" — there is no rollup to serve it, and we must fall back to a **raw columnar scan**. So the query layer is not "rollups instead of raw"; it is **a router that picks per query**. Figure 9 is that decision tree, and it is the crux of the read path.

![Decision tree routing an incoming query to a cached rollup when it matches a pre-aggregated dimension set, or to a quota-guarded raw columnar scan when the dimensions are ad-hoc](/imgs/blogs/capstone-designing-a-multi-tenant-saas-analytics-platform-9.webp)

The router's logic: if the query matches a known rollup's dimensions, serve it from the rollup (and from an in-memory cache in front of the rollup, since curated dashboards are the same query over and over). If it is novel, fall back to a raw scan — but **only after passing it through the per-tenant quota and a memory cap**, because a raw scan is exactly the operation a noisy neighbor weaponizes (section 7). Three layers, fastest first: cache (sub-50ms, serves the repeated dashboard query), rollup (tens of ms, serves any pre-aggregated question), raw scan (seconds, serves the long tail, bounded by quota). Most reads never reach the raw store, which is precisely why a few thousand QPS of dashboards costs almost nothing.

There is a subtlety on the cache that bites teams: **the freshest hour is still changing.** If you cache "today's events" you cache a moving target. The fix is to cache *closed* time buckets aggressively (yesterday's hourly rollups never change, cache them for hours) and serve the current, still-filling hour from the live rollup with a short TTL. This split — immutable past, mutable present — is the same pattern that makes time-series caching tractable everywhere, and it is the difference between a cache that helps and a cache that serves stale dashboards. Concretely, a query for "last 7 days" decomposes into six fully-closed days (cached for hours, served from memory) plus today's partial day (short TTL, recomputed from the live rollup), and the router stitches the cached closed buckets to the live partial bucket — so the cache hit rate stays high even though "the answer" technically changes every second as new events land.

The query *API surface* is itself a design decision, and constraining it is a senior move. We do **not** expose raw SQL to tenants, because raw SQL is unbounded — a tenant can write a self-join across a year of data that no quota anticipated, and the support burden of arbitrary SQL is endless. Instead we expose a *constrained query DSL*: a small set of analytics primitives — counts and breakdowns over a time range, funnels (did users do A then B then C), retention (of users who did A in week 0, how many did B in week N), and segmentation (filter by property). Each primitive maps to a known query shape the engine can plan, bound, and often serve from a rollup. The constraint is the feature: because the query surface is small and structured, we can pre-aggregate for it, cache it, quota it, and reason about its cost — none of which is possible against arbitrary SQL. The router below shows the structural choice between rollup and raw, with the per-tenant guard always in the raw path.

```python
# Query router: pick the cheapest path that can answer the query,
# and never let a raw scan run without the per-tenant guard.
def route(query, tenant):
    cached = cache.get(query.cache_key())     # repeated dashboard query
    if cached and query.window_is_closed():   # immutable past -> trust cache
        return cached
    rollup = match_rollup(query)               # does a pre-agg cover these dims?
    if rollup:
        result = read_rollup(rollup, query)    # tens of ms, tiny scan
        if query.window_is_closed():
            cache.put(query.cache_key(), result, ttl=HOURS)
        return result
    # No rollup -> raw scan, but ONLY behind the tenant bulkhead.
    guard = tenant.query_guard()               # concurrency + memory + time caps
    with guard.acquire_or_reject():            # 429 if tenant over concurrency
        return raw_scan(query, limits=guard.limits())  # killed if over caps
```

#### Worked example: designing a rollup that cuts query cost

A tenant's most-viewed dashboard is "daily active users and purchases, last 90 days, split by country." Served from raw, each refresh scans ~90 days of that tenant's events — say 500 million rows — extracts `user_id`, `name`, `country`, `ts`, computes a distinct-user count and a purchase count per day per country, and returns. On columnar that is a few seconds and meaningful CPU; multiplied by every viewer refreshing every 30 seconds, it is a constant, expensive load on the cluster that competes with everyone else's queries.

Now define a rollup: `(tenant_id, day, country)` -> `(purchase_count, distinct_users)`. Distinct users is the tricky column — you cannot sum distinct counts across buckets — so we store a **HyperLogLog sketch** per bucket instead of an exact count, which merges across buckets to give a distinct estimate within ~1% error (an accuracy analytics happily accepts). The rollup for 90 days times ~50 countries is ~4,500 rows. The dashboard query now reads 4,500 rows and merges 4,500 tiny sketches: **single-digit milliseconds, and effectively free in CPU.** The win is not just latency; it is that the rollup is computed *once* by the stream processor as events arrive, and then read millions of times, instead of recomputed on every read. We traded a small, bounded write-time cost and a 1% accuracy give on distinct counts for a ~1000x read-time saving. That trade — pay once at write, save forever at read — is the soul of pre-aggregation, and naming what you give up (flexibility, exactness on distincts) is what makes it a senior decision rather than a magic trick.

## 7. Multi-tenancy in depth: the central theme

Everything so far would be true of a single-customer analytics system. What makes this *hard* — and what makes it the capstone — is that thousands of tenants share the platform, and a design that is correct for one tenant can be catastrophic across many. Multi-tenancy is not a feature you add at the end; it contaminates every layer, and getting it right is most of the engineering. The first decision is the tenancy *model*, and Figure 5 lays out the three choices.

![Multi-tenancy matrix comparing shared pool, siloed per tenant, and bridge models across isolation, cost efficiency, ops overhead, and noisy-neighbor risk](/imgs/blogs/capstone-designing-a-multi-tenant-saas-analytics-platform-5.webp)

**Shared (pool) model**: all tenants share the same infrastructure, distinguished only by a `tenant_id` column. Cheapest by far — you pay for one cluster, packed densely, and a new tenant costs almost nothing to onboard. But the blast radius is the whole platform: one tenant's runaway query, one tenant's ingest spike, one tenant's bad data can degrade everyone. Isolation is weak and it is enforced only in software (a `WHERE tenant_id = ?` that, if ever forgotten, leaks data across tenants). **Siloed (silo) model**: every tenant gets dedicated infrastructure — their own cluster, their own log topics, their own storage. Isolation is perfect; a tenant cannot affect another because they share nothing, and a data leak is physically impossible. But the cost is brutal — you cannot pack tenants, the long tail of tiny tenants each needs a baseline of dedicated capacity, and operating thousands of separate stacks is an ops nightmare. **Bridge model**: shared where it is safe, siloed where it matters. Shared collectors and shared log, but per-tenant resource quotas, per-tenant query concurrency limits, and the option to peel the biggest tenants off onto dedicated capacity. Most isolation of the silo at most of the cost-efficiency of the pool.

The senior choice for a product-analytics platform is the **bridge model**, and the reasoning is the power-law from section 1. The long tail of small tenants rides the shared pool for free — they are cheap, they do not threaten anyone, packing them densely is the whole economic model. The handful of whales get peeled onto dedicated or semi-dedicated capacity, because they are the ones who *can* hurt everyone and they are big enough to pay for isolation. You do not pick one model for all tenants; you pick a model *per tenant tier*. That insight — that tenancy is a per-tenant decision, not a platform-wide one — is the thing that separates a design that scales from one that falls over the first time a whale signs up.

### 7.1 Noisy-neighbor isolation

The signature multi-tenant failure is the **noisy neighbor**: one tenant consumes shared resources to the point that everyone else's SLO breaks. In an analytics platform there are two flavors. **Ingest noisy neighbor**: a tenant fires 100x their normal event volume (a bug in their SDK, a bot attack on their app, a Black Friday spike) and saturates the shared collectors and log partitions. **Query noisy neighbor**: a tenant runs a query that scans a year of raw data, pins every CPU on the cluster decompressing and aggregating, and every other tenant's dashboard times out behind it. Both are real, both are common, and both are solved by the same principle: **per-tenant quotas plus a bulkhead.** Figure 7 shows the difference between a system without and with that isolation.

![Before and after comparison showing a giant tenant saturating a shared pool and degrading all tenants, versus per-tenant concurrency caps and query memory limits bounding the blast radius](/imgs/blogs/capstone-designing-a-multi-tenant-saas-analytics-platform-7.webp)

For ingest, the fix is **per-tenant rate limiting at the collector** — the exact mechanism from [rate limiting and backpressure](/blog/software-development/system-design/rate-limiting-and-backpressure). Each tenant has an ingest quota (events per second, scaled to their plan). Exceed it and the collector returns 429 with a `Retry-After`, and the SDK backs off — the tenant's *own* events queue up or shed, but the platform stays healthy and **other tenants are untouched.** Critically, the rate limiter is per-tenant, so the whale's spike consumes the whale's quota, not the shared capacity. We also reserve per-partition headroom so one tenant's burst, even within quota, cannot monopolize a log partition shared with others — which is why we spread each tenant across several partitions rather than pinning them to one.

For queries, the fix is a **bulkhead**: a per-tenant cap on concurrent queries and on the memory/CPU a single query may consume. ClickHouse, Druid, and friends expose exactly these knobs — `max_concurrent_queries_for_user`, `max_memory_usage`, `max_execution_time`. A tenant gets, say, 4 concurrent queries and 10 GB of memory per query; a fifth query queues, and a query that would exceed the memory cap is *killed* rather than allowed to OOM the node and take down everyone. The bulkhead pattern — partition resources so one consumer's failure cannot drain the pool — is the [cascading-failures and bulkheads](/blog/software-development/system-design/cascading-failures-circuit-breakers-and-bulkheads) lesson applied to query concurrency. The key mental model: **every shared resource needs a per-tenant meter and a per-tenant cap, or a single tenant eventually consumes all of it.**

```yaml
# Per-tenant guardrails — the bulkhead that bounds blast radius.
# Applied at the query router before any raw scan is allowed to run.
tenant_quotas:
  default:
    ingest_eps: 5000             # events/sec; 429 + Retry-After above this
    max_concurrent_queries: 4    # 5th query queues, never starves others
    max_query_memory_gb: 10      # query exceeding this is KILLED, not OOMs node
    max_query_seconds: 30        # long ad-hoc scan is bounded, not unbounded
    raw_scan_rows_limit: 5e9     # cap a single scan; force a rollup above it
  whale_tier:                    # peeled onto semi-dedicated capacity
    ingest_eps: 500000
    max_concurrent_queries: 32
    max_query_memory_gb: 64
    dedicated_nodes: true        # bridge model: isolate the giants
```

### 7.2 Data isolation and security

Performance isolation keeps tenants from slowing each other down; **data isolation** keeps them from *seeing* each other's data, and it is a harder, stricter requirement because a single leak is a breach, not a slow dashboard. In the shared pool, every tenant's events live in the same tables distinguished by `tenant_id`, which means the *only* thing standing between tenant A and tenant B's data is a `WHERE tenant_id = ?` clause — and the day someone writes a query that forgets that clause, or an injection bypasses it, you have a cross-tenant data breach that ends customer trust and possibly the company. This is the [architecture-level security](/blog/software-development/system-design/architecture-level-security-authn-authz-zero-trust) concern, and it deserves defense in depth, not a single `WHERE` clause.

The defenses stack. First, **the tenant_id is never client-supplied for reads** — it is derived from the authenticated session/API key server-side and injected by the query router, so a malicious client cannot ask for another tenant's id. Second, **a query-builder layer**, not raw SQL from the application, constructs every query with the tenant predicate baked in structurally, so it is impossible to emit a query without it — you cannot forget a clause the framework always adds. Third, **row-level security or per-tenant views** in the store itself as a backstop, so even a query that somehow omitted the predicate is filtered by the database. Fourth, for the whales and for compliance-sensitive tenants, **physical isolation via the silo model** — separate storage, separate encryption keys per tenant — so the highest-value targets are not protected by software alone. And **encryption with per-tenant keys** so that even at the storage layer one tenant's data is cryptographically separated from another's. The principle is that data isolation must survive a bug: a forgotten `WHERE` clause should be caught by the next layer down, not be the only line of defense.

It is worth being concrete about the threat model, because "tenant isolation" is a phrase people nod at without pricing. The realistic breach is not a sophisticated attacker; it is a *bug in our own code*. An engineer adds a feature, writes a query, and forgets the tenant predicate, and now a dashboard shows tenant A a number computed over tenants A and B — a silent cross-tenant leak that a code review might miss and that no test catches unless the test specifically checks isolation. That is why the structural defense (the query-builder that *cannot* emit a tenant-less query) matters more than any policy: you remove the possibility of the bug rather than asking engineers to remember. The second realistic breach is the cache: if the cache key does not include the tenant id, tenant A's cached dashboard can be served to tenant B on a key collision — a class of bug that has caused real cross-tenant leaks in real products. So the cache key *always* includes the tenant id, structurally, the same way the query predicate does. The senior framing: in a shared multi-tenant system, every cache key, every query, every log line, and every metric carries the tenant id, and the systems that leak are the ones where one of those four forgot.

There is also an *ingest*-side isolation concern that is easy to miss: a tenant must not be able to write events that *claim* to be another tenant's. The tenant id on every ingested event comes from the authenticated API key, never from the event payload — if the SDK sends a `tenant_id` field in the body, the collector ignores it and uses the key's tenant. Otherwise a tenant who discovered another tenant's id could pollute their data, inflate their counts, or inject events into their funnels. The rule mirrors the read side: identity is server-derived from authentication, never client-asserted, on both the write and the read path.

### 7.3 Per-tenant cost attribution

The last multi-tenant concern is the one that pays the bills: knowing **what each tenant costs.** In a shared pool this is genuinely hard, because the cluster cost is one big number and tenants share everything — there is no per-tenant invoice from your cloud provider. But without per-tenant cost you cannot price correctly, cannot identify the tenant whose ad-hoc queries cost more than their subscription, and cannot bill for overages. This is the [FinOps / cost-as-a-design-constraint](/blog/software-development/system-design/cost-as-a-design-constraint-finops) discipline applied to multi-tenancy.

You attribute cost by **metering the resources each tenant consumes** and translating them to dollars. On ingest: count events ingested and bytes stored per tenant — storage cost is directly attributable because you know each tenant's row count and compression. On query: meter the CPU-seconds and bytes-scanned per tenant per query (the store reports these), since query cost is dominated by scan volume. Sum these per tenant per day and you have a defensible cost-per-tenant number: storage cost (their share of the TB) plus compute cost (their share of CPU-seconds) plus ingest cost (their share of collector and log throughput). With that number you can see that the median tenant costs cents while a whale running unrolled-up ad-hoc scans costs more than they pay — and that visibility is what lets you push that whale toward rollups, onto a higher tier, or onto the per-query billing that aligns their incentives with yours. **You cannot optimize or price what you do not measure**, and in a shared multi-tenant system, measuring per-tenant cost is a deliberate engineering effort, not a free output.

## 8. Cross-cutting concerns: reliability, observability, multi-region, schema evolution

A real platform is more than its happy-path dataflow. Four cross-cutting concerns turn the design from a whiteboard sketch into something you can operate, and each one connects back to a mechanism deep-dive in this series.

**Reliability and graceful degradation.** The ingest path must degrade gracefully, never catastrophically — that is the [reliability and graceful degradation](/blog/software-development/system-design/reliability-slos-error-budgets-and-graceful-degradation) discipline. If the columnar store is down, ingest keeps working because the log buffers; events accumulate and drain when the store recovers, and freshness degrades from 30 seconds to minutes, which is a survivable degradation rather than data loss. If the rollup pipeline falls behind, dashboards serve slightly stale aggregates with a "data as of HH:MM" label rather than erroring. If the raw-scan path is overloaded, ad-hoc queries get queued or rejected with a clear message while dashboards (served from cache and rollups) keep working — we shed the loose-SLO load to protect the tight-SLO load. The rule: identify what *must* keep working (ingest, then dashboards) and design every failure to degrade the optional parts first. We also apply [circuit breakers](/blog/software-development/system-design/cascading-failures-circuit-breakers-and-bulkheads) on the path from stream processor to columnar store — if the store starts timing out, the breaker opens, the processor stops hammering it and lets the log backlog grow, and a slow store does not cascade into a stuck pipeline.

**Observability.** You cannot operate a multi-tenant system blind, and the [observability](/blog/software-development/system-design/observability-metrics-logs-traces-by-design) discipline says you instrument by design, not after the first outage. The non-obvious requirement here is that **every metric is per-tenant.** Aggregate ingest rate hides the whale; per-tenant ingest rate shows you the tenant whose SDK just started looping. Aggregate query latency hides the tenant whose dashboards are timing out; per-tenant p99 query latency shows you exactly who is suffering and whether it correlates with a noisy neighbor. We tag every metric, log, and trace with `tenant_id` so that when a tenant opens a support ticket — "my dashboards are slow" — we can pull *their* latency, *their* query mix, and *their* resource consumption in seconds rather than guessing. The lag of the stream processor (how far behind the log it is) is the single most important operational metric, because it is the leading indicator of a freshness-SLO breach; we alert on it before it becomes customer-visible.

**Multi-region.** Tenants are global, and the [multi-region and geo-distribution](/blog/software-development/system-design/multi-region-and-geo-distribution) trade-offs apply. We run ingest in every region close to where events originate — an SDK in Europe hits a European collector and a European log, minimizing the ingest latency that the SDK perceives. Storage and query, though, we keep mostly **regional** rather than globally replicated, because replicating petabytes of analytics data across regions is ruinously expensive and analytics tolerates a tenant's data living in one region. A tenant is *homed* to a region (often the one where most of their users are, or where data-residency law requires — a European tenant's data must stay in Europe for GDPR), and their dashboards query that region. This avoids the cross-region replication cost entirely and satisfies data-residency requirements, at the price that a tenant's home region going down makes *their* dashboards unavailable until failover — a trade we accept because regional outages are rare and re-ingesting from a buffered log on recovery is cheaper than continuous cross-region replication of petabytes.

**Schema evolution.** The event schema is *not fixed* — a tenant adds a property next Tuesday and expects to query it Wednesday, which is the [schema and API evolution](/blog/software-development/system-design/schema-and-api-evolution-at-scale) problem in its purest form. This is why we store properties as an open map (`Map(String, String)` or a JSON/columnar-nested type) rather than rigid columns: a new property is just a new key, no migration, no downtime, queryable immediately. For the hot, frequently-queried properties we *promote* them to real materialized columns (extracted by the stream processor) because a dedicated column is far faster to scan than a key in a map — so the schema *evolves* from flexible-but-slow (map) to fast-but-fixed (column) for the properties that earn it, without ever forcing a tenant through a migration. The event *envelope* (the required fields: tenant, name, ts, user) is versioned and backward-compatible; the property *bag* is free-form by design. That split — a rigid, versioned envelope around a flexible, evolving payload — is how you absorb constant schema change without constant migrations.

## 9. Optimization and cost: tiering, compression, and the bill

The optimizations are not an afterthought; in an analytics platform at this scale, **cost *is* the design constraint** — the difference between a profitable platform and one that bleeds money is entirely in how cleverly you store and query. We have already met the three big levers; this section ties them to dollars. Figure 8 is the tiering stack that does most of the heavy lifting.

![Storage tiering stack showing an in-memory cache for the last minutes, hot columnar SSD for the last thirty days, warm columnar for thirty to one hundred eighty days, and cold object storage beyond at a tenth of the cost](/imgs/blogs/capstone-designing-a-multi-tenant-saas-analytics-platform-8.webp)

**Lever one: tiering hot to cold.** We established 5 TB/day of growth. Keeping a year (1.8 PB) on NVMe SSD would dominate the budget, but 90% of queries hit the last 30 days — the recent window is what dashboards show. So we tier: the last 30 days on fast SSD (the hot tier, where dashboards and most ad-hoc queries live), 30–180 days on cheaper warm storage, and everything older on **object storage at roughly one-tenth the per-TB cost.** A query for old data is slower (object storage has higher latency, and we accept that — old-data queries are rare and have a looser SLO), but the savings are enormous: instead of paying SSD prices for 1.8 PB, we pay SSD prices for 150 TB and object-storage prices for the rest. Time-partitioning (section 5) makes this nearly free to implement — aging a partition is moving a self-contained chunk, and the store does it automatically via a TTL rule.

**Lever two: columnar compression.** This is the lever we already spent in the estimate, and it is worth making explicit as an *optimization*, not just a property. The 10x-plus compression from columnar encoding (dictionary, delta, run-length) is not automatic — it depends on the **sort order**. Data sorted by `(tenant_id, ts)` clusters similar values together (a tenant's events of the same type, in time order, with the same country) so the encoders find long runs to compress. Get the sort order wrong (say, sort by a random id) and compression collapses from 10x to 2x — your storage bill *quintuples* with no schema change, just a worse `ORDER BY`. So the sort key is a cost decision, not just a query-performance decision, and a senior treats it as such.

**Lever three: pre-aggregation as cost control.** Rollups (section 6) are not only a latency optimization; they are a *compute-cost* optimization. Every dashboard refresh served from a rollup is a scan that *did not happen* on the raw store, which is CPU-seconds you did not pay for. Since dashboards are served thousands of times per minute and rollups are computed once, the compute saving is multiplied by every refresh. The optimization framing: move work from read-time (where it is paid per query, repeatedly) to write-time (where it is paid once as the event flows through). That is the highest-leverage cost move in the whole system, and it is why the rollup pipeline earns its complexity.

#### Worked example: the monthly bill and where it goes

Put dollars on it, order-of-magnitude. The hot tier was ~40 NVMe nodes for 300 TB (150 TB times replication factor 2) plus query headroom; at a few thousand dollars per node-month, the hot store is the dominant line — call it on the order of \$120,000–\$160,000/month. The cold tier, holding the bulk of historical data in object storage at ~\$0.02/GB-month, holds say 1.5 PB for ~\$30,000/month — a fraction of what the same data would cost on SSD (which would be roughly \$300,000+/month). Ingest (collectors plus the Kafka log) is throughput-bound, on the order of \$40,000–\$60,000/month for the fleet and the log cluster at replication factor 3. The single biggest cost *risk* is unbounded ad-hoc scans on the hot tier — a handful of whales running un-rolled-up queries can spike compute cost unpredictably, which is exactly why the per-tenant query quotas (section 7.1) are a cost control as much as an isolation control. The lesson: in this system, the bill is dominated by hot storage and by the long tail of expensive raw scans, so the two highest-value optimizations are *tiering data off the hot tier* and *routing queries to rollups* — and both also happen to be the two we already designed for latency. Good optimizations tend to compound like that.

## 10. Stress-test: what breaks at 10x, at a whale, at a region failure?

A design you have not tried to break is a hypothesis, not a plan. Walk it through the four failures that actually take down analytics platforms — this is the [testing distributed systems / chaos](/blog/software-development/system-design/testing-distributed-systems-chaos-and-load) mindset of attacking your own design before production does.

**The 10x ingest spike.** A tenant's app goes viral, or every tenant peaks at once (New Year's Eve). Ingest jumps from 500k to 5M events/s. What breaks? Nothing fatal, *by design*, and this is the payoff of the durable-log architecture. The collectors autoscale (stateless, so add instances), and the log *absorbs* the spike — producers keep appending at 5M/s, consumers (stream processors) fall behind, the backlog grows, and freshness degrades from 30 seconds to maybe several minutes as the processors work through the backlog. No events are lost; the log is the shock absorber. We add stream-processor capacity to drain the backlog faster, and freshness recovers. The one thing we watch: log *retention* must be long enough that a multi-hour backlog does not age events out before they are processed — size the log's disk for the worst-case backlog duration. The 10x spike is a *freshness* incident, not a *loss* incident, and that distinction is the whole reason the log is in the middle.

**The whale (noisy neighbor).** A single giant tenant sends half the platform's events and runs the most expensive queries. Without the bridge model and per-tenant quotas, this tenant degrades everyone — saturates shared collectors, monopolizes log partitions, pins query CPU. *With* the section-7 design, the whale is contained: their ingest is capped to their (large, paid-for) quota and spread across partitions, their queries are bulkheaded to a concurrency and memory cap, and if they are big enough they are peeled onto semi-dedicated nodes so their raw scans never touch the shared pool. The whale can hurt *themselves* (hit their own quota, queue their own queries) but cannot hurt the long tail. This is the failure that the entire multi-tenancy section exists to prevent, and the test is: can one tenant, behaving as badly as possible, violate another tenant's SLO? With the quotas and bulkheads, no.

**The query that scans everything.** An analyst (or a buggy dashboard) issues "every event, all time, no filter, grouped by user." On a naive system this pins every CPU and times out everyone else. Our defenses: the query router checks it against rollups (no match — it's pathological), routes it to raw scan, and there hits the **per-tenant memory cap and execution-time limit** — the query is *killed* at 30 seconds or 10 GB rather than allowed to consume the cluster. The tenant gets a clear error ("query too expensive, narrow your time range or dimensions") and everyone else is unaffected. The bulkhead turned an unbounded, cluster-killing operation into a bounded, single-tenant rejection. The senior insight: in a multi-tenant system, *every* operation needs a bound, because eventually a tenant will issue the unbounded version, accidentally or not.

**The region failure.** A tenant's home region goes dark. Their ingest fails over to a healthy region (collectors and log are per-region and we can route their SDK traffic elsewhere, buffering until their home region returns), so events are not lost — they queue in the failover region's log. Their *dashboards*, however, are unavailable until the home region recovers or until we fail their storage over, because we deliberately chose not to continuously replicate petabytes cross-region (section 8). We accept this: a rare regional outage makes a tenant's *historical* dashboards briefly unavailable, but no events are lost and recent data re-ingests from the buffered log. The trade was cost (no continuous cross-region replication) for a bounded availability hit on dashboards during a rare event — a trade we made consciously and can defend in the review.

## 11. How the series' patterns all show up here

The reason this is the capstone: nearly every pattern from the series appears, and seeing them compose is the lesson. The [transactional outbox](/blog/software-development/message-queue/transactional-outbox-pattern-reliable-publishing) shows up if we need to publish derived events reliably (e.g., a tenant-facing webhook when a threshold is crossed) — we write the event and the outbox row in one transaction so the webhook cannot be lost or duplicated. **Idempotent ingest** is the server-assigned `event_id` plus windowed dedup that makes the log's at-least-once delivery safe ([idempotency and deduplication](/blog/software-development/message-queue/idempotency-and-deduplication-making-at-least-once-safe)) — a retried event is dropped, not double-counted. **Circuit breakers and bulkheads** ([cascading failures](/blog/software-development/system-design/cascading-failures-circuit-breakers-and-bulkheads)) bound the blast radius between the stream processor and the store, and between tenants. **Backpressure** ([rate limiting and backpressure](/blog/software-development/system-design/rate-limiting-and-backpressure)) is the per-tenant ingest limit and the log absorbing spikes. **Cells/bulkheads** map onto the bridge tenancy model — peeling whales onto isolated capacity is cell-based isolation at the tenant grain.

The *consistency* choices come straight from [consistency models](/blog/software-development/system-design/consistency-models-a-practical-guide-for-architects): analytics is the canonical place to choose **eventual consistency and like it.** A dashboard that is 30 seconds stale is fine; a count that is approximate (HyperLogLog distinct estimates within 1%) is fine; we deliberately trade exactness and freshness for throughput and cost, the exact opposite of the payment system in this series. That contrast is the deepest lesson of the whole series: the *same* engineer, facing the *same* CAP and PACELC trade-offs, chooses consistency-and-reject for payments and availability-and-approximate for analytics — because the senior move is never to apply a pattern reflexively, but to let the *requirements* pick the trade-off. The patterns are tools; judgment is choosing which tool the constraints demand.

## 12. Case studies

Real platforms made these exact choices, and their public engineering writing confirms the design pressures we reasoned to.

**ClickHouse-based analytics (PostHog, Cloudflare, many others).** ClickHouse exists because OLTP databases cannot serve analytics at scale, and the teams that adopted it did so for precisely the reasons in section 5: columnar storage, aggressive compression, and vectorized aggregation turn billion-row scans from minutes into seconds. PostHog rebuilt its analytics on ClickHouse specifically to escape the row-store wall, and Cloudflare uses ClickHouse to serve analytics over an enormous volume of HTTP request events with the same time-partitioned, columnar pattern. The lesson each confirms: the storage engine is the decision, and "match the store to the access pattern" beats "use the database I know." The bridge-model intuition — share the cluster but cap per-tenant queries — is how a multi-tenant ClickHouse deployment survives a noisy neighbor.

**Druid and the Lambda-to-streaming evolution (Netflix, Lyft, others).** Apache Druid was built for exactly this shape — real-time ingestion of event streams into a columnar, time-partitioned store with pre-aggregation (Druid calls it "rollup") built into the ingestion path. Teams that adopted Druid did so to hit sub-second dashboard latency over high-volume event data, which is the section-6 rollup story made into a product. The architectural lesson is that pre-aggregation at ingest time is not an optimization you bolt on later; the systems built for this problem make it a first-class part of the write path, because that is where the cost-per-read win lives.

**The noisy-neighbor incident (a recurring multi-tenant post-mortem shape).** The single most common multi-tenant analytics incident, written up by team after team, is the same story: one tenant runs an unbounded query (or fires a runaway volume of events) on a shared cluster, the cluster's CPU or memory saturates, and *every* tenant's dashboards go down. The fix is always the same and always retrofitted painfully: per-tenant query concurrency limits, per-query memory caps that kill rather than OOM, and peeling the biggest tenants onto dedicated capacity. The lesson is to build the bulkheads *before* the incident, because the incident is not "if" but "when" — the first whale to sign up will run the cluster-killing query, accidentally, within the first month. Designing the quotas in from day one (section 7.1) is the difference between a Tuesday and a multi-tenant outage.

**The rollup cost win (a common internal optimization story).** Teams repeatedly report the same win: dashboards served from raw scans cost a fortune in cluster CPU and miss the latency SLO, and the single highest-leverage fix is to identify the curated dashboards (which are few and repetitive) and pre-aggregate them into rollups. The reported gains are routinely one-to-three orders of magnitude in both latency and compute cost — exactly the section-6 worked example. The lesson: the curated-dashboard workload is small, fixed, and known, which makes it perfectly suited to pre-aggregation; spend the rollup complexity there and let the rare ad-hoc query pay full price on the raw store.

## 13. When to reach for this architecture (and when not to)

**Reach for the full pipeline** — durable log, stream processing, columnar store, rollups, per-tenant quotas — when you have genuine event-analytics scale: high-volume semi-structured events, multiple tenants, dashboard *and* ad-hoc query patterns, and a freshness requirement measured in seconds-to-minutes. This is the right design for a product-analytics SaaS, an observability/telemetry backend, an ad-tech event platform, or any "store billions of events and slice them" problem. The architecture earns its complexity precisely because the constraints (write scale, query latency, multi-tenancy, cost) all bind at once.

**Do not reach for it** when you are smaller than you think. If you have one tenant (an internal analytics tool), drop the entire multi-tenancy layer — it is most of the complexity and you do not need it. If your event volume is millions per day rather than billions, a single columnar database (a managed ClickHouse, or even Postgres with a columnar extension, or a warehouse like BigQuery/Snowflake queried directly) handles it without the streaming pipeline — you do not need Kafka in the middle to buffer a trickle. If your queries are all known dashboards and never ad-hoc, you may not need the raw store at all — just the rollups. And if you need exact, transactional, point-lookup access (this is a user's account balance, not an aggregate), this is the wrong architecture entirely; you want the OLTP design from the [payment system](/blog/software-development/system-design/design-a-payment-system) post. The senior move is to match the architecture's complexity to the problem's actual constraints — and to *delete* the parts of this design you do not need, because every component you keep is a component you have to operate.

A useful staging path: start with a managed columnar warehouse and direct queries (no streaming, no rollups) until query latency or cost forces your hand; add rollups when dashboards get slow; add the streaming pipeline when ingest volume or freshness forces it; add per-tenant quotas the moment you have a second tenant who could hurt the first. Build the pieces as the constraints arrive, not all at once on day one — the [evolutionary architecture](/blog/software-development/system-design/evolutionary-architecture-designing-for-change) discipline applies even to the capstone.

## Key takeaways

- **Clarify before you constrain, constrain before you sketch.** "Design an analytics platform" has no answer until you know who the tenants are, how big the whale is, how fresh the data must be, and which read patterns matter. The first ten minutes of questions determine the next ten months of architecture.
- **Rank your SLOs and let the ranking drive every trade-off.** Ingest availability beats query latency beats freshness; data isolation is a hard line, performance isolation is a knob. When two SLOs conflict — and under load they will — the ranking tells you which to sacrifice.
- **Match the store to the access pattern, not to scale alone.** Analytics reads few columns over many rows and aggregates; that access shape *demands* a columnar, time-partitioned store and makes a row store catastrophically wrong, regardless of how big or small the data is.
- **The durable log is the shock absorber.** Putting Kafka between the untrusted, spiky outside world and the steady columnar writes turns a 10x ingest spike into a survivable *freshness* incident instead of a fatal *loss* incident, and gives you replay for free.
- **Pre-aggregation trades flexibility for latency, and that trade is usually right.** Curated dashboards are few, fixed, and repetitive — pre-compute them into rollups, serve them in milliseconds, and pay the cost once at write-time instead of every read. Fall back to raw scans only for the ad-hoc long tail, behind a quota.
- **Multi-tenancy contaminates every layer; design it in from day one.** The bridge model (share the pool, cap per tenant, peel the whales) plus per-tenant quotas, per-query memory caps, and defense-in-depth data isolation is the only thing standing between one noisy whale and a platform-wide outage.
- **Every shared resource needs a per-tenant meter and a per-tenant cap.** Ingest rate, query concurrency, query memory, scan rows — bound all of them per tenant, because eventually one tenant issues the unbounded version, and the bound is what keeps their blast radius from becoming everyone's.
- **Cost is a design constraint, and the cost levers are the latency levers.** Tiering data off the hot SSD tier, compressing via the right sort order, and routing reads to rollups are simultaneously your biggest cost savings and your biggest latency wins — good optimizations compound.
- **Let the requirements pick the consistency trade-off.** Analytics chooses eventual consistency, approximate distinct counts, and seconds-of-staleness *gladly* — the exact opposite of a payment system. The senior skill is not knowing patterns; it is knowing which pattern the constraints demand.
