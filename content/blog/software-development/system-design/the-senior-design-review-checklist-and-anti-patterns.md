A junior engineer in a design review asks, "Will this work?" A senior engineer asks, "How will this break, what does it cost, and what happens when the thing it depends on is down?" That difference — from *does it function* to *how does it fail and what do we pay* — is the entire distance this 40-post series has been walking. This final post is the map of that distance compressed into something you can actually carry into a meeting: a checklist you run on any design in your head in twenty minutes, and a gallery of anti-patterns you learn to smell from across the room.

Most design reviews are bad. Not because the reviewers are unskilled, but because nobody has decided what the review is *for*. So it drifts. People argue about variable names while a single point of failure sits unexamined three boxes to the left. Someone proposes Kafka because Kafka is interesting, and nobody asks whether the system has enough traffic to justify a log at all. The author leaves either bruised or falsely reassured, and three months later production teaches the lesson the review was supposed to teach for free. A good review is the cheapest place in the entire software lifecycle to catch an expensive mistake — the cost curve we drew back in [evolutionary architecture](/blog/software-development/system-design/evolutionary-architecture-designing-for-change) says a decision caught at design time costs 1×, in code 10×, in data 100×, and in a public contract 1000× — and yet most teams squander it.

![A tree grouping the nine design-review dimensions into correctness, resilience, and economy families with one key question under each](/imgs/blogs/the-senior-design-review-checklist-and-anti-patterns-1.webp)

The figure above is the spine of this post and, honestly, the spine of the series. Nine review dimensions, grouped into three families: correctness (does it meet the requirements and keep its data right), resilience (does it survive failure and stay operable), and economy (does it cost what it should and stay as simple as the problem allows). Each dimension carries exactly one question — the question that, in my experience, catches the most expensive class of mistake in that dimension. By the end of this article you will be able to read a design doc critically, run all nine dimensions in your head, name the anti-patterns by their smell, and — the part that actually matters — do it in a way that improves the design and the author instead of just scoring points. That last part is not soft-skills garnish. A review that humiliates the author teaches the team to stop bringing designs to review, which means the next expensive mistake ships unreviewed. The culture *is* the mechanism.

## 1. What a design review is actually for

Before the checklist, the purpose, because the purpose determines what you do with the checklist. A design review has exactly one job: **catch expensive mistakes while they are still cheap to fix.** Everything else — knowledge sharing, onboarding, getting buy-in, creating a paper trail — is a welcome side effect, but if you optimize for those and lose the main thing, you have a meeting that feels productive and prevents nothing.

This framing immediately tells you what *not* to do. You do not bikeshed. Bikeshedding — the term comes from C. Northcote Parkinson's observation that a committee will approve a nuclear reactor in minutes but argue for an hour about the color of the bike shed, because everyone has an opinion about bike sheds and nobody understands reactors — is the natural gravitational pull of every review. The hard, expensive, load-bearing decisions are intimidating and require real thought, so the group's attention slides toward the easy, cheap, opinion-shaped ones. A senior's first job in a review is to *resist the slide*: to keep dragging attention back to the boxes where a wrong choice costs a quarter of engineering time, and to explicitly deprioritize the ones where a wrong choice costs an afternoon. "We can change the endpoint naming in a follow-up; I want to spend our time on whether this shared database survives the second team landing on it."

The second thing the framing tells you: a review is a *risk-ranking* exercise, not a *correctness-proving* exercise. You are not going to verify the design is correct — you can't, you don't have the time, and correctness is mostly determined by code you haven't seen yet. What you can do is find the two or three places where, if the design is wrong, it is wrong *expensively and irreversibly*, and concentrate every minute of scarce review attention there. A review that finds one genuine single point of failure is worth more than a review that leaves forty tidy comments about formatting.

And the third: a review's output is a *decision*, not a feeling. "Looks good to me" is not an output. "Ship it as-is," "ship it after fixing the missing timeout and the dual write," or "this needs a redesign of the data-ownership model before we proceed" — those are outputs. The author should leave knowing exactly what is blocking, what is a suggestion, and what is a question. The most damaging review is the ambiguous one, where the author can't tell if your concern about the shared database was a blocker or a musing, so they ship it as-is and find out in the postmortem.

There's a cultural precondition that makes all of this possible, and it's worth stating plainly because teams get it wrong constantly: **the review critiques the design, never the designer.** The moment a review becomes a referendum on whether the author is smart, two things happen. Authors stop proposing ambitious designs (too risky to be wrong in public) and reviewers stop raising hard concerns (too socially expensive to imply a colleague missed something). Both failure modes kill exactly the value the review exists to create. The blameless framing from [the outage postmortem post](/blog/software-development/system-design/anatomy-of-an-outage-lessons-from-real-postmortems) applies identically here: assume the author is competent and made the best decision available given what they knew, and treat every gap as a gap in the *shared* understanding, not a personal failing. "Help me understand how this handles a region failure" lands completely differently from "you forgot region failover," even when they point at the same hole.

## 2. Reading a design doc the way a senior reads it

You cannot review what you haven't understood, and most reviewers skip the understanding step. They open the doc, see a familiar word — "Kafka," "sharding," "eventually consistent" — and immediately form a reaction to the word rather than to the design. A senior reads in passes, and the first pass is not for finding problems. It is for reconstructing the author's *intent*: what problem are they solving, what constraints are they under, and what does success look like. You cannot judge whether a design is good until you know what it is trying to be good *at*.

![A pipeline showing a senior review moving from reading for intent to questioning load-bearing claims to stress-testing to a ship-or-revise decision](/imgs/blogs/the-senior-design-review-checklist-and-anti-patterns-4.webp)

The figure shows the four-pass shape. **Read for intent** first — what and why, no judgment. **Question the load-bearing claims** second — find the sentences the whole design rests on and poke them. **Stress-test** third — take the design as given and ask what breaks at 10× load, at a region failure, at a hot key, at a poison message. **Decide** last. The discipline is in not collapsing these. The most common reviewer mistake is jumping straight from a glance to a verdict, which means you are reacting to surface keywords and missing the load-bearing claim entirely.

What is a load-bearing claim? It is a sentence that, if false, sinks the design. "Reads dominate writes 200:1, so we can serve from a replica." If that ratio is actually 5:1, the whole replica-serving strategy is wrong, and everything downstream of it is built on sand. "Each user's data fits comfortably in one shard." If a single celebrity user blows past a shard's capacity, the partition key is wrong and you have a hot-shard problem that resharding alone won't fix. A senior reads with a highlighter — mental or literal — marking every sentence of the form "because X, we can Y," because those are the joints where the design either holds or fails. Most reviewers read the *boxes and arrows*. The boxes and arrows are downstream of the claims. Review the claims.

A trick I use on any design doc, learned the hard way: **find the number.** A design that doesn't state a single quantity — no QPS, no data volume, no latency target, no growth rate — has not been thought through, and that absence is itself the most important finding. You cannot evaluate whether Postgres is enough without knowing the write rate. You cannot evaluate whether the cache helps without knowing the read/write ratio and the hit rate. The estimation discipline from [back-of-the-envelope estimation](/blog/software-development/system-design/back-of-the-envelope-estimation-for-system-design) is not a separate skill from review — it *is* review. When a doc has no numbers, your highest-leverage move is not to critique the architecture; it's to ask, "What's the expected write rate at launch, and at the one-year projection?" and watch the design either firm up or reveal that nobody knows.

## 3. The checklist, dimension by dimension

Now the checklist itself. Nine dimensions. The goal is not to mechanically tick every box on every design — that turns review into compliance theater. The goal is to *internalize* the nine questions so that scanning a design triggers the relevant ones automatically, the way a doctor's eyes go to the right places without a literal checklist most of the time, but the checklist exists for when it matters and for the things easy to forget.

![A matrix mapping each review dimension to its single key question, the mistake it catches, and its review leverage](/imgs/blogs/the-senior-design-review-checklist-and-anti-patterns-5.webp)

The matrix above is the compressed form: each dimension, its one question, the mistake it catches, and its leverage — how much it's worth spending review minutes on. We'll walk each one, but the leverage column is the meta-point: requirements, data, and failure are high-leverage because mistakes there are expensive and hard to reverse; consistency and cost are medium; and a few dimensions barely register in early review. Spend your minutes accordingly.

### Requirements and SLOs

The first question on any design: **are the non-functional requirements even stated, and is the SLO matched to the business?** Most designs over-specify the functional requirements (what the system does) and completely omit the non-functional ones (how fast, how available, how consistent, how durable). And those non-functionals drive nearly every interesting architectural decision. A 99.9% availability target and a 99.999% target are different *systems*, not the same system tuned differently — the second requires multi-region active-active, redundant everything, and a degradation story for every dependency, while the first can survive on a single well-run region with good backups.

The senior catch here is the *mismatch* between the SLO and the business. I have seen teams design five-nines availability for an internal admin tool that ten people use during business hours — a heroic, expensive over-engineering of a system whose actual requirement is "works most of the time during the day." I have also seen the opposite, and it's worse: a payments path designed with a casual "should be pretty reliable," no stated target, no error budget, which means no one ever decided how reliable it needs to be, which means it will be exactly as reliable as it accidentally turns out to be. The [reliability and SLO post](/blog/software-development/system-design/reliability-slos-error-budgets-and-graceful-degradation) makes the full case; in review, the one question is: **does the stated reliability target match what this system is actually worth to the business, and is there a number at all?** No number is a finding. A number wildly mismatched to the business is a finding.

### Scale and estimation

Second: **do the numbers work, and what breaks at 10×?** Take the design as drawn and run the arithmetic. If the doc claims 50,000 writes per second to a single Postgres primary, that claim is false on its face — a single primary tops out one to two orders of magnitude below that for most workloads — and the whole design needs sharding it doesn't have. If the doc says "we'll cache everything," ask what the working set size is; if it's 8 TB, "cache everything" means an 8 TB cache, which is a different cost conversation than the author expected.

The 10× question is the senior's signature stress test, and it earns its keep because growth is the one thing you can count on for a successful system. Take every number in the design and multiply by ten. Where does it fall over first? That first failure point is your design's *real* ceiling, and knowing it changes the conversation from "does this work" to "does this work for long enough that we'll have time to fix the next thing." Sometimes the answer is "it falls over at 3× and 3× is six weeks away," which is an emergency the review just turned up for free. Sometimes it's "it falls over at 50× and we'll be a different company by then," which is fine — you don't pre-build for 50×, you just confirm you'll see it coming. The capacity discipline from [capacity planning and autoscaling](/blog/software-development/system-design/capacity-planning-and-autoscaling) feeds directly into this question.

### Data

Third, and this is one of the highest-leverage dimensions because data decisions are the hardest to reverse: **is this the right store, does it match the access pattern, and can the schema evolve?** The mechanism deep-dives — [B-trees vs LSM-trees for architects](/blog/software-development/system-design/storage-engines-btrees-vs-lsm-trees-for-architects) and [choosing a datastore](/blog/software-development/system-design/choosing-a-datastore-sql-nosql-newsql) — give you the menu. In review, you are checking that the choice *follows the access pattern* rather than the team's familiarity or the latest conference talk.

The access-pattern question is the one people skip. A datastore is not "good" in the abstract; it is good *for a shape of reads and writes*. Point lookups by key, range scans by time, secondary-index queries, huge append throughput, strong transactional updates across rows — these are different shapes, and no single store is best at all of them. A key-value store wins point lookups and pays on range scans; a relational store wins transactional updates and pays on raw write volume; a wide-column store wins write throughput and pays on ad-hoc queries. The choice follows the workload, not the team's comfort with a particular technology.

The senior move is to ask, "What is the dominant access pattern, and does the chosen store win that pattern?" If the dominant pattern is range scans over time-series data and the design picked a key-value store with no range support, that's a finding that will surface as a pile of inefficient client-side filtering six months in. The B-tree-versus-LSM distinction from [storage engines for architects](/blog/software-development/system-design/storage-engines-btrees-vs-lsm-trees-for-architects) is the mechanism underneath: read-optimized versus write-optimized storage is a real fork, and a design that picks the write-optimized engine for a read-heavy point-lookup workload is paying compaction overhead for nothing. And the *evolution* sub-question — can this schema change without downtime — connects to [schema and API evolution at scale](/blog/software-development/system-design/schema-and-api-evolution-at-scale): a design that has no story for adding a column to a 500-million-row table is a design that will require downtime to change, and "requires downtime to change data" is, on a successful system, a slow-motion emergency.

### Consistency

Fourth: **what is the weakest consistency model that still meets the requirement, and where specifically is strong consistency actually needed?** This is the dimension where over-engineering hides most comfortably, because "strongly consistent" sounds responsible and "eventually consistent" sounds sloppy, so the default drift is toward stronger guarantees than the business needs — and strong consistency is expensive in latency, availability, and operational complexity, as [CAP and PACELC](/blog/software-development/system-design/articulating-tradeoffs-cap-pacelc-and-beyond) lays out.

The senior catch runs in *both* directions. Over-strong: a social feed that uses a distributed transaction to guarantee every follower sees a new post atomically — when "the post shows up within a few seconds, in any order" is the actual requirement, and eventual consistency would have been cheaper, faster, and more available. Under-strong: an inventory system that uses eventual consistency for the "items remaining" count — and oversells the last unit to three customers because three replicas each thought they had the last one. The [consistency models post](/blog/software-development/system-design/consistency-models-a-practical-guide-for-architects) is the full guide; the review question is sharp: **name the one or two operations that genuinely need strong consistency (usually money, inventory, and uniqueness constraints), use the weakest model that works everywhere else, and make sure the design didn't apply one blanket guarantee to the whole system.** A single global consistency level is almost always wrong — it's either too weak for the money path or too strong for everything else.

### Failure

Fifth, and tied with data and requirements for highest leverage: **what is the blast radius of each failure, what is the degradation story, and where are the single points of failure?** This is the dimension juniors omit entirely, because a junior assumes the dependencies are up. A senior assumes the opposite — *everything is down right now somewhere* — and the only question is whether the design noticed.

![A graph showing one missing timeout cascading through blocked threads, retry amplification, and pool exhaustion to a full outage](/imgs/blogs/the-senior-design-review-checklist-and-anti-patterns-9.webp)

The figure traces how a *single* omitted timeout — the smallest possible design flaw — cascades into a full outage: the dependency slows, threads block waiting on it with no deadline, clients retry and triple the load, the thread pool exhausts, and now every request 5xxes even though the original slow dependency was only one of many. We'll dwell on this anti-pattern below, but the review lesson is that failure handling is not a feature you add later; it is woven through the design or it is absent, and absent failure handling produces exactly this cascade. The [cascading failures post](/blog/software-development/system-design/cascading-failures-circuit-breakers-and-bulkheads) — and the circuit-breaker and bulkhead patterns it covers — is the toolbox. In review you check three things: every external call has a *timeout* and a *bounded retry*; every dependency has a *degradation story* ("if recommendations is down, we show popular items instead of erroring"); and there is no *single point of failure* whose loss takes the whole system down. The SPOF hunt is the highest-value thirty seconds in any review — just trace each box and ask "what if this one is gone?"

### Operability

Sixth: **can you observe it, deploy it, and reverse a deploy?** A design can be functionally perfect and operationally a disaster. The three sub-questions: Is it **observable** — does the design emit the metrics, logs, and traces you'd need to debug it at 3 a.m., or will the first production incident be diagnosed blind? Is it **deployable** — can you ship a change safely, incrementally, behind a flag, or does every deploy require a coordinated big-bang? And is it **reversible** — when the deploy goes wrong, can you roll back in minutes, or are you stuck because a schema migration ran forward and there's no path back? The [observability post](/blog/software-development/system-design/observability-metrics-logs-traces-by-design) makes the case that observability is a design property, not an afterthought; the review question is simply whether the author thought about *operating* the thing they're building, not just building it.

The reversibility sub-question deserves special emphasis because it's the one teams discover too late. A deploy you can roll back in two minutes is a different risk profile from a deploy you cannot undo, and the difference is usually a single irreversible step buried in the change — a database migration that drops a column, a data backfill that mutates rows in place, a message format change that the new consumers depend on. The senior catch in review is to find the irreversible step and ask whether it can be made reversible: the [expand-contract migration pattern](/blog/software-development/system-design/schema-and-api-evolution-at-scale) exists precisely so that schema changes ship in reversible stages — add the new column (reversible), dual-write to it (reversible), backfill (reversible), switch reads (reversible), and only *then*, after the new path is proven in production, drop the old column (the one irreversible step, deferred to last and done deliberately). A design that does the irreversible drop in the same deploy as the risky new code has fused a roll-forward-only schema change to code that might need rolling back, which means when the code is wrong you cannot retreat. The review question — "what is the rollback procedure, and is there a step in this change that can't be undone?" — routinely surfaces a deploy that looked safe and wasn't.

There's a second operability trap worth naming because it's invisible until 3 a.m.: a design that's observable in *aggregate* but not *per-request*. You have a dashboard showing p99 latency is fine, but when one customer reports their requests are slow, you have no way to trace *that customer's* request through the system to find where it stalls. The fix is distributed tracing with a propagated request ID, so that any single request can be reconstructed end to end. The review catch is to ask, "When a customer says it's slow for them but the dashboards look green, how do you debug it?" If the answer is "we'd add logging and wait for it to happen again," the design isn't observable enough — it can tell you *that* something is wrong but not *what*, and the gap between those two is the length of the outage.

### Security

Seventh: **what are the trust boundaries, and what is the blast radius of a leak?** You don't need to be a security specialist to run the architectural security check. Two questions cover most of it. First, **where are the trust boundaries** — at which points does data cross from untrusted to trusted, and is it validated and authenticated there? An internal service that trusts its caller completely is fine until that caller is reachable from the internet through a path nobody mapped. Second, **what is the blast radius of a single compromised credential or leaked secret** — if this one service's database password leaks, can the attacker read every customer's data, or just this service's slice? The [zero-trust architecture post](/blog/software-development/system-design/architecture-level-security-authn-authz-zero-trust) goes deep; in review, the catch is the design that has a hard outer shell and a soft, fully-trusting interior, where one breach anywhere is a breach everywhere.

The blast-radius framing is the one that distinguishes a senior security review from a checklist-compliance one. Compliance asks "is it encrypted, is it authenticated, are secrets in a vault" — necessary, but it's a list of *controls*. The senior asks the *consequence* question: assume a control fails — a secret leaks, a token is stolen, an internal service is compromised — and trace what the attacker can reach from there. A design where one leaked database credential exposes every tenant's data is fragile no matter how good the encryption is, because security failures are not "if" but "when," and the only thing you control is how much one failure costs. The architectural fixes are about *limiting blast radius*: per-tenant or per-service credentials so one leak exposes one slice, network segmentation so a compromised service can't reach the whole fleet, and least-privilege so the stolen token can do little. The review question — "if this one credential leaks, what's the worst an attacker does?" — is the security analog of the SPOF hunt, and it finds the same shape of problem: a single point whose failure is catastrophic because nobody bounded the damage.

### Cost

Eighth: **what are the unit economics, and what is the dominant cost line item?** Cost is a design constraint, not a finance problem you hand off, and the [FinOps post](/blog/software-development/system-design/cost-as-a-design-constraint-finops) argues this at length. The review question has two parts. The **unit economics**: what does it cost to serve one user, one request, one GB stored — and does that number stay sane as you scale, or does it have a term that grows faster than revenue? A design where cost per user is flat is healthy; a design where cost per user grows because every user's data is replicated to every region is a time bomb. The **dominant line item**: what is the single biggest cost, because that's where optimization pays off and where a wrong choice hurts most. If 80% of the bill is cross-region data transfer, the design conversation should be about data locality, not about shaving compute.

### Simplicity

Ninth, and the one a senior weights more heavily with every passing year: **is this the simplest design that meets the requirements, and what can we remove?** Every component is a thing to build, operate, monitor, secure, pay for, and eventually debug at 3 a.m. The most reliable system is the one with the fewest moving parts that still does the job. The review question — and it should be asked of *every* design — is "what can we delete and still meet the requirements?" Not "what could we add to be safe," which is the question that produces the over-engineered mess in the next section. The whole discipline of this series bends toward this: the senior solution is usually *smaller* than the junior one, because the junior adds components to feel safe while the senior removes components to *be* safe.

## 4. The optimization lens: which questions actually pay

Nine dimensions is a lot to run on every design when reviews are time-boxed and you have three other things due. So the optimization question — the highest-leverage move in this entire post — is: **which of the nine questions catches the most expensive mistakes per minute spent?** Not all dimensions are equal. Spend your scarce attention where the cost-of-being-wrong is highest and the cost-of-checking is lowest.

![A stack ranking review dimensions by leverage with requirements and data at the top and naming and style at the bottom](/imgs/blogs/the-senior-design-review-checklist-and-anti-patterns-7.webp)

The stack ranks the dimensions by leverage, top to bottom. **Requirements and SLOs** sit at the top because a wrong requirement makes the entire design solve the wrong problem — the most expensive error possible, and one that's cheap to catch by just asking "what's the actual target?" **Data model and store choice** are next because they're the hardest to reverse: a wrong partition key discovered in production is a multi-month migration, while catching it in review is one question. **Failure and blast radius** rank high because the cost of a missed SPOF is a customer-facing outage, and the check — trace each box, ask "what if it's gone" — is fast. Below those, **scale at 10×** is cheap to check and catches hidden walls. Then **operability and cost**, which are recurring drag rather than acute failure — important, but a mistake there bleeds slowly rather than exploding. And at the bottom, **naming and style** — real, but the lowest cost-of-being-wrong in the whole stack, which is precisely why bikeshedding gravitates there and precisely why a senior consciously deprioritizes it.

The practical rule that falls out of this: **review top-down, and stop when the budget runs out.** If you have twenty minutes, spend fifteen on requirements, data, and failure, and let the naming comments wait for the async follow-up. A review that thoroughly examines the top three dimensions and skips the bottom three is far more valuable than one that evenly distributes attention and runs out of time before reaching the SPOF. The mistake is the *even* review — the one that gives the bike shed the same minutes as the reactor.

There's a measurable version of this leverage claim, and it's worth stating in the unit the cost curve uses. A requirements mistake caught in review costs roughly one conversation; the same mistake caught in production after the system is built on the wrong assumption costs a redesign — call it 100× to 1000× by the cost-of-change curve. A naming mistake caught in review costs one comment; caught later, it costs a rename — maybe 5×, because names rarely leak into data or contracts the way structural decisions do. So per minute of review, the requirements question is literally one to two orders of magnitude more valuable than the naming question. That's not a vibe; it's the arithmetic of where decisions calcify.

## 5. The anti-patterns, by smell

The checklist tells you what to look for. The anti-patterns tell you what you'll *find*, because the same handful of mistakes recur across every team, every domain, every decade. Learning them by smell — the surface symptom that signals the deeper problem — is what lets a senior glance at a design and say "that's a distributed monolith" before the author has finished explaining it. Pattern recognition is compressed experience.

![A matrix listing six anti-patterns with their smell, why each one bites, and the standard fix](/imgs/blogs/the-senior-design-review-checklist-and-anti-patterns-2.webp)

The matrix collects six of the most common anti-patterns with their smell, their failure mode, and the fix. We'll walk these and several more. The structure for each is the same: the **smell** (what you see in the design that triggers recognition), **why it bites** (the failure it produces, often months later), and the **fix** (what the senior reaches for). The reason for the smell/bite/fix structure is that you review with the smell, you justify the concern with the bite, and you stay constructive by leading with the fix.

![A tree sorting anti-patterns into coupling, failure-handling, and resource categories](/imgs/blogs/the-senior-design-review-checklist-and-anti-patterns-6.webp)

And it helps to file the anti-patterns into three categories, because the category tells you which checklist dimension would have caught it. The tree sorts them: **coupling mistakes** (the distributed monolith, the shared database, the dual write) are caught by the data and simplicity dimensions; **failure-handling mistakes** (missing timeouts, synchronous chains, unnoticed SPOFs) are caught by the failure dimension; and **resource mistakes** (unbounded queues and retries, snowflake servers) are caught by the scale and operability dimensions. Recognizing the category is the fast path to the fix.

### Over-engineering and premature scale

**The smell:** a design for 1,000 users that has a service mesh, an event bus, six datastores, and a CQRS read model. Microservices before there's a team large enough to need them. A multi-region active-active setup for a product that has no users outside one country. Any sentence of the form "we'll need this when we scale" attached to a system that is nowhere near scaling.

**Why it bites:** every component you add is a component you must build, deploy, monitor, secure, and debug — and you pay all of that cost *now*, for a scale you have *later*, if ever. The over-engineered system is slower to ship (so you might die before reaching the scale you built for), harder to operate (more parts, more failure modes, more 3 a.m. pages), and ironically *less* reliable than the simple version, because reliability is roughly inverse to the number of things that can break. The team that built twelve microservices for a thousand users spends its energy operating the mesh instead of finding product-market fit.

![A before-and-after contrasting an over-engineered twelve-service design with a right-sized monolith plus Postgres for the same load](/imgs/blogs/the-senior-design-review-checklist-and-anti-patterns-3.webp)

The figure makes the cost concrete: the same 1,000-user load, served two ways. The over-engineered version — twelve microservices, Kafka, CQRS, six datastores — runs perhaps \$9,000 a month and takes a quarter to build before it serves a single user. The right-sized version — one modular monolith, Postgres, a Redis cache, maybe one extracted service — runs perhaps \$300 a month and ships in a week. Both serve the load. One of them lets you iterate; the other lets you operate infrastructure. The [evolutionary architecture post](/blog/software-development/system-design/evolutionary-architecture-designing-for-change) makes the full argument for monolith-first; the review fix is the simplicity question applied ruthlessly: **what can we remove and still meet the requirements?** Usually: almost all of it.

**The fix:** match the architecture to the *current* load plus a reasonable runway, not to the load you fantasize about. Build the seams (modular boundaries inside the monolith) so you *can* split later cheaply, but don't split until the pain of not-splitting is real. Defer the hard-to-reverse decisions; the [one-way-door framing](/blog/software-development/system-design/evolutionary-architecture-designing-for-change) tells you which those are.

### The distributed monolith

**The smell:** services split by noun rather than by bounded context. A request that requires seven synchronous service-to-service calls to complete. Services that can't be deployed independently — there's a "release train" where everything ships together because service A's change requires service B's change. Shared libraries that, when updated, force every service to redeploy in lockstep.

**Why it bites:** the distributed monolith is the worst of both worlds. You took on every cost of microservices — network latency between components, partial-failure handling, distributed debugging, operational overhead of N deployments — and got *none* of the benefit, because the services are so coupled they still deploy and fail together. A real monolith at least has the virtue of in-process calls and atomic deploys. The distributed monolith has cross-network calls *and* coordinated deploys: maximum cost, minimum independence.

![A before-and-after contrasting a chatty seven-hop distributed monolith with well-bounded services using async events and a database per service](/imgs/blogs/the-senior-design-review-checklist-and-anti-patterns-8.webp)

The figure contrasts the two. On the left, the distributed monolith: chatty synchronous RPC (seven hops per request, so seven chances to add latency and seven chances to fail), a shared database that couples every service to one schema, and lockstep deploys. On the right, well-bounded services: asynchronous events where possible (so a slow consumer doesn't block the producer), a database per service (so each service owns its data and can evolve its schema independently), and the ability to deploy one service without touching the others. The difference is not "more services" versus "fewer" — it's *where you draw the boundaries*. Bounded contexts, not nouns.

**The fix:** split by bounded context — a chunk of the domain that owns its data and has a clear, narrow interface to the rest. If two services share a database, they are one service wearing two hats; give each its own store. If a request needs seven synchronous hops, the boundaries are wrong — either those services should be one, or the chain should be asynchronous. The test for a good boundary: can this service be deployed, scaled, and fail *independently* of the others? If no, the boundary is decorative.

### The shared database

**The smell:** multiple services reading and writing the same database tables. Team A's service joins across team B's tables. A schema change requires a meeting with three teams. Nobody can confidently say who writes to the `users` table because everyone does.

**Why it bites:** the shared database is the most common cause of the distributed monolith, and it bites in slow motion. The database schema becomes a *public contract* shared by every service that touches it — but an *implicit* one, with no version, no owner, and no change process. So nobody can change it safely: adding a column is fine, but renaming or removing one might break a service three teams away that nobody remembers depends on it. The schema calcifies. Worse, the services are now coupled at the *data* layer, the hardest layer to decouple (remember the cost curve — data changes cost 100× more than code changes). You wanted independent services; the shared database silently re-coupled them.

**The fix:** each service owns its data; other services get to it through the owner's *API*, not by reaching into its tables. When service B needs service A's data, B calls A's interface (or consumes A's events), which means A can change its schema freely as long as its interface holds. This is the single most important rule for keeping services actually independent, and it's the one most violated under deadline pressure, because reaching into another team's table is *faster today* and catastrophic in eighteen months. The migration path from a shared database to owned data is real work — covered in part by [change data capture and the outbox pattern](/blog/software-development/database/change-data-capture-and-the-outbox-pattern) — but the review catch is simply: **does more than one service write these tables? If so, that's the finding.**

### The dual write

**The smell:** code that writes to two systems in sequence — "save to the database, then publish to Kafka," or "write to Postgres, then update Elasticsearch," or "update the row, then invalidate the cache." Two writes, no transaction spanning them, and an implicit assumption that both succeed.

**Why it bites:** the two writes are not atomic, and there is no way to make them atomic across two different systems. So eventually — and at scale, "eventually" means "many times a day" — the process crashes between the first write and the second. Now the database has the new row but Kafka never got the event, or Postgres updated but Elasticsearch didn't, or the row changed but the cache still serves the old value. The two systems have silently diverged, and nobody notices until a customer reports that their order shows as paid in one place and unpaid in another. Dual writes are a *correctness* bug disguised as a *plumbing* detail, and they are the single most common source of "the data is inconsistent and we don't know why" in distributed systems.

**The fix:** the [transactional outbox pattern](/blog/software-development/database/change-data-capture-and-the-outbox-pattern). You make *one* atomic write — to the database — that includes both the business data and an "outbox" record describing the event to publish. A separate process reads the outbox and publishes to Kafka, retrying until it succeeds. Now there is no window where one write happened and the other didn't, because there was only ever one write. The [distributed transactions, outbox, and sagas post](/blog/software-development/system-design/distributed-transactions-outbox-and-sagas) covers the full pattern and its cousin, the saga, for multi-step workflows. In review, the smell is unmistakable: any two sequential writes to different systems with no outbox is a dual write, and a dual write is a future inconsistency. Flag it every time.

### The missing timeout

**The smell:** a call to an external service, database, or cache with no timeout configured. A connection pool with no acquisition timeout. An HTTP client using library defaults (which are often *no timeout* or an absurd default like five minutes). The smell is an *absence* — the timeout that isn't there — which makes it easy to miss in review because you're looking for what's wrong rather than what's not present.

**Why it bites:** this is the cascade from figure 9, and it's worth understanding precisely because it is the most common single cause of total outages. A dependency slows down — not even fails, just slows, from 50ms to 5 seconds. Every thread that calls it now blocks for 5 seconds instead of 50ms, holding its worker thread and its connection the whole time. With a fixed thread pool — and every system has a fixed thread pool somewhere — the pool fills with threads all waiting on the slow dependency. New requests can't get a thread, so they queue, then time out at the *client*, which retries, tripling the inbound load. The pool is now exhausted, every request 5xxes, and the service that was merely *slow* one hop away has caused a *total outage* here. The original slow dependency might have recovered on its own in thirty seconds; the missing timeout turned a thirty-second blip into a multi-minute outage and a thundering-herd recovery.

**The fix:** every external call gets a timeout, and the timeout is *shorter* than you think — tied to your latency budget, not to the dependency's worst case. If your endpoint must respond in 200ms, a downstream call cannot have a 5-second timeout; it gets maybe 100ms, and on timeout you degrade (serve a default, return a partial result) rather than wait. Pair the timeout with a *bounded retry* (the next anti-pattern) and a *circuit breaker* (from [the cascading-failures toolbox](/blog/software-development/system-design/cascading-failures-circuit-breakers-and-bulkheads)) so that when the dependency is genuinely down, you stop hammering it and fail fast. In review: trace every arrow that leaves the box, and for each one ask "what's the timeout, and what happens when it fires?" An arrow with no answer is a finding.

### The synchronous chain that should be asynchronous

**The smell:** a user-facing request that must complete a long chain of work synchronously before responding — "create order → charge card → reserve inventory → send confirmation email → update analytics → respond to user." Six steps, all in the request path, all of which must succeed for the user to get a response, and the user waits for all of them.

**Why it bites:** the request is now as slow as the *sum* of all six steps, and as available as their *product*. If each step is 99.9% available, six steps in series is 99.9%⁶ ≈ 99.4% available — you've turned three nines into two by chaining. And the latency is the sum, so one slow step (the email provider having a bad minute) makes the whole request slow, even though the user doesn't care about the email being sent *before* they see "order confirmed." You've coupled the user's latency and availability to operations they don't need synchronously.

**The fix:** do synchronously *only* what the user needs to see a correct response — usually "create order" and maybe "charge card" — and push everything else (email, analytics, even inventory reservation in some designs) onto an asynchronous queue. The user gets a fast response; the rest happens reliably in the background, with retries, decoupled from the request path. The [queues and event streaming post](/blog/software-development/system-design/queues-and-event-streaming-for-architects) covers the patterns. The review question: **for each step in this chain, does the user actually need it done before we respond?** Usually most of the chain can move off the hot path, and moving it both speeds up the response and decouples its availability.

### The cache without invalidation

**The smell:** a cache with a TTL and *no* invalidation on write. Data is cached for, say, five minutes, and when the underlying data changes, the cache keeps serving the old value until the TTL expires. The design says "we'll cache it" and stops there, with no plan for what happens when the cached thing changes.

**Why it bites:** TTL-only caching means *every* write produces a window of stale reads up to the TTL long. For data that rarely changes or where staleness is harmless (a list of countries), that's fine. For data that changes and where staleness matters — a user's account balance, a feature flag, a permission — serving a five-minute-old value is a correctness bug. The classic version: a user changes their password or revokes a session, and the old credential keeps working for five minutes because the auth cache wasn't invalidated. The [caching pitfalls post](/blog/software-development/system-design/caching-strategies-and-the-pitfalls-that-bite) catalogs this and its evil twin, the thundering herd on expiry.

**The fix:** decide explicitly per cache whether TTL-only staleness is acceptable, and if it isn't, invalidate (or update) the cache on write. The review question is the one the design skipped: **when the underlying data changes, what happens to the cached copy?** "Nothing, it expires in five minutes" is an acceptable answer for some data and a bug for other data — the senior catch is making sure the author *decided* which, rather than defaulting into staleness by omission.

### The unbounded queue and the unbounded retry

**The smell:** a queue with no maximum depth. A retry policy with no cap on attempts and no backoff — "retry until it succeeds." A buffer that grows to absorb load. Anything that can grow without limit when the thing draining it falls behind.

**Why it bites:** unbounded things don't fail gracefully; they fail catastrophically and late. An unbounded queue absorbs a backlog silently — looks healthy, metrics green — right up until it exhausts memory or disk and crashes, or until the backlog is so deep that messages are hours stale by the time they're processed, which for many systems is worse than dropping them. An unbounded retry without backoff is a *retry storm*: when a dependency fails, every client retries immediately and forever, multiplying load on the already-struggling dependency by the retry count, guaranteeing it can never recover — the [backpressure post](/blog/software-development/system-design/rate-limiting-and-backpressure) calls this the difference between a system that sheds load and one that amplifies it. Unbounded resources convert a recoverable overload into an unrecoverable collapse.

**The fix:** bound everything. Queues get a max depth and a policy for what happens when full (reject, shed, or apply backpressure upstream — never silently grow forever). Retries get a *cap* on attempts, *exponential backoff* (wait longer each time), and *jitter* (randomize the wait so all clients don't retry in sync). Past the cap, the message goes to a dead-letter queue for inspection rather than retrying into the void. The review smell is any "retry until success" or any queue without a stated depth limit; the fix is a number on the bound.

### The snowflake server

**The smell:** a server that was configured by hand, has accumulated manual tweaks over years, and cannot be reproduced from code. "Don't reboot prod-db-3, it has the special config." A machine nobody dares touch because nobody knows exactly what's on it. Infrastructure that exists as a *state* rather than as *code that produces the state*.

**Why it bites:** the snowflake is a single point of failure with extra steps. When it dies — and hardware dies — you cannot rebuild it, because the knowledge of how it was configured lived in the fingers of someone who has since left. Recovery, which should be "run the provisioning script and restore the data," becomes archaeology. The snowflake also can't be scaled (you can't make ten of a thing you can't make one of reproducibly) and can't be safely patched (you don't know what a change will break). It's the operability dimension's classic failure: the system works until the moment it needs to be *operated*, and then it doesn't.

**The fix:** infrastructure as code. Every server is reproducible from a script — Terraform, Ansible, a container image, whatever — so that "rebuild this machine" is a command, not a séance. Cattle, not pets. In review, the smell is any mention of manual configuration, "we set this up by hand," or any server with a proper name and a special status; the fix is making it reproducible before it becomes load-bearing.

### Resume-driven architecture

**The smell:** technology chosen because it's interesting, trendy, or good on a résumé, rather than because the problem needs it. Kubernetes for three services that could run on a single VM. A new database because a conference talk was compelling. Microservices, GraphQL, event sourcing, a service mesh — adopted as a *goal* rather than a *response to a constraint*. The tell is that the design leads with the technology ("we'll use X") rather than with the problem ("we need to do Y, and X is the cheapest way").

**Why it bites:** the wrong technology, chosen for the wrong reason, imposes its costs forever while solving a problem you didn't have. The team now operates Kubernetes — a genuinely complex system with its own failure modes and a steep operational tax — to run a workload that a single autoscaling group would have handled. Every hour spent learning and operating the unnecessary technology is an hour not spent on the product. And these choices are sticky: once the new database is in production, ripping it out is a migration, so the résumé-driven choice calcifies into a permanent tax.

**The fix:** technology choices justify themselves against a *constraint*, not a preference. The review question — asked gently, because the author may not realize they've done it — is "what problem does this solve that the simpler option doesn't?" If the answer is a real constraint (we genuinely have 50,000 writes per second and a single Postgres can't), great. If the answer is "it's more scalable" with no number showing the simpler option fails, that's résumé-driven, and the fix is the boring option that works.

### Ignoring the tail

**The smell:** a design that reasons about *average* latency. "The average response time is 40ms, so we're fine." SLOs stated as averages. Capacity planned for mean load. Any analysis that uses the mean and never mentions p99 or p99.9.

**Why it bites:** users don't experience the average; they experience the *tail*, and at scale the tail is where everyone lives. If your p99 latency is 2 seconds, then 1 in 100 requests is slow — and a page that makes 100 backend calls (entirely normal) will, on average, have *one* slow call dragging the whole page to 2 seconds. The tail dominates the user experience precisely because requests fan out: with 100 sub-requests, the page is as slow as the slowest of 100, which is your p99, not your average. A design optimized for mean latency can have a perfectly healthy 40ms average and a user-facing experience that's routinely 2 seconds because the tail was never examined. The mechanism — tail latency amplification under fan-out — is one of the most important and most ignored facts in system design.

**The fix:** reason in percentiles, always. State SLOs as p99 (and sometimes p99.9), not averages. Plan capacity for the load that keeps p99 under target, which is more headroom than the mean would suggest. And attack the tail specifically — it usually comes from a few sources (garbage collection pauses, cold caches, lock contention, a slow shard) that mean-based analysis hides entirely. The review catch: **any latency claim stated as an average is incomplete — ask for the p99.** It's a one-word question that routinely surfaces a 10× gap between the number the author quoted and the number users feel.

### The SPOF nobody noticed

**The smell:** there isn't one, really — that's the whole problem. The single point of failure hides in a component everyone takes for granted: the one Redis instance the whole fleet uses for rate limiting, the single NAT gateway all egress traffic flows through, the one config service every other service reads from at startup, the certificate that expires, the single DNS provider. It's not labeled "SPOF"; it's labeled "obvious infrastructure," which is exactly why nobody questions it.

**Why it bites:** because it's the single thing whose failure takes *everything* down, and because it's invisible until it fails. Redundancy was added everywhere it was *expected* to be needed — multiple app servers, replicated databases — and skipped on the one component that seemed too fundamental to fail. Then it fails, and the carefully-redundant everything-else is useless because it all depends on the one thing that didn't have a backup.

**The fix:** the SPOF hunt, run deliberately in every review. Trace every box and every shared dependency and ask, mechanically, "if this one thing is gone, what still works?" The components where the answer is "nothing" are your single points of failure, and each one needs either redundancy or a conscious, documented decision to accept the risk. The discipline is to run this on the *boring* boxes — the load balancer, the DNS, the config store, the shared cache — because the exciting boxes already got the redundancy attention. The SPOF nobody noticed is, by definition, in the box nobody looked at.

## 6. Trade-offs: thoroughness vs velocity of a review

Every recommendation in this post has a cost, and the meta-recommendation — run a thorough nine-dimension review with a full anti-pattern sweep — has the biggest one: *time*. A genuinely thorough review takes hours, and you don't have hours for every design, and a process that makes every change expensive to review just routes changes *around* review, which is worse than a lighter review. So the senior's actual skill is calibrating review depth to the decision's stakes. Naming what you optimize, naming what you pay — the same discipline the whole series has been about — applied to the review process itself.

| Review depth | What you gain | What you pay | When it wins |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Rubber stamp** (skim, LGTM) | Near-zero latency; changes flow fast | Catches almost nothing; expensive mistakes ship | Trivial, reversible changes; two-way doors with low blast radius |
| **Lightweight** (top-3 dimensions, 20 min) | Catches the high-leverage mistakes cheaply | Misses operability, cost, naming issues | Most changes; the default for an experienced team |
| **Full checklist** (all 9 + anti-pattern sweep) | Catches nearly everything; shared understanding | Hours of senior time; can slow delivery | New systems, one-way doors, anything touching money or data ownership |
| **Adversarial** (red-team, threat-model, chaos plan) | Catches subtle and security failures | Days of effort; needs specialists | Payment systems, auth, anything where a failure is a headline |

The decision rule is the one Bezos gave us and the [evolutionary architecture post](/blog/software-development/system-design/evolutionary-architecture-designing-for-change) elaborated: **match review depth to reversibility and blast radius.** A two-way door — a change you can cheaply undo, with a small blast radius — gets a lightweight review or a rubber stamp, because the cost of being wrong is low and the cost of a heavy review isn't worth it. A one-way door — a data model, a partition key, a public API, a security boundary — gets the full checklist or the adversarial treatment, because being wrong there is a multi-month, multi-team disaster. The mistake teams make is *uniform* review depth: either rubber-stamping the irreversible decisions (and shipping disasters) or full-reviewing the trivial ones (and grinding delivery to a halt while engineers learn to route around the process). Variable depth, matched to stakes, is the senior calibration.

#### Worked example: running the checklist on a sample design and finding three issues

Let's make this concrete. A team brings a design for a **notification service**: when an event happens (order shipped, payment failed, friend request), the service sends the user an email, a push notification, and an in-app notification. The design, summarized:

> "An API endpoint receives a notification request. It writes the notification to Postgres, then publishes to Kafka for the email worker, then calls the push provider's API, then updates the in-app feed in Redis, then returns 200 to the caller. We expect about 500 notifications per second at launch, growing. We cache the user's notification preferences in Redis with a 1-hour TTL. The service runs on three EC2 instances behind a load balancer."

Run the checklist top-down. **Requirements:** the SLO isn't stated — what's the latency target, what's the delivery guarantee (at-least-once? at-most-once?)? That's the first finding: *no stated reliability target for a delivery system*, where the guarantee fundamentally shapes the design. **Scale:** 500/sec is fine for three instances and Postgres; at 10× (5,000/sec) Postgres writes might strain but it's not the first wall — the synchronous push-provider call is, because the provider's latency now sits in the request path. Hold that thought. **Data:** Postgres for notification records is reasonable (point lookups and recent-history range scans), and Redis for the feed is fine. No finding here.

Now the issues surface. **Issue one — the dual write:** the design writes to Postgres, *then* publishes to Kafka, *then* writes to Redis, with no outbox and no transaction spanning them. If the process crashes after the Postgres write but before the Kafka publish, the notification record exists but no email is ever sent — a silent delivery failure, exactly the divergence the dual-write anti-pattern predicts. **Fix:** transactional outbox — write the notification and an outbox record in one Postgres transaction, and let a relay publish to Kafka. **Issue two — the synchronous chain:** the endpoint calls the push provider *synchronously* in the request path, so the caller's latency and availability now depend on a third party. If the push provider has a slow minute, every notification request is slow, and if it's down, requests fail entirely — even though the caller doesn't need the push *sent* before getting a 200. **Fix:** push the push-provider call onto the async path (Kafka, like email), respond as soon as the notification is durably recorded. **Issue three — the cache without invalidation:** preferences are cached with a 1-hour TTL and no invalidation on write, so when a user turns *off* email notifications, they keep receiving them for up to an hour. For a preferences-respecting system, that's a compliance-adjacent bug, not a cosmetic one. **Fix:** invalidate the preference cache on preference write.

Three real issues — a dual write, a synchronous chain, and a stale cache — found in one top-down pass, each catchable by a single checklist question, each cheap to fix now and expensive to fix in production. That's the review earning its keep. Notice none of the findings were about naming or style; the high-leverage questions found the high-leverage problems.

#### Worked example: spotting the anti-pattern in a described architecture and fixing it

A second scenario, this time to practice pattern recognition. A team describes their architecture in a review:

> "We have eight microservices. The `OrderService`, `UserService`, `InventoryService`, and `BillingService` all read and write the same MySQL database — it's simpler than each having its own. A typical checkout makes synchronous REST calls: the frontend calls `OrderService`, which calls `UserService` to validate the user, then `InventoryService` to check stock, then `BillingService` to charge, then back to `InventoryService` to decrement, then `OrderService` writes the order. We deploy all eight services together every two weeks because a change in one usually needs a matching change in another."

Name the anti-pattern. The tells are stacked: a **shared database** (four services on one MySQL), a **synchronous chain** (checkout makes five sequential cross-service calls), and **lockstep deploys** ("deploy all eight together because a change in one needs a change in another"). Together, these three smells diagnose a **distributed monolith** — services split by noun, coupled at the data layer, unable to deploy independently, paying full microservice cost (network hops, partial failure, eight deployments) for zero microservice benefit.

The fix is a re-bounding, not a rewrite. **Data ownership first:** give each service its own data — `BillingService` owns billing tables, `InventoryService` owns stock, and they expose APIs rather than sharing MySQL. This is the load-bearing change, because the shared database is what's forcing the lockstep deploys; once each service owns its schema, it can evolve independently. **Then the chain:** the five synchronous hops in checkout are both a latency problem (sum of five) and an availability problem (product of five). Make the parts that don't need to be synchronous asynchronous — decrementing inventory and writing the final order record can happen via events after the charge succeeds, using a [saga](/blog/software-development/system-design/distributed-transactions-outbox-and-sagas) to handle the case where a later step fails (release the reserved inventory, refund the charge). **Then the boundaries:** if `OrderService` and `InventoryService` are so coupled that nearly every order change requires an inventory change, maybe they're *one* bounded context that was wrongly split — consider merging them. The general lesson: the fix for a distributed monolith is rarely "more microservices" and often "fewer, better-bounded services with owned data and async edges."

## 7. Case studies: the review that paid for itself, and the anti-pattern in the wild

### A review that caught a costly mistake

The most valuable design review I've witnessed caught a partition-key mistake in a multi-tenant analytics system — the same class of system as [the capstone](/blog/software-development/system-design/capstone-designing-a-multi-tenant-saas-analytics-platform). The design partitioned event data by `event_date`, which seemed natural: time-series data, range scans by date, clean partitions. A reviewer asked the scale question — "what does our query pattern actually look like?" — and the answer was that *every* query filtered by `tenant_id` first (each customer only ever sees their own data) and *then* by date. Partitioning by date alone meant every tenant's query touched every partition, and one large tenant's heavy queries would scan across all date partitions, starving every other tenant. Worse, the largest tenant's data in any single date partition could blow past the partition size limit — a classic hot-partition problem that [partitioning and sharding](/blog/software-development/system-design/partitioning-and-sharding-without-downtime) warns about.

The fix, applied in the review, was to partition by `(tenant_id, event_date)` — composite, tenant-first — so each tenant's queries hit only that tenant's partitions, and a hot tenant couldn't starve the others. The cost of catching it in review: one question and a fifteen-minute discussion. The cost of catching it in production — after billions of events had been written under the wrong key — would have been a full repartitioning migration of live data, the multi-month, can't-easily-reverse, data-layer disaster the cost curve punishes hardest. One reviewer asking "what's the actual query pattern?" — the data dimension's key question — saved a quarter of engineering time. That ratio, one question versus one quarter, is why design reviews exist.

### The distributed monolith and shared database in the wild

The most famous public example of escaping a distributed monolith is the broad industry movement, documented in countless engineering blogs and conference talks through the 2010s, of teams that adopted microservices, split by the wrong boundaries, landed in a distributed monolith, and had to walk it back. The recurring story has the same shape every time: a team splits a monolith into many small services to "move faster," splits them by technical layer or by noun rather than by bounded context, ends up with services that share databases and call each other synchronously in long chains, and discovers they now deploy *slower* than the monolith did — because every change ripples across services that were never truly decoupled, and every deploy is a coordination problem. Several high-profile teams publicly described *consolidating* services back together (sometimes called "right-sizing" or, half-jokingly, "the macroservice") once they realized the boundaries, not the count, were the problem.

The shared-database version of this story is even more common and less often told, because it's embarrassing rather than heroic. A team gives each new service its own codebase but — under deadline pressure, because it's faster *today* — lets them all keep talking to the original shared database. For a while it works. Then the schema needs to change, and the change requires coordinating four teams who all depend on the same tables, none of whom fully know what the others read. The schema freezes. New features that need schema changes get blocked or hacked around. The "microservices" are revealed to be a monolith with extra network hops, coupled at the one layer that's hardest to decouple. The escape is always the same and always expensive: give each service its own data, move cross-service reads to APIs or [event streams via CDC](/blog/software-development/database/change-data-capture-and-the-outbox-pattern), and pay down the coupling debt over many quarters. The lesson the review should have taught for free: *the moment two services share a database, they are one service, and the review is the cheapest place to say so.*

### The missing-timeout outage, generalized

The cascade in figure 9 is not hypothetical; it's the modal shape of the large public outages catalogued in [the postmortem post](/blog/software-development/system-design/anatomy-of-an-outage-lessons-from-real-postmortems). A dependency slows, calls without timeouts pile up, thread pools or connection pools exhaust, retries amplify the load, and a slowdown in one component becomes a total outage across everything that calls it. The specific trigger varies — a slow database query, a degraded downstream API, a garbage-collection pause, a saturated network link — but the *amplifier* is almost always the same two design omissions: no timeout (so slow becomes blocked) and unbounded retry (so blocked becomes a storm). These are exactly the anti-patterns a failure-dimension review catches, and the reason they recur in postmortem after postmortem is that they are *absences* — the timeout that isn't there, the retry cap that was never set — and absences are what reviews most often miss. The discipline of tracing every outbound arrow and asking "timeout? retry bound? circuit breaker?" is dull, and it prevents a disproportionate share of real outages.

## 8. When to reach for a heavy review (and when not to)

Run the **full nine-dimension checklist plus anti-pattern sweep** when the decision is *irreversible or high-blast-radius*: a new system's foundational architecture, a data-model or partition-key choice, a public API contract, anything touching money, auth, or customer data ownership, a multi-region topology, or a technology adoption that will be sticky. These are the one-way doors; the cost of the heavy review is trivial next to the cost of walking back through the door.

Run a **lightweight top-three review** (requirements, data, failure) for the broad middle: most feature work, most service changes, most designs from an experienced team. You're spending your scarce attention on the high-leverage dimensions and trusting the team on the rest. This should be your *default*.

**Skip the formal review entirely** — or make it a quick async glance — for genuinely trivial, reversible, low-blast-radius changes: a config tweak behind a flag, a copy change, an internal tool used by five people. A process that forces ceremony onto two-way doors just teaches people to resent and route around review, which corrodes the culture that makes review work at all.

And reach for the **adversarial review** — red-teaming, threat modeling, an explicit chaos and load-test plan from [testing distributed systems](/blog/software-development/system-design/testing-distributed-systems-chaos-and-load) — only for the genuinely dangerous: payment systems, authentication, anything where a single failure is a regulatory event or a headline. This is expensive and needs specialists; spend it where a failure is catastrophic, not where it's merely annoying.

The anti-pattern of *review itself* is uniform depth — treating every change with the same ceremony. The senior calibration is variable depth matched to stakes, which is, fittingly, the exact same trade-off reasoning the whole series has taught applied to the act of reviewing.

## 9. Key takeaways — the grand summary of the series

This is the last post, so these takeaways are not just about reviews; they're the distilled philosophy of all forty.

- **A design review exists to catch expensive mistakes while they're cheap.** Risk-rank, don't bikeshed; produce a decision, not a feeling; critique the design, never the designer. The cost curve makes review the highest-ROI thirty minutes in the lifecycle.
- **Run the nine dimensions top-down by leverage.** Requirements, data, and failure first — they're expensive to get wrong and cheap to check. Naming and style last. The even review is the wrong review.
- **The weakest model that works is the senior default.** The strongest consistency, the most services, the most components — these are not the responsible choices; they're the expensive ones. Reach for the minimum that meets the requirement, and name what you'd pay for more.
- **Learn the anti-patterns by smell.** Shared database, dual write, distributed monolith, missing timeout, synchronous chain, cache without invalidation, unbounded retry, snowflake server, résumé-driven architecture, ignoring the tail, the unnoticed SPOF. Each has a recognizable smell, a predictable bite, and a known fix. Pattern recognition is compressed experience, and now you have it without the scars.
- **Everything is down right now somewhere.** Design with that assumption. Every external call gets a timeout and a bounded retry; every dependency gets a degradation story; every box gets the question "what if this one is gone?" Failure handling is woven in, not bolted on.
- **Data decisions are the ones that calcify.** Code is cheap to change, data is expensive, public contracts are the most expensive of all. Defer the hard-to-reverse decisions; put the seams where change is likely; own your data so you can evolve it.
- **Cost and simplicity are design dimensions, not afterthoughts.** Know your unit economics and your dominant cost line. Ask "what can we remove?" on every design — the senior solution is usually smaller, because adding components feels safe while removing them *is* safe.
- **Match the rigor to the stakes.** One-way doors get the heavy review; two-way doors get a glance. Uniform ceremony is its own anti-pattern. The same trade-off reasoning that designs the system also calibrates how you review it.
- **The meta-lesson of all forty posts:** senior system design is disciplined trade-off reasoning under real constraints. Name what you optimize. Name what you pay. Design for change and for failure, because both are certain. There is no architecture without a trade-off; there is only the trade-off you made on purpose and the one that made itself while you weren't looking. The senior's whole job is to make them on purpose.
