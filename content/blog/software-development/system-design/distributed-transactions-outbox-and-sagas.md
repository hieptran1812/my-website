There is a moment in almost every service decomposition where someone draws a box around the orders database, a second box around payments, a third around inventory, and then — without quite noticing they have done it — writes the words "and then we charge the card and decrement stock" on a whiteboard as if those two writes were a single atomic step. They are not. The instant you split the data into two stores, the `BEGIN ... COMMIT` that used to make "create the order and decrement the stock" all-or-nothing is gone, and nothing has replaced it. A senior notices this the way a structural engineer notices a load-bearing wall someone has penciled a doorway through: not because anything is wrong *yet*, but because the failure is now scheduled. It will arrive the first time a process crashes in the half-second between the two writes, and at production scale that half-second comes up many times a day.

This post is about the gap between "I want this to be atomic" and "I have two databases that cannot share a transaction." That gap has a name — the **dual-write problem** — and it has a small family of well-understood solutions, each of which trades something real for something else real. Figure 1 shows the whole stakes in one picture: a naive dual write that commits the order row and then crashes before publishing the event, silently dropping it, versus the transactional outbox that writes the event in the *same* local transaction so it cannot be lost. The rest of the post is the reasoning behind that picture and the patterns that grow out of it.

![Before and after comparison of a naive dual write that commits an order row and crashes before publishing the event versus a transactional outbox that writes the event in the same local transaction](/imgs/blogs/distributed-transactions-outbox-and-sagas-1.webp)

By the end you should be able to do five concrete things. First, recognize a dual write on sight and name the exact failure window it opens. Second, explain in a design review why two-phase commit is mostly avoided at scale — and the narrow cases where it is still the right call. Third, design a transactional outbox plus a relay (or its log-based cousin, change data capture) and defend the crash-safety argument step by step. Fourth, decompose a multi-service workflow into a saga with compensating actions, choosing orchestration or choreography deliberately and handling the lack of isolation that sagas inherently have. Fifth — and this is the senior move that the others serve — design your *service boundaries* so that most transactions stay local and you never need any of this machinery in the first place. The mechanism deep-dives for the [saga pattern](/blog/software-development/database/saga-pattern-distributed-transactions), the [outbox](/blog/software-development/message-queue/transactional-outbox-pattern-reliable-publishing), and [change data capture](/blog/software-development/database/change-data-capture-and-the-outbox-pattern) live in the database and message-queue folders; this post is the architect's decision layer that sits on top of them and tells you *which* to reach for and *when*.

## 1. Why "just use a transaction" stops working

Inside a single relational database, atomicity is nearly free and you have probably stopped thinking about how remarkable it is. You wrap two statements in `BEGIN` and `COMMIT`, and the database guarantees that either both land or neither does, even if the machine loses power between them. It can promise this because both writes go through one write-ahead log on one storage engine under one transaction manager; the database controls every byte of the operation and can replay or roll back from its log on recovery. The atomicity is local because the *authority* is local — one component decides the outcome and persists that decision durably before acknowledging.

The moment two pieces of state live in two different stores, that single authority is gone. "Charge the card" is a write to the payment service's database (or a third-party API), and "decrement stock" is a write to the inventory service's database. There is no shared log, no shared transaction manager, no single component that can guarantee both-or-neither. Each store can commit or fail independently, and — this is the part that bites — the *network between them* can fail independently of both. You can successfully charge the card, and then the call to decrement stock can time out, and now you are holding money for an order whose inventory you never reserved, with no automatic way to undo the charge. The relational `COMMIT` had a partner — `ROLLBACK` — that fired automatically on any failure. Across services, there is no automatic rollback. There is only whatever undo logic you remembered to write.

It is worth being precise about *what* you lose, because "we don't have transactions anymore" is too vague to design against. A single-database transaction gives you the four ACID properties, and across a service boundary each one degrades differently. **Atomicity** is the one everyone notices: both-or-neither is gone, replaced by partial completion that you must detect and reconcile. **Consistency** in the ACID sense (invariants hold at commit) becomes *eventual* — there is a window where the order exists but the stock has not yet been decremented, and any reader in that window sees an inconsistent world. **Isolation** is the quiet one that surprises people: a single transaction is invisible to others until it commits, but a multi-step cross-service workflow is *visible at every intermediate step* — another transaction can read the order *after* it is created but *before* it is paid for, something a database would never let happen. **Durability** is the one you usually keep, because each individual local commit is still durable; it is the only ACID property that survives the boundary intact. The whole game of distributed transactions is recovering acceptable versions of atomicity, consistency, and isolation without the single authority that used to provide them for free. The consistency-model vocabulary for reasoning about that eventual window is worked out in the [consistency models guide](/blog/software-development/system-design/consistency-models-a-practical-guide-for-architects); here we focus on the write-side patterns that produce it.

There is a second, subtler thing you lose, and it is the one that turns a clean design into a debugging nightmare: you lose the ability to reason about the system as if operations are *instantaneous*. Inside a transaction, the world flips from "before" to "after" at the commit instant, and no observer ever sees a half-state. Across services, every multi-step operation has a *duration*, and during that duration the system is in a state that is neither the old consistent world nor the new one — the order exists but is unpaid, the payment is captured but the inventory is unreserved. Every one of these intermediate states is a state some reader can observe, some retry can land in, and some crash can freeze. The bugs that come out of distributed transactions are almost all "what happens if we crash *right here*, in the middle?" — and there are now many "right here" points where before there was exactly one (the single commit). The discipline this demands is to enumerate the crash points explicitly and decide what each one leaves behind, which is exactly what the next section does for the simplest possible case.

The senior instinct, before reaching for any pattern, is to ask whether the boundary itself is the mistake. If "create order" and "decrement stock" genuinely must be atomic and you find yourself fighting the network to make them so, perhaps order and inventory should not have been split into separate stores in the first place — or perhaps the *invariant* you are trying to protect ("never oversell") can be enforced in a different way that does not require the two writes to be simultaneous. We will return to this, because it is the most powerful move available and the one juniors skip straight past on their way to building a saga. But first, the bug itself.

## 2. The dual-write problem, exactly

Strip the scenario to its smallest form. A service needs to do two things as a unit: change its own state (write a row to its database) and tell the outside world (publish an event, or call another service). The canonical example: an order service inserts an `orders` row and then publishes an `OrderCreated` event so that the inventory and shipping services can react. Two operations, two systems — the database and the message broker — and no transaction spans both. This is the **dual write**, and it is one of the most common and most under-appreciated bugs in service-oriented systems.

Walk the four orderings and watch three of them go wrong. **Ordering A — write DB, then publish, both succeed:** the happy path, and the reason the bug hides. In a test, in a demo, under light load, this is what happens, so the code ships. **Ordering B — write DB succeeds, then the process crashes before publishing:** the order row exists, but `OrderCreated` was never sent. Inventory never reserves stock, shipping never hears about it, and the order sits in the database in a limbo no downstream system knows about. The event is *lost*, silently, with no error anywhere — the database commit succeeded, after all. **Ordering C — publish first, then the DB write fails:** now the event went out claiming an order was created, downstream systems reserve stock and start a fulfillment, and then the order row never persisted, so it does not actually exist. The event is a *phantom*; downstream acted on something that is not real. **Ordering D — write DB, publish, but the publish is at-least-once and the broker redelivers:** the event goes out *twice*, and unless consumers are idempotent, stock gets decremented twice. The event is *duplicated*.

So a dual write can lose an event, emit a phantom event, or duplicate an event, depending on exactly where the crash lands. Notice that *no ordering of the two operations fixes this*. Putting the publish before the commit trades "lost event" for "phantom event"; putting it after trades "phantom" for "lost." There is no third position. The two operations cannot be made atomic by reordering them, because the atomicity problem is not about order — it is about the absence of a transaction spanning two systems. Figure 1 is exactly Ordering B: the most common one in practice, because the natural code shape is "save the entity, then publish about it," and the crash window sits right between those two lines.

The failure window is small but it is not zero, and "small but not zero" multiplied by a high request rate is a steady drip of corruption. If the gap between commit and publish is, say, 5 milliseconds, and the service handles 2,000 orders per second, and the process crashes or is killed (a deploy, an OOM, a node eviction) a few times a day, then a few times a day you are inside that 5ms window for some order, and that order's event vanishes. You will not see it in the logs as an error. You will see it weeks later as a support ticket: "I was charged but my order never shipped." The dual write is the bug that does not announce itself; it accrues quietly and surfaces as a reconciliation discrepancy or an angry customer. A senior treats *any* "save then publish" or "save then call another service" as a dual write that needs the outbox until proven otherwise.

## 3. Two-phase commit, and why scale mostly avoids it

The textbook answer to "make two databases commit atomically" is **two-phase commit (2PC)**, and it is worth understanding precisely both because it genuinely solves the atomicity problem and because understanding it tells you why large systems mostly refuse to use it. 2PC introduces a **coordinator** that drives a transaction across multiple **participants** (the order DB, the payment DB, the inventory DB) in two rounds. In the **prepare** phase, the coordinator asks every participant "can you commit this? promise me you will be able to." Each participant does the work up to the point of commit, writes it durably to a prepared state, takes the necessary locks, and votes *yes* or *no* — and a *yes* vote is a binding promise: having voted yes, the participant *must* be able to commit if told to, so it holds its locks and waits. In the **commit** phase, if every participant voted yes, the coordinator tells all of them to commit; if any voted no, it tells all of them to abort. Figure 3 shows the shape and, crucially, where it breaks.

![Graph of two-phase commit showing a coordinator sending prepare to three participant databases that lock and wait, with a coordinator crash leaving the participants blocked holding locks](/imgs/blogs/distributed-transactions-outbox-and-sagas-3.webp)

The protocol is correct: it really does give you atomic commit across multiple stores. The problem is what it costs, and the costs are why "use 2PC" is the wrong answer at scale far more often than it is the right one. There are four, and a senior can recite them in a design review.

**The coordinator is a single point of failure, and worse, a single point of *blocking*.** Look at the dangerous window in figure 3: a participant has voted *yes* (so it is holding locks and cannot unilaterally decide) and then the coordinator crashes *before* sending the commit-or-abort decision. The participant is now stuck. It cannot commit (the coordinator might have decided abort), and it cannot abort (the coordinator might have decided commit, and other participants might already have committed). It must *block* — hold its locks, refuse new work that conflicts — until the coordinator comes back and tells it the decision. This is the famous **blocking** property of 2PC: a coordinator failure at the wrong moment freezes participants indefinitely, holding locks that block other transactions, until recovery. In a system where the coordinator and its log are themselves highly available you can shrink this window, but you cannot eliminate the fundamental fact that participants surrender their autonomy the moment they vote yes.

**It holds locks for the duration of a network round trip — or several.** A local transaction holds its locks for microseconds. A 2PC transaction holds locks across the prepare round trip, the coordinator's decision, and the commit round trip — milliseconds at best, and far longer if any participant is slow or the coordinator hiccups. Those held locks are contention: every other transaction that wants the same rows waits. As throughput rises, the locks held across network round trips become the bottleneck, and 2PC's throughput collapses under contention exactly when you need it most. **It adds latency** — every distributed transaction is now at least two network round trips to every participant, so your p99 is the slowest participant's response time, twice. And **it does not scale horizontally**: the coordinator is a centralized chokepoint, the lock-holding limits concurrency, and adding participants only makes the slowest-participant problem worse. This is the same coordination cost that the [consensus and coordination](/blog/software-development/system-design/consensus-and-coordination-in-distributed-systems) deep-dive analyzes from the agreement angle; 2PC is a coordination protocol and pays coordination's price.

So where *is* 2PC still used? In narrow, honest cases. Inside a single organization's tightly-controlled infrastructure where all participants are databases under one administrative domain and latency is low — for example, distributed SQL engines and some message-broker-to-database bridges use 2PC-style protocols internally, but they pair it with a *replicated, consensus-backed coordinator* (so the coordinator is not a SPOF) and they accept the latency because correctness is non-negotiable for their use case. The Kafka transaction protocol and the XA standard are 2PC-family mechanisms used precisely this way. The pattern is: 2PC is acceptable when you can make the coordinator highly available, the participants are few and fast and co-located, and the transaction is rare relative to the workload. It is unacceptable when participants are independent services with their own availability requirements, when latency matters, or when one participant is a third party you do not control (a payment gateway will never vote in *your* prepare phase). For the order-payment-inventory workflow that motivates this post, 2PC is the wrong tool, and the right tools are the outbox and the saga.

## 4. The transactional outbox: making one local write atomic with a publish

The outbox pattern dissolves the dual-write problem with a move so simple it is almost disappointing: **stop trying to write to two systems atomically, and write to one system twice.** Instead of "commit the order row, then publish to Kafka," you "commit the order row *and* an outbox row in the *same* local transaction, then let a separate relay read the outbox and publish to Kafka." The state change and the event are now both rows in the *same* database, so the local `COMMIT` makes them atomic for free — the very atomicity you could not get across the database/broker boundary, you *do* get within the single database, because that boundary no longer runs through your atomic operation. Figure 2 shows the flow.

![Pipeline showing an order service writing the order and outbox rows in one local transaction, a relay polling unsent rows, and publishing them at-least-once to Kafka](/imgs/blogs/distributed-transactions-outbox-and-sagas-2.webp)

The crash-safety argument is the whole point, so walk it explicitly. Suppose the process crashes at the worst possible moment. **If it crashes before the local commit:** neither the order row nor the outbox row persisted — the transaction rolled back, no event will be published, and no order exists. Consistent. **If it crashes after the local commit but before the relay publishes:** both the order row *and* the outbox row are durably committed; the event is *not yet* published, but it is *sitting safely in the outbox table* waiting. When the service (or the relay) recovers, the relay reads the still-unpublished outbox row and publishes it. The event is delayed, not lost. **If it crashes after the relay publishes but before the relay marks the row sent:** the event went out, but the outbox row still says "unsent," so on recovery the relay publishes it *again* — a duplicate. And duplicates are fine, because the publish is **at-least-once** and consumers are **idempotent** (section 7). There is no ordering of the crash that loses the event, because the event's durability is tied to the same commit as the state change. That is the entire pattern: the outbox converts an impossible cross-system atomic write into a trivial same-system atomic write, and pushes the residual problem (duplicates) onto the consumer, where it is cheap to solve.

Here is the outbox table and the write that fills it. The key is that the `INSERT` into `outbox` is in the same transaction as the business write.

```sql
-- The outbox lives in the SAME database as the business tables.
CREATE TABLE outbox (
    id           BIGSERIAL PRIMARY KEY,
    aggregate    TEXT        NOT NULL,   -- e.g. 'order'
    event_type   TEXT        NOT NULL,   -- e.g. 'OrderCreated'
    payload      JSONB       NOT NULL,   -- the event body
    created_at   TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL DEFAULT now(),
    published_at TIMESTAMPTZ              -- NULL until the relay publishes it
);
CREATE INDEX outbox_unpublished ON outbox (id) WHERE published_at IS NULL;
```

```python
# The application write: business row + outbox row in ONE local transaction.
def create_order(conn, order):
    with conn.transaction():                      # BEGIN
        conn.execute(
            "INSERT INTO orders (id, customer_id, amount_cents, status) "
            "VALUES (%s, %s, %s, 'created')",
            (order.id, order.customer_id, order.amount_cents),
        )
        conn.execute(
            "INSERT INTO outbox (aggregate, event_type, payload) "
            "VALUES ('order', 'OrderCreated', %s)",
            (json.dumps({"order_id": order.id,
                         "amount_cents": order.amount_cents}),),
        )
    # COMMIT here: order row and outbox row are now atomic. No dual write.
```

And here is the relay — a separate loop (its own process, or a leader-elected worker) that drains the outbox and publishes. Note it marks rows published *after* a successful broker ack, which is what makes the residual failure a duplicate rather than a loss.

```python
# The relay: poll unpublished rows, publish, then mark published.
def relay_loop(conn, producer):
    while True:
        rows = conn.execute(
            "SELECT id, event_type, payload FROM outbox "
            "WHERE published_at IS NULL ORDER BY id LIMIT 100 "
            "FOR UPDATE SKIP LOCKED"            # lets multiple relays share work
        ).fetchall()
        for row in rows:
            # Publish with a stable key so the consumer can dedup (section 7).
            producer.send(topic="orders",
                          key=str(row.id),       # outbox id == dedup id
                          value=row.payload)
            producer.flush()                     # block until broker acks
            conn.execute(
                "UPDATE outbox SET published_at = now() WHERE id = %s",
                (row.id,),
            )
        if not rows:
            time.sleep(0.2)                       # idle poll interval
```

Two implementation notes a senior insists on. First, the relay must publish in `id` order *per aggregate* if downstream ordering matters — an `OrderCreated` must reach the broker before `OrderShipped` for the same order; partitioning the outbox poll by aggregate id preserves this. Second, `FOR UPDATE SKIP LOCKED` lets you run several relay instances that divide the outbox between them without double-publishing, which is how you scale the relay's throughput. The poll-based relay adds latency (the poll interval — 200ms above) between the commit and the publish; for most event-driven workflows that is invisible, but when it is not, the log-based variant in the next section removes it.

A third note that catches teams in production: the relay must not let a single poisoned outbox row wedge the whole pipeline. If row 4,001 has a payload the broker rejects (too large, malformed after a bad migration), a naive relay that processes strictly in `id` order will retry that one row forever and never advance, silently halting *all* event publishing behind it — a far worse outage than the original dual write, because now *every* downstream is starved, not just one event. The fix is the same shape as a dead-letter queue: after N failed publish attempts on a row, move it to a `outbox_dead` table (or set a `failed_at` column the poll query skips), alert an operator, and keep advancing. The relay's availability must not be hostage to one bad row. This is the recurring senior instinct — the mechanism that protects you (ordered, durable replay) becomes a liability the moment a single item cannot make progress, so you build the escape hatch *before* you need it, not during the incident. The same poison-row discipline applies to the inbox consumer in section 9 and to every saga step: bounded retries, then a dead-letter and an alert, never an unbounded retry that wedges the pipeline.

## 5. Change data capture: the log-based outbox

Polling an outbox table works and is dead simple, but it has two mild costs: the poll interval adds latency, and the poll itself is load on your database. **Change data capture (CDC)** removes both by reading the database's *replication log* directly instead of polling a table. Every transactional database already writes a durable, ordered log of every committed change — Postgres's write-ahead log, MySQL's binlog — for its own crash recovery and replication. CDC tooling (Debezium is the common one) connects as a replication consumer, reads that log as a stream, and turns each committed row change into an event. Because the log only contains *committed* changes in commit order, CDC inherits the database's atomicity and ordering for free: an event appears in the CDC stream if and only if its transaction committed, and in exactly the order it committed.

There are two ways to use CDC, and the distinction matters. The naive way is to point CDC at your *business* tables and treat every row change as an event — when an `orders` row appears, emit `OrderCreated`. This is tempting because it needs no outbox table at all, but it leaks your internal schema into your event contract: rename a column and you break every consumer, and the events carry whatever the table happens to hold rather than a clean, versioned domain event. The disciplined way — the **outbox plus CDC** combination, which is what most mature shops run — is to keep the outbox table from section 4 but point CDC at *the outbox table* instead of polling it. You get the clean, explicitly-authored event contract of the outbox *and* the low-latency, no-extra-load delivery of CDC. The relay disappears entirely; Debezium is the relay, and it reads the log rather than the table. The [change data capture and outbox deep-dive](/blog/software-development/database/change-data-capture-and-the-outbox-pattern) works through the log-reading mechanics; the architect's takeaway is that CDC is the *same* outbox pattern with the polling replaced by log-tailing, trading a piece of operational machinery (a Debezium connector) for lower latency and less database load.

The trade is real and worth naming. Polling relay: trivial to operate (it is your code, your deploy, your database), but it polls (load) and has poll-interval latency. CDC relay: near-zero latency and no polling load, but you now run and operate a CDC pipeline (connectors, offset management, schema handling, its own failure modes) and you are coupled to your database's replication-log format. For a team already running Kafka and Debezium, CDC-on-the-outbox is the better default. For a team with one service and a poll loop that works, the polling relay is perfectly senior — do not add a CDC pipeline to save 200ms you do not need. This is the recurring theme: the fancier mechanism is not automatically the right one.

#### Worked example: sizing the outbox relay at 2,000 orders/second

Put numbers on the relay so the design is concrete, not hand-wavy. A checkout service commits 2,000 orders per second at peak, each producing one `OrderCreated` outbox row of roughly 500 bytes. The polling relay batches 100 rows per query and runs the query as often as it can keep up. **Throughput:** 2,000 rows/second means the relay must publish 2,000 events/second; at 100-row batches that is 20 batches/second, one every 50ms, comfortably faster than the 200ms idle poll (the idle poll only matters when there is *no* work). A single Kafka producer sustains tens of thousands of small messages per second, so one relay instance handles this with headroom; you would add a second relay (via `SKIP LOCKED`) for redundancy, not throughput. **Latency:** end-to-end commit-to-broker latency is dominated by the poll interval when the relay is idle and by the batch processing time when it is busy — call it p50 around 25ms and p99 under 250ms for the polling relay, versus single-digit-millisecond p99 for CDC. **Outbox table growth:** at 2,000 rows/second, the table grows by 7.2 million rows/hour; you must *delete published rows* on a schedule (e.g. an hourly job removing rows where `published_at` is older than a day) or the table and its index bloat and the poll slows down. **The win to measure:** before the outbox, the dual-write bug dropped some fraction of events in the commit-to-publish window — even at a conservative one lost event per ten thousand, that is 0.2 lost orders/second, or roughly 17,000 silently-dropped order events per day, each a potential "charged but never shipped" ticket. The outbox drops that to zero lost events (duplicates only, which consumers dedup). The cost is one table, one relay process, and an hourly cleanup job. That is the trade a senior takes without a second thought: a little operational machinery to convert a steady drip of silent data loss into zero loss plus harmless duplicates.

## 6. Sagas: a sequence of local transactions with compensations

The outbox makes a *single* service's write-and-publish reliable. But the order-payment-inventory workflow is not one write — it is a *sequence* of writes across *three* services, and you need the whole sequence to either complete or cleanly undo. This is what a **saga** is: a multi-step business transaction implemented as a sequence of **local** transactions, one per service, where each step has a **compensating action** that semantically undoes it if a later step fails. There is no global lock, no coordinator holding everyone hostage; each service commits its own local transaction independently and immediately, and if the chain fails partway, the saga runs the compensations *in reverse* to walk the system back to a consistent state. Figure 4 shows the order saga and its compensations.

![Graph of an orchestrated saga where an orchestrator drives create order then charge payment then reserve stock, and a failed inventory step triggers a refund and an order cancellation as compensations](/imgs/blogs/distributed-transactions-outbox-and-sagas-4.webp)

The forward path of the order saga: **(1)** create the order (status `pending`), **(2)** charge the payment, **(3)** reserve inventory, **(4)** mark the order `confirmed`. Each is a local transaction in its own service, committed independently. Now suppose step 3 fails — the item just sold out. There is no transaction to roll back, because steps 1 and 2 already *committed* in their own databases; the money is really charged and the order really exists. So the saga runs **compensations** in reverse: compensate step 2 by *refunding* the payment, compensate step 1 by *cancelling* the order. The end state — order cancelled, payment refunded, no stock reserved — is consistent, reached not by rolling back uncommitted work (there is none) but by applying *new* committed transactions that semantically undo the old ones.

This is the crucial mental shift, and it is why a saga is not "distributed rollback." A compensation is not a rollback; it is a **new forward transaction that produces the inverse business effect**. You cannot un-charge a card by erasing the charge — the charge committed, it is in the ledger, the customer's statement may already show it. You compensate by issuing a *refund*, which is its own committed transaction that the ledger records alongside the charge. The saga's consistency is the consistency of double-entry bookkeeping: you do not delete mistakes, you post correcting entries. This means every compensation must be designed as a real, auditable business operation — a refund, a cancellation, a stock release — and not as a magic undo, because there is no magic undo across committed transactions in separate databases.

Two properties make compensations actually work in practice, and a saga that ignores them is fragile. First, **every step and every compensation must be idempotent**, because the saga executor will retry them — a refund command might be delivered twice, and refunding twice is a second, unintended payout. The [idempotency and exactly-once](/blog/software-development/system-design/idempotency-and-exactly-once-by-design) deep-dive is the precondition for safe sagas; you make each step carry a stable id so a retried command is recognized and skipped. Second, **compensations must be commutative-enough or ordered carefully**, because a compensation might arrive before the thing it compensates has fully settled. The classic trap: the saga charges the card (step 2), the reserve-stock command (step 3) fails fast, and the *refund* compensation races the *charge confirmation* — if the refund tries to refund a charge that has not yet appeared as settled, it must either wait (semantic lock) or be safe to apply against a not-yet-settled charge. Real saga implementations handle this with **semantic locks** (a status field marking the order `pending` so other transactions know not to act on it yet) and by making compensations tolerant of being applied in either order relative to a slow confirmation. The [saga pattern deep-dive](/blog/software-development/database/saga-pattern-distributed-transactions) works the state-machine mechanics; here the architect's point is that idempotency and a `pending`-style status field are not optional extras — they are what make a saga correct.

## 7. The isolation problem, and how sagas live without it

The quiet, dangerous truth about sagas is the one juniors miss: **sagas give up isolation.** In a database transaction, no other transaction can see your intermediate state — the order does not exist to anyone else until you commit the whole thing. In a saga, every step commits *immediately and independently*, which means the intermediate state is *visible to the whole system*. After step 1 commits, the order exists and any other process can read it — even though the payment has not happened and inventory is not reserved. This is the **lack of isolation**, and it produces anomalies that a single-database transaction would have prevented: a *dirty read* (another transaction reads the order before the saga finishes, then the saga compensates and the order vanishes from under it), or a *lost update* (two sagas touch the same account concurrently and one stomps the other). These are the same anomalies the [isolation levels](/blog/software-development/database/isolation-levels-and-the-anomalies-they-prevent) work catalogs — except here you do not have a database isolation level to lean on, so you must reconstruct the protection by hand.

There are three techniques, and a senior reaches for them in this order. **Semantic locks** are the most common: add a status field that marks an entity "in-saga" so other operations know to treat it specially. The order sits in `pending` while the saga runs; other transactions that read a `pending` order know it is not yet real and refuse to act on it (a query for "confirmed orders" simply excludes it), and the field flips to `confirmed` or `cancelled` only at the saga's end. The status field *is* the semantic lock — it is application-level isolation that other readers respect by convention. This is why the order saga has a status field and why every read path must be written to honor it. **Commutative updates** are the second technique: design the operations so that order does not matter, which sidesteps the lost-update anomaly. A balance modeled as "credit \$50" / "debit \$30" (relative deltas) is commutative — applying them in any order yields the same balance — whereas "set balance to \$70" (absolute) is not, because a concurrent saga's set would clobber it. Modeling money movements as commutative deltas means two concurrent sagas touching the same account do not lose each other's updates. **Reread / versioning** is the third: a saga rereads an entity (or checks a version number) right before acting, so it notices if another saga changed it underneath and can re-plan. These three — semantic locks, commutative updates, and versioned rereads — are the saga's substitute for the isolation a transaction would have given for free, and choosing among them is part of designing the saga, not an afterthought.

The honest summary is that a saga buys you availability and avoids the blocking and coordination cost of 2PC, and it *pays* in lost isolation that you must claw back with application-level discipline. That is a real trade, and it is acceptable for a huge class of business workflows precisely because business workflows already tolerate intermediate states ("your order is being processed") that map naturally onto saga status fields. It is *not* acceptable where you genuinely need serializable isolation across services — and the right response there is usually to redraw the boundary so the operation is one local transaction, not to bolt serializability onto a saga.

## 8. Orchestration versus choreography

A saga's steps and compensations have to be *coordinated* somehow, and there are two styles, with a sharp trade between them. Figure 6 contrasts them.

![Before and after comparison of choreography where services react to each other's events with no central view versus orchestration where one orchestrator commands each step with an explicit state machine](/imgs/blogs/distributed-transactions-outbox-and-sagas-6.webp)

**Choreography** has no central controller. Each service listens for events and reacts: the order service emits `OrderCreated`; the payment service hears it, charges, and emits `PaymentCharged`; the inventory service hears *that*, reserves stock, and emits `StockReserved`; and compensations flow the same way in reverse (a `StockReservationFailed` event triggers the payment service to refund). It is elegantly decentralized — services are loosely coupled, each only knows about events, and there is no orchestrator to be a bottleneck or single point of failure. The cost is that *the saga as a whole exists nowhere*. There is no single place that knows "we are on step 3 of the order saga"; the workflow is an emergent property of who-reacts-to-what, scattered across services. This makes choreographed sagas genuinely hard to understand, trace, and debug — to know why an order is stuck, you trace events across N services and reconstruct the flow in your head. Worse, the implicit event chains can form **cycles** (service A's event triggers B, whose event triggers A) that nobody designed and nobody can see, and adding a step means touching multiple services that each need to learn a new event. Choreography is lovely for short, stable, 2–3-step flows and becomes a liability as the workflow grows.

**Orchestration** has a central **orchestrator** (often itself a service, sometimes a durable-workflow engine like Temporal or AWS Step Functions) that holds the saga's state machine explicitly and *commands* each step: it tells payment "charge," waits for the result, tells inventory "reserve," and on a failure issues the compensations in reverse. The saga lives in *one place* — the orchestrator's state — so it is observable ("this saga is on step 3, waiting for inventory"), traceable, and easy to change (add a step in one place). The cost is that the orchestrator is a component you build, deploy, and make highly available, and it is a (mild) coupling point — every service the saga touches is a step the orchestrator knows about. For anything beyond a trivial flow, the senior default is orchestration, because the operational benefit of a single observable state machine dominates: when an order is stuck at 3am, "look at the orchestrator's saga state" beats "trace events across six services." Choreography's loose coupling is real but is usually outweighed by orchestration's debuggability once a workflow has more than a couple of steps or any nontrivial compensation logic.

Here is the shape of an orchestrated step plus its compensation, the unit you assemble a saga from. Note the idempotency key threaded through every command and the explicit compensation method.

```python
# A saga step: a forward action plus its compensation, both idempotent.
class ChargePaymentStep:
    name = "charge_payment"

    def execute(self, ctx):
        # Idempotency key derived from the saga id so retries dedup.
        key = f"saga:{ctx.saga_id}:charge"
        return payment_client.charge(
            customer_id=ctx.customer_id,
            amount_cents=ctx.amount_cents,
            idempotency_key=key,            # retried command -> same charge
        )

    def compensate(self, ctx):
        # Compensation is a NEW forward transaction: a refund, not an undo.
        key = f"saga:{ctx.saga_id}:refund"
        return payment_client.refund(
            charge_id=ctx.charge_id,
            idempotency_key=key,            # retried refund -> single refund
        )
```

```python
# The orchestrator: run steps forward; on failure compensate in reverse.
def run_saga(steps, ctx):
    done = []
    for step in steps:
        try:
            result = step.execute(ctx)      # commits a local txn in its service
            ctx.record(step.name, result)
            done.append(step)
        except StepFailed:
            # Walk back: compensate completed steps in reverse order.
            for completed in reversed(done):
                completed.compensate(ctx)   # idempotent, retried until acked
            ctx.mark("aborted")
            return
    ctx.mark("completed")
```

## 9. Idempotent consumers and the inbox pattern

The outbox publishes **at-least-once**, which means consumers *will* sometimes receive the same event twice — on a relay restart, a broker redelivery, a saga retry. The whole outbox argument depended on duplicates being harmless, and they are only harmless if the consumer is **idempotent**. The clean way to make a consumer idempotent is the **inbox pattern**: the mirror image of the outbox. Before applying an event's effect, the consumer records the event's id in an `inbox` table *in the same local transaction as the effect*; if the id is already there, it is a duplicate, so the consumer skips the effect and just acks. Figure 7 shows the flow.

![Pipeline showing a redelivered event hitting an inbox claim that records the message id, applying the effect only on first sight in the same transaction and skipping a duplicate](/imgs/blogs/distributed-transactions-outbox-and-sagas-7.webp)

The mechanism is an atomic claim. The consumer attempts to `INSERT` the message id into the inbox; if the insert succeeds (first time seen), it proceeds to apply the effect in the *same* transaction and commits both together; if the insert hits a unique-constraint violation (already seen), it is a duplicate, so it rolls back, skips the effect, and acks the message. Because the inbox insert and the effect commit atomically, there is no window where the effect happens but the id is not recorded (which would let a redelivery re-apply it) or the id is recorded but the effect did not happen (which would lose it). The stable id must be a **business-meaningful** id — the outbox row id, or an order id, not the broker's redelivery sequence number, which changes on redelivery and would defeat the dedup.

```sql
CREATE TABLE inbox (
    message_id TEXT PRIMARY KEY,           -- the stable dedup id
    processed_at TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL DEFAULT now()
);
```

```python
# Idempotent consumer: claim the id and apply the effect in one transaction.
def handle(conn, event):
    try:
        with conn.transaction():                  # BEGIN
            conn.execute(
                "INSERT INTO inbox (message_id) VALUES (%s)",
                (event.message_id,),               # outbox id == dedup id
            )
            apply_effect(conn, event)              # decrement stock, etc.
        # COMMIT: id recorded AND effect applied atomically.
    except UniqueViolation:
        pass                                       # duplicate: skip, then ack
    broker.ack(event)
```

This consumer-side idempotency is what closes the loop. The outbox guarantees the event is *not lost* (at-least-once); the inbox guarantees the duplicate is *not double-applied* (effectively-once). At-least-once delivery plus an idempotent consumer equals exactly-once *effect*, which is the only kind of exactly-once you can actually have — there is no exactly-once *delivery* across an unreliable network, a point the [idempotency deep-dive](/blog/software-development/system-design/idempotency-and-exactly-once-by-design) and the broader [queues and event streaming](/blog/software-development/system-design/queues-and-event-streaming-for-architects) guide both make at length. The outbox and the inbox are a matched pair: the outbox is idempotent publishing, the inbox is idempotent consuming, and together they make the channel reliable end to end.

## 10. The "exactly-once" reality and making eventual consistency acceptable

Two things a senior says out loud in a design review so nobody is surprised in production. **First, "exactly-once" is a marketing word for "at-least-once plus dedup."** When a vendor's slide says exactly-once, they mean either exactly-once *effect* via deduplication inside a closed system (real, and what the outbox+inbox gives you) or exactly-once *delivery within a boundary they control* (also real, but it evaporates the moment a side effect crosses out of that boundary — your payment API call is not covered). There is no exactly-once delivery across an unreliable network in the general case; the two-generals problem forbids it. So you design for at-least-once everywhere and dedup at every side effect, and you stop chasing a delivery guarantee that does not exist.

**Second, the outbox and saga both produce *eventual* consistency, and the business has to find that acceptable — so you design for that.** There is a window where the order exists but inventory has not yet been decremented, a window where the saga has charged but not yet confirmed. The architecture's job is to make those windows *invisible or tolerable* to the user, and there are concrete techniques. **Status fields** turn the intermediate state into an honest UI state: the order shows "processing" (its `pending` status) rather than either "confirmed" or "error," which is truthful and sets the right expectation. **Read-your-writes** consistency hides the lag for the *acting user*: after a user places an order, route *their* reads to a path that reflects their own just-made change (read from the primary, or read a session-pinned view) so *they* always see their order even while it propagates to everyone else — the [consistency models guide](/blog/software-development/system-design/consistency-models-a-practical-guide-for-architects) details how to provide this. **Convergence guarantees** matter too: you promise not instant consistency but *bounded* eventual consistency — "every consumer reflects the event within N seconds" — and you monitor the lag so you can alert when it exceeds the bound. Eventual consistency is acceptable to a business when the window is short, bounded, monitored, and represented honestly in the UI; it is *unacceptable* when the window is unbounded, invisible, or hides a state the user can act on incorrectly. The senior move is to make the window a first-class, named, observable part of the design rather than an embarrassing secret.

## 11. The most senior move: design boundaries so the transaction stays local

Everything above is the machinery you reach for *when you must* coordinate across services. The most senior move is to arrange your design so you need it as rarely as possible — because every distributed transaction you avoid is a category of failure you do not have to operate. Figure 8 is the decision tree, and the first branch it asks is not "outbox or saga?" but "can you avoid needing either?"

![Decision tree starting from a cross-service consistency need, branching to keep the transaction local by redrawing the boundary, or use an outbox plus saga for a real workflow, with two-phase commit as a last resort](/imgs/blogs/distributed-transactions-outbox-and-sagas-8.webp)

The avoidance move has two forms. The first is **redrawing the service boundary** so that the data that must change together lives in *one* service and therefore *one* local transaction. If "create order" and "reserve inventory" truly must be atomic, perhaps order and inventory are one bounded context, not two, and the split was premature — the [evolutionary architecture](/blog/software-development/system-design/evolutionary-architecture-designing-for-change) lens helps here: services should be split along boundaries that *do not* require cross-service transactions, and a boundary that forces a distributed transaction on a common operation is a smell that the boundary is in the wrong place. A senior treats "this operation needs a distributed transaction" as evidence to *re-examine the decomposition*, not just as a problem to solve with a saga. The second form is **relaxing the invariant** so the two writes do not have to be simultaneous. "Never oversell" *feels* like it requires atomically checking and decrementing stock at order time, but most commerce systems do not enforce it that way — they *accept* the order optimistically, reserve stock asynchronously, and *cancel-and-refund* the rare oversell, because overselling 0.1% of orders and cleanly refunding them is cheaper and far more available than a synchronous cross-service lock on every order. The invariant became "oversell is rare and always reconciled" instead of "oversell is impossible," and that reframing turns a hard distributed transaction into an easy local one plus a compensation. Knowing which invariants can be relaxed — and getting the business to agree the relaxed version is acceptable — is the highest-leverage skill in this whole topic. Most "we need distributed transactions" requirements are actually "we need this invariant to *usually* hold and *always* reconcile," which the outbox and saga handle comfortably and 2PC is overkill for.

## Trade-offs: the decision matrix

Every approach buys one property and pays in another, and a senior names the bill before recommending. Figure 5 visualizes the same comparison the table below makes explicit.

![Matrix comparing two-phase commit, outbox plus relay, saga, and avoiding the need across consistency, availability, complexity, and blast radius](/imgs/blogs/distributed-transactions-outbox-and-sagas-5.webp)

| Approach | Consistency | Availability | Complexity | Latency | Blast radius | When it wins |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **2PC** | Atomic (strong) | Blocks on coordinator/participant failure | High protocol + coordinator HA | 2+ round trips, locks held | All participants freeze together | Few, fast, co-located participants under one admin domain; HA coordinator; rare txns |
| **Outbox + relay** | Eventual | High (async, no blocking) | Low (one table + a relay) | +poll interval (or near-zero with CDC) | One service; failure is delay, not loss | Reliable "change state and publish" — the default for any save-then-publish |
| **Saga** | Eventual; no isolation | High (each step local) | High (steps + compensations + isolation discipline) | Multi-step, async | Per step; compensations bound the damage | Multi-service workflow that tolerates intermediate states |
| **Avoid (one local txn)** | Strong | High | None | One commit | None | When you can redraw the boundary or relax the invariant — always try first |

The senior reading of this table: there is no single best approach, only the right approach for the shape of the problem. If the data that must change together can live in one service, *avoid* — it is free and has the smallest blast radius. If you have a single service that must change state and tell the world, *outbox* — it is cheap, reliable, and the correct default for every dual write. If you have a genuine multi-service workflow that tolerates intermediate states (and almost all business workflows do), *saga* — orchestrated, with idempotent steps and semantic locks. And reach for *2PC* only in the narrow case of a few fast co-located participants under one administrative domain where you can make the coordinator highly available and the transaction is rare. The mistake is reaching past *avoid* and *outbox* straight to a saga because sagas sound sophisticated, or reaching for 2PC because "atomic" sounds safest — atomic across services is the most expensive guarantee in this table and the one you need least often.

## Optimization: where the cost actually is, and how to measure the win

The optimization lens for distributed transactions is not "make the transaction faster" — it is "*need* fewer of them, and make the ones you keep cheap and reliable." Four levers, with numbers.

**1. Eliminate distributed transactions by boundary design — the biggest win by far.** Every cross-service transaction you remove eliminates a whole class of failure, a chunk of latency, and the operational burden of compensations or coordination. Measure this as the *fraction of your operations that touch one service versus many*: a well-decomposed system has the overwhelming majority of operations local, with cross-service workflows being the exception. If a common operation (placing an order, sending a message) requires a distributed transaction, that is the highest-value thing to redesign, because it is on the hot path. The win is measured in availability (one fewer dependency in the critical path means one fewer thing that can take you down) and in p99 latency (a local commit is sub-millisecond; a saga is a multi-hop async workflow).

**2. Make the outbox relay's residual cost small.** The poll-based relay adds the poll interval to event latency and adds poll load to the database; CDC removes both. Measure the commit-to-broker latency (p50/p99) and the relay's database load (queries/second, rows scanned), and if either matters, switch the outbox to CDC — typically dropping p99 from a couple hundred milliseconds to single digits and removing the poll load entirely. Do *not* switch if neither number is a problem; the CDC pipeline has its own operational cost.

**3. Keep compensations rare by ordering saga steps cheapest-and-most-likely-to-fail first.** A saga that fails *late* has more compensations to run, each of which is a network call that can itself fail and must be retried. So order the steps to fail *early* and *cheaply*: validate and reserve the scarce, likely-to-fail resource (inventory) *before* the expensive, hard-to-undo one (the payment), so a failure happens before you have anything costly to compensate. Reserving stock first and charging last means a sold-out item fails the saga with *zero* compensations needed — you never charged the card. Measure the *compensation rate* (compensations run per saga) and the *step failure distribution*; reordering steps so the common failure happens first can drop the compensation rate by an order of magnitude.

**4. Batch and parallelize where the workflow allows.** The relay batches outbox rows (100 at a time above) rather than publishing one per query — a 10× to 100× reduction in database round trips. Saga steps that are genuinely independent (reserve inventory *and* validate the shipping address) can run in parallel rather than sequentially, cutting the saga's wall-clock latency. Measure the saga's end-to-end p99 and look for sequential steps that have no real dependency; parallelizing them is free latency.

#### Worked example: an order saga's latency and failure budget

Make the optimization concrete with the order saga. The forward path is four steps: create order (local, ~5ms), reserve inventory (cross-service call, ~30ms), charge payment (cross-service call to a gateway, ~80ms), confirm order (local, ~5ms). **Sequential latency:** 5 + 30 + 80 + 5 = 120ms p50, and p99 is dominated by the payment gateway's tail, call it ~400ms. **The reordering win:** putting reserve-inventory (30ms, fails ~2% of the time on hot items) *before* charge-payment (80ms, fails ~0.5%) means the common failure (sold out) costs *zero* compensations — you fail at 35ms having charged nobody. If you had charged first, every sold-out order would require a refund compensation: at 2,000 orders/second and a 2% sold-out rate, that is 40 refunds/second of pure compensation traffic, each a gateway call that can fail and retry. Reordering eliminates all 40/second. **The compensation budget:** with the better ordering, only the rare case where inventory succeeds but payment fails (~0.5% of orders that reach payment) needs a compensation — a stock *release*, which is a local inventory operation, cheap and reliable. So at 2,000 orders/second you run on the order of 10 compensations/second, all local stock-releases, instead of 40/second of remote refunds. **The reliability number to track:** the *saga completion rate* (sagas that reach `completed` without manual intervention) should be well above 99.9%; the residual is stuck sagas (a compensation that keeps failing, an orchestrator that lost state) that need an operator, and you size an on-call runbook around that residual rate. The optimization story here is entirely about ordering and locality: reorder so failures are early and cheap, keep compensations local, and the saga's cost collapses from "40 remote refunds a second" to "10 local releases a second."

## Stress test: what breaks at 10×, at a crash, at a duplicate

A design is only as good as its behavior under the failures it was built for, so stress the order-fulfillment design against the three canonical breakages.

**A crash between the DB write and the event publish (the dual write).** This is the failure the outbox exists for, so it must pass. If the checkout service commits the `orders` row and then the process is killed (a deploy, an OOM) before publishing, the outbox row committed *with* the order, so on recovery the relay finds the unpublished row and publishes it. The event is delayed by the recovery time, not lost. Without the outbox, this same crash drops the event silently — the order exists, no downstream system knows. Verdict: pass *with* outbox, silent data loss *without*. This is why "save then publish" is never acceptable as written.

**A saga step fails midway (compensation).** Suppose the saga creates the order and charges payment, then the inventory service is down when the reserve-stock step runs. The orchestrator's step fails, it walks `done` in reverse, and it issues the *refund* compensation and the *cancel-order* compensation. Both are idempotent, so even if the orchestrator itself crashes and re-runs the compensations, the refund happens once and the order is cancelled once. The end state — order cancelled, payment refunded — is consistent. Figure 9 traces exactly this sequence with timestamps: order at T+0, payment at T+40ms, inventory *fails* at T+90ms, refund at T+120ms, order cancelled at T+160ms. The danger to check is the compensation *itself* failing (the refund call times out): the orchestrator must retry it (idempotently) until it succeeds, and if it cannot succeed after a bounded retry, it must surface a stuck-saga alert for an operator rather than silently giving up — a charged-but-not-refunded saga is a money bug that must never be swallowed.

![Timeline of a saga that creates an order, charges payment, fails on inventory, then refunds and cancels the order in reverse to reach a consistent end state](/imgs/blogs/distributed-transactions-outbox-and-sagas-9.webp)

**A duplicate event (at-least-once redelivery).** The relay publishes at-least-once, so the inventory consumer *will* sometimes get `OrderCreated` twice — on a relay restart, a broker redelivery, or a saga retry. Without idempotency, the second delivery decrements stock again, overselling. With the inbox pattern, the second delivery hits the unique-constraint violation on the message id, skips the effect, and acks. Stock is decremented exactly once in *effect* despite the at-least-once delivery. Verdict: pass *with* the inbox, double-decrement *without*. Note the dedup id must be the stable outbox id, not the broker's redelivery sequence — using the wrong id is the subtle way this stress test fails even when an inbox is present.

**And at 10× scale?** Push from 2,000 to 20,000 orders/second. The outbox table grows 10× faster (72M rows/hour), so the cleanup job and the index strategy must keep up, and you likely move from a polling relay to CDC to avoid the poll load on a now-busier database. The relay scales by running more instances with `SKIP LOCKED`. The orchestrator becomes a scaling concern — a single orchestrator instance holding all saga state is a bottleneck, so you partition sagas across orchestrator instances by saga id (or adopt a durable-workflow engine built to shard). The compensation traffic scales linearly, which is why the cheapest-failure-first ordering matters more at scale, not less. The eventual-consistency window does *not* widen if the relay and consumers keep up, but it *does* widen the moment they fall behind, so the lag must be monitored with an alert on the convergence bound. Nothing in the design fundamentally breaks at 10×, but three things need attention: relay throughput (CDC + more instances), orchestrator partitioning, and the consistency-lag alert.

## Case studies

**An order-fulfillment saga (the canonical workflow).** The order-payment-inventory flow this post uses is not hypothetical — it is the shape of nearly every commerce checkout. The mature implementation is an *orchestrated* saga (one observable state machine for "where is this order"), with steps ordered so the scarce, likely-to-fail resource (inventory) is reserved before the expensive, hard-to-undo one (payment), so the common failure (sold out) costs zero compensations. Each step carries a saga-derived idempotency key, the order carries a status field that acts as a semantic lock (`pending` → `confirmed`/`cancelled`), and the UI shows "processing" during the eventual-consistency window. The lesson: the saga's correctness lives in three unglamorous details — idempotent steps, a status field other readers honor, and step ordering that minimizes compensations — not in the orchestration framework you pick.

**A dual-write bug (the silent data loss).** The most common and most under-diagnosed bug in a freshly-decomposed service architecture is the dual write: a service that saves an entity and then publishes an event in two separate operations, losing the event on any crash in between. It does not show up in tests (the happy path always works) or under light load (the window is small). It surfaces weeks later as a reconciliation discrepancy — "we have 14,000 orders this month with no corresponding shipment event" — or as support tickets for orders that were charged but never fulfilled. The post-mortem is always the same: a `repository.save(order)` immediately followed by a `broker.publish(event)`, with no transaction spanning them and a crash window between. The fix is always the same: move the publish into the outbox, written in the same local transaction as the save. The deeper lesson is that the dual write is *invisible* until it has already lost data, so a senior audits for the pattern proactively — grep for "save then publish" — rather than waiting for the discrepancy to surface.

**An outbox rollout (incremental, low-risk).** Adopting the outbox in an existing service that currently dual-writes is satisfyingly incremental, which is part of why it is the right default. You add the outbox table and start writing outbox rows in the same transaction as the business write — but you keep the old direct publish too, *temporarily*, so nothing changes for consumers yet. Then you stand up the relay (or CDC connector) reading the outbox and publishing, and you make consumers idempotent (the inbox) so the now-duplicated events (old direct publish *and* new relay publish) are deduplicated harmlessly. Finally, once the relay is proven, you remove the old direct publish, and the dual write is gone. At no point is there a flag-day cutover, because the idempotent consumer absorbs the overlap. The lesson: the outbox is not just a correct design, it is a *migratable* one — you can roll it into a running system that already has the bug without downtime, precisely because at-least-once-plus-idempotency tolerates the transitional double-publishing.

**The narrow place 2PC survives (distributed SQL).** Distributed SQL databases — the kind analyzed in the broader datastore work — do run 2PC-family protocols internally to commit a transaction that spans multiple shards atomically. The reason it is acceptable there and not for your order saga: the participants are *shards of one database* under one administrative domain, co-located in one data center or region, with fast links; the coordinator is *replicated via consensus* so it is not a single point of failure; and the database accepts the latency because cross-shard atomic commit is its product promise. This is the honest home of 2PC — inside an infrastructure component that controls all participants, makes the coordinator HA, and is built to pay the latency. The lesson for an application architect: you almost never build 2PC yourself across your *services*; when you benefit from 2PC, it is hidden inside a database or a Kafka transaction you are using, where the coordinator-HA and co-location preconditions are met for you.

## When to reach for this (and when not to)

**Reach for the outbox** for *every* "change state and then publish an event" or "change state and then call another service" — which is to say, for every dual write. It is cheap (one table plus a relay, or a CDC connector you may already run), it is the correct default, and the alternative is silent data loss on the next crash. There is no situation where a raw dual write is acceptable in a system that cares about its data; the outbox is the floor, not an optimization.

**Reach for a saga** when you have a genuine multi-service workflow that must complete-or-cleanly-undo and the business tolerates intermediate states (almost all do). Use *orchestration* by default for its observability; reserve *choreography* for short, stable, 2–3-step flows where loose coupling genuinely matters more than a central view. Always make steps idempotent and add a status field as a semantic lock — these are not optional.

**Reach for 2PC** essentially never at the application level. The cases where it is right — few fast co-located participants, one admin domain, an HA coordinator, rare transactions — are cases where it is usually already provided *for* you inside a distributed database or a message-broker transaction. If you find yourself about to *build* 2PC across your own services, stop and re-examine the boundary; you almost certainly want the outbox plus a saga, or a redrawn boundary, instead.

**Reach for avoidance first, always.** Before any of the above, ask whether the data that must change together can live in one service (one local transaction) or whether the invariant can be relaxed to "usually holds, always reconciles." This is the move that removes the problem instead of solving it, and it is the most senior thing on this list. A design review that goes straight to "which saga style?" without first asking "can we avoid needing a saga?" has skipped the most valuable question.

**Do not reach for distributed transactions to paper over a bad decomposition.** If a common, hot-path operation requires coordinating three services, the decomposition is the bug, not the coordination. Fix the boundary. And do not chase "exactly-once delivery" — it does not exist; design for at-least-once plus idempotent dedup and you have the only exactly-once you can actually get.

## Key takeaways

- **The instant data splits across two stores, `COMMIT`/`ROLLBACK` is gone and nothing replaces it.** Any "save then publish" or "save then call a service" is a dual write that loses, duplicates, or phantoms an event on a crash. Treat every one as needing the outbox until proven otherwise.
- **No ordering fixes a dual write.** Publishing before the commit trades lost-event for phantom-event; after trades phantom for lost. The fix is not reordering — it is making one local write atomic with the event via the outbox.
- **2PC is correct but expensive, and its worst property is blocking.** A coordinator crash after the prepare votes freezes participants holding locks. It is acceptable only for few, fast, co-located participants under one admin domain with an HA coordinator — usually hidden inside a database, not built by you.
- **The outbox converts an impossible cross-system atomic write into a trivial same-system one.** Write the state change and the event in one local transaction; a relay (poll-based or CDC) publishes at-least-once. No crash loses the event; the residual is duplicates, which idempotent consumers absorb.
- **CDC is the same outbox with polling replaced by log-tailing.** It trades a piece of operational machinery for near-zero latency and no poll load. Use it when the latency or load matters; do not add a CDC pipeline to save milliseconds you do not need.
- **A saga is local transactions plus compensations, and a compensation is a new forward transaction, not a rollback.** You do not un-charge a card; you refund it. Every step and compensation must be idempotent, and order steps so the cheap, likely-to-fail one runs first to minimize compensations.
- **Sagas give up isolation; you reconstruct it by hand.** Semantic locks (a status field other readers honor), commutative updates (deltas not absolutes), and versioned rereads are the substitutes for the isolation a transaction would have given for free.
- **Prefer orchestration over choreography once a workflow is non-trivial.** A single observable saga state machine beats reconstructing the flow from events scattered across services when an order is stuck at 3am.
- **Exactly-once delivery does not exist; design for at-least-once plus idempotent dedup.** The outbox publishes at-least-once, the inbox dedups on a stable business id, and together they give exactly-once *effect* — the only kind there is.
- **The most senior move is to not need any of this.** Redraw the boundary so the transaction stays local, or relax the invariant to "usually holds, always reconciles." Most "we need a distributed transaction" requirements are really that — and the outbox and a saga handle them while 2PC is overkill.
