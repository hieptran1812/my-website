A chat system looks like the easiest thing in the world to build. Two people, one text box, a send button — what could be hard? You build the v1 in an afternoon: a REST endpoint that writes a row to a table, and the recipient's app polls that endpoint every few seconds to see if anything new arrived. It works in the demo. It works for ten users. Then it goes to production, the user count climbs, and you discover that the cheap, obvious design has quietly committed you to three of the hardest problems in distributed systems at once: holding millions of *live* connections open so a message can arrive in milliseconds instead of seconds, *routing* a single message to the exact server that holds the recipient's connection out of thousands of servers, and doing it all while guaranteeing that messages arrive in the right *order*, exactly once as the user perceives it, whether the recipient is online, offline, asleep with the app killed, or in a 200-person group. The send button is easy. Everything behind it is a staff-level system.

The reason chat is a canonical interview and a canonical real system is that it forces you to confront the persistent-connection problem head-on. A normal web service is request/response: the client asks, the server answers, the connection closes, and the server remembers nothing. Chat is the opposite — the *server* needs to initiate delivery to a client that is just sitting there, which means the connection must stay open, which means your servers are now *stateful* in the one way that hurts most: each one holds a population of live sockets, and a message for one of those users has to find its way back to the one specific server holding that one specific socket. That single requirement — server-initiated delivery over a persistent connection — is the seed from which the entire architecture grows, and figure 1 is the shape it grows into: clients hold long-lived sockets to a connection-gateway tier, a stateless message service does the durable work, and a session registry is the phone book that lets a message find the right socket.

This post is the architect's decision layer for designing a chat system. We will not re-derive how an LSM-tree stores a sorted log or how a message broker guarantees ordering within a partition — those mechanisms live in their own deep-dives and I will cross-link them. Instead we reason about the choices a senior makes and *defends in a design review*: WebSocket versus long-poll and why the persistent connection wins; how to size and shard the connection tier that holds the live sockets; how the session registry routes delivery and what it costs when it is stale; how to get per-conversation ordering and at-least-once delivery with client-side dedup instead of expensive global coordination; why presence (the little green dot) is secretly the most expensive feature in the product; how group fan-out scales; why the storage layer wants a wide-column, write-heavy, time-ordered shape; and what end-to-end encryption does to all of it. By the end you will be able to draw the architecture, run the capacity math, name every failure mode, and explain the trade-offs the way the person who has actually operated one of these would.

## 1. Frame and scope: what "design a chat system" actually means

Before any boxes get drawn, a senior nails down scope, because "chat system" spans a 1:1 SMS replacement and a Slack-style workspace with threads, reactions, search, and bots — wildly different builds. We will design the core that every one of them needs: **reliable, ordered, low-latency delivery of messages between users, online or offline, in 1:1 and group conversations, at a scale of hundreds of millions of users.** We will treat search, rich media transcoding, and bot platforms as out of scope for the deep dive but note where they bolt on.

The functional requirements are deceptively short. A user can send a message to a conversation (1:1 or group). A user receives messages addressed to their conversations in near-real-time when online, and reliably-eventually when offline. Messages within a conversation appear in a consistent order for all participants. The sender sees delivery status (sent, delivered, read). Users see presence (online/last-seen) and typing indicators. That is the whole product surface, and every clause hides a distributed-systems problem.

The **non-functional** requirements are where the design lives, and naming them precisely is the senior move that the [requirements-and-SLOs](/blog/software-development/system-design/turning-vague-asks-into-requirements-and-slos) discipline exists to enforce. Delivery latency for an online recipient should be p99 under a couple hundred milliseconds — chat *feels* broken above that. Messages must be durable: once the sender's app shows a single check (sent), that message must never be lost, even if every server it touched crashes a millisecond later. Ordering must hold per conversation. The system must survive a connection-server crash without dropping messages, survive a region outage with degraded but non-lossy behavior, and absorb the daily diurnal wave plus event spikes (New Year's Eve at midnight, a World Cup goal) without falling over. And it must do all this *cheaply per user*, because chat monetizes thinly — you cannot spend a dollar a month on infrastructure per free user.

The non-negotiable that shapes everything is the combination of **durability plus ordering plus low latency**. Each is easy alone. Durability alone is a database. Low latency alone is a fire-and-forget push. Ordering alone is a sequence counter. Holding all three at once, for an offline-capable, multi-device, group-capable product at nine-figure scale, is the actual job. Keep that triple in mind: every decision below is a negotiation between those three pulling in different directions, with cost as the fourth force.

## 2. Estimation: sizing the connection tier, the write path, and storage

A senior never designs in the abstract; they put numbers on it first, because the numbers decide the architecture. The estimation discipline here is the same [back-of-the-envelope](/blog/software-development/system-design/back-of-the-envelope-estimation-for-system-design) work that turns hand-waving into a concrete fleet. Let us size a WhatsApp-class system.

Assume 2 billion registered users, of whom 500 million are *daily active* and, at peak, 100 million are *concurrently connected* — that is, holding a live socket at the same moment. Concurrent connections is the number that sizes the connection tier, and it is the scary one: 100 million sockets that must stay open, mostly idle, each consuming kernel memory and a file descriptor.

**Connection tier.** A well-tuned connection server — and "well-tuned" is doing heavy lifting, as we will see in section 4 — can hold on the order of 1 million concurrent connections on a single beefy node. WhatsApp famously demonstrated millions per box on FreeBSD years ago; a million per node is a sane modern planning number. So 100 million concurrent connections divided by 1 million per node is **roughly 100 connection-gateway nodes** to hold the live population, plus generous headroom (say 150–200 nodes) for failover, rolling deploys, and uneven distribution. That is a small fleet for a massive user base, which tells you the connection tier should be *thin and cheap* — it holds sockets and shovels frames, nothing more.

**Message write path.** Suppose 500 million daily active users send 40 messages each per day on average — that is 20 billion messages per day. Twenty billion divided by 86,400 seconds is about **230,000 messages/second average**, and real traffic is peaky, so plan for a peak of perhaps **1 million messages/second**. Each message write is small — a few hundred bytes of metadata plus the body — but it is a *durable append* that may then fan out to multiple recipients and devices, so the *delivery* rate is a multiple of the *send* rate. In a chat with an average of, say, 1.5 effective recipients per message (mostly 1:1, some groups), and an average of ~2 devices per user, the live-delivery push rate is several million frames/second at peak. This is why the message service must be stateless and horizontally scalable while the connection tier holds the sockets.

#### Worked example: storage for one year of messages

Let us size durable storage. Twenty billion messages/day, each averaging ~300 bytes of stored payload (sender id, conversation id, sequence number, timestamp, status flags, and a short text body), is 20e9 × 300 bytes = **6 TB/day** of raw message data. Over a year that is ~2.2 PB raw. With a replication factor of 3 for durability, that is **~6.6 PB/year** of physical storage for message bodies and metadata, before compression. Text compresses well (say 3×), pulling it toward ~2.2 PB/year replicated for the text path; media (images, voice notes) dominates total bytes and lives in object storage / a CDN, not the message store. The lesson the math teaches: the *metadata and text* store is a multi-petabyte, write-heavy, append-only, time-ordered system, and that shape — not raw size — is what dictates a wide-column engine. A senior reads "20 billion small ordered appends per day, read back as recent-first ranges per conversation" and immediately knows the access pattern points at an LSM-backed, partition-by-conversation store, which we justify in section 9 and which connects directly to the [storage-engines](/blog/software-development/system-design/storage-engines-btrees-vs-lsm-trees-for-architects) and [choosing-a-datastore](/blog/software-development/system-design/choosing-a-datastore-sql-nosql-newsql) decisions.

**Presence.** Here is the number that surprises people. If 100 million users are online and the naive presence design pushes every status change to every contact, and the average user has, say, 200 contacts, then a single wave of users coming online in the morning can generate *tens of billions* of presence notifications. Presence fan-out is **N users × M contacts**, and at this scale that product is larger than the actual message traffic. This is why presence gets its own optimization section (section 7): done naively it is the single most expensive feature in the entire product, dwarfing the messages it decorates.

The estimation gives us the skeleton: ~100–200 thin connection nodes holding 100M sockets, a stateless message service pushing millions of frames/second at peak, a multi-petabyte wide-column store, a session registry that must answer millions of lookups/second, and a presence subsystem that will eat the fleet alive if you let it fan out naively. Now we design each.

## 3. The API and the protocol: why a persistent connection, not polling

The first architectural fork is the wire protocol, and it determines everything downstream. The recipient needs to learn about a new message *the instant it exists*, which means the **server must initiate**. Plain HTTP request/response cannot do server-initiated delivery — the server can only answer questions the client asked — so the obvious-but-wrong first design is **polling**: the client asks "anything new?" every few seconds. Figure 2 contrasts polling against the persistent socket, and the gap is brutal.

![Before-and-after comparison showing HTTP long-polling generating millions of mostly-empty requests per minute with multi-second delivery lag, versus a single persistent WebSocket that stays idle for free and pushes new messages in under two hundred milliseconds](/imgs/blogs/design-a-chat-and-messaging-system-2.webp)

**Short polling** — ask every 3 seconds — has two fatal costs. First, latency: a message that arrives just after a poll waits up to the full interval, so delivery lag is on the order of seconds, which feels broken. Second, waste: if 100 million clients each poll every 3 seconds, that is ~33 million requests/second of "anything new?", and 99%+ of them return *nothing*. You are paying for a request storm whose entire job is to discover that almost nothing happened. **Long polling** improves the latency — the server holds the request open until something arrives or a timeout fires, so delivery is prompt — but it still pays a request and a full HTTP round-trip (and on mobile, a radio wake) per message cycle, and it ties up a server thread or connection per waiting client without the benefits of a real duplex channel.

The right answer is a **persistent, bidirectional connection**: **WebSocket** over TLS (`wss://`) is the standard choice, with **MQTT** as the alternative that Facebook Messenger famously adopted for its low overhead on mobile, and **gRPC bidirectional streams** as a modern option inside controlled environments. The properties that matter: the connection opens once (one TLS handshake amortized over the whole session), stays open while the app is foregrounded, costs almost nothing while idle, and lets the **server push** a frame the instant a message exists — delivery p99 in the low hundreds of milliseconds. One idle socket replaces a perpetual storm of empty polls. The cost you take on is the one this whole post is about: now your servers hold open connections and are stateful, which is the persistent-connection problem.

The application protocol on top of the socket is a small, framed message envelope. A senior keeps it explicit and versioned. Here is the shape of the core operations.

```protobuf
// Framed envelope over a single WebSocket. Every frame is one of these types.
message Frame {
  enum Type { SEND = 0; ACK = 1; DELIVER = 2; RECEIPT = 3; PRESENCE = 4; TYPING = 5; PING = 6; }
  Type type = 1;
  string conversation_id = 2;
  int64  client_seq      = 3;   // sender-local monotonic counter, for dedup
  string idempotency_key = 4;   // uuid the sender generates ONCE per message
  int64  server_seq      = 5;   // assigned by server, authoritative ordering
  bytes  payload         = 6;   // text or E2EE ciphertext; server may not read it
  int64  ts              = 7;
}
```

The two fields that earn their keep are `idempotency_key` and `client_seq`. The sender generates the idempotency key *once* when the user hits send and reuses it on every retry. This is what makes at-least-once delivery safe end to end — the server and the recipient both dedup on it, the mechanism the [idempotency-and-exactly-once](/blog/software-development/system-design/idempotency-and-exactly-once-by-design) post derives in full. The `client_seq` lets the *sender's own other devices* order and dedup. The `server_seq`, assigned authoritatively on persist, is the per-conversation ordering token everyone sorts by. Get this envelope right and ordering plus dedup mostly fall out of it.

## 4. The connection tier: holding millions of live sockets

The connection gateway is the heart of the persistent-connection problem and the tier that most distinguishes a chat system from a normal web service. Its job is narrow and extreme: **terminate and hold millions of long-lived WebSocket connections, and shovel frames between the socket and the backend.** Everything about its design follows from "hold a million mostly-idle sockets per node, cheaply, and survive losing one."

The first thing a senior insists on is that the connection tier be **as stateless as possible *about message logic*** while necessarily being **stateful about the sockets themselves**. The node owns the TCP/TLS connection — that is irreducibly stateful, you cannot move a live socket to another box — but it should hold *no business state* beyond "which user is on this socket." All durability, sequencing, and fan-out happen in the stateless message service behind it. This split (figure 1) is what keeps the connection tier thin enough to be cheap and disposable enough to crash without data loss.

Holding a million connections per node is an exercise in not wasting per-connection resources. Each idle socket should cost only its kernel socket buffers and a small amount of userspace bookkeeping. This is why the canonical implementations use an **event-driven, non-blocking** runtime rather than a thread-per-connection model — a thread per connection at a million connections is a million threads, which is absurd. WhatsApp built theirs on **Erlang/OTP**, whose lightweight processes (millions per node, cheap mailboxes, supervision trees) map almost perfectly onto "millions of independent, mostly-idle, occasionally-messaged connections" — few runtimes are more purpose-built for this shape. Discord built theirs in **Elixir** (also on the Erlang BEAM VM) for the same reason. Others use Go (goroutines), C++ with epoll, or Rust with tokio. The runtime differs; the principle does not: **one cheap unit of concurrency per connection, event-driven, no blocking threads.**

```python
# Sketch of a connection-gateway handler. Real ones are in Erlang/Elixir/Go;
# this is the control flow, not production code.
async def handle_socket(ws, user_id, device_id):
    # On connect: register WHERE this user is, so messages can be routed here.
    await session_registry.set(user_id, device_id, node=THIS_NODE, ttl=30)
    await subscribe_local(user_id)  # local in-process mailbox for this user

    try:
        # Refresh the registry lease while the socket is alive (heartbeat).
        async for frame in ws:                 # event-driven: only wakes on a frame
            if frame.type == "PING":
                await session_registry.touch(user_id, device_id, ttl=30)
            elif frame.type == "SEND":
                # Connection tier does NO durability; hand to stateless message svc.
                await message_service.submit(frame)   # returns fast; svc persists+fans out
                await ws.send(ack(frame.idempotency_key))
    finally:
        # On disconnect: drop the registration so routing knows we're gone.
        await session_registry.remove(user_id, device_id, node=THIS_NODE)
```

Three production details a senior will not skip. **Heartbeats**: a TCP connection can be silently dead (the client lost signal in a tunnel) and the server will not know for a long time; an application-level ping/pong every 20–30 seconds detects dead sockets fast and doubles as the lease that keeps the session registry entry fresh. **Backpressure**: if a recipient's socket is slow (bad mobile network) and messages are arriving faster than it drains, the per-connection send buffer grows; you must cap it and apply [backpressure](/blog/software-development/system-design/rate-limiting-and-backpressure), spilling overflow to the durable store rather than letting one slow client OOM the node. **Graceful drain on deploy**: when you roll the fleet, you do not hard-kill a node holding a million sockets — you stop accepting new connections, signal clients to reconnect (ideally staggered, to avoid a thundering herd), and let the population migrate. The reconnect storm is a real failure mode we stress-test in section 11.

Load balancing onto this tier is its own subtlety, and it leans on the [L4-to-L7 load balancing](/blog/software-development/system-design/load-balancing-from-l4-to-l7) decisions. WebSocket starts as an HTTP upgrade, then becomes a long-lived TCP flow, so an **L4 load balancer** that pins a connection to a node for its lifetime is the natural fit — you do not want to re-balance a live socket. The balancer spreads *new* connections; once established, a connection lives on one node until it dies. Because the connection is sticky for its lifetime anyway, the registry, not the balancer, handles routing *to* a user.

## 5. The session registry: routing a message to the right socket

Here is the crux problem, the one that makes chat hard and that figure 3 exists to make concrete. A message for Bob arrives at the stateless message service. Bob is online — somewhere — on one of 100+ connection nodes. **Which one?** Without an answer, the message service would have to broadcast "is Bob here?" to every node, which is O(nodes) per message and does not scale. The **session registry** is the phone book that answers it in one lookup: it maps `user_id → {device_id → gateway_node}` for every currently-connected device.

![Diagram of the session registry routing a message: the message service looks up recipient Bob in a Redis-backed registry, learns he is on gateway node seven, delivers the frame to that one node which pushes to Bob's device, and falls back to a mobile push provider only when no live socket is registered](/imgs/blogs/design-a-chat-and-messaging-system-3.webp)

The registry is written on every connect and deleted on every disconnect, as the handler sketch in section 4 showed. It is read on the delivery path of *every single message*, so at peak it serves on the order of millions of lookups/second. That read rate plus the tiny-value, key-by-user shape points squarely at an **in-memory key-value store** — Redis is the common choice — sharded by user id, with each entry carrying a **short TTL (say 30 seconds) refreshed by the connection's heartbeat**. The TTL is the safety net: if a connection node dies without cleanly removing its entries (a crash, a network partition), those stale entries expire on their own within the TTL window rather than poisoning routing forever. This is a deliberate lease pattern — the connection node holds a lease on its registry entries and must renew it or lose it.

The registry's consistency model is a senior judgment call, and it is a place to *deliberately choose weak consistency*. The registry can be momentarily stale — Bob just reconnected to a different node, or just disconnected — and that is *fine*, because the design tolerates it: if the registry points at the wrong node, that node simply finds it has no socket for Bob, reports the miss, and the message falls back to the offline path (store-and-push). **A stale registry degrades to "treat as offline," never to "lose the message."** That tolerance is what lets you run the registry as a fast, eventually-consistent cache rather than a strongly-consistent coordination service — a textbook application of choosing the [consistency model](/blog/software-development/system-design/consistency-models-a-practical-guide-for-architects) that the use case actually needs instead of paying for linearizability you do not require.

The delivery flow with the registry is exactly figure 3: message service looks up Bob → registry says "node 7" → message service sends the frame to node 7 over an internal RPC (or an internal pub/sub topic node 7 subscribes to) → node 7 finds Bob's in-process mailbox and pushes the frame down his socket. If the registry says "no entry," Bob is offline and we take the push-notification path. One lookup, one direct hop. No broadcast.

A clean way to implement the message-service-to-node hop at scale is an **internal message bus**: each connection node subscribes to a topic keyed by the users it holds (or by node id), and the message service publishes delivery frames to that topic. This decouples the message service from knowing node network addresses and gives you a natural buffer. It is the same producer/broker/consumer shape the [queues-and-event-streaming](/blog/software-development/system-design/queues-and-event-streaming-for-architects) post covers, applied to the live-delivery fan-out. Whether you use direct RPC or a bus is a trade-off between hop latency (RPC is lower) and operational decoupling (a bus is more resilient and elastic); many systems use direct RPC for the live hop because every millisecond counts and add a bus for the durable fan-out.

The optimization that keeps this hop cheap is to **make the live path do as little as possible and never block on durability there.** The message service has already persisted before it routes (section 6), so the registry lookup plus the node hop is pure in-memory and network work — no disk on the critical delivery path. A senior measures this hop and protects it: the registry lookup should be a sub-millisecond Redis GET (often pipelined or batched when a message has multiple recipients), and the node hop should be a single small RPC or pub/sub publish. The trap that quietly kills live-path latency is doing a *synchronous* durable write or a *synchronous* cross-region call inside the deliver step; you keep those out of the hot path and let them happen asynchronously, accepting that the durable copy is the source of truth and the live push is a best-effort fast lane on top of it. The mental discipline is: the durable store guarantees *you never lose it*, and the live path's only job is to make it *arrive fast when it can* — so the live path is allowed to fail (the message is safe), which means it is allowed to be cheap, which means it can be fast. Coupling the live path to durability would make it both slow and a place where a delivery failure could lose data; decoupling them gives you both speed and safety.

## 6. Message delivery: online push, offline store, and the guarantees

Now we can assemble the full delivery path, and the senior framing is: **persist first, then deliver, and let the recipient's liveness fork the delivery mechanism.** Figure 4 is the pipeline; figure 8 is the decision tree for which path a given message takes.

![Pipeline of message delivery showing a message persisted and assigned a per-conversation sequence number first, then a fork on whether the recipient is online, delivering over the live socket if online or handing to a mobile push provider if offline](/imgs/blogs/design-a-chat-and-messaging-system-4.webp)

The **order of operations is non-negotiable: durably persist and sequence the message before you acknowledge or deliver it.** When Alice's send arrives, the message service (1) writes it to the durable store and assigns the authoritative per-conversation `server_seq`, then (2) acks Alice (her app shows the single check — sent), then (3) attempts delivery to the recipient. If you deliver before persisting and the server crashes between the deliver and the would-be write, you have shown the message on one device and lost it forever — a torn delivery. Persisting first means the message is *recoverable* no matter what fails after; the worst case is a duplicate delivery, which dedup handles, not a lost message. **Prefer at-least-once with dedup over at-most-once, always, for chat.** Losing a message is unforgivable; showing it twice is a bug you can engineer away.

The fork on liveness (figure 8) is the heart of delivery:

- **Recipient online** (registry has a live socket): push the frame down the socket via the registry hop from section 5. Sub-second delivery. The recipient's app, on receiving it, sends back a **delivery receipt** (the second check), and later, when the user actually views it, a **read receipt** (the blue checks). These receipts are themselves small messages routed back to the sender the same way.
- **Recipient offline** (no live socket): the message is already durably stored, so nothing is lost. The system enqueues it in the recipient's **per-user inbox / sync queue** and triggers a **push notification** through the platform provider — **APNs** for iOS, **FCM** for Android — to wake the app or at least show a banner. When the recipient next connects, the connection handshake includes a **sync**: "give me everything since my last `server_seq`," and the gateway streams the backlog from the durable store. This is the store-and-forward path, and it is why an offline user who reconnects sees the missed messages in order.

```python
# Message service: persist-then-deliver. Stateless; horizontally scalable.
def submit(frame):
    # 1) Dedup at the server on the idempotency key (at-least-once safety).
    if store.seen(frame.conversation_id, frame.idempotency_key):
        return ack(frame.idempotency_key)          # retry of an already-stored msg

    # 2) Durable append + authoritative per-conversation sequence assignment.
    server_seq = store.append(frame.conversation_id, frame)   # atomic seq + write
    frame.server_seq = server_seq

    # 3) Ack the SENDER first (single check) — message is now safe.
    sender_ack(frame.idempotency_key, server_seq)

    # 4) Deliver to each recipient device: online push or offline store+notify.
    for rcpt in recipients_of(frame.conversation_id):
        for device in devices_of(rcpt):
            node = session_registry.get(rcpt, device)         # who holds the socket?
            if node:
                deliver_to_node(node, rcpt, device, frame)     # live push
            else:
                inbox.enqueue(rcpt, device, server_seq)        # store-and-forward
                push_provider.notify(rcpt, device)             # APNs / FCM wake
```

Note the multi-device dimension, which juniors forget: a user has a phone, a laptop, a tablet, and a message must reach *all* of their online devices and be *queued for the offline ones*. The registry maps user → many devices → many nodes, and delivery fans out per device. Read receipts and delivery state must then reconcile across devices: if you read a message on your laptop, your phone should stop showing it as unread. That cross-device sync is itself a small replicated-state problem layered on top of delivery, and it is worth being explicit about how it works, because it is where a lot of "ghost unread" and "message read on one device, still bold on another" bugs come from in real products.

The clean model treats **each device as an independent consumer of the user's message stream, with its own cursor.** A user's messages (for all their conversations, or per conversation) form an ordered stream, and each device tracks "the last `server_seq` I have applied" per conversation. Delivery to a device is then: bring this device's cursor up to the head. An online device gets live pushes that advance its cursor in real time; an offline device's cursor is stale, and on reconnect it says "I am at seq X for these conversations, send me the delta," and the gateway streams everything after X in order. Read state is just *another* cursor — the highest `server_seq` this user has *read* in a conversation — and it is itself a small message that propagates to the user's other devices the same way any message does. When you read up to seq 50 on your laptop, your laptop emits a "read up to 50" event, the message service stores it and pushes it to your phone, and your phone advances its read cursor to 50 and clears the unread badge. Modeling read state as a monotonic per-conversation cursor (not a per-message boolean) is the trick that makes cross-device reconciliation simple and idempotent: applying "read up to 50" twice, or out of order with "read up to 48," is harmless because you only ever move the cursor forward. That monotonic-cursor design is a small instance of the [consistency-model](/blog/software-development/system-design/consistency-models-a-practical-guide-for-architects) discipline — pick a state representation whose updates commute and you get multi-device convergence almost for free.

The subtle cost is that delivery state now multiplies by device count: a message to a 1:1 chat where both users have 3 devices is up to 6 delivery operations and 6 cursor updates, and a group message multiplies that by membership. This is why the section-2 estimation used ~2 devices per user as a delivery multiplier, and why the storage layer keeps message bodies stored *once* per conversation (not once per device) and lets devices read the shared conversation — fan-out-on-read at the *device* level even when you fan out per *user*. Storing one body and N cursors, rather than N copies of the body, is the difference between linear and multiplicative storage growth as users add devices.

## 7. Ordering, sequence numbers, and making at-least-once safe

Ordering is the guarantee users notice instantly when it breaks — a reply appearing above the message it replies to is jarring and looks like a bug to everyone in the chat. The senior insight is that you do **not** need a globally ordered total log across the whole system (that would be a coordination nightmare at this scale); you need **ordering *per conversation***, and that is dramatically cheaper. Figure 7 shows how a per-conversation sequence number delivers it.

![Timeline showing per-conversation sequence numbers: the server assigns gapless sequence numbers as messages arrive, a retried message keeps its original sequence so it is not duplicated, and the client detects a gap between sequence numbers and refetches the missing message to fill it](/imgs/blogs/design-a-chat-and-messaging-system-7.webp)

The mechanism is a **monotonic, gapless sequence number assigned per conversation by a single authority at persist time.** Conversation 42's messages get `server_seq` 1, 2, 3, … in the order the message service commits them. Every client sorts a conversation's messages by `server_seq`, and now everyone — sender, recipient, every device — agrees on the order, regardless of clock skew or network reordering, because they all sort by the same authoritative integer. Wall-clock timestamps are *not* a reliable order key (clocks skew, two messages can share a millisecond), which is why the sequence number, not the timestamp, is the ordering token. This is the same logic the [message-ordering and partitioning](/blog/software-development/message-queue/message-ordering-and-partitioning-guarantees) deep-dive establishes for log-structured systems: ordering is cheap *within a partition* and expensive *across* them, so you make the conversation your partition.

Who assigns the sequence? The cleanest design makes the **conversation the unit of partitioning** so that all writes for a conversation route to one place that can assign sequence numbers without cross-node coordination — the same conversation always hits the same shard, and that shard hands out the next integer. This is exactly why the conversation id is the partition key in the storage layer (section 9): co-locating a conversation's writes makes gapless sequencing a local operation, not a distributed-consensus operation. If you needed a sequence shared across shards you would be reaching for [consensus](/blog/software-development/system-design/consensus-and-coordination-in-distributed-systems), and the whole point of partitioning by conversation is to *avoid* paying for that on the hot path.

The sequence number does triple duty. It **orders** (sort by it). It **detects loss**: if a client has seq 42 and the next thing it receives is 44, it *knows* it is missing 43 (a gap) and can refetch it from the store — the system self-heals dropped messages without the user noticing, the "gap → refetch" arc in figure 7. And it anchors **sync**: an offline client reconnecting says "my last seq for conversation 42 is 41, send me everything after," and gets a clean ordered backlog.

Now, dedup — the other half of "at-least-once made safe." Because the network is unreliable, the sender retries un-acked sends, and the same logical message can arrive at the server more than once. The **idempotency key** (generated once per message, reused on every retry) lets the server recognize the duplicate: if it has already stored a message with that key for that conversation, it returns the *existing* `server_seq` and does not append a second copy (the "retry → same seq" arc in figure 7). The recipient *also* dedups on the key, in case a duplicate slips through to delivery. This is the [idempotency-and-deduplication](/blog/software-development/message-queue/idempotency-and-deduplication-making-at-least-once-safe) pattern applied end to end, and it is what lets the entire system run at-least-once — the cheap, reliable semantic — while the *user experience* is exactly-once. The hard-won senior rule: **you do not get exactly-once on the wire; you get at-least-once on the wire plus dedup at the edges, which composes to exactly-once as perceived.**

#### Worked example: a duplicate during a network blip

Alice sends "on my way" with idempotency key `k-9f3`. The frame reaches the server, which persists it as conversation-seq 42 and starts the ack back to Alice. Alice's phone switches from Wi-Fi to cellular mid-flight; the ack never arrives. Alice's app, seeing no ack after its retry timeout, resends the *identical* frame — same key `k-9f3`. The server receives it, checks its dedup index, finds `k-9f3` already stored at seq 42, and simply returns the existing ack with seq 42 — **no second message is created.** Bob, who may have already received the first copy via live push, also checks `k-9f3` against what he has and ignores the duplicate if it reaches him twice. Net result: Alice's message appears exactly once, at the right position (seq 42), despite the wire having carried it twice. The cost of this guarantee is a dedup index keyed by `(conversation_id, idempotency_key)` with a retention window long enough to outlive any plausible retry (minutes to hours), which is a bounded, cheap structure. That bounded dedup window in exchange for never showing a torn or duplicated message is one of the best trades in the whole design.

## 8. Presence and typing: the most expensive cheap feature

Presence — the green dot, "last seen," "typing…" — is the feature that looks trivial and quietly tries to bankrupt your fleet, which is why it earns its own optimization section and figure 6. The estimation in section 2 already flagged it: naive presence fan-out scales as **users × contacts**, and at 100 million online users with hundreds of contacts each, that product *exceeds* the actual message traffic by an order of magnitude. The little green dot can cost more than the messages.

![Before-and-after comparison of presence fan-out showing the naive approach pushing every status change to all five hundred of a user's contacts and amplifying flapping connections into a write storm, versus a subscribe-on-view plus debounce model that pushes only to open chats and coalesces rapid flips, cutting writes by roughly ninety-five percent](/imgs/blogs/design-a-chat-and-messaging-system-6.webp)

Why is naive presence so expensive? Every time a user's status flips — comes online, goes offline, starts typing, stops typing — the naive design pushes that change to *everyone who might be looking*, which it conservatively treats as all of the user's contacts. But status flips constantly: a phone on a flaky network reconnects every few minutes (NAT timeouts, signal drops), each reconnect is an online→offline→online flap, and each flap fans out to every contact. Typing indicators are worse — they fire on keystroke cadence. Multiply per-user flap rate by contact count by online population and you get the write storm in figure 6's "before" column. Presence done naively is a self-inflicted denial-of-service.

The senior optimizations, in figure 6's "after" column, attack it from three directions:

- **Subscribe-on-view, not push-to-all.** A user only cares about the presence of contacts whose chat is *currently on their screen* (or in their visible conversation list). So presence is **pull/subscribe**: when Alice opens her chat with Bob, her client *subscribes* to Bob's presence, and only then does Bob's status get delivered to Alice. The fan-out collapses from "all contacts" to "the handful of conversations actually visible right now," which is a 10–100× reduction. Most of your contacts are not looking at you most of the time, and presence should cost nothing for those.
- **Debounce and coalesce.** Do not propagate every flip. Apply a debounce window (say 10–30 seconds): a user who flaps online/offline/online within the window is reported with their *settled* state once, not three times. Typing indicators get an even tighter coalesce and a short auto-expiry (a "typing" that is not refreshed within a few seconds is dropped) so a keystroke storm becomes at most one update per few seconds per viewer.
- **Approximate, not exact.** Presence is inherently soft state — "last seen 2 minutes ago" is a perfectly good answer, and nobody needs the green dot to be accurate to the millisecond. Storing presence as **soft state in an in-memory store with a TTL** (the same kind of store as the session registry, often co-located with it) means you never durably persist a flip, never replicate it for durability, and let staleness be the default. Presence that is allowed to be approximate is cheap; presence that must be exact is ruinous.

The combined effect (figure 6) is roughly a 95% cut in presence write volume versus naive — the difference between presence being a rounding error on your fleet and presence being the dominant cost. The senior lesson generalizes: **for soft, high-churn, low-value-per-update signals, the optimization is to deliver less — subscribe to what is visible, coalesce the rest, and let it be approximate.**

## 9. Message storage: why a wide-column, time-ordered store

Now the durable store, where the access pattern dictates the engine and the [choosing-a-datastore](/blog/software-development/system-design/choosing-a-datastore-sql-nosql-newsql) reasoning earns its keep. Figure 9 shows the layout; the rest of this section justifies it.

![Stack diagram of the message storage layout showing the partition key as conversation id, the clustering key as descending sequence, each row holding sender, body, and timestamp, an LSM write-optimized engine underneath, and a TTL or cold tier for archiving old messages](/imgs/blogs/design-a-chat-and-messaging-system-9.webp)

Read the access pattern off the requirements. **Writes** are an enormous, never-ending stream of small appends — 20 billion/day, each a new message at the end of some conversation. **Reads** are "give me the most recent N messages of this conversation" (opening a chat shows the latest screen-full) and "give me everything in this conversation after seq X" (sync after reconnect). Both reads are **range scans over one conversation, ordered by sequence.** There are essentially no random-access reads of individual ancient messages and no cross-conversation joins on the hot path. That access shape — massive ordered appends, range-scan reads per partition, no joins — is the canonical fit for a **wide-column store** like **Cassandra**, **ScyllaDB**, or **HBase**, backed by an **LSM-tree** engine.

The data model (figure 9) makes the access pattern free:

```sql
-- Cassandra-style: partition by conversation, cluster (order) by sequence.
CREATE TABLE messages (
    conversation_id  text,
    server_seq       bigint,
    message_id       uuid,
    sender_id        text,
    body             blob,        -- text, or E2EE ciphertext the server can't read
    sent_at          timestamp,
    PRIMARY KEY ((conversation_id), server_seq)   -- partition key, clustering key
) WITH CLUSTERING ORDER BY (server_seq DESC);     -- newest first = cheapest read
```

`PRIMARY KEY ((conversation_id), server_seq)` does the heavy lifting. **Partition key = `conversation_id`** co-locates an entire conversation's messages on one set of replicas, so "the latest N of this conversation" is a single-partition read — no scatter-gather. It also gives you the local sequence authority from section 7: all writes for a conversation land on the same partition, so assigning the next gapless `server_seq` needs no cross-node coordination. **Clustering key = `server_seq DESC`** stores the messages physically sorted newest-first, so the most common read (open chat → latest screen) is the cheapest possible operation: read the head of the partition.

Why LSM and not a B-tree here? Because the workload is **write-dominated** — billions of appends, mostly never re-read after the recipient catches up — and an [LSM-tree](/blog/software-development/system-design/storage-engines-btrees-vs-lsm-trees-for-architects) turns those writes into sequential, append-friendly flushes with high write throughput, whereas a B-tree pays a random-write penalty per insert that a chat write rate would crush. The trade you accept is read amplification (a read may touch several SSTables) and compaction overhead, both of which are well-controlled for the "recent range per partition" read shape because recent data clusters in the freshest SSTables. This is precisely the [B-tree-vs-LSM](/blog/software-development/system-design/storage-engines-btrees-vs-lsm-trees-for-architects) decision made for a write-heavy, range-scan workload.

Two more storage decisions a senior makes. **Tiering / TTL**: most messages are read within minutes of being sent and then almost never again, so you tier — hot recent data on fast storage, cold history aged into cheaper object storage or compacted aggressively, with optional TTL for products that expire old messages. This keeps the hot path's working set small and the cost per stored message low. **Sharding the conversation space**: because some conversations are tiny (1:1) and some are huge (a 100k-member broadcast group), you partition by conversation but watch for the hot-partition problem — a single giant, extremely active conversation can become a hotspot that one partition's replicas cannot serve. The mitigation is the same [partitioning-and-sharding](/blog/software-development/system-design/partitioning-and-sharding-without-downtime) discipline you would apply anywhere: detect the hot conversation and split it (e.g., bucket by a coarse time window so a busy group's writes spread across sub-partitions), trading the perfect single-partition range scan for survivable load.

The replication and durability posture of the store is its own deliberate choice. A wide-column store typically uses leaderless quorum replication, and the knob a senior turns is the read/write consistency level. For message *writes* you want enough replicas to ack that you never lose a message a sender saw confirmed — a quorum write (e.g., two of three replicas) gives durability without paying for all-replica acks on the hot path. For message *reads* on the recent timeline, a lower consistency level is often acceptable because the sequence number lets the client detect and refetch any gap anyway — so you can read from one fast replica and let gap-detection cover the rare stale read. This is the [replication-strategies](/blog/software-development/system-design/replication-strategies-and-their-failure-modes) and quorum reasoning applied to chat: tune the write side for durability (never lose a confirmed message) and the read side for latency (the sequence number is your safety net against staleness). Picking these levels by *what the sequence number already protects* rather than by reflex is the senior move.

#### Worked example: read amplification on opening a busy chat

Consider opening a chat that received 2,000 messages today across several active SSTables. The client asks for the latest 50 messages — a `server_seq DESC LIMIT 50` range scan on one partition. In an LSM store, "the latest 50" almost certainly live in the freshest SSTable (recent writes cluster in the newest, un-compacted files), so the read touches one or two SSTables and a row cache, not all of them — read amplification is bounded *because* the clustering order matches the read order. Contrast a poorly chosen schema that clustered by `message_id` (random) instead of `server_seq`: "the latest 50" would scatter across every SSTable, the read would touch all of them, and read amplification would scale with history size — opening an old, busy chat would get slower forever. The lesson the example drives home: the clustering key is not a detail, it is *the* performance decision, because it determines whether your most common read is O(1) in history size or O(history). Cluster by the key you read in order by — here, the sequence number — and the LSM engine's read amplification stays flat as conversations grow to millions of messages.

## 10. Group chat fan-out and end-to-end encryption

**Group fan-out** is where 1:1 logic generalizes and where a sharp scaling cliff appears. A message to a 1:1 chat is delivered to one other user (across their devices). A message to a group of N members must be delivered to up to N − 1 recipients, each across their devices, each either online (live push) or offline (store + notify). The decision tree in figure 8 covers it: after persist-and-sequence, the group branch fans out to every member, taking the online or offline sub-path per member.

![Decision tree for choosing the delivery path after a message is persisted and sequenced, branching first on whether the recipient is online, then on one-to-one versus group for online recipients, and on whether an offline recipient has a push token, with each leaf naming the concrete delivery mechanism](/imgs/blogs/design-a-chat-and-messaging-system-8.webp)

The architectural choice in groups is **fan-out-on-write versus fan-out-on-read**, the same fork the [news-feed](/blog/software-development/system-design/design-a-news-feed-and-timeline) design wrestles with. **Fan-out-on-write** (push the message into every member's inbox/queue at send time) gives the lowest read latency — each member's sync is a simple read of their own queue — but the write cost scales with group size, and for a huge group a single message becomes thousands of writes and pushes. **Fan-out-on-read** (store the message once in the conversation, and let each member read the shared conversation on open/sync) keeps writes O(1) per message but pushes the fan-out work to read time and complicates per-member delivery state. Most chat systems use **fan-out-on-write for typical groups** (fast reads, manageable write multiple) and **fan-out-on-read for very large groups / broadcast channels** (where writing to a million inboxes per message is untenable). The senior move is to make the threshold explicit and switch strategies above it — exactly the kind of "hybrid, switch at a measured threshold" decision that separates a design that survives a viral group from one that melts.

The hard case is the **huge-group message storm**: a celebrity posts to a 500k-member broadcast group. Fan-out-on-write would be 500k delivery operations for one message — a self-inflicted spike. The mitigations: fan-out-on-read for these groups (store once, members pull), aggressive rate limiting on who can post to mega-groups, and treating broadcast as a fundamentally different product (one-to-many publish) rather than as a big symmetric group. We stress-test this in section 11.

**End-to-end encryption (E2EE)** changes the architecture in one profound way: **the server cannot read message contents.** In an E2EE system (WhatsApp and Signal use the Signal Protocol), messages are encrypted on the sender's device with keys only the recipients' devices hold, and the server stores and forwards opaque ciphertext — the `body` blob in the schema is bytes the server cannot decrypt. The architectural consequences a senior must call out:

- **No server-side search or content features.** The server cannot index message text it cannot read, so full-text search must happen *on the client* over the locally decrypted history, or not at all. Content moderation, smart replies, and server-side analytics over message bodies all become impossible by construction. This is a *product* constraint that flows directly from the *architecture* choice, and it must be surfaced in the design review, not discovered later.
- **Key management and multi-device.** Each device has its own keys; adding a device, or messaging a group, requires distributing/agreeing keys per device (sender-keys schemes for groups, so you encrypt once per group epoch rather than once per member-device). This is real complexity that lives in a key-distribution service, and it is the part of E2EE most likely to have subtle bugs.
- **Metadata still leaks and still matters.** Even with bodies encrypted, the server necessarily sees *metadata* — who talks to whom, when, how often, how big the messages are — because it must route them. Sequence numbers, conversation membership, and timing are all visible to the server. "End-to-end encrypted" protects content, not the social graph, and a senior states that limit plainly rather than implying perfect privacy.

- **Backup and history restore become a key problem, not a data problem.** Because the server holds only ciphertext, a user who loses their device cannot just "download their history from the server" and read it — the keys died with the device. So E2EE products must solve encrypted backup separately: an end-to-end-encrypted cloud backup protected by a user-held password or recovery key, where the server stores the encrypted backup blob but still cannot read it. This is a genuinely hard usability-versus-security trade (a forgotten backup password means lost history forever), and it is a direct architectural consequence of "the server cannot read messages" that teams routinely underestimate until a user support queue fills with "where did my chats go."

The upshot: E2EE is mostly orthogonal to the delivery, ordering, and storage machinery — the server still persists, sequences, routes, and fans out *opaque blobs* exactly as before — but it amputates every feature that needs to read content, adds a key-management subsystem, and turns history backup into its own encrypted-recovery problem. You design the transport identically and accept the feature constraints knowingly, surfacing each one in the design review so nobody is surprised when "add server-side search" turns out to be impossible by construction.

## 11. Stress-test: what breaks at 10×, on a crash, on a spike

A design is only as good as its behavior on the unhappy path, so a senior stress-tests it the way [chaos and load testing](/blog/software-development/system-design/testing-distributed-systems-chaos-and-load) prescribes — deliberately breaking it on paper before production breaks it for real. Three scenarios expose the weak points.

**A connection-server crash → the reconnect storm.** A connection node holding a million sockets dies (a hardware fault, a kernel panic, a bad deploy). Instantly, a million clients detect their socket is dead and try to reconnect *at once*. If your reconnect logic is naive — immediate retry, no jitter — those million clients hammer the remaining nodes and the session registry simultaneously, a **thundering herd** that can take down the nodes that were supposed to absorb the failover, turning one node's death into a cascade. The mitigations are mandatory, not optional: **exponential backoff with jitter** on the client so the million reconnects spread over tens of seconds instead of arriving in one spike; **connection-establishment rate limiting** at the gateway so a node sheds excess new-connection load instead of collapsing; and **capacity headroom** so the surviving N−1 nodes can actually hold the orphaned sockets (this is why section 2 planned 150–200 nodes for 100M sockets, not a tight 100). Crucially, **no messages are lost** in this scenario, because the connection tier holds no durable state — the dead node's users reconnect, sync from the durable store via "everything after my last seq," and recover their full history. The crash is a latency-and-load event, not a data-loss event, *because* we made the connection tier stateless about message logic. That is the payoff of the section-4 split.

**A presence fan-out spike.** It is 9 a.m. in a populous timezone and tens of millions of users wake their phones within a few minutes, every one flipping offline→online. Naive presence (section 8) would fan each of those flips to hundreds of contacts — tens of billions of presence writes in a few-minute window, a self-inflicted DDoS that buries the message path under presence noise. With the section-8 optimizations — subscribe-on-view, debounce, approximate soft state — the spike collapses: a flip only reaches the handful of viewers currently looking at that user, debouncing coalesces the morning's flap-fest into single settled updates, and nothing durable is written. The same spike that melts the naive design is a non-event for the optimized one. This is the clearest demonstration in the whole system that **the optimization is not a nice-to-have — it is the difference between surviving the morning wave and not.**

**A huge-group message storm.** Someone posts to a 500k-member group. Under fan-out-on-write this is 500k delivery operations from one send — a write and push spike that hammers the store, the registry, and the push providers all at once, and can starve every other conversation sharing those resources. The mitigations layer up: **fan-out-on-read for large groups** so the single write stays O(1) and members pull on open (section 10); **rate limiting** on posting to mega-groups so one user cannot trigger unbounded fan-out at will; **isolating mega-group traffic** onto its own capacity so it cannot starve the 1:1 hot path (a bulkhead); and **batching pushes** to the platform providers (APNs/FCM accept batched sends) so 500k notifications go out as efficient batches rather than 500k individual calls. The senior framing: a mega-group is a *broadcast* workload wearing a *chat* costume, and you must recognize and isolate it as such or it will define your worst day.

#### Worked example: sizing the reconnect storm

Take the crash concretely. One node holding **1,000,000 sockets** dies. If every client reconnects immediately with no backoff, the surviving fleet sees a 1,000,000-connection spike in roughly the few seconds it takes clients to notice — call it **~200,000 new-connection attempts/second** for ~5 seconds, all hitting both the gateways and the session registry (each reconnect is a registry write). A registry sized for steady-state churn can be overwhelmed by that. Now add **exponential backoff with jitter** spreading reconnects uniformly over, say, **60 seconds**: 1,000,000 reconnects / 60s ≈ **~17,000 attempts/second** — a 12× reduction in peak, well within headroom. Add per-node connection-rate limiting (each surviving node accepts new connections up to a safe ceiling and briefly rejects the rest, which clients retry on their backoff schedule) and the spike flattens further. The numbers show why jitter is not a nicety: the *same* million reconnects either arrive as an unsurvivable 200k/s wall or a comfortable 17k/s trickle, and the only difference is one client-side line of backoff-with-jitter. Cheap insurance against a self-inflicted outage.

## 12. Trade-offs and rejected alternatives

A senior never presents a single design as obvious; they present the option space and *defend the choice by naming its cost*. Figure 5 is the delivery-approach decision matrix that anchors this section.

![Matrix comparing delivery approaches against latency, per-conversation ordering, offline support, and implementation cost, showing that live-push-only is fastest but loses offline reach and ordering, while at-least-once with dedup gives strong ordering and full offline support at higher implementation cost](/imgs/blogs/design-a-chat-and-messaging-system-5.webp)

Read figure 5 column by column. **Live-push-only** (fire the message at the socket, no durable store) is the lowest latency and cheapest to build, but it has *no* offline support and *no* real ordering or loss-recovery — close the app and the message is gone. It is fine for ephemeral signals (typing, presence) and disqualified for actual messages. **Best-effort fire-and-forget** is the same trap with even less to recommend it. **Store-then-push** (persist first, then live-push or notify) buys full offline support and a foundation for ordering at moderate cost — it is the baseline real design. **At-least-once + dedup** adds the idempotency-key machinery on top of store-then-push to make retries safe, giving the strongest ordering and full offline support, and it costs the most to implement (dedup indexes, sequence authority, client-side gap detection). The matrix makes the recommendation defensible: **for real messages, at-least-once + store-then-push is the choice, and its cost — the dedup and sequencing machinery — is the price of never losing or reordering a message.** For ephemeral signals, live-push-only is correct precisely *because* their value does not justify that cost.

Now the explicit **rejected alternatives**, each with the reason a senior would say no:

- **Rejected: long-polling instead of WebSocket.** Simpler to implement and firewall-friendly, but pays a request-per-cycle tax and multi-second latency at scale (section 3). Acceptable only as a *fallback* for clients that cannot hold a WebSocket; never the primary path.
- **Rejected: a single globally-ordered message log.** Tempting because it makes ordering trivially total, but it forces global coordination/consensus on the hot write path and does not scale to a million writes/second. Per-conversation ordering (section 7) gives users everything they perceive at a fraction of the cost. Reaching for global order here is paying for a guarantee the product does not need.
- **Rejected: a relational store as the primary message store.** A B-tree-backed SQL database is wonderful for transactional, relational, random-access workloads — and chat is none of those on the hot path. Billions of ordered appends read back as per-conversation ranges is an LSM/wide-column workload (section 9). Using Postgres as the primary message store invites the random-write penalty and the sharding pain that the wide-column store solves natively. (Postgres is great for *user/account/relationship* data alongside it — pick the store per access pattern, not one store for everything.)
- **Rejected: strongly-consistent session registry.** Linearizable routing data sounds safer, but the registry tolerates staleness by design (stale → treat as offline → store-and-forward, never loss), so paying for strong consistency on a multi-million-QPS lookup path is pure waste (section 5). Eventually-consistent soft state with a TTL is the right call.
- **Rejected: synchronous fan-out-on-write for all group sizes.** Lowest read latency, but a mega-group turns one send into hundreds of thousands of writes (section 11). The hybrid — fan-out-on-write below a threshold, fan-out-on-read above it — is the only design that survives a viral group.

The through-line: every "no" is justified by a *cost the rejected option pays that the workload does not justify spending*. That is the entire discipline of [articulating trade-offs](/blog/software-development/system-design/articulating-tradeoffs-cap-pacelc-and-beyond) — there is no free lunch, so name the lunch you are buying and the one you are declining.

## 13. Case studies: how real systems do it

Real architectures are the proof that these decisions ship, and three public ones each teach a concrete lesson.

**WhatsApp — Erlang connection servers, extreme density.** WhatsApp became the canonical example of holding enormous connection counts on tiny fleets: a famously small engineering team served hundreds of millions of users, and they demonstrated on the order of millions of concurrent connections per server on tuned FreeBSD, built on **Erlang/OTP**. The lesson for your design: the connection tier's runtime choice is load-bearing, and a concurrency model built for "millions of cheap, mostly-idle, supervised processes" (Erlang's actor model) maps onto the persistent-connection problem better than thread-per-connection ever can. Density per node is a *design* outcome, not just a tuning one — it is why section 2's "1M sockets/node" is a defensible planning number and why section 4 insists on an event-driven runtime. WhatsApp also leaned on the store-and-forward model: the server holds an offline user's messages and delivers on reconnect, deleting them from the server once delivered — durable enough to never lose, ephemeral enough to keep storage lean.

**Discord — Elixir gateways and a wide-column store for billions of messages.** Discord publicly described running its real-time gateway on **Elixir** (Erlang's BEAM again — the same lesson, independently arrived at) and storing its messages first in **Cassandra** and later migrating to **ScyllaDB** to handle a workload measured in *trillions* of stored messages with a partition-by-channel, cluster-by-time access pattern — almost exactly the schema in section 9. Their public writing on hot-partition problems (a single extremely busy channel overwhelming one partition) and the fixes (bucketing, careful partition-key design) is the real-world version of the hot-conversation problem in section 9. The lesson: the wide-column, partition-by-conversation, LSM-backed store is not a textbook nicety — it is what a system storing trillions of chat messages actually runs, and its sharp edge is exactly the hot partition this design anticipates.

**Facebook Messenger — MQTT for mobile efficiency.** Messenger publicly adopted **MQTT** as its mobile transport rather than raw WebSocket, because MQTT's tiny fixed header and built-in publish/subscribe and QoS semantics fit constrained mobile networks and battery budgets well — fewer bytes per keep-alive, lighter reconnect. The lesson: the *persistent connection* is the invariant (server-initiated push over a long-lived socket), but the *exact protocol* is a tunable choice driven by your clients' constraints — WebSocket, MQTT, and gRPC streams are all valid points, and a senior picks by the client environment (battery, network, firewall) rather than by fashion.

The common thread across all three: a **stateful, dense, event-driven connection tier** in front of a **stateless message path** writing to a **partition-by-conversation, time-ordered, write-optimized store**, with **store-and-forward** covering offline and **per-conversation ordering** covering correctness. The same skeleton, three times, at three of the largest scales in the industry. That convergence is the strongest evidence the design is right.

## 14. When to reach for this (and when not to)

The full architecture above is a *lot* of machinery, and a senior is as good at *not* building it as at building it. Reach for the complete persistent-connection-plus-session-registry-plus-wide-column design when you genuinely have: large concurrent-connection counts (hundreds of thousands to millions of simultaneous live users), a hard low-latency delivery requirement (chat that must *feel* instant), offline-capable clients that need store-and-forward, and a write-heavy, ordered, multi-petabyte message history. That is messaging at consumer scale, and there the machinery pays for itself.

Do **not** reach for it when your real requirements are smaller, because every piece of it is a cost. If you have **thousands, not millions, of concurrent users**, a single managed real-time service (Pusher, Ably, a hosted WebSocket gateway like AWS API Gateway WebSockets, or Firebase/Firestore real-time listeners) gives you persistent delivery without operating a connection fleet, a session registry, or a wide-column cluster — buy it, do not build it. If your "chat" is really **low-frequency notifications** (an order shipped, a comment reply) rather than live conversation, you do not need persistent connections at all — push notifications plus a "fetch on open" sync is dramatically simpler and entirely adequate. If you need **chat inside a product but not at planetary scale**, an off-the-shelf chat SDK (Stream, SendBird) hands you delivery, presence, and history as a service, and your engineering effort is better spent on your actual product.

The senior anti-pattern is building the WhatsApp architecture for a workload that a hosted WebSocket service and a single Postgres table would serve for years. The session registry, the Erlang gateway tier, the Cassandra cluster, the presence-optimization machinery — each is *justified by a scale you must actually have*. Match the build to the load: under-build and you re-architect under fire; over-build and you operate a fleet your traffic never needed. The decision of *whether* to build this is itself the most senior call in the whole exercise, and it follows the same [requirements-first discipline](/blog/software-development/system-design/turning-vague-asks-into-requirements-and-slos) as everything else — name the real numbers, then build to them and no further.

## 15. Key takeaways

- **The persistent connection is the seed of the whole design.** Server-initiated delivery requires a long-lived socket, which makes the connection tier stateful about sockets — and the entire architecture (gateway tier + session registry + stateless message path) exists to manage that one consequence. WebSocket/MQTT beats polling on both latency and waste; polling survives only as a fallback.
- **Keep the connection tier thin and stateless about message logic.** It holds sockets and shovels frames; all durability, sequencing, and fan-out live in the stateless message service behind it. This split is exactly what makes a connection-server crash a recoverable latency event instead of a data-loss event.
- **Route with a session registry, not a broadcast.** An in-memory, sharded, TTL-leased map from user→device→node turns delivery into one lookup and one direct hop. Run it as fast eventually-consistent soft state, because a stale entry degrades safely to "treat as offline," never to message loss.
- **Persist before you deliver, and prefer at-least-once with dedup.** Durably store and assign the per-conversation sequence number *first*, ack the sender second, deliver third. Losing a message is unforgivable; a duplicate is a dedup problem the idempotency key already solves.
- **Order per conversation with a gapless sequence number, not a clock.** Make the conversation the partition so sequencing is a local operation, and let the sequence do triple duty: order, gap-detection (refetch the missing seq), and reconnect-sync. You never need a globally ordered log.
- **Presence is the most expensive cheap feature — deliver less.** Subscribe-on-view instead of push-to-all, debounce/coalesce churn, and treat it as approximate soft state with a TTL. Naive presence fan-out (users × contacts) can exceed your entire message traffic; the optimization is the difference between surviving the morning wave and not.
- **Storage shape follows access pattern: wide-column, partition-by-conversation, LSM-backed.** Billions of small ordered appends read back as per-conversation ranges is not a relational workload; it is the canonical LSM/Cassandra/Scylla shape, and the partition key gives you both cheap reads and local sequencing for free.
- **Group fan-out and E2EE are the scaling and constraint multipliers.** Use fan-out-on-write below a threshold and fan-out-on-read above it so a mega-group cannot melt the fleet; and accept that end-to-end encryption amputates every server-side content feature (search, moderation) by construction while leaving the transport unchanged.
- **Match the build to the load.** Under millions of concurrent users, buy a hosted real-time service or a chat SDK. The full fleet is justified only by scale you must actually have; the most senior decision is *whether* to build it at all.
