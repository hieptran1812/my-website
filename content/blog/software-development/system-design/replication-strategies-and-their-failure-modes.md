Every replication strategy is a bet about what will fail. You do not choose single-leader, multi-leader, or leaderless replication because one is "better" — you choose because you have decided, often without saying so out loud, which failure you are willing to eat. Single-leader bets that the leader rarely dies and that when it does you can promote a replica fast enough; the price of that bet, paid in full during a bad failover, is the last couple of seconds of acknowledged writes silently vanishing. Multi-leader bets that you can merge concurrent writes after the fact; the price is a class of conflicts that have no correct answer, only a least-wrong one. Leaderless bets that you can out-vote staleness with quorums; the price is that "consistent" becomes a tunable you have to compute, not a guarantee you can assume. A senior does not memorize these three options as a menu. A senior holds them as three different debts, and picks the debt the business can afford.

I have been on both ends of these debts coming due. I have watched a perfectly healthy MySQL primary fail over cleanly in staging and lose four seconds of production orders the first time it happened for real, because async replication had been lagging under load and nobody had measured it. I have watched a multi-leader setup that "just worked" for eighteen months produce a customer-visible inventory count of negative seven, because two regions decremented the same row during a partition and the last-write-wins clock skew picked the wrong winner. I have watched a leaderless cluster serve a deleted record back to life because a sloppy quorum accepted a write on hinted-handoff nodes and read repair brought the tombstone's predecessor forward. None of these were exotic. All of them were the documented, expected failure mode of the strategy in use, hitting a team that had adopted the strategy for its happy-path properties and never costed the unhappy path.

This post is the architect's field guide to that costing. We will start with *why* we replicate at all — because the four reasons (availability, read scale, geo-locality, durability) pull in different directions and a single strategy rarely serves all four — and then walk each topology in the order a design review walks it: single-leader first (the default that is right more often than people admit), then multi-leader (the one you adopt when geography forces your hand), then leaderless quorums (the one you adopt when you want to delete the leader as a failure point entirely). For each one I will name the happy-path win, derive the failure mode with concrete numbers, and show the production incident shape it produces. Figure 1 frames the whole thing: a single leader fanning writes out to followers that lag by wildly different amounts, which is the root of nearly every consistency anomaly we will discuss.

![Diagram of a single leader streaming writes to three followers that lag by eight milliseconds, forty milliseconds, and over two seconds, with read clients fanning out across all three](/imgs/blogs/replication-strategies-and-their-failure-modes-1.webp)

The `database/` folder on this blog already deep-dives the *mechanisms*: how [leader, multi-leader, and leaderless replication move bytes](/blog/software-development/database/distributed-replication-leader-multi-leader-leaderless), how [synchronous, asynchronous, logical, and physical replication differ at the log level](/blog/software-development/database/database-replication-sync-async-logical-physical), and how [quorums, anti-entropy, and read repair converge replicas](/blog/software-development/database/quorums-anti-entropy-and-read-repair). This post is the decision layer on top of those. I will not re-derive how a WAL ships; I will tell you how to *choose* between sync and async per use case to hit a durability target, how to *compute* the stale-read window your users will actually see, how to *tune* N/R/W for read-heavy versus write-heavy traffic, and how to defend all of it in a review when someone asks "what happens during a region outage?" By the end you should be able to look at a workload and say, with numbers, which replication debt it can afford.

## 1. Why we replicate, and why no single strategy serves all four reasons

Before you can choose a strategy you have to be honest about *why* you are replicating, because there are four distinct reasons and they do not point the same direction. Conflating them is the most common root cause of a replication design that satisfies the slide deck and fails the on-call rotation.

**Availability.** You keep more than one copy so that the death of one machine does not take the data offline. This is the reason most people say first, and it pushes you toward fast, automatic failover. But "available" is slippery: available to *read* is far cheaper than available to *write*, because a write needs a single agreed-upon place to go (or a conflict-resolution story), while a read can come from any copy. A system can be 100% read-available and 0% write-available during a leader election. Naming which one the business needs is the first fork.

**Read scale.** You keep more than one copy so that read traffic can fan out across many machines instead of hammering one. A read-heavy workload — a social feed at 50:1 read/write, a product catalog at 200:1 — can serve reads from a dozen followers and absorb 10x the read QPS of a single node. This reason pushes you toward *many* asynchronous followers, because synchronous followers are expensive and you do not need them to be fresh to serve a catalog page. Concretely: a leader that saturates at 30,000 reads/second on its own becomes a cluster that serves 30,000 writes-and-fresh-reads on the leader *plus* 30,000 × N stale-tolerant reads across N followers — add nine followers and you have lifted read capacity from 30k to ~300k QPS without touching the write path, for the linear cost of nine more machines. Read scale and strong consistency are in direct tension, though: every follower you add for read scale is another place a stale read can come from, so the capacity you bought is *stale-tolerant* capacity, and reads that must be fresh still have to go to the leader or to a lag-bounded follower. The architect's number to watch is the ratio of fresh-required reads to total reads — if it is small (most reads tolerate seconds of staleness), read replicas scale you cheaply; if it is large (most reads must be fresh), replicas help far less and you are really looking at a partitioning problem, not a replication one.

**Geo-locality.** You keep copies near your users so that a reader in Frankfurt does not pay a 90ms cross-Atlantic round trip to read data mastered in Virginia. This reason pushes you toward placing replicas in multiple regions — and the moment you do that, the latency of *keeping those replicas in sync* becomes the dominant cost in your design, because that sync now crosses a WAN. Geo-locality is the reason people reach for multi-leader, and it is also the reason multi-leader's conflict problem becomes unavoidable: if Frankfurt and Virginia both accept writes locally to stay fast, they *will* sometimes write the same row at the same time.

**Durability.** You keep copies so that a disk failure, a corrupt page, or a botched deploy on one node does not lose the data. Durability is about *not losing acknowledged writes ever*, which is a stricter bar than availability. A system can fail over availably and still violate durability if the promoted replica was missing the last few writes. Durability pushes you toward synchronous replication to at least one other node before you acknowledge — and that synchronous hop is a latency tax on every single write, paid forever, in exchange for never losing an acknowledged one.

Here is the senior framing: these four reasons rank differently for different data, *in the same system*. The orders table wants durability and write-availability above all (losing an order is unacceptable; a 5ms slower write is fine). The session cache wants read scale and read-availability and does not care about durability at all (a lost session just re-authenticates). The product catalog wants geo-locality and read scale and tolerates seconds of staleness happily. If you pick *one* replication strategy for all three because it is simpler operationally, you are over-paying for two of them and under-protecting the third. The architect's move is to rank the four reasons per dataset and let the ranking choose the strategy, which is exactly what the decision tree later in this post encodes.

## 2. Single-leader replication: the default, and why it is usually right

Single-leader replication — one node accepts all writes and streams them to read-only followers — is the boring default, and the boring default is correct far more often than ambitious engineers want it to be. Postgres, MySQL, MongoDB, Redis, and almost every relational database you will run in production use it. The reason it dominates is that it makes the hardest problem in replication — write conflicts — *disappear by construction*. If every write goes through one leader, the leader serializes them; there is a single, unambiguous order of writes, and therefore no conflict to resolve, ever. Multi-leader and leaderless both exist primarily to recover properties you give up when you reject single-leader, so you should understand exactly what single-leader gives you before you reject it.

The mechanics, briefly (the [sync/async replication deep-dive](/blog/software-development/database/database-replication-sync-async-logical-physical) has the full version): the leader writes each change to its write-ahead log, durably persists it, and streams that log to each follower, which replays it to reconstruct the same state. Reads can be served by the leader (always fresh) or by any follower (possibly stale). The single knob that matters most for an architect is *when the leader acknowledges the write to the client* relative to *when followers have it* — and that knob is the difference between synchronous, asynchronous, and semi-synchronous replication.

**Asynchronous replication** acknowledges the client the moment the leader has the write durably on its own disk, then ships it to followers in the background. This is the fast path: the write latency is just the leader's local fsync, typically a few milliseconds. It is also the lossy path: if the leader dies after acknowledging a write but before any follower received it, that write is gone, and a promoted follower will come up without it. The replication lag — how far behind the followers are — is unbounded and grows under load, during long transactions on the follower, and during network hiccups. Async is the default in most systems because it is fast and because the loss window is usually small. "Usually" is doing a lot of work in that sentence.

**Synchronous replication** holds the client acknowledgement until at least one follower confirms it has the write durably. Now the write latency includes a round trip to that follower — a few milliseconds in the same datacenter, 30 to 90+ milliseconds across regions. In exchange, you have a guarantee: every acknowledged write exists on at least two nodes, so a failover loses nothing. Synchronous replication is the durability play, and it has a vicious failure mode of its own that we will get to: if the synchronous follower is *down*, the leader cannot acknowledge *any* writes, so a single replica failure stalls all writes. That is the availability paradox of synchronous replication, and it is why nobody runs fully synchronous in practice.

**Semi-synchronous replication** is the pragmatic middle: the leader waits for one follower to acknowledge (durability for that write) but the configuration *falls back to async* if no follower responds within a timeout (availability when a replica is down). This is what most serious single-leader deployments actually run — MySQL's `rpl_semi_sync` with a timeout, or Postgres synchronous replication with multiple candidates and `synchronous_standby_names` set so any one of several can satisfy the requirement. It gives you "lose at most the in-flight write during the failover instant" instead of "lose the last N seconds," at the cost of one round trip on the steady-state path. Figure 2 lays the trade-offs of all the strategies side by side, and you can already see single-leader sync and async as two distinct rows with opposite failover-safety and write-latency profiles.

![Matrix comparing single-leader synchronous, single-leader asynchronous, multi-leader, and leaderless quorum replication across write latency, read scale, conflict handling, failover safety, and consistency](/imgs/blogs/replication-strategies-and-their-failure-modes-2.webp)

The senior point about single-leader is this: you do not pick "sync" or "async" for the *database*, you pick it *per write path*, often within the same database. Postgres lets you set `synchronous_commit` per transaction. So the order-placement transaction runs `synchronous_commit = on` (or `remote_apply`) and pays the round trip for durability; the analytics-event insert runs `synchronous_commit = off` and takes the fast lossy path because losing an analytics event during a failover is invisible. Same leader, two durability tiers, chosen by the cost of losing that specific write. That per-operation thinking is the whole game.

```sql
-- Per-transaction durability tier in Postgres on a single leader.
-- Money path: wait for a standby to confirm before acknowledging.
BEGIN;
SET LOCAL synchronous_commit = remote_apply;   -- durable on a replica before COMMIT returns
INSERT INTO orders (id, user_id, amount_cents, status)
VALUES ('ord_8f2a', 91823, 4999, 'placed');
COMMIT;   -- returns only after a synchronous standby has applied it

-- Telemetry path: fire-and-forget, do not pay the replica round trip.
BEGIN;
SET LOCAL synchronous_commit = off;            -- acked on leader fsync only; lossy on failover
INSERT INTO page_views (session_id, url, ts) VALUES ('s_31', '/pricing', now());
COMMIT;   -- returns at local commit; may be lost if the leader dies before shipping
```

## 3. Replication lag and the read-your-writes anomaly

Asynchronous followers lag, and replication lag is not a bug — it is the defining property of asynchronous replication and the source of the most common user-visible anomaly in any read-replica system. A user updates their profile name, the write lands on the leader and is acknowledged, the user's next page load reads from a follower that is 600 milliseconds behind, and the user sees their *old* name. To the user, their write was lost. It was not lost; it just is not on that follower *yet*. This is the **read-your-writes** (also called read-after-write) consistency violation, and figure 7 shows both the broken version and the fix.

![Before and after comparison showing a user reading a stale profile name from a lagging follower versus pinning the user's reads to the leader for ten seconds so they always see their own write](/imgs/blogs/replication-strategies-and-their-failure-modes-7.webp)

The reason this matters so much to an architect is that read-your-writes violations are *invisible in testing and obvious in production*. In a test, the follower is microseconds behind, so you never reproduce it. In production at load, the follower is hundreds of milliseconds to seconds behind, and every user who reads their own recent write hits it. The fix is not to make followers faster (you cannot fully win that race); the fix is to *route* reads that need to see a recent write away from lagging followers. The standard techniques, in rough order of cost:

- **Read from the leader for a window after a write.** After a user writes, route *that user's* reads to the leader for the next N seconds (10s is a common choice, comfortably above your p99 lag). Cheap, effective, and the most common production approach. The cost is leader read load, which is fine because only recently-writing users hit it.
- **Read from a follower whose lag you have measured below a threshold.** Track each follower's lag (Postgres exposes `pg_last_xact_replay_timestamp`, MySQL `Seconds_Behind_Source`) and route consistency-sensitive reads only to followers under, say, 200ms of lag, falling back to the leader if none qualify.
- **Logical timestamps / monotonic reads.** The client remembers the log position (LSN) of its last write and demands a follower that has replayed at least that far. This gives true read-your-writes without always hitting the leader, at the cost of plumbing the LSN through your application and replica-selection layer.

#### Worked example: computing the stale-read window from lag and traffic

Let us make the staleness concrete, because "the follower is a bit behind" is not an engineering statement. Suppose you run a single leader with five asynchronous read followers. The leader sustains 12,000 writes/second. Your monitoring says replication lag is p50 = 40ms, p99 = 1.2 seconds, and worst-case (during the daily bulk import) = 2.3 seconds. You serve 200,000 reads/second across the five followers, and an estimated 3% of reads are a user reading data they themselves wrote in the last 5 seconds.

How many stale reads per second do you serve if you do *nothing*? A read served by a follower is stale for a given record if the read arrives after the write was acknowledged but before that follower replayed it. The vulnerable population is the read-your-recent-write traffic: 3% of 200,000 = 6,000 reads/second land in the danger zone. The probability one of those is stale depends on how its timing falls inside the lag distribution. At p50 lag of 40ms, a read issued more than 40ms after the write is fine on a median follower; a read issued *within* 40ms of the write (a fast double-take — submit then immediately reload) is stale on roughly half the followers. Realistically a small but non-trivial fraction — call it the share of these reads arriving within one lag-window of their write — turns stale. Even at a conservative 5% of the 6,000, that is **300 stale reads/second**, every second, all day. That is not a corner case; that is a steady drizzle of "the site lost my change" support tickets.

Now apply the fix: route reads to the leader for 10 seconds after a write, for that user. The 6,000 read-your-recent-write reads/second move to the leader. The leader, which was handling only writes, now also takes 6,000 reads/second — a 50% increase in leader load, but these are cheap point reads and the leader has headroom. Stale reads in the read-your-writes population drop to **zero**. The remaining 194,000 reads/second still hit followers and can be stale by up to 2.3 seconds, but those are reads of data the user did *not* just write, where 2.3 seconds of staleness is invisible (someone else's like count, a slightly old catalog price). You spent 50% more leader read capacity to eliminate the only staleness anyone notices. That is the trade, quantified, and that is the sentence you say in the design review.

The general formula worth carrying in your head: **maximum stale-read window ≈ worst-case replication lag**, and the *rate* of noticeable stale reads ≈ (fraction of reads that are read-your-recent-write) × (read QPS) × (fraction of those arriving within a lag-window). Drive any factor to zero and the problem disappears. Routing recent-writer reads to the leader drives the first factor to zero for the population that cares.

## 4. Failover: where async replication quietly loses your writes

Failover is the moment single-leader replication's bill comes due. The leader dies — a kernel panic, a disk failure, an availability-zone outage, a botched deploy — and the system must promote a follower to be the new leader so writes can resume. The mechanics of detection and promotion are well understood (a consensus layer or an orchestrator like Patroni, Orchestrator, or a managed service's control plane notices the leader is unreachable, picks the most up-to-date follower, and points the write traffic at it). The *architecture* question is not how promotion works; it is **how much data the promotion loses**, and the answer is determined entirely by your sync-versus-async choice made long before.

With **synchronous (or semi-sync) replication**, the promoted follower is guaranteed to have every acknowledged write, because acknowledgement *required* a follower to have it. Failover loses nothing. With **asynchronous replication**, the promoted follower has only the writes that happened to have streamed to it before the leader died — which is everything up to (leader's log position − replication lag). If the leader was 1.8 seconds ahead of the best follower at the moment it died, the promotion silently discards 1.8 seconds of acknowledged writes. The clients that issued those writes received a success response. The data is gone. Nobody gets an error. Figure 3 contrasts the two failover paths.

![Before and after diagram contrasting asynchronous failover that promotes a replica missing the last 1.8 seconds of acknowledged writes against synchronous failover where the new leader has every acknowledged write](/imgs/blogs/replication-strategies-and-their-failure-modes-3.webp)

This is the failure mode that bites hardest because it is *silent and delayed*. The async failover completes in seconds, the dashboards go green, the incident channel says "failover successful, writes resumed," and everyone moves on. Three hours later, a customer support ticket arrives: "I placed an order, I got a confirmation, my card was charged, and now the order doesn't exist." Reconciliation finds 340 orders that were acknowledged but never made it to the new leader. They were in the 1.8-second window. This is not hypothetical; it is the textbook consequence of async failover under lag, and it is why a senior's very first question about any single-leader design is "what is the failover data-loss window, and is it zero?"

There is a second-order tuning trap in failover worth naming, because it pits two failures against each other: the **detection timeout**. The orchestrator decides the leader is dead after it fails health checks for some interval. Set that interval too long (say 30 seconds) and a real leader death causes a 30-second write outage while everyone waits for the orchestrator to act. Set it too short (say 2 seconds) and a transient blip — a GC pause, a brief network hiccup, a slow disk flush — gets misread as death, triggering an *unnecessary* failover that, if the old leader was not actually dead, is exactly the setup for split-brain. So the detection timeout is a direct trade between *availability during a real failure* (shorter is better) and *false-positive failovers that risk split-brain* (longer is better). The senior resolution is not to pick a magic number but to *change the question*: make false-positive failovers safe via fencing (so an unnecessary promotion cannot cause split-brain) and only then tighten the timeout for fast recovery. Without fencing, you are stuck choosing between slow recovery and dangerous twitchiness; with fencing, you get fast recovery *and* safety, which is why fencing is the unlock that makes aggressive failover tuning possible at all.

The architect's defenses, in order:

- **Use semi-synchronous replication for the writes you cannot lose.** This makes the failover loss window *zero for those writes* because they were never acknowledged until a replica had them. You pay the round-trip latency only on the durable-tier writes (see the per-transaction `synchronous_commit` pattern above).
- **Make failover wait for the most-up-to-date replica and let it catch up.** A good orchestrator promotes the follower with the highest replayed LSN and, before accepting writes, pulls any remaining log from a more-advanced replica if one exists. This shrinks the window but cannot eliminate it under pure async — a write that reached *no* follower is unrecoverable.
- **Keep the old leader's WAL and reconcile.** When the dead leader comes back, its WAL contains the lost writes. Mature systems (and Postgres's `pg_rewind`) can diff the old leader against the new timeline and surface the writes that were dropped, so you can at least *detect* and replay or refund them rather than discover them via support tickets.
- **Never auto-failover on async without a fence.** Which brings us to split-brain.

## 5. Split-brain: when two nodes both think they are the leader

Split-brain is the failure mode that turns a replication strategy into a data-corruption incident, and it is the one most worth your fear. It happens when a network partition makes the leader *unreachable from the failover orchestrator* but the leader is *still alive and still accepting writes*. The orchestrator, seeing no leader, promotes a follower. Now you have two leaders: the original (still taking writes from clients that can reach it) and the new one (taking writes from clients that the orchestrator repointed). Both are accepting writes. Both think they are authoritative. When the partition heals, you have two divergent histories of the same data and no correct way to merge them — one side's writes *must* be discarded. Figure 4 traces the timeline.

![Timeline of a split-brain incident where a partition hides the leader, a failover promotes a replica, both nodes accept writes for over a minute, the partition heals, one side is rolled back losing data, and fencing is added afterward](/imgs/blogs/replication-strategies-and-their-failure-modes-4.webp)

Walk the timeline. At T+0 a partition isolates the leader from the orchestrator (but not necessarily from all clients — this is the crucial, evil detail). At T+15s the orchestrator's health checks time out and it promotes a follower. From T+20s to T+90s, both nodes accept writes: clients on one side of the partition write to the old leader, clients on the other side write to the new leader. At T+90s the partition heals. Now both nodes claim to be leader and their data has diverged — the same row has been updated to different values on each side, new rows exist on one side that the other never saw. At T+95s the system (or a human) must choose a winner, and the loser's writes from those 70 seconds are *rolled back and lost*. Sixty to ninety seconds of acknowledged writes, gone, with no clean way to even enumerate what was lost without a painful diff.

The defense against split-brain is **fencing**: making it *impossible* for a demoted or partitioned node to keep accepting writes. The mechanisms (covered mechanically in the [consensus deep-dive](/blog/software-development/database/raft-consensus-from-scratch), and which you should reach for here) are:

- **Quorum-based leadership.** A node may only act as leader if it can confirm a majority of the cluster agrees it is leader. A leader on the minority side of a partition *cannot* get a majority, so it steps down and refuses writes. This is exactly what Raft and Paxos give you, and it is why consensus-backed systems (etcd, ZooKeeper, CockroachDB, Spanner) do not split-brain: a partitioned-off leader cannot achieve quorum, so it cannot write. We forward to the [consensus and coordination post](/blog/software-development/system-design/consensus-and-coordination-in-distributed-systems) for how to wire this into a design.
- **STONITH ("shoot the other node in the head").** Before promoting a new leader, the orchestrator forcibly *kills* the old one — power-fences it via IPMI, detaches its network, or revokes its storage lease — so it physically cannot keep writing. Brutal, effective, and standard in high-availability clusters.
- **Leases / fencing tokens.** The leader holds a time-bounded lease that it must renew through the coordination service. If it cannot renew (because it is partitioned off), the lease expires and it stops accepting writes *on its own*, before any new leader is promoted. Each write carries a monotonically increasing fencing token, and the storage layer rejects any write bearing a token older than the highest it has seen — so even a zombie leader's late writes are rejected downstream.

The senior rule is unconditional: **never run automatic failover on a single-leader system without a fencing mechanism.** Automatic failover without fencing is not high availability; it is an automated split-brain generator. The most expensive replication incidents in the public record are split-brains where promotion happened but the old leader was never fenced. This is the lesson behind the famous GitHub October 2018 incident, which we examine in the case studies.

## 6. Multi-leader replication: buying write-locality, paying in conflicts

You reach for multi-leader replication when single-leader's one-write-region constraint becomes the bottleneck — almost always for **geography**. If you have users on three continents and you want every user's writes to be fast, you cannot route all writes to a single leader in one region, because a user in Tokyo writing to a leader in Virginia eats a 150ms+ round trip on every write. Multi-leader puts a leader in each region; each region accepts writes locally and fast, and the leaders replicate to each other asynchronously in the background. The win is real and large: local write latency everywhere, and continued write-availability in a region even if it is cut off from the others. The price is the problem single-leader made disappear, now back with interest: **write conflicts**.

A conflict happens when two leaders accept writes to the same data concurrently — before either has seen the other's write. User in Frankfurt sets the document title to "Q3 Plan" at the same wall-clock moment a collaborator in Virginia sets it to "Q3 Roadmap." Both writes succeed locally and fast. When the regions sync, each leader discovers a write it never saw, to the same field, with no agreed order. Which one wins? There is no *correct* answer — both users intended their value — only a *resolution policy*, and every policy is a different way of being wrong. The mechanism deep-dive on [multi-leader and leaderless replication](/blog/software-development/database/distributed-replication-leader-multi-leader-leaderless) covers how the writes propagate; the architect's job is choosing the resolution policy and understanding what it silently destroys.

**Last-write-wins (LWW).** Attach a timestamp to each write; on conflict, keep the one with the higher timestamp, discard the other. Dead simple, and the default in Cassandra and many systems. The catastrophe is twofold. First, "last" depends on *clocks*, and clocks on different machines skew — even with NTP, by milliseconds to tens of milliseconds, and worse during clock-sync glitches. So "last write wins" really means "the write from the machine with the faster-running clock wins," which has nothing to do with which write the user actually made last. Second, LWW *silently discards* the losing write. The Frankfurt user's "Q3 Plan" does not produce an error or a merge prompt; it simply vanishes, and that user is now editing a document that secretly reverted their change. LWW is acceptable only when losing a concurrent write is genuinely fine (a cache entry, a presence indicator), and dangerous everywhere else.

**Version vectors (vector clocks).** Instead of a wall-clock timestamp, each write carries a vector that records how many writes each replica has seen. This lets the system *detect* whether two writes were truly concurrent (neither saw the other) or causally ordered (one happened after the other). Causally ordered writes are applied in order with no conflict. Genuinely concurrent writes are *surfaced as a conflict* — the system keeps both ("siblings") and hands the decision to the application or the user ("you and a collaborator both edited this; here are both versions"). Version vectors do not *resolve* conflicts; they *honestly detect* them, which is strictly better than LWW silently losing data, at the cost of complexity and of forcing your application to handle siblings.

**CRDTs (Conflict-free Replicated Data Types).** Data structures designed so that concurrent updates *always* merge to a deterministic, sensible result with no coordination — a counter that sums all increments, a set whose union is taken, a sequence (for collaborative text) that interleaves edits by construction. CRDTs make conflicts *impossible by design* for the operations they support, which is why they power collaborative editors and offline-first apps. The price is that you must model your data as a CRDT, which is not free and does not fit every domain — a CRDT counter is great, but "the document title is a single string and the last human intent should win" is not naturally a CRDT.

The senior framing of multi-leader: **adopt it only when geography or offline operation forces your hand, and then choose your conflict policy by asking "what does it cost to silently lose a concurrent write here?"** If the answer is "nothing" (presence, a counter), LWW or a CRDT is fine. If the answer is "a user's work disappears" (a document, a setting, an inventory count), LWW is malpractice — use version vectors to surface the conflict, or a CRDT to merge it correctly, or do not use multi-leader for that data at all. Multi-leader is not a general-purpose upgrade over single-leader; it is a specialized tool for a specific constraint, and most systems that "need multi-leader" actually need single-leader plus read replicas plus a CDN.

```python
# Last-write-wins is a clock-comparison. The bug is that it trusts clocks.
def resolve_lww(write_a, write_b):
    # 'ts' comes from each writer's local wall clock; NTP skew makes this a lie
    # about which write the user actually made last. The loser vanishes silently.
    return write_a if write_a.ts >= write_b.ts else write_b

# Version vectors DETECT concurrency instead of guessing an order.
def compare(vv_a, vv_b):
    a_dominates = all(vv_a.get(k, 0) >= vv_b.get(k, 0) for k in set(vv_a) | set(vv_b))
    b_dominates = all(vv_b.get(k, 0) >= vv_a.get(k, 0) for k in set(vv_a) | set(vv_b))
    if a_dominates and not b_dominates:
        return "a_newer"          # causal: apply a, no conflict
    if b_dominates and not a_dominates:
        return "b_newer"          # causal: apply b, no conflict
    return "concurrent"           # neither saw the other -> keep BOTH as siblings
```

## 7. Leaderless replication and quorum math

Leaderless replication, the Dynamo-style approach used by Cassandra, ScyllaDB, Riak, and DynamoDB's internals, deletes the leader as a concept entirely. There is no node that owns writes. Instead, the client (or a coordinator node acting for it) sends each write to *several* replicas in parallel and considers the write successful once *W* of them acknowledge. Reads are sent to *several* replicas in parallel and the client takes the answer once *R* of them respond, using version metadata to pick the freshest value among the responses. There is no leader to fail, no failover, no split-brain in the single-leader sense — the failure point you were worried about simply does not exist. What you get instead is a *tunable* consistency model governed by the relationship between N (replicas per key), W (write quorum), and R (read quorum). The [quorums, anti-entropy, and read repair deep-dive](/blog/software-development/database/quorums-anti-entropy-and-read-repair) covers the convergence mechanics; here is the architect's tuning math.

The core inequality is **R + W > N**. If the read quorum and write quorum overlap — that is, if the set of R nodes you read from is guaranteed to share at least one node with the set of W nodes any write went to — then every read sees at least one replica that has the latest write, and (with version metadata to pick the newest) the read returns fresh data. If R + W ≤ N, the read and write sets can be *disjoint*, and a read can entirely miss the latest write, returning stale data. So R + W > N is the dividing line between "strongly consistent quorum reads" and "possibly stale reads," and it is the first thing to check on any leaderless design. Figure 5 lays out the common N=5 configurations and what each one tolerates.

![Matrix showing N equals five quorum configurations from write-all to read-all, whether R plus W exceeds N, and the read latency, write latency, and node failures tolerated by each](/imgs/blogs/replication-strategies-and-their-failure-modes-5.webp)

The genius of the N/R/W knobs is that you can *slide the cost* between reads and writes while keeping consistency, by changing where you split the quorum:

- **Read-heavy workload → small R, large W.** Set R=1, W=N. Reads hit one node and are fast; writes must reach all N and are slow. Since R + W = N + 1 > N, reads are still consistent. You have made the rare writes pay so the abundant reads fly. Cost: a single node being down blocks *all writes* (W=N tolerates zero write failures), which is fragile.
- **Write-heavy workload → large R, small W.** Set W=1, R=N. Writes hit one node and are fast; reads must reach all N and are slow. Still consistent (R + W > N). You have made the rare reads pay so the abundant writes fly. Cost: a single node down blocks all reads.
- **Balanced → R = W = ⌈(N+1)/2⌉.** For N=5, R=W=3. Both reads and writes contact a majority; both pay a moderate latency; the system tolerates ⌊N/2⌋ = 2 node failures for *both* reads and writes while staying consistent. This is the default for good reason — it is the configuration that survives failures symmetrically.

#### Worked example: tuning N, R, W for durability and read latency

You are designing the storage for a user-preferences service on a leaderless store. Requirements: the data must survive the loss of any **two** nodes without becoming unavailable or losing a write (durability target), reads must be strongly consistent (a user must see their own preference change immediately), and the workload is **read-heavy** at 50,000 reads/second versus 2,000 writes/second. You have a per-node read latency of p99 = 4ms and a cross-rack hop of ~1ms. What N, R, W do you pick, and what do you get?

Start with durability and availability: to tolerate losing any 2 nodes while still forming both a read and a write quorum, you need N large enough that a quorum survives 2 failures. With N=5, a majority quorum of 3 survives 2 failures (3 of the remaining 3 nodes can still form the quorum). So **N=5**. Now consistency: you need R + W > 5, so R + W ≥ 6. Now optimize for the read-heavy traffic: you want R as small as the consistency and availability constraints allow, pushing the cost onto the 25x-rarer writes. The smallest R that still tolerates 2 node failures on reads is R=3 (with R=3 you can still read if 2 nodes are down). To keep R + W ≥ 6 with R=3, you need W ≥ 3, so **W=3**. That gives **N=5, R=3, W=3** — the balanced configuration — tolerating 2 failures on both paths, strongly consistent, with reads contacting 3 nodes in parallel.

What is the read latency? The read waits for the 3rd of 3 parallel responses (R=3 means you need all 3 of the quorum you queried, or the 3rd-fastest if you query more). Reading 3 nodes in parallel, the latency is the *slowest* of the 3, which at p99 = 4ms per node and parallel dispatch is ~4ms plus the rack hop, call it ~5ms p99. That meets a tight read SLO. Could you do better? If you relaxed the durability target to tolerate only 1 failure, you could use N=3, R=2, W=2 — fewer nodes to coordinate, slightly cheaper — but you would survive only one node loss, violating the stated requirement. If you wanted *even faster* reads and were willing to make writes much slower, you could go R=1, W=5: reads hit one node (~4ms, but now only the *fastest* matters so effectively faster tail behavior on reads) while writes must reach all 5 (slow, and zero write-failure tolerance). For a service where a single node down must not block 2,000 writes/second of preference updates, W=5 is too brittle. So **N=5, R=3, W=3** is the defensible answer: it hits the durability target, gives strong reads, and keeps both paths available through 2 failures. That reasoning — start from durability and availability requirements, derive the consistency constraint, then optimize the split toward the dominant traffic — is exactly the chain you walk in the review.

There is a critical asterisk on quorum consistency that separates people who have read the Dynamo paper from people who have *operated* a Dynamo-style store: **R + W > N does not actually guarantee you read the latest write**, because of **sloppy quorums**. When nodes from the "home" replica set for a key are unavailable, a sloppy quorum lets the write be accepted by *other, healthy* nodes (hinted handoff) so the write does not fail. But those nodes are not in the read quorum's home set, so a subsequent read of R home nodes can miss the hinted-handoff write entirely — the W nodes and the R nodes did not overlap, even though R + W > N, because the W set included nodes outside the home group. Sloppy quorums trade strict consistency for write availability during failures, and they are *on by default* in many systems. If you need the R + W > N guarantee to actually hold, you must disable sloppy quorums (and accept that writes can fail when home nodes are down). This is the kind of footgun that makes "we configured a quorum, so we're consistent" a sentence a senior never says without checking the sloppy-quorum setting.

## 8. Convergence: read repair and anti-entropy

Leaderless systems let replicas drift — a write that reached the W quorum but not the other replicas leaves those other replicas stale until something fixes them. Two mechanisms drag replicas back into agreement, and an architect should know both because they have different latency and load profiles. Figure 9 shows the read-repair path in action.

![Diagram of a leaderless quorum read where two nodes return a fresh version and one returns a stale version, the read quorum picks the fresh value, and read repair writes the fresh version back to the stale node](/imgs/blogs/replication-strategies-and-their-failure-modes-9.webp)

**Read repair** fixes staleness *as a side effect of reads*. When a read contacts R replicas and finds they disagree — two return version 7, one returns version 5 — the coordinator returns the newest (v7) to the client *and* writes v7 back to the stale replica, repairing it inline. This is elegant because frequently-read data heals itself for free on the read path. Its weakness is symmetric: *rarely-read* data never gets repaired, because no read ever touches it to notice the divergence. Data that is written, replicated to W nodes, and then not read for months can have stale replicas sitting around the whole time. If one of the fresh replicas dies before that data is ever read, the staleness can become a *loss* — which is exactly how deleted records come back to life (a tombstone that was not propagated to a replica, that replica still holding the old value, and read repair under certain orderings resurrecting it). This is why tombstones in Cassandra have a `gc_grace_seconds` that must exceed the repair interval.

**Anti-entropy** is the background sweep that fixes *everything*, read or not. A background process compares replicas (efficiently, using Merkle trees so it does not have to ship all the data — it ships hashes and only reconciles the subtrees that differ) and repairs any divergence it finds. This catches the rarely-read data that read repair misses. The cost is that anti-entropy is *expensive* — it consumes CPU, disk I/O, and network on an ongoing basis, and a poorly-tuned repair can saturate a cluster. The architectural decision is the *repair interval*: too frequent and you waste capacity; too infrequent and you risk the resurrection-of-deleted-data window exceeding `gc_grace_seconds`. Senior teams schedule full repairs to complete comfortably within the grace window (e.g. a weekly repair with a 10-day grace) and monitor that repairs are actually finishing. A repair that silently stalls is a latent data-loss bug.

The replication path that all this fixes has more layers than people picture, and each layer is a place lag accumulates or a write can be lost. Figure 8 stacks them.

![Stack of the replication path layers from a client write down through the leader write-ahead log, log shipping, the cross-region network, and replica replay, ending at the point the write becomes visible to reads](/imgs/blogs/replication-strategies-and-their-failure-modes-8.webp)

Reading figure 8 as a mental model of where your replication lag *comes from* is worth the architect's time: the client write is 1ms, the leader's WAL fsync is a few ms, log shipping adds a few ms, and then the cross-region network hop — 90ms across an ocean — dwarfs everything else, before the replica even replays and fsyncs. The lag a user experiences is the *sum* down that stack, and the dominant term for any cross-region replica is the WAN hop. This is why "just add a replica in another region for read-locality" silently changes your consistency story: that replica is structurally ~90ms+ behind, so reads from it are *at least* 90ms stale, always, by physics. You cannot tune that away; you can only decide whether the data tolerates it.

## 9. The latency tax and availability paradox of synchronous replication

Synchronous replication is the durability play — it makes the failover loss window zero — but it charges two distinct prices, and a senior names both before recommending it. The first price is obvious once you say it out loud; the second is the one that surprises teams in production.

The first price is the **latency tax**. Every synchronous write pays a round trip to the synchronous follower *before* it can be acknowledged. In the same datacenter that is a few milliseconds; across an availability zone it is a millisecond or two; across regions it is 30 to 90+ milliseconds, every write, forever, on the steady-state path that runs 99.99% of the time. This is not a failure-time cost; it is a *normal-operation* cost. If your checkout flow has a 200ms p99 budget and you put the order write on a cross-region synchronous replica, you have just spent 90ms of that budget on durability — which may be the right call, but it is a call, and it competes with every other thing in the budget. The discipline is to put the synchronous follower as *close* as durability allows: a synchronous replica in another availability zone of the *same* region gives you durability against a single-machine or single-AZ failure at a ~1-2ms tax, while a cross-region synchronous replica gives you durability against a whole-region failure at a ~90ms tax. Most systems want the cheap same-region-other-AZ synchronous replica for the loss-window-zero guarantee, plus a *cheap async* cross-region replica for disaster recovery, rather than an expensive cross-region synchronous one. That placement — sync within the region, async across regions — is the single most common "optimized" single-leader topology, and it is optimized precisely because it buys zero-loss failover at AZ-failure granularity without paying the WAN tax on every write.

The second price is the **availability paradox**, and it is the one that bites teams who set up "real" synchronous replication and then have an outage caused *by their own durability feature*. Fully synchronous replication says a write cannot be acknowledged until the synchronous follower confirms it. So what happens when the synchronous follower is *down*? The leader cannot get the confirmation, so it cannot acknowledge the write, so it *blocks all writes* — the entire system becomes write-unavailable because a *follower*, not the leader, failed. You added a replica to improve availability and instead created a second single point of failure: now *either* node going down stops writes. This is the synchronous-replication availability paradox, and it is why nobody runs strictly synchronous replication with a single synchronous standby in production.

The escapes are exactly the ones we have been building toward. **Semi-synchronous with a timeout** falls back to async if the synchronous follower does not respond within (say) 100ms, so a down follower degrades you to async (lossy on failover) rather than to fully unavailable — you trade the durability guarantee for availability *only while the follower is down*, which is the right trade because a down follower is rare and a write outage is intolerable. **Multiple synchronous candidates** (Postgres `synchronous_standby_names = 'ANY 1 (s1, s2, s3)'`) require *any one* of several standbys to confirm, so a single standby being down does not block writes — you only lose the guarantee if a *majority* of candidates are down simultaneously, which is the same robustness a quorum gives you. This is, not coincidentally, the same insight that makes leaderless quorums robust: requiring "any W of N" instead of "this specific one" is what turns a brittle single-dependency into a failure-tolerant quorum.

```bash
# Postgres: durable on ANY 1 of three standbys, so one standby down does NOT block writes.
# This dodges the synchronous-replication availability paradox while keeping zero-loss failover.
synchronous_standby_names = 'ANY 1 (standby_az_b, standby_az_c, standby_dr)'
synchronous_commit = remote_apply   # default tier; per-txn override for the lossy fast path

# MySQL semi-sync with a timeout: degrade to async if no replica acks in 100ms (availability over durability while a replica is down).
rpl_semi_sync_source_enabled       = ON
rpl_semi_sync_source_timeout       = 100      # ms; after this, fall back to async automatically
rpl_semi_sync_source_wait_for_replica_count = 1
```

The senior summary of synchronous replication: it is the only way to get a zero-loss failover, you should use it for the writes you cannot lose, you should place the synchronous follower as close as your failure-domain requirement allows to minimize the tax, and you must configure it as "any 1 of several" with a fallback so that your durability feature does not become your availability outage. State all four of those when you propose it, and the review has nothing left to ask.

## 10. The trade-off matrix and choosing a strategy

Here is the decision laid out as a matrix — the artifact you put on the screen in a design review. Every strategy wins a column and pays in another; there is no row that wins everything, and a design that claims one is hiding a cost.

| Strategy | Write latency | Read scale | Conflict handling | Failover safety | Consistency | When it wins |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Single-leader, sync/semi-sync** | High (replica round trip) | Good (many followers) | None needed (one writer) | Safe — zero loss on failover | Strong on leader; tunable on followers | Money, orders, anything you cannot lose; one write region |
| **Single-leader, async** | Low (local fsync) | Good (many followers) | None needed (one writer) | Risky — loses last N seconds | Read-your-writes needs routing | Default; high write throughput where small loss is tolerable |
| **Multi-leader** | Low (local region) | Good | Hard — conflicts are inherent | No failover (each region independent) | Eventual; conflict policy decides | Multi-region writes, offline/collaborative apps |
| **Leaderless quorum** | Medium (R+W coordination) | Tunable (slide R/W) | Version-based (LWW / vectors) | No single point to fail over | Tunable via R+W>N | No-failover availability; tunable per-op consistency |

The matrix shows the shape, but the *order* in which you ask the questions is what makes the choice fast and defensible. Figure 6 encodes that order as a decision tree: the first fork is not about latency or scale, it is about **how many regions need to accept writes**, because that single answer eliminates two of the three families immediately.

![Decision tree starting from how many write regions are needed, branching to single-leader for one region then to sync or async by consistency need, and to multi-leader or leaderless for many regions by conflict-merge ability](/imgs/blogs/replication-strategies-and-their-failure-modes-6.webp)

Walk the tree. **Do you need more than one region to accept writes?** If no — and most systems do not, because most write traffic can tolerate routing to one region — you are in single-leader, and the only remaining question is sync versus async, decided by whether you can tolerate losing the last few seconds of writes on a failover (orders → semi-sync; analytics → async). If yes, you genuinely need multi-region writes, then: **can you merge concurrent writes sensibly?** If your data is mergeable (counters, sets, collaborative text → CRDTs) or you can tolerate surfacing conflicts (version vectors), multi-leader fits. If you would rather eliminate the leader-as-failure-point entirely and tune consistency per operation, leaderless quorum fits. Notice what the tree does *not* do: it does not start with "what's the fastest" or "what scales best," because every strategy can be made to scale reads. The binding constraint is almost always the write topology and the cost of the failure mode, and that is what the first fork captures.

The **optimization lens** ties the placement decisions together. Once you have a family, the levers are: *how many replicas* (more replicas = more read scale and durability, but more cost and, for sync, more write latency and more nodes that can block a quorum), *where to put them* (replicas near readers cut read latency but a cross-region sync replica taxes every write ~90ms), and *sync versus async per write path* (durable tier pays the round trip, lossy tier does not). The way you *measure* the win is concrete: replication lag p50/p99 (drives your stale-read window), failover data-loss window in seconds (must be zero for the durable tier), write p99 (the sync tax), read p99 per follower, and the rate of noticeable stale reads computed as in the worked example. A senior does not say "we'll add replicas for performance"; a senior says "we'll add two same-region async followers to take read p99 from 12ms to 5ms and absorb 3x read QPS, keep one semi-sync standby so the order-path failover loss window is zero, and *not* add a sync cross-region replica because the 90ms write tax fails our checkout SLO — that region gets an async read replica only."

## 11. Stress-testing the design: what breaks at a region outage, a partition, a lagging promotion

A design is only as good as its behavior at the edges, so before you ship a replication design you walk it through the three stress tests that map directly onto the failure modes above. This is the part juniors skip and seniors lead with.

**Stress test 1: a full region outage.** A region — leader and its same-region followers — goes dark. For a single-leader design with all replicas in one region, this is total outage: no leader, no followers, nothing to promote. The fix the stress test forces is *at least one replica in another region*, which then raises the failover question: is that cross-region replica async (then a failover to it loses the lag window, which across a WAN at high write rates can be seconds) or sync (then every steady-state write was paying the 90ms tax all along)? There is no free option; the stress test makes you choose and price it. For a multi-leader or leaderless design spanning regions, a region outage is survivable by construction — the surviving regions keep accepting writes — which is precisely the property you adopted those topologies for. The outage stress test is what reveals whether your replica *placement* matches your availability requirement, and it usually reveals that a "highly available" single-region design is nothing of the sort.

**Stress test 2: a network partition.** The cluster splits into two groups that cannot talk. For single-leader with automatic failover, this is the split-brain trap from section 5 unless you have fencing — so the stress test's pass condition is "show me the fencing mechanism (quorum leadership, STONITH, or leases) that prevents two leaders." If you cannot name it, the design fails the partition test. For leaderless quorum, a partition means each side can serve only the keys for which it holds a quorum; the minority side cannot form a quorum for most keys and so reads/writes there fail (consistency preserved, availability reduced) — which is the [CAP](/blog/software-development/database/cap-theorem-and-pacelc) choice made concrete. For multi-leader, both sides keep accepting writes (high availability) and you *bank* a pile of conflicts to resolve on heal — so the partition test for multi-leader is "show me the conflict-resolution policy and prove it does not silently lose a user's work." Each topology has a different pass condition, and stating it is the deliverable.

**Stress test 3: a lagging replica gets promoted.** Even with everything configured well, the most-up-to-date replica at failover time might still be seconds behind (it was lagging under load). What happens? Under async, the promotion loses that lag window of acknowledged writes. The stress test forces you to put a *number* on it: measure your replication lag p99 *under peak load* (not at rest, where it lies), and that number *is* your worst-case failover loss window unless you have semi-sync on the durable path. If your lag p99 under load is 1.5 seconds and your peak write rate on the durable path is 800 writes/second, a worst-case promotion loses up to ~1,200 acknowledged writes. If those are orders, that is unacceptable and the stress test has just justified semi-sync replication for the order path, with a hard number to defend it. This is the test that turns "we have replication" into "our failover loss window for orders is zero and for analytics is bounded by 1.5s, and here is why that's the right split."

The discipline across all three: name the failure, name the topology's response, name the pass condition, and put a number on the cost. A design review that has walked these three for a replication design is a design review that will not be surprised at 3am.

## 12. Case studies: the failures, as they actually happened

Abstract failure modes land harder when you see them as named incidents. Three teach the three topologies' signature failures.

**GitHub, October 2018 — split-brain on a single-leader failover.** A routine network maintenance event caused a 43-second connectivity loss between GitHub's US East Coast and US West Coast data centers. The MySQL orchestration layer, seeing the primary as unreachable, promoted a replica in the other region — but the original primary was still alive and still taking writes from clients on its side. For those 43 seconds, both coasts had a primary accepting writes, and the cross-region replication between them was now flowing in a way that left the two clusters diverged. When connectivity returned, the system could not safely reconcile the two write histories automatically; reconciling them required taking the site into degraded mode for over 24 hours to repair data integrity by hand and replay. The concrete lesson is exactly section 5's rule: automatic failover across a network boundary, without a fencing mechanism strong enough to prevent the old primary from continuing to accept writes, is a split-brain generator. The fix space is fencing — quorum-gated promotion, leases, or refusing cross-region auto-failover — and GitHub's public post-mortem is the canonical text on why "the failover worked" can be the beginning of the incident, not the end.

**Async-failover data loss — the silent lost-writes incident.** This pattern recurs across many teams and is worth describing as a composite because it is so common. A team runs a single-leader database with asynchronous replication to a standby (the default in a vanilla setup). Under normal load replication lag is tens of milliseconds, so nobody worries. Then a traffic spike (a sale, a viral event) pushes write load up, replication lag climbs to one to three seconds, and *at that moment* the primary's host has a hardware fault and dies. Failover promotes the standby — but the standby was 2 seconds behind under the spike, so it comes up missing every write from those 2 seconds. The clients that issued those writes got success responses; their orders, comments, or payments were acknowledged and are now gone. The incident is discovered hours later via reconciliation or customer reports, never via an alert, because nothing errored. The lesson, from section 4: **your failover data-loss window equals your replication lag at the moment of failure, and lag is highest exactly when failures are most likely (under load).** Measure lag under peak load, and put the writes you cannot lose on semi-synchronous replication so their loss window is zero regardless of lag.

**A multi-leader conflict mess — last-write-wins eats inventory.** A retailer runs inventory across two regions with multi-leader replication and last-write-wins conflict resolution, because LWW was the default and it "worked in testing." Two regional warehouses both decrement the stock count for a popular SKU during a brief inter-region partition: Region A sets count from 10 to 7 (sold 3), Region B sets count from 10 to 6 (sold 4), concurrently. When the partition heals, LWW compares the two writes' timestamps and keeps the one with the higher clock value — say Region B's "6" — silently discarding Region A's "7." But Region A's *sales actually happened*; those 3 units left the building. The system now believes there are 6 in stock when there are 3, and oversells. The deeper bug is that LWW resolved a conflict on a value that should never have been resolved by overwrite at all — inventory decrements are *increments on a counter*, which is a CRDT operation (sum all the decrements: 10 − 3 − 4 = 3), not a last-write-wins string. The lesson, from section 6: **LWW silently destroys concurrent writes, and for any data where concurrent writes both represent real intent (inventory, balances, collaborative edits), LWW is the wrong policy** — model the data as a CRDT so concurrent operations merge correctly, or use version vectors to surface the conflict for explicit resolution. The retailer's fix was to move inventory to a counter CRDT, after which "10 minus 3 minus 4 equals 3" held regardless of partition or clock skew.

**Dynamo-style read repair and the resurrected delete.** A composite drawn from the well-documented Cassandra tombstone failure mode. A record is deleted (a tombstone written) and replicated to a write quorum, but one replica misses the tombstone because it was briefly down and hinted handoff did not reach it. That replica still holds the *old* value. Anti-entropy repair is scheduled less frequently than `gc_grace_seconds`, so the tombstone is garbage-collected on the nodes that have it *before* the lagging replica is ever repaired. Now a read of the lagging replica finds the old value, sees no tombstone anywhere to contradict it, and read repair *propagates the resurrected value back* to the other replicas — the deleted record returns from the dead. The lesson, from section 8: **in leaderless systems, `gc_grace_seconds` must exceed your worst-case repair interval, and repairs must actually complete**, or deletes are not durable. This is the failure mode behind the "we deleted it and it came back" class of incidents in eventually-consistent stores, and the fix is operational discipline around repair scheduling, not a code change.

## 13. When to reach for each strategy (and when not to)

A decisive recommendation, because "it depends" is only useful when you say what it depends on.

**Reach for single-leader (with read replicas) by default.** For the overwhelming majority of systems — a SaaS app, an e-commerce backend, an internal tool, anything with a single primary write region — single-leader with a few read replicas is correct, and the burden of proof is on anyone proposing more complexity. Add semi-synchronous replication for the write paths you cannot lose (money, orders) so the failover loss window is zero there; leave async for high-volume low-value writes (telemetry). Add read replicas for read scale and route read-your-writes traffic to the leader for a short window. This handles availability, read scale, and durability well. Its limit is geography: if you need fast *writes* on multiple continents, single-leader cannot give it to you, and that — not "we want to scale" — is the only real reason to leave it.

**Reach for multi-leader only when you genuinely need multi-region writes or offline operation, and your data tolerates a conflict policy.** Collaborative editors, offline-first mobile apps, and truly multi-region-active systems are the legitimate cases. Do not adopt multi-leader for read scale (read replicas do that) or for availability (leaderless or a well-fenced single-leader does that more simply). And before adopting it, answer the conflict question for *each* dataset: data that is mergeable (counters, sets, text) fits a CRDT; data where you can surface conflicts to users fits version vectors; data where neither is true does not belong in multi-leader at all. Defaulting to LWW is the trap — it is the conflict policy that looks free and silently loses data.

**Reach for leaderless quorum when you want to eliminate the leader as a failure point and tune consistency per operation.** Dynamo-style stores shine for high-write-availability workloads that can express their consistency needs as R/W quorums per operation — a write that must be durable uses a higher W, a read that must be fresh uses R such that R + W > N. They remove failover and split-brain-by-promotion from your worry list. The costs you take on: you must understand quorum math, you must check whether sloppy quorums are silently breaking your R + W > N guarantee, and you must operate anti-entropy repair with discipline or deletes resurrect. Do not reach for leaderless if your team cannot operate repair scheduling, or if your data needs cross-row transactions (leaderless stores are weak there — that is what [NewSQL exists for](/blog/software-development/database/cockroachdb-distributed-sql-deep-dive)).

**Across all three: never run automatic failover without fencing, never put writes you cannot lose on pure async, and never adopt a conflict policy without naming what it silently discards.** Those three rules prevent the three signature incidents above.

## 14. Key takeaways

- **Replication is choosing which failure you can afford.** Single-leader risks lost writes on failover; multi-leader risks conflicts; leaderless makes consistency a tunable you must compute. Pick the debt the business can pay, per dataset, not per system.
- **Sync versus async is a per-write-path choice, not a database setting.** Put money and orders on semi-sync (failover loss window = zero) and telemetry on async (fast, lossy is fine). Postgres `synchronous_commit` and MySQL semi-sync let you do this in one database.
- **Your failover data-loss window equals your replication lag at the moment of failure** — and lag is worst under load, exactly when failures happen. Measure lag p99 under peak load; that number is your loss window unless semi-sync makes it zero.
- **Read-your-writes is the staleness anomaly users actually notice.** Route a user's reads to the leader for a short window after they write; followers can be seconds stale for everyone else's data without anyone caring.
- **Automatic failover without fencing is an automated split-brain generator.** Quorum-gated promotion, STONITH, or fencing-token leases are mandatory, not optional. "The failover worked" can be the start of the incident.
- **R + W > N is the quorum consistency line — and sloppy quorums silently break it.** Check the sloppy-quorum/hinted-handoff setting before you claim a leaderless store is consistent.
- **Tune the quorum split toward your dominant traffic:** read-heavy → small R, large W; write-heavy → small W, large R; default to the balanced majority (R = W = ⌈(N+1)/2⌉) for symmetric failure tolerance.
- **Last-write-wins silently destroys concurrent writes.** For inventory, balances, and collaborative data, model as a CRDT (merge correctly) or use version vectors (surface the conflict) — never LWW.
- **In leaderless stores, `gc_grace_seconds` must exceed the repair interval and repairs must actually finish,** or read repair resurrects deleted data.
- **Walk every replication design through three stress tests before shipping:** a region outage (does placement match the availability need?), a partition (where is the fence / conflict policy?), and a lagging promotion (what is the numeric loss window?).
