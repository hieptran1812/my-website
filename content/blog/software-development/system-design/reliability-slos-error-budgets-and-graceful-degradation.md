The word "reliable" is where most design reviews go quietly wrong. Someone writes "the system must be highly available" in a requirements doc, everyone nods, and the meeting moves on — and nobody has actually decided anything, because "highly available" is a wish, not a number. A wish cannot be measured, cannot be budgeted, cannot be traded off against velocity, and cannot tell you whether you are allowed to ship on Friday. A senior engineer's first move when they hear "it must be reliable" is not to start adding redundancy; it is to ask the only question that turns the wish into engineering: *reliable how, measured by what, against which target, and what happens when we miss?* That chain — a measurement (SLI), a target (SLO), a budget for missing the target (error budget), and a deliberate plan for what the system does when it cannot meet the target (graceful degradation) — is the entire discipline. Figure 1 is the spine of this whole post, and everything else hangs off it.

![Pipeline showing a service level indicator measured as a success ratio flowing into a service level objective target, then into an error budget of 43.2 minutes per month, and finally into a ship-or-freeze release decision](/imgs/blogs/reliability-slos-error-budgets-and-graceful-degradation-1.webp)

Here is the claim that organizes everything below: reliability is not a quality you bolt on at the end with more servers; it is a property you design for, *measure*, *budget*, and *spend* — and the systems that stay up are the ones that planned exactly how they would fail before they failed. The opposite of a reliable system is not an unreliable one; it is a *brittle* one — a system that works perfectly until one dependency hiccups and then collapses completely, converting a 1% problem into a 100% outage. The reliable system, when the same dependency hiccups, sheds that 1% of functionality and keeps serving the other 99%. The difference between those two outcomes is almost never raw capacity. It is whether somebody sat down in the design phase and answered, for each dependency, "what do we do when this is down?" — and wrote code that does it.

This post will give you the tools to answer that question rigorously. You will learn to choose good SLIs (the four golden signals, and how to write a request-success-ratio and a latency-threshold SLI correctly), to run the error-budget math that connects 99.9% to "43.2 minutes per month" to "freeze releases on Tuesday," to do the availability arithmetic that explains why adding a hard dependency *multiplies* your failure rate while adding a redundant replica *divides* it, to understand why MTTR usually dominates user-perceived reliability far more than MTBF, and to design the graceful-degradation patterns — fallbacks, defaults, kill switches, load shedding, brownouts, serving stale-but-available over fresh-but-down — that let a system bend instead of break. This sits directly downstream of [turning vague asks into requirements and SLOs](/blog/software-development/system-design/turning-vague-asks-into-requirements-and-slos), which covered how to *elicit* the SLO from a stakeholder; here we go deep on what to do with it once you have it.

## 1. SLI, SLO, error budget: the three words that make reliability an engineering problem

Start with vocabulary, because the three terms are constantly conflated and the conflation is the source of most of the confusion. They form a strict hierarchy, each defined in terms of the one before it.

An **SLI** — a Service Level Indicator — is a *measurement*. It is a number you can actually compute from real telemetry, almost always expressed as a ratio of good events to total events over a window: `good / total`. "The fraction of HTTP requests in the last 28 days that returned a non-5xx status within 300ms" is an SLI. It is concrete, it has units, and you can graph it. The single most important property of an SLI is that it is *measurable from the user's side* — it should reflect what the user actually experiences, not what your servers feel like internally. CPU utilization is a great internal metric but a terrible SLI, because a user does not care about your CPU; they care whether their request succeeded and how long it took.

An **SLO** — a Service Level Objective — is a *target* you set for an SLI: a threshold and a window. "The success-ratio SLI must be at least 99.9% over a rolling 28-day window" is an SLO. The SLO is a decision, not a measurement. It encodes a business judgment about how reliable this thing needs to be — and crucially, it is almost never "as reliable as possible," because the last few nines cost more than they are worth for the vast majority of systems. The SLO is where you draw the line between "reliable enough" and "we will spend engineering effort to fix this."

An **error budget** is the SLO's most powerful and least intuitive child: it is `1 - SLO`, the *allowed amount of unreliability*. If your SLO is 99.9% success over 28 days, your error budget is 0.1% of requests — or, expressed as time, 0.1% of 28 days, which is **43.2 minutes**. That budget is a quantity you are *allowed to spend*. This is the reframe that makes the whole discipline click: unreliability is not a moral failing to be driven to zero; it is a *budget* allocated to you, and a budget that goes *unspent* is a sign you set your SLO too high and are over-investing in reliability at the expense of feature velocity. The error budget converts the eternal, unwinnable fight between "ship fast" (product) and "keep it up" (operations) into a single shared number that both sides can read off a dashboard. There is budget left? Ship. Budget exhausted? Stop shipping and spend the next sprint on reliability until the budget refills. Nobody has to argue; the number decides.

The arithmetic of the budget is worth committing to memory, because it shows up in every reliability conversation. A 30-day month is 43,200 minutes. So:

- **99%** (two nines) → 1% unreliability → **432 minutes/month** (7.2 hours) of allowed downtime.
- **99.9%** (three nines) → **43.2 minutes/month**.
- **99.95%** → **21.6 minutes/month**.
- **99.99%** (four nines) → **4.32 minutes/month**.
- **99.999%** (five nines) → **26 seconds/month**.

Each additional nine divides your allowed downtime by ten. That tenfold shrink in the budget is the entire story of why each nine is so much harder than the last, and it is the subject of Figure 2.

## 2. The cost of nines: why each nine is roughly ten times the last

![Matrix showing availability levels from 99 percent to 99.999 percent with their downtime per month, downtime per year, how the budget feels, and the relative engineering cost rising roughly tenfold per nine](/imgs/blogs/reliability-slos-error-budgets-and-graceful-degradation-2.webp)

The most expensive sentence a stakeholder can casually utter in a design review is "let's just make it five nines." It sounds like a small step up from four nines — one more nine, how hard can it be? — and it is one of the most consequential cost decisions in the entire design, because the cost of reliability is not linear in the number of nines. It is roughly *exponential*: each nine costs on the order of ten times more than the previous one to achieve and maintain. Figure 2 lays the levels side by side, and the pattern in the rightmost column is the one to internalize: 1x, ~10x, ~30x, ~100x, ~1000x.

Why does it explode like that? Because at each level, the *budget that absorbs human reaction time* shrinks below what humans can do, so you have to remove humans from the loop, and removing humans is expensive. At 99% — 7.2 hours per month — a human can get paged, wake up, open a laptop, diagnose, and fix, several times a month, and you are still inside budget. At 99.9% — 43.2 minutes — you have room for maybe one or two human-paced incidents a month, but a slow response to any single incident blows the whole month. At 99.99% — 4.32 minutes — there is *no time for a human to even read the page*, let alone act; the system must detect and recover automatically, which means automated failover, health-checked load balancing, pre-provisioned standby capacity, and a deploy pipeline that can roll back in seconds without a human deciding to. At 99.999% — 26 seconds per month — you cannot have a human anywhere on the critical path, you cannot tolerate a single full restart of a stateful component, and you are now paying for active-active multi-region redundancy, exhaustive chaos testing, and an organization that treats every config change as a potential outage. Each of those steps is roughly an order of magnitude more engineering, more infrastructure, and more operational discipline than the last.

The senior lesson here is to **set the SLO from the business need, not from a desire for perfection**, and to recognize that for most internal services and many user-facing ones, three nines is plenty and four is a stretch goal you reach for selectively on the few flows that genuinely warrant it. A checkout path might deserve 99.95%; the "people also bought" widget on the same page deserves maybe 99% and should be allowed to disappear entirely without anyone caring. Setting both to 99.99% because it felt safer would multiply your costs while protecting a recommendation widget nobody would miss. The discipline of [turning a vague ask into a concrete SLO](/blog/software-development/system-design/turning-vague-asks-into-requirements-and-slos) is precisely the discipline of *not* defaulting everything to the highest tier.

#### Worked example: sizing an error budget and deciding whether to freeze

You run a payments API with an SLO of **99.95% success over a rolling 28-day window**. A 28-day window is 40,320 minutes. Your error budget is `(1 - 0.9995) × 40,320 = 0.0005 × 40,320 =` **20.16 minutes** of allowed "bad" time in the window — or, in request terms, if you serve 50 million requests in 28 days, you are allowed `0.0005 × 50,000,000 =` **25,000 failed requests** before you breach.

Now an incident hits. A bad deploy on day 14 causes a 4% error rate for 18 minutes before someone rolls it back. During those 18 minutes you were serving your normal rate of, say, 1,250 requests/second, so total requests in the window were `18 × 60 × 1,250 = 1,350,000`, of which 4% failed: `0.04 × 1,350,000 =` **54,000 failed requests**. That single incident spent `54,000` against a budget of `25,000` — you are **216% over budget**. Even if you had spent zero budget all month before this, one 18-minute deploy at a 4% error rate has put you more than two months' worth of budget in the hole.

What does a senior do with that number? First, the error-budget *policy* — agreed in advance, written down — kicks in: budget exhausted means a **release freeze**. No new features ship until the budget recovers, which (because it is a rolling 28-day window) means you wait roughly until that incident rolls out of the back of the window, or you ship *only* reliability fixes that demonstrably reduce future burn. Second, you do a blameless postmortem on the deploy that burned it. The point of the freeze is not punishment; it is that the number is telling you, objectively, that you have been shipping faster than your reliability can support, and the only honest response is to rebalance until the budget is healthy again. The freeze is automatic and unarguable precisely so that it does not become a political negotiation between product and ops every single time.

## 3. Choosing good SLIs: the four golden signals, done right

An SLO is only as good as the SLI underneath it, and choosing the SLI is where most teams quietly sabotage themselves. The classic failure is measuring something easy instead of something that reflects the user's experience. The canonical starting framework is Google's **four golden signals**: *latency* (how long requests take), *traffic* (how much demand), *errors* (the rate of failed requests), and *saturation* (how full the system is). For SLIs specifically, the two that almost always matter most are the two that the user directly feels: error rate and latency. Traffic and saturation are vital *operational* signals — they tell you *why* the others are moving — but they are usually not themselves SLIs because the user does not experience your QPS or your queue depth.

The two SLI archetypes you will write most often are the **request-success-ratio SLI** and the **latency-threshold SLI**, and both have subtle correct forms.

A request-success-ratio SLI is `count(good requests) / count(valid requests)`. Two words in that definition do enormous work. "Good" must be defined from the *user's* perspective: a 500 is bad, obviously, but so is a 200 that took 30 seconds, and so is a 200 that returned a corrupt or empty body. "Valid" is the denominator subtlety: you must *exclude* requests that failed because the client did something wrong. A 400 Bad Request or a 404 for a resource that genuinely does not exist is not *your* unreliability — it is the client's error or a legitimate not-found — and counting those against your error budget means a buggy client integration or a crawler hitting dead URLs can burn your budget and freeze your releases for a problem you did not cause. The correct denominator is "requests that, had we been perfectly healthy, we should have served successfully." Getting this wrong is the single most common SLI mistake: teams put *all* non-2xx responses in the numerator of badness, then spend the next quarter confused about why their SLO is "violated" by a flood of 401s from an expired API token.

A latency-threshold SLI is the second archetype, and the correct form is *not* "average latency below X." Averages lie about reliability because they hide the tail, and the tail is exactly what users feel — the p99 request that takes 4 seconds is a real, angry user even if the average is a comfortable 80ms. The correct latency SLI is a *ratio*: "the fraction of valid requests served faster than a threshold." Concretely: "99% of requests complete in under 300ms" is a latency-threshold SLI. Notice it has *two* numbers — the threshold (300ms) and the target proportion (99%) — and both are part of the SLO. This formulation is robust because it directly counts how many users had a fast experience and how many did not, with no average to hide behind. You typically run *several* latency SLIs at once: maybe "90% under 100ms AND 99% under 500ms AND 99.9% under 2s," which together pin the whole shape of the distribution rather than a single point on it.

Here is a concrete Prometheus-style definition of a success-ratio SLI that correctly excludes client errors, the kind of thing you would actually deploy:

```promql
# Success ratio SLI: good responses over valid responses, last 28 days.
# "Valid" excludes 4xx (client's fault); "good" excludes 5xx (our fault).
sum(rate(http_requests_total{code!~"5..", code!~"4.."}[28d]))
/
sum(rate(http_requests_total{code!~"4.."}[28d]))
```

And the matching error-budget burn alert, which fires not on a single bad minute but on a *burn rate* — how fast you are spending the budget relative to the rate that would exactly exhaust it over the window:

```promql
# Fast-burn alert: budget is being spent 14.4x faster than sustainable.
# At 14.4x, you'd burn an entire 28-day budget in under 2 days -> page now.
(
  1 - (
    sum(rate(http_requests_total{code!~"5..", code!~"4.."}[1h]))
    /
    sum(rate(http_requests_total{code!~"4.."}[1h]))
  )
) > (14.4 * (1 - 0.999))
```

That burn-rate idea — alert on *the speed of spending*, not on instantaneous error rate — is the operational core of running SLOs, and it connects tightly to [observability designed in from the start](/blog/software-development/system-design/observability-metrics-logs-traces-by-design), because you cannot compute any of these SLIs without good request-level telemetry. A burn-rate alert at 14.4x means "at this pace the whole month's budget is gone in two days" — a true emergency — while a slower-burn alert at 1x means "we will exactly exhaust the budget by month-end if nothing changes," which is a warning, not a page. Layering a fast-burn page on a slow-burn ticket is the standard pattern: you get woken up only for real fires.

One more SLI subtlety that trips teams up: the choice between a **rolling window** and a **calendar window**, and it changes the behavior of your error budget materially. A *calendar* window (the budget resets at the first of every month) is simpler to reason about and aligns with reporting, but it has a sharp edge: a bad incident on the 30th and an identical one on the 1st land in different budget months, so the *same* reliability can produce wildly different "did we breach?" answers depending on the calendar, and a team that exhausts its budget on the 2nd faces a 28-day freeze. A *rolling* window (always "the last 28 days") is smoother and more honest — the budget continuously refills as old failures age out the back of the window — which is why most mature SLO practices use rolling windows despite the slightly harder mental accounting. The practical consequence for the freeze policy: with a rolling window, a release freeze ends *gradually* as the incident that triggered it ages out, rather than snapping open at a calendar boundary. Whichever you choose, choose it deliberately and write it into the error-budget policy, because the window is not a cosmetic detail; it determines exactly when you are allowed to ship again after a bad week.

A final note on *what to count* as the event. For request-driven services the unit is naturally the request, but for systems where "a request" is fuzzy — a streaming connection, a long-lived websocket, a batch pipeline — you need a different event. Time-based SLIs ("fraction of one-minute windows in which the service was healthy") work well for long-lived connections; data-freshness SLIs ("fraction of records propagated within the staleness target") work for pipelines. The principle is constant: pick the event that most directly maps to *a user being happy or unhappy*, count good over valid, and resist the temptation to count what is easy to measure instead of what actually reflects the experience. An SLI that does not move when users are suffering is worse than no SLI, because it gives false confidence precisely when you most need a true signal.

## 4. Availability math: serial chains multiply failure

Now the arithmetic that explains more production outages than any other single idea: when you compose components, *how do their availabilities combine?* The answer depends entirely on whether they are in **series** (you need all of them) or in **parallel** (you need any of them), and the difference is the difference between a system that crumbles and one that endures. Figure 4 shows both shapes at once.

![Graph contrasting a serial chain of auth and profile database whose availabilities multiply down to about 99.85 percent against two parallel replicas at 99 percent each whose redundancy yields 99.99 percent](/imgs/blogs/reliability-slos-error-budgets-and-graceful-degradation-4.webp)

**Series: availabilities multiply, so the whole is always worse than any part.** If your request must pass through an auth service at 99.95% availability *and* a profile database at 99.9% availability *and* a rendering service at 99.95%, and any one of them failing fails the request, then the composite availability is the *product*: `0.9995 × 0.999 × 0.9995 ≈ 0.99840`, or about 99.84%. Three components that are each individually better than 99.9% combine into a system that is *worse* than any of them — because every hard dependency you add is another thing that can independently take you down, and the failure probabilities accumulate. This is the single most important and most counterintuitive fact in availability math: **adding a hard dependency can only lower your availability, never raise it.** A senior internalizes this as a design constraint: every synchronous, must-succeed call you add to a request path is a tax on your availability, and the tax compounds. A request path with ten serial hard dependencies, each at a respectable 99.9%, has a composite availability of `0.999^10 ≈ 0.990` — you have spent ten three-nines services to build a two-nines system.

**Parallel: failure probabilities multiply, so redundancy makes the whole better than any part.** If instead you have two replicas of a service, each at 99% availability, and the request succeeds if *either* one is up, then the system fails only when *both* fail simultaneously. The probability of both failing is `(1 - 0.99) × (1 - 0.99) = 0.01 × 0.01 = 0.0001`, so the system availability is `1 - 0.0001 = 0.9999`, or 99.99%. Two 99% replicas in parallel give you 99.99% — four nines from two two-nines components. This is the mathematical engine behind all redundancy: in series you multiply *availabilities* (and the product shrinks); in parallel you multiply *failure probabilities* (and that product shrinks, so availability grows). Redundancy is the only lever that can push a component above its individual ceiling, which is why every high-availability design is, at its heart, a careful arrangement of parallel paths around the things that must not fail and a ruthless minimization of serial hard dependencies on the things that must.

The architect's move that falls directly out of this math is to **convert serial hard dependencies into either parallel redundant ones or soft (optional) ones**. You cannot always add redundancy — sometimes there is genuinely one source of truth — but you can very often demote a dependency from "hard" (request fails if it fails) to "soft" (request degrades if it fails), and a soft dependency *drops out of the multiplication entirely* because its failure no longer fails the request. That demotion is the bridge from this section's math to the graceful-degradation patterns that are the heart of the post, and it is illustrated in Figure 3.

![Before and after comparison where a hard blocking call to a recommendations service with a 30 second timeout cascades into a full page error, versus a soft call with a 200 millisecond timeout that catches the failure and serves a popular-items fallback so the page stays successful](/imgs/blogs/reliability-slos-error-budgets-and-graceful-degradation-3.webp)

#### Worked example: computing a composite SLO from a serial chain

Your product page is assembled from four backend calls, all currently *hard* dependencies (the page errors if any one fails). Their individual availabilities, measured over the last quarter, are: identity service 99.97%, product catalog 99.95%, inventory service 99.9%, recommendations service 99.5%. Because they are serial and hard, the page's composite availability is the product:

```
0.9997 × 0.9995 × 0.999 × 0.995 = 0.99320  ->  99.32%
```

So your page, assembled from four individually-healthy services, can promise only **99.32%** — that is about **4.9 hours of downtime per month**, far worse than any single component and nowhere near the 99.95% the business wanted. Where is the damage concentrated? The recommendations service at 99.5% is dragging everything down: it alone accounts for almost three-quarters of the unavailability in that product. And here is the senior insight that the math hands you for free — *recommendations is the least critical thing on the page.* Nobody abandons a purchase because the "you might also like" rail is missing. So the fix is not to make recommendations more reliable (expensive); it is to make it **soft** — wrap it in a 150ms timeout with a fallback to a cached popular-items list — so that its failure no longer fails the page. Once recommendations is soft, it drops out of the product entirely, and the composite becomes:

```
0.9997 × 0.9995 × 0.999 = 0.99820  ->  99.82%
```

You just bought 50 basis points of availability — cutting monthly downtime from ~4.9 hours to ~1.3 hours — by writing about ten lines of fallback code, with no new infrastructure, no extra replicas, and no money. That is the single highest-leverage reliability move available to most teams, and it comes straight out of taking the multiplication seriously. The remaining gap to 99.95% is then a question of whether inventory at 99.9% should get redundancy or its own soft fallback (serve "in stock" optimistically from cache when the live check is down, and reconcile at checkout) — a smaller, more deliberate decision now that the cheap win is banked.

## 5. MTBF, MTTR, and why recovery time usually wins

Availability has a second decomposition that matters as much as the serial/parallel one, and it reframes where you should spend your reliability effort. Availability over time can be written as:

```
Availability = MTBF / (MTBF + MTTR)
```

where **MTBF** is Mean Time Between Failures (how long, on average, the system runs before it breaks) and **MTTR** is Mean Time To Recovery (how long, on average, it takes to get back up once it has broken). The formula says availability is the fraction of total time spent *up*, where each failure costs you one MTTR of downtime and they arrive every MTBF.

The reason this decomposition matters is that it tells you *which lever to pull*, and the answer is usually the less glamorous one. Most engineering instinct goes toward raising MTBF — making failures rarer through more testing, more hardening, more careful code. That is valuable, but it has sharply diminishing returns and a hard ceiling: you cannot make failures arbitrarily rare, because some of them come from outside your control entirely (a cloud AZ goes down, a dependency you do not own breaks, a fiber cut). MTTR, by contrast, is almost entirely within your control and usually has *enormous* headroom for improvement, because most organizations' recovery is slow for boring, fixable reasons: nobody noticed for ten minutes (bad alerting), then the on-call spent twenty minutes figuring out what broke (bad observability), then rolling back took fifteen minutes because the deploy pipeline is slow (bad tooling), then they hesitated because they were not sure rollback was safe (no rehearsal).

Watch what the formula does with these. Suppose a system fails once a month (MTBF ≈ 30 days = 43,200 minutes) and takes 60 minutes to recover (MTTR = 60). Availability is `43,200 / (43,200 + 60) = 0.99861`, about 99.86%. Now *halve the MTTR* to 30 minutes — better alerting and one-click rollback — and availability becomes `43,200 / (43,200 + 30) = 0.99931`, about 99.93%. You went from missing three nines to comfortably exceeding it, *without preventing a single failure*, purely by recovering twice as fast. Alternatively, *double the MTBF* (failures now every two months) while keeping MTTR at 60: `86,400 / (86,400 + 60) = 0.99931` — the same improvement, but doubling MTBF is typically far harder and far more expensive than halving MTTR. Because user-perceived reliability is dominated by *how long you are down*, and because MTTR is cheaper to improve and has more headroom, **the highest-return reliability investment for most teams is making recovery fast, not making failure rare.** Fast rollback, good alerting that catches problems in seconds not minutes, runbooks that turn a novel incident into a known procedure, and feature flags that let you disable a broken feature without a deploy — these all attack MTTR, and they are where a senior tells a team to spend first. This is also why the [observability-by-design post](/blog/software-development/system-design/observability-metrics-logs-traces-by-design) is a reliability post in disguise: you cannot recover quickly from what you cannot see quickly.

## 6. Redundancy, N+1 vs N+2, and the lie of independence

The parallel-availability math from Section 4 is the engine of redundancy, but it hides an assumption that, when it breaks, has caused some of the largest outages in computing history. The formula "two 99% replicas give 99.99% because both fail only `0.01 × 0.01` of the time" *assumes the two failures are independent.* They almost never fully are, and the gap between the math's promise and reality is where redundancy quietly betrays you.

First, the sizing vocabulary, because seniors reason about redundancy in `N+k` terms. **`N`** is the number of units you need to carry your load. **`N+1`** is one spare beyond that, so you survive a single unit failing — the standard for most production systems. **`N+2`** is two spares, which you need not because two units fail simultaneously often, but because you must survive *one failure while another unit is already out for maintenance* — a planned drain plus an unplanned failure overlapping. The cost is stark and linear in the spare count: at `N=4`, `N+1` is 5 units (25% overhead), `N+2` is 6 (50% overhead). Each spare is idle capacity you pay for continuously to buy survival of one more concurrent failure, and the decision of `+1` versus `+2` is a direct trade of money against how catastrophic a double-failure-during-maintenance would be. For a stateless web tier, `N+1` is usually plenty. For the control plane that, if down, prevents you from *recovering* anything else, `N+2` (or more) earns its cost.

Now the lie. The independence assumption fails through **correlated failure** — a single root cause that takes out multiple "redundant" replicas at once, collapsing the `0.01 × 0.01` you were counting on into something closer to `0.01`. The sources of correlation are depressingly common: both replicas in the *same rack* (shared power, shared top-of-rack switch), both in the *same availability zone* (shared cooling, shared network fabric), both running the *same software version* (so the same bug or the same poisoned deploy kills both), both depending on the *same configuration service* (one bad config push fails them together), both behind the *same load balancer* (which is now your real single point of failure), or both saturated by the *same traffic spike* (correlated demand, not just correlated failure). When the correlation is total — a shared dependency that, when it fails, fails every replica — your redundancy provides *zero* availability benefit on that failure mode, because you are back to a single point of failure wearing a redundant costume.

The architect's response is to **spread redundancy across failure domains, not just across instances.** Two replicas in the same AZ protect you against a single machine dying but not against the AZ losing power; two replicas in *different* AZs protect against both. Three replicas across three AZs is the standard high-availability shape precisely because it tolerates a full AZ loss while still having a quorum — and quorum systems like the ones the [replication strategies post](/blog/software-development/system-design/replication-strategies-and-their-failure-modes) details are built exactly to survive the loss of a whole failure domain, not just an instance. The discipline is to enumerate your failure domains (machine, rack, AZ, region, software version, config source, dependency) and ask, for each, "if this entire domain fails, how many of my redundant copies go with it?" Wherever the answer is "all of them," you do not have redundancy on that axis; you have the *appearance* of redundancy, which is more dangerous than none because it lulls you into promising availability the math does not actually support.

There is a second-order trap worth naming: redundancy that *increases* correlated risk. Adding more replicas adds more machines that can independently fail in ways that trigger a *failover*, and if your failover mechanism is itself buggy or slow, more replicas can mean *more* failover events, each a small reliability risk. Auto-failover that flaps — promoting and demoting replicas under transient network blips — has caused outages that the un-redundant system would have ridden out. The lesson is that redundancy is not free availability; it is a *mechanism* with its own failure modes, and a redundant system is only as reliable as its failover logic, its health-checking, and its independence across domains. "We have three replicas" is the beginning of the reliability analysis, not the end of it.

## 7. Graceful degradation: the patterns that let a system bend

Everything so far points at one architectural conclusion: the systems that stay up are the ones that have a *plan* for partial failure, and that plan is **graceful degradation** — the deliberate design of reduced-but-functional behavior for when a dependency or capacity is unavailable. Degradation is the opposite of the brittle all-or-nothing default, where any failure anywhere fails the whole request. A well-degraded system has a spectrum of states between "fully working" and "completely down," and it walks down that spectrum step by step under stress instead of falling off the cliff. The decision of *how* to degrade a given failure is itself structured, and Figure 6 captures the decision tree.

![Decision tree for how a failure should degrade, branching on whether the data is critical such as money or auth versus optional such as recommendations or counts, then leading to fail closed, serve stale cache, return a safe default, or hide the feature](/imgs/blogs/reliability-slos-error-budgets-and-graceful-degradation-6.webp)

The patterns, roughly from most to least graceful:

**Fallback to a default or alternate source.** When the primary source of some data fails, serve a sensible default or a secondary source instead of erroring. Recommendations service down? Serve a static "popular this week" list computed hourly. Personalization service down? Serve the generic, non-personalized experience. The fallback is *worse* than the real thing but vastly better than an error, and for optional features the user often does not even notice. The key engineering requirement is that the fallback path must be *cheaper and more reliable* than the primary — a fallback that itself calls a flaky service has bought you nothing.

**Serve stale-but-available over fresh-but-down.** This is one of the most powerful patterns and one of the most under-used. When the system of record for some data is unavailable, serving the *last known good* value from a cache is almost always better than serving an error — a product price that is five minutes stale is fine; a missing product page is not. The pattern is "stale-while-revalidate" or "serve-stale-on-error": you keep a cached copy with a soft TTL, and when the origin is down you extend the cache's life and keep serving the stale copy rather than failing. The trade-off is a bounded window of staleness, which you must decide is acceptable per data type (stale price for five minutes: fine; stale bank balance: depends; stale "is this user banned" check: probably not). The [caching strategies post](/blog/software-development/system-design/caching-strategies-and-the-pitfalls-that-bite) covers the mechanics of stale-serving and its consistency pitfalls in depth; here the architectural point is simply that a cache is not only a latency tool, it is a *reliability* tool, because it is a redundant copy of the data that survives the origin being down.

**Feature flags and kill switches.** Every non-trivial feature should be wrapped in a flag that lets you turn it off *without a deploy*. When a feature starts misbehaving — burning error budget, hammering a struggling dependency, leaking a bug — the kill switch lets you remove it from the request path in seconds, which is the single fastest MTTR tool you have. Flags turn "we need to roll back a deploy" (minutes, risky) into "we flip a config" (seconds, safe). They are also how you *pre-build* degradation: the fallback paths above are usually gated behind flags so you can force the degraded mode on deliberately (to test it, or to shed a struggling dependency proactively before it fully fails).

**Load shedding.** When you are over capacity, *refuse some work cheaply* rather than accepting all of it and serving none of it well. Drop the least-important requests (a health-check probe, a low-priority background sync, a free-tier request) so that the important ones (a paying customer's checkout) still get resources. The senior framing, which the [rate-limiting and backpressure post](/blog/software-development/system-design/rate-limiting-and-backpressure) develops in full, is that load shedding converts an *availability* collapse (serve 0% because you tried to serve 100%) back into a *capacity* outcome (serve the 70% you can, cleanly reject the 30% you cannot). A system that sheds load is *up* for most users; a system that accepts everything and dies is *down* for all of them.

**The brownout pattern.** Instead of a binary up/down, a service can dynamically *reduce the quality or cost of its responses* as it gets more loaded — disabling expensive optional computation, returning smaller result sets, skipping enrichment steps, lowering image resolution — to stay within its capacity. This is degradation as a continuous dial rather than a switch: under light load you serve the full rich experience; as load climbs, you progressively shed the expensive trimmings to keep core latency in budget; at the extreme you serve a bare-bones response. The brownout keeps the *core* function alive across a much wider load range than an all-or-nothing service could, by trading response richness for survival.

The unifying principle across all of these is **make dependencies soft and degrade instead of failing**. Every one of these patterns is a way of demoting a hard dependency to a soft one — and from the availability math in Section 4, a soft dependency drops out of the failure multiplication. Graceful degradation is not a nicety bolted on for polish; it is the direct architectural consequence of taking the serial-chain math seriously and refusing to let optional things take down essential ones.

## 8. Failure isolation: timeouts, retries, breakers, and bulkheads

Graceful degradation decides *what to serve* when something fails. Failure *isolation* decides *how to contain* a failure so it does not spread — because the deadliest outages are not single failures, they are *cascades*, where one slow component poisons everything that depends on it. The four classic isolation mechanisms form a layered defense, shown in Figure 8, and a senior deploys them together because each catches a stage the others miss.

![Stack of failure-isolation layers from a 200 millisecond timeout that bounds every call, to a 10 percent retry budget with jitter, to a circuit breaker that trips at a 50 percent error rate, to a bulkhead pool that isolates a single dependency](/imgs/blogs/reliability-slos-error-budgets-and-graceful-degradation-8.webp)

**Timeouts: bound every call, no exceptions.** The most basic and most violated rule of resilient systems is that *every* network call must have a timeout, and the timeout must be *tight* — set from the dependency's p99 latency under healthy conditions plus a margin, not from some comfortable round number like 30 seconds. The reason is brutal: a call with no timeout (or a 30-second one) does not just fail slowly, it *holds a resource* — a thread, a connection, a goroutine — for the entire wait. When a downstream dependency slows from 50ms to 5 seconds, every in-flight request to it now holds its thread 100x longer, and your thread pool drains and your service stops accepting *any* requests, including ones that have nothing to do with the slow dependency. The slow dependency did not just degrade itself; it took down your whole service by exhausting your concurrency. A tight timeout converts "hang forever holding a thread" into "fail fast and free the thread," which is the precondition for every other isolation pattern.

**Retries: with a budget, jitter, and backoff — or they amplify the outage.** Retries are the most double-edged tool in the box. A retry on a transient blip is great. But naive retries are how a thirty-second blip becomes a forty-minute outage, because when a dependency is struggling, *every* client retries, and the retries are *additional load on the already-overloaded dependency*, which makes it slower, which causes more timeouts, which causes more retries — a **retry storm** that feeds on itself. The discipline that prevents this has three parts. First, **exponential backoff with jitter**: wait longer between each retry, and add randomness so that thousands of clients do not synchronize their retries into a thundering wave (without jitter, every client that failed at T retries at exactly T+1s, recreating the spike). Second, a **retry budget**: cap retries as a *fraction* of total requests — for example, "retries may be at most 10% of base traffic" — so that a widespread failure cannot multiply your load by 3x; once the retry budget is exhausted, you stop retrying and fail fast. Third, **retry only idempotent operations**, because retrying a non-idempotent write after a timeout (where the first attempt may have succeeded) double-executes it — the [idempotency-by-design post](/blog/software-development/system-design/idempotency-and-exactly-once-by-design) is the prerequisite for retrying safely at all.

**Circuit breakers: stop calling a thing that is clearly down.** A circuit breaker watches the error rate to a dependency and, when it crosses a threshold (say 50% errors over a rolling window), *trips* — it stops sending calls to that dependency entirely and immediately returns a failure (or, better, triggers the fallback) without even attempting the call. This does two things: it gives the struggling dependency *breathing room* to recover instead of being hammered by doomed requests, and it makes *your* failures fast (an instant trip instead of a timeout-length wait). After a cooldown the breaker goes *half-open*, lets a trickle of test requests through, and closes again if they succeed. The breaker is the automated version of the human judgment "this thing is clearly broken, stop poking it." It is the layer that actually *ends* a cascade, where timeouts merely slow it.

**Bulkheads: isolate resources so one failure cannot sink the ship.** Named after the watertight compartments in a ship's hull, a bulkhead partitions your resources so that exhaustion in one place cannot drain another. The classic application: give each downstream dependency its *own* connection/thread pool, so that if dependency A goes slow and saturates its pool, dependency B's pool is untouched and requests that only need B keep flowing. Without bulkheads, all dependencies share one pool, and the slowest one drains it for everyone — the thread-exhaustion cascade from the timeout discussion, now affecting unrelated features. Bulkheads are the most expensive of the four (you over-provision pools that are usually idle) but the most effective at preventing one bad dependency from becoming a total outage. You also bulkhead by *tenant* (one noisy customer cannot exhaust shared capacity) and by *request class* (background jobs cannot starve user-facing traffic).

These four compose into a defense in depth: the timeout bounds the damage of a single slow call, the retry budget keeps recovery attempts from amplifying, the breaker ends a sustained cascade, and the bulkhead confines whatever slips through to a single compartment. Figure 7 puts these (and the degradation patterns) into a single decision matrix so you can see what each costs.

## 9. Trade-offs: what each reliability technique actually costs

No reliability technique is free, and the senior move — the entire reason this series exists — is to never recommend one without naming its cost. Figure 7 is the decision matrix: each technique against the availability it buys, the latency it adds, the complexity it introduces, and the dollars it costs.

![Matrix comparing redundancy, circuit breaker, timeout with retry budget, bulkhead isolation, graceful degradation, and load shedding across availability gain, latency cost, complexity, and dollar cost](/imgs/blogs/reliability-slos-error-budgets-and-graceful-degradation-7.webp)

Reading the matrix the way a senior would in a design review:

| Technique | Availability gain | Latency cost | Complexity | \$ cost | When it wins |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Redundancy (N+1, N+2)** | High — turns serial ceilings into parallel multiplication | None (often improves p99) | Medium — failover, health checks, state sync | **High** — you pay for idle standby capacity | The dependency is critical, has no acceptable fallback, and you can afford the duplicate. |
| **Circuit breaker** | High — ends cascades | Low — adds a fast-fail check | Medium — tuning thresholds, half-open logic | Low | Any call to a dependency that can fail and stay failed; nearly always worth it. |
| **Timeout + retry budget** | Medium — prevents thread exhaustion and storms | Adds one bounded hop on retry | Low — but must be tuned correctly | Low | Every network call (timeouts) and every idempotent one (retries). Default on. |
| **Bulkhead isolation** | High — confines one failure | None | **High** — separate pools, more config | Medium — over-provisioned pools sit idle | One dependency or tenant must not be able to take down others. |
| **Graceful degradation** | High — soft deps leave the failure product | None — fallback is usually faster | **High** — you build and maintain a second path | Low — mostly engineering time | The feature is optional or has an acceptable reduced form. Highest leverage per dollar. |
| **Load shedding** | Medium — preserves the core under overload | None — sheds cheaply at the edge | Low | None | Demand can exceed capacity and some requests are less important than others. |

The pattern the matrix reveals is the senior's reliability priority order. **Graceful degradation and circuit breakers are the highest-leverage** — they buy a lot of availability for low dollar cost, paid mostly in engineering effort and complexity, which is exactly the trade you want. **Timeouts and retry budgets are the cheap table stakes** — low cost on every axis, so there is no excuse not to have them everywhere. **Redundancy is the expensive heavy artillery** — it buys the most raw availability but you pay real money for idle capacity, so you reserve it for the genuinely critical, fallback-less components. The mistake juniors make is reaching for redundancy first (it feels like "the reliability thing") when degradation and breakers would have bought more availability for a fraction of the cost. A senior spends the engineering-cheap, dollar-cheap options exhaustively before spending money on duplicate hardware. This same instinct — exhaust the design-level wins before the spend-level ones — is what the [CAP/PACELC trade-off post](/blog/software-development/system-design/articulating-tradeoffs-cap-pacelc-and-beyond) trains for the consistency dimension.

## 10. Dependency reliability budgeting: you can't be more reliable than your weakest hard dependency

Here is a hard constraint that humbles many ambitious SLOs: **your SLO cannot exceed the availability of your weakest *hard* dependency.** From the serial-chain math, if your request path includes a hard dependency at 99.9%, then *your* composite availability is at most 99.9% — you can be no better than the floor that your worst required component sets, because every time it is down, you are down. Promising your users 99.95% while depending synchronously on a 99.9% database is writing a check the math will bounce.

This turns SLO-setting into a *budgeting* exercise across your dependency graph. You have a target — say 99.95% for your service — and that target is a budget of unavailability (0.05%) that you must *allocate* across everything in your critical path: your own code, plus every hard dependency. If you depend on three hard services, their unavailabilities *plus* your own must sum to no more than your budget. So you go to each dependency, find out (or measure) its SLO, and check whether the sum fits. If your three hard dependencies are at 99.99%, 99.98%, and 99.97%, their combined unavailability is roughly `0.0001 + 0.0002 + 0.0003 = 0.0006`, leaving you only `0.0005 - 0.0006 < 0` — you are *already over budget* before you have written a line of your own code. The math is telling you the SLO is infeasible with those dependencies as hard.

You then have exactly four moves, and naming them is the senior's job:

1. **Lower your SLO** to something the dependency floor can actually support. The honest move when the others are too expensive.
2. **Make a dependency soft** (degrade on its failure), removing it from the multiplication — the cheap, high-leverage move from Section 4.
3. **Add redundancy** to a dependency to raise its effective availability — the expensive move, justified only for the critical fallback-less ones.
4. **Push the dependency to improve its own SLO** — possible if it is an internal team you can negotiate with, impossible for a third party whose SLA is fixed (and note an external SLA is a *contractual* number, often weaker than observed availability, and it is the number you must budget against for anything you do not control).

For third-party dependencies, this budgeting is unforgiving: if you build on a cloud database with a 99.99% SLA, you *cannot* contractually promise your users more than 99.99% on any path that hard-depends on it, full stop — and in practice you should budget for less, because their SLA is a floor they pay penalties below, not a guarantee. The architectural response to "we need more reliability than our provider's SLA" is always one of the four moves above, usually a combination of making the dependency soft where possible and adding cross-provider or cross-region redundancy where it is not. This is the reliability lens on the [replication strategies and their failure modes](/blog/software-development/system-design/replication-strategies-and-their-failure-modes) discussion: replication is how you turn one datastore's availability ceiling into a parallel, higher one.

## 11. The optimization lens: spend the error budget deliberately

The whole discipline comes together in a single reframe that separates seniors from the rest: **the error budget is a resource to spend deliberately, not a number to drive to zero.** This is the optimization angle, and it inverts the naive instinct. The naive view treats every error as a failure to be eliminated, every nine as better than the last, and reliability as a one-way ratchet toward perfection. The senior view treats reliability as an *allocation problem*: you have a fixed budget of unreliability, and your job is to *spend it where it buys the most* and *save it where it does not matter.*

What does spending the budget deliberately look like in practice? Several concrete moves:

**Spend budget on velocity when you have it.** If your SLO is 99.9% and you have been running at 99.97% for two months, you have a *surplus* of error budget you are not using — which means you have been too conservative and are leaving feature velocity on the table. The correct response is to *ship faster*, take more deploys, run more experiments, because the budget exists precisely to absorb the occasional regression that fast shipping causes. An unused error budget is a sign of over-investment in reliability, exactly as an unused financial budget is a sign you under-invested in growth. This is the counterintuitive heart of the discipline: **a healthy reliability practice deliberately *uses up* its error budget**, because a budget that is never spent is a target set too high.

**Spend budget on the right failures.** When you must take an outage — a risky migration, a necessary maintenance window — *schedule it* and spend the budget on your terms (3 a.m. on a Sunday, announced, brief) rather than waiting for the budget to be spent *for* you by a surprise incident at peak. Planned spending is cheaper than unplanned spending, in budget and in user trust.

**Make dependencies soft to free budget.** Every dependency you demote from hard to soft *returns its unavailability to your budget*, because its failures no longer count against you. The optimization move from Section 4 — softening the recommendations service to buy 50 basis points — is fundamentally a budget-reclamation move: you took unavailability that was being spent on a thing nobody cares about and gave it back to spend on things they do.

**Measure the win in the currency of the budget.** When you do a reliability project, quantify it in *budget terms*: "adding the circuit breaker cut our cascade-driven budget burn from an average of 18 minutes/month to under 2" is a measurable, defensible win. "It feels more stable" is not. The error budget gives reliability work the same numeric accountability that latency numbers give performance work — and that accountability is what lets you *stop* a reliability project when it stops paying, instead of gold-plating toward a nine nobody needs.

The deepest version of this lens: reliability is *one term in an optimization* whose other terms are velocity, cost, and latency, and the error budget is the exchange rate between them. You are not maximizing reliability; you are *optimizing the whole system* subject to a reliability *constraint*, and the budget is how you trade across the constraint boundary deliberately rather than accidentally. A team that drives reliability to its maximum has not won — it has over-spent on one term and starved the others, the same mistake as a team that ignores reliability entirely, just in the opposite direction.

## 12. Stress-testing the design: spike, cascade, and storm

A design is only as good as its behavior under the failures it was built to survive, so a senior never finishes a reliability design without walking it through the specific failures it must withstand. Three are mandatory, and Figure 5 traces the error-budget consequence of the worst of them.

![Timeline of an error budget over a 28-day window, starting full at 43.2 minutes, burning steadily, then a bad deploy on day 14 burning at 14 times the sustainable rate spending 30 minutes in 12 minutes, triggering rollback and a release freeze before the window resets](/imgs/blogs/reliability-slos-error-budgets-and-graceful-degradation-5.webp)

**Stress test 1: a hard dependency goes down. Does the system degrade or cascade?** Walk the request path and, for each dependency, ask "what happens the instant this returns errors or hangs?" If the answer is "the request fails" or worse "the request hangs until timeout, holding a thread," you have a hard dependency with no isolation, and at scale that is a cascade waiting to happen. The fix is the Section 6–7 toolkit: tight timeout (so a hang becomes a fast failure), fallback (so the failure degrades), circuit breaker (so a sustained failure stops being attempted), and bulkhead (so the failure cannot drain shared resources). The test passes when every dependency, on failure, produces a *degraded but successful* response or a *fast, isolated* failure — never a hang, never a cascade. The before/after of this exact test is Figure 9: the fail-closed version turns one widget's outage into a blank 500 page; the fail-degraded version drops the widget and serves the rest.

![Before and after comparison where failing closed turns a recommendations call failure into an unhandled error and a blank 500 page that is zero percent usable, versus failing degraded which catches the failure, serves a popular-items fallback, and returns the full page minus one widget with a 200 status](/imgs/blogs/reliability-slos-error-budgets-and-graceful-degradation-9.webp)

**Stress test 2: an error-budget burn. Does the policy actually fire?** Simulate the Section 2 worked example — a bad deploy burns the month's budget in twelve minutes — and check that the *organizational* machinery responds: does a fast-burn alert page someone within seconds (not after the budget is gone)? Does the rollback take seconds (good MTTR) or minutes (you are still burning while you fumble)? Does the release-freeze policy kick in automatically, or does it require a meeting that will not happen until Monday? Figure 5 traces this: the budget sits healthy until day 14, then a 14x burn spends thirty minutes of budget in twelve, the rollback-plus-freeze stops the bleeding, and the budget only fully recovers when the incident rolls out of the back of the 28-day window. The test passes when the burn is *detected* fast, *stopped* fast, and *governs* the next decision automatically.

**Stress test 3: a retry storm. Do retries amplify the outage?** Take a dependency that slows to a 50% error rate and ask what your clients do. If every client retries every failure immediately, you have just *tripled* the load on an already-struggling dependency (the original request plus two retries), guaranteeing it cannot recover — the storm from Section 7. The test passes only if: retries are capped by a budget (≤10% of base traffic), backed off with jitter (no synchronized waves), and short-circuited by a circuit breaker once the error rate is clearly sustained (so you stop retrying a thing that is clearly down). A senior runs this test explicitly because the retry storm is the most counterintuitive failure mode — the *recovery mechanism itself* becomes the thing preventing recovery — and it is invisible until you deliberately reason about the load that retries add during a partial failure.

The discipline that ties these three together is the same one the [rate-limiting and backpressure post](/blog/software-development/system-design/rate-limiting-and-backpressure) and the [CAP/PACELC trade-offs post](/blog/software-development/system-design/articulating-tradeoffs-cap-pacelc-and-beyond) both teach: you *rehearse the unhappy path in the design review*, naming exactly what saves you in each failure, rather than discovering it during the incident. A reliability design that has not been walked through spike, cascade, and storm is not a design; it is a hope.

#### Worked example: designing a graceful-degradation fallback with numbers

Concretely, design the degradation for a single feature so the numbers are real. Your e-commerce product page calls a **personalized recommendations** service. Healthy, it responds in p50 35ms / p99 120ms, and it is available 99.5% of the time — the weak link from Section 4's worked example. It is an *optional* feature: nobody abandons a cart because recommendations are missing.

The hard-dependency version (no isolation): the page calls recs synchronously with a default 30-second timeout and no fallback. When recs is down (0.5% of the time, ~3.6 hours/month), the page errors. Worse, when recs is merely *slow* — say a GC pause pushes its p99 to 8 seconds — every product-page request holds a thread for 8 seconds, the page service's thread pool (200 threads) drains within seconds at 1,000 req/s, and the *entire* product page goes down, not just recs. One optional widget's slowness has caused a full-page outage. This is the cascade.

The degraded version, designed with numbers:

- **Timeout: 150ms.** Set just above the healthy p99 of 120ms. If recs cannot answer in 150ms, we do not wait — we abandon the call and free the thread. This caps the latency cost of a slow recs at 150ms instead of 8 seconds, and it means recs slowness can never drain the thread pool.
- **Fallback: cached popular-items list.** A background job computes the top-50 popular items hourly and stores them in a local cache. When the recs call times out or errors, we serve this list. It is generic, not personalized — worse, but present. Building it costs maybe a day of work and a tiny hourly batch job.
- **Circuit breaker: trip at 50% errors over a 10-second window.** When recs is clearly down, the breaker opens and we skip the call entirely, serving the fallback *immediately* (0ms instead of 150ms) and giving recs room to recover. After a 30-second cooldown the breaker half-opens to test.
- **Bulkhead: recs gets its own 20-thread pool**, separate from the page's main pool. Even in the pathological case, recs can occupy at most 20 threads, never the whole 200.

The result, in numbers: the page's availability with recs *hard* was 99.5% on that dimension. With recs *soft*, the page no longer fails when recs fails — it degrades to the popular-items list — so recs drops out of the page's availability product entirely (Section 4 showed this lifting the composite from 99.32% to 99.82%). The cost: about 1.5 days of engineering, a small hourly batch job, and a 150ms worst-case latency penalty *only* during recs failures (and 0ms once the breaker opens). The user impact during a recs outage: a generic recommendation rail instead of a personalized one, which most users will not even notice. That is the trade — and written out with numbers like this, it is the easiest design-review approval you will ever get.

## 13. Case studies: error budgets, a cascade, and a degradation save

Three real-world stories, each teaching one of the pillars above.

**Google SRE and the error-budget contract.** The error-budget model originates in Google's Site Reliability Engineering practice, and its most important contribution is *organizational*, not technical. Before error budgets, the relationship between the product team (wanting to ship) and the reliability team (wanting stability) was a permanent, unwinnable argument — every launch was a negotiation, every incident a blame fight. Google's insight was to make the SLO a *contract* with an automatic consequence: the product team gets to ship freely *as long as the error budget has room*, and the moment it is exhausted, a release freeze kicks in automatically until the budget recovers. This converts a political fight into a shared number. The product team is now *incentivized* to invest in reliability (because reliability buys them shipping velocity), and the SRE team is incentivized *not* to over-engineer (because an unspent budget means the product team was needlessly throttled). The lesson for any organization: the error budget's value is less in the math and more in the way it *aligns incentives* so that "ship fast" and "stay up" stop being enemies. This is why the error budget belongs in your *requirements* conversation, the subject of [turning vague asks into requirements and SLOs](/blog/software-development/system-design/turning-vague-asks-into-requirements-and-slos), not just your ops dashboards.

**A cascading-failure outage: the retry-storm pattern.** Large public post-mortems from AWS, Cloudflare, and others repeatedly tell the same shape of story, and it is worth treating as an archetype because the details vary but the mechanism does not. A component slows down — a config push, a dependency hiccup, a capacity blip. Clients of that component, seeing failures and slowness, *retry*. The retries pile additional load onto the already-struggling component, slowing it further, causing more failures, causing more retries. Meanwhile, the slow calls hold threads/connections in the *callers*, draining their pools, so the callers start failing too — and *their* callers retry, propagating the saturation up the stack. Within minutes a localized slowdown has become a system-wide outage, and the brutal irony is that the recovery mechanism (retries) is the thing preventing recovery. The 2017 AWS S3 outage, several Cloudflare incidents, and many others share this DNA: a small trigger amplified by retries and resource exhaustion into something far larger. The lesson is the Section 7 toolkit, and the meta-lesson is that **your resilience mechanisms can become your failure mechanisms if you do not budget them** — retries without a budget, timeouts that are too generous, and shared pools without bulkheads are not safety features, they are amplifiers.

**A graceful-degradation save: Netflix and the fallback-everything culture.** Netflix is the canonical example of degradation-as-architecture, and the lesson is the inverse of the cascade story: a system engineered so that almost every dependency is *soft* simply does not cascade. Netflix's Hystrix library (and its successors) wrapped essentially every inter-service call in a timeout, a circuit breaker, and a fallback, so that when any given microservice failed, the calling service degraded rather than failed — a recommendation row might fall back to a non-personalized list, a piece of metadata might be omitted, but the user could still browse and play. Combined with their deliberate practice of *injecting* failures in production (Chaos Monkey killing instances to prove the fallbacks work), the result is a system where a single service outage produces a slightly degraded experience for some users rather than an outage for all of them. The lesson is cultural as much as technical: degradation only works if the fallback paths are *exercised*, because an untested fallback is just a second bug waiting for the worst possible moment to surface. Netflix's contribution was making "what happens when this dependency is down?" a *required, tested* answer for every call, not an afterthought — which is the entire thesis of this post compressed into an engineering culture. (Chaos and failure injection are the subject of the forthcoming [testing distributed systems post](/blog/software-development/system-design/testing-distributed-systems-chaos-and-load) in this series; the connection is that you cannot trust a degradation path you have not deliberately broken.)

## 14. When to reach for each technique (and when not to)

A decisive recommendation section, because "it depends" without a default is useless.

**Always, on every service, no exceptions:** tight timeouts on every network call, and retries with a budget, jitter, and backoff on idempotent calls. These are table stakes — cheap on every axis, and their absence is the root cause of most cascades. If a code review shows a network call with no timeout, that is a blocking comment, every time.

**Reach for graceful degradation first for any optional feature.** Before you spend money on redundancy, ask whether the failing thing even *needs* to succeed. Most pages are assembled from a small critical core and a large optional periphery, and the periphery should *all* be soft. This is the highest-leverage, lowest-cost reliability work available, and it should be exhausted before anything expensive. Default to degrade.

**Reach for circuit breakers on any dependency that can fail and stay failed** — which is essentially all of them. The breaker is cheap and ends cascades; there is rarely a reason not to have one on a remote call. The only tuning judgment is the trip threshold and cooldown, set from the dependency's normal error rate.

**Reach for bulkheads when one dependency or tenant must not be able to take down others** — a shared multi-tenant service, a service with one critical dependency and several optional ones, a system where background work shares resources with user-facing work. The cost (idle over-provisioned pools) is real, so reserve bulkheads for the isolation boundaries that genuinely matter rather than partitioning everything.

**Reach for redundancy (N+1, N+2) only for the genuinely critical, fallback-less components**, and budget the money first. Redundancy is the expensive move; it is the right move for the single source of truth that has no acceptable degraded form (the primary database, the auth system, the payment ledger), and the wrong first move for anything that could instead be made soft. N+1 means one spare beyond what you need (survive one failure); N+2 means two (survive a failure *during* a maintenance window). Each additional spare is more idle cost, justified by how catastrophic that component's outage is.

**Do NOT chase nines you do not need.** The most important "when not to": do not set a 99.99% SLO on something a 99% SLO would serve, do not add redundancy to optional features, do not drive your error budget to zero, and do not pay the ~10x-per-nine cost on flows where the business would not notice an extra nine. Reliability gold-plating is a real and expensive failure mode, and a senior is as willing to say "this is reliable enough, stop" as to say "this needs more." The discipline cuts both ways.

## 15. Key takeaways

- **Reliability is measured, budgeted, and spent — not wished for.** The chain SLI → SLO → error budget turns "make it reliable" into a number you can trade against velocity. If you cannot state your SLO as a number with a window, you have not started.
- **Each nine costs roughly ten times the last** (1x → 10x → 100x → 1000x). Set the SLO from the business need, not from a desire for perfection, and reserve the high nines for the few flows that genuinely warrant them.
- **Serial hard dependencies multiply failure; parallel redundancy multiplies success.** Adding a hard dependency can only lower your availability. The cheapest reliability win is usually demoting a hard dependency to a soft one, which removes it from the failure product entirely.
- **MTTR usually dominates user-perceived reliability more than MTBF**, and MTTR is cheaper to improve. Invest in fast detection, fast rollback, kill switches, and runbooks before you invest in making failures rarer.
- **Degrade, do not fail.** Fallbacks, safe defaults, stale-but-available cache, feature flags, load shedding, and the brownout pattern are how a system bends instead of breaks. Every optional dependency should be soft.
- **Isolate failures in layers:** tight timeouts bound a slow call, retry budgets keep recovery from amplifying, circuit breakers end cascades, bulkheads confine what slips through. Naive retries without a budget are an outage amplifier, not a safety feature.
- **You cannot be more reliable than your weakest hard dependency.** Budget your SLO across your dependency graph; if it does not fit, lower the SLO, soften a dependency, add redundancy, or push the dependency to improve — and name which.
- **Spend the error budget deliberately.** An unused budget means you over-invested in reliability and under-invested in velocity. The budget is the exchange rate between reliability, speed, and cost — optimize the whole system subject to the constraint, don't maximize one term.
- **Rehearse the unhappy path in the design review.** Walk every design through spike, cascade, and storm, and name exactly what saves you in each. A design that has not been stress-tested on paper is a hope, not a design.
