Going multi-region is the single biggest jump in complexity a system ever makes, and most of the teams that make it could not, in a design review, tell you which of the three legitimate reasons drove them to it. They went multi-region because a big customer asked "are you multi-region?" in a security questionnaire, or because an architect wanted the diagram to look serious, or because someone read that a competitor was active-active and did not want to be left behind. None of those is a reason. A reason is one of exactly three things: you need to survive a full region outage (availability), you need writes or reads to be fast for users on another continent (latency), or the law requires certain data to physically stay inside a jurisdiction (residency). If you cannot point at one of those three with a number attached, you do not have a multi-region problem — you have a single-region system that someone wants to make four times harder to operate for the feeling of seriousness. Figure 1 lays out the topologies and what each one costs across the dimensions a senior actually weighs, and the entire rest of this post is about reading that table honestly.

I have watched this jump go badly more times than I have watched it go well. I watched a team spend nine months building active-active across two regions, ship it, and then discover that their "active-active" database was silently routing every write back to a single primary across the Atlantic — they had paid for two regions and gotten one region's write latency *plus* a 150ms round trip on every write, the worst of both worlds, because nobody had asked where the system of record actually lived. I watched another team flip on automated cross-region failover, feel proud, and then take a 26-hour outage when a 40-second network blip promoted a standby while the original primary was still alive and accepting writes — a textbook split-brain that a human-in-the-loop runbook would have prevented. And I watched a third team get a GDPR finding in an audit because their "EU customers" data was being replicated to read replicas in three US regions for performance, which is exactly the thing the residency requirement existed to prevent.

This post is the architect's guide to not being any of those three teams. We will start with the physics — the speed of light is a hard floor under cross-region latency, and once you internalize that you stop trying to buy your way out of it with money. Then we will walk the topologies in the order a design review walks them: single-region first (the default that is right far more often than ambition admits), then active-passive (warm standby and failover, the cheapest real multi-region posture), then active-active (the hardest, where conflict resolution becomes unavoidable and most teams underestimate it by an order of magnitude). We will get concrete about data — where the system of record lives, read replicas versus multi-master, the conflict-resolution menu (CRDT, last-write-wins, version vectors), and pinning data to a region for residency. We will cover routing (GeoDNS, anycast, latency-based), failover (RTO, RPO, the promotion runbook, why automated failover is dangerous), cell-based and shuffle-sharding for blast-radius isolation, and the brutal economics of cross-region egress. The optimization lens runs through all of it: keep reads local, route writes to the home region, async-replicate, and buy only the consistency you can afford across the WAN. By the end you should be able to look at an SLO and a latency target and say, with RTO and RPO numbers, which topology the business can afford — and defend it when someone asks "what happens when us-east-1 goes down?"

The `database/` and `message-queue/` folders on this blog already deep-dive the *mechanisms* underneath all of this: how [synchronous and asynchronous replication move bytes](/blog/software-development/database/database-replication-sync-async-logical-physical), how [leader, multi-leader, and leaderless topologies coordinate writes](/blog/software-development/database/distributed-replication-leader-multi-leader-leaderless), and how [Spanner uses TrueTime to get external consistency across regions](/blog/software-development/database/spanner-truetime-and-external-consistency). This post is the decision layer on top of those. I will not re-derive how a WAL ships across a WAN; I will tell you how to *choose* a topology against an SLO, how to *compute* the stale-read window your users will see, how to *cost* the failover in RTO and RPO, and how to *defend* the whole thing in a review.

## 1. The speed of light is the budget you cannot expand

Start with the one constraint you cannot engineer around, because every other decision in this post is downstream of it. Light travels about 300,000 km/s in a vacuum, but in fiber it is roughly two-thirds of that — call it 200,000 km/s. Virginia to Frankfurt is about 6,600 km of great-circle distance, but real fiber routes are longer and not straight, so the cable path is closer to 7,500 km. One way at 200,000 km/s is about 37ms; the round trip is about 75ms *of pure propagation*, before you add a single millisecond of switching, serialization, TLS handshakes, or queueing. In practice the measured round-trip time (RTT) between those regions sits around 85 to 95ms. Virginia to Singapore is more like 230ms RTT. Virginia to Sydney is around 200ms. These are not numbers you can tune down with a faster server or a fatter pipe — they are set by geography and the speed of light, and they are the hard floor under everything.

This single fact kills the most seductive multi-region fantasy before you ever write a line of code: synchronous, strongly consistent writes across continents, cheaply. If you want a write in Virginia to be confirmed durable in Frankfurt before you acknowledge it to the user — true synchronous cross-region replication — every such write pays at least one cross-region round trip, ~90ms, on top of its local work. A request that was a crisp 8ms p99 in one region becomes a 100ms+ p99 the moment you make it cross-region-synchronous. And if you need a *quorum* across three regions (a common strong-consistency setup), the write waits for the second-fastest region to acknowledge, which is the median cross-region RTT, not the minimum. There is no clever protocol that beats this; consensus protocols like Raft and Paxos still have to send messages at the speed of light. Google's Spanner gets external consistency across regions, but it does so by *waiting out* clock uncertainty with TrueTime and by accepting exactly this cross-region commit latency — [the Spanner deep-dive](/blog/software-development/database/spanner-truetime-and-external-consistency) walks through why even a system engineered specifically for this pays the WAN tax on writes. The lesson a senior takes from the physics is not "multi-region is impossible." It is: **you can have low write latency or strong cross-region consistency, but across continents you cannot have both cheaply, so decide which one this data actually needs.**

The corollary that makes multi-region tractable is equally important: *reads* do not have this problem if you replicate the data ahead of time. A read served from a local replica is a local read — single-digit milliseconds — regardless of how far the system of record is. The entire optimization game in multi-region, which we will return to repeatedly, is to make the common path (reads, and writes to the user's home region) local, and to push the unavoidable cross-region latency onto the rare path (cross-region writes, replication, failover). Get that split right and a global system feels local to every user. Get it wrong — synchronous writes on the hot path, or reads that hairpin across an ocean to a far-away primary — and you have built a slow global system that costs four times as much as a fast local one.

## 2. Single-region: the default you should have to argue your way out of

Before any topology with the word "region" plural in it, the honest default is a single region with multiple availability zones (AZs). One region, three AZs, synchronous replication across the AZs (because intra-region RTT is sub-millisecond to low-single-digit milliseconds — you can afford sync there), automatic AZ failover. This survives the failure of any one data center, gives you strong consistency cheaply, and is operationally simple enough that a small team can run it. The overwhelming majority of systems — SaaS apps, internal tools, most e-commerce, most B2B — are correctly served by exactly this, and the burden of proof is on whoever proposes more.

It is worth being precise about what a single multi-AZ region does and does not protect against, because the gap is exactly the case multi-region exists to cover. A multi-AZ region survives: a single server dying, a rack losing power, a whole data center (AZ) going offline, a bad deploy that you can roll back. It does *not* survive: a whole-region outage (a control-plane failure, a regional network partition, a regional power event, a fat-fingered change to a regional service). Those happen — every major cloud has had region-wide degradations — but they are rare, measured in a handful of hours per year at most for a given region, and often shorter. So the question that decides single-region versus multi-region is not "could a region ever fail?" (yes) but "is the cost of the rare region failure, weighed against its probability, larger than the cost of running multi-region all year?" For a system whose SLO is 99.9% (about 8.7 hours of allowed downtime per year), a single multi-AZ region usually fits comfortably — a region event a couple of times a year for a couple of hours each is within budget if your single-region availability is otherwise high. For a system whose SLO is 99.99% (about 52 minutes per year), a single region event can blow the entire annual budget in one afternoon, and *that* is the availability case for multi-region.

The senior framing here is to make this a *numbers* conversation, not a *feelings* one. Write down the SLO. Estimate the single-region availability you can actually achieve (including the probability and duration of region-wide events for your provider and region). If the single-region number meets the SLO with margin, you are done — go single-region and spend the saved complexity budget on something that moves the product. If it does not, you have an availability-driven reason for multi-region, and now you can size the cheapest topology that closes the gap, which is almost always active-passive, not active-active. The mistake is skipping this arithmetic and jumping straight to active-active because it sounds the most robust. The most robust topology you cannot operate correctly is *less* available than the simpler one you can.

## 3. The three reasons, and how each picks a topology

There are exactly three reasons that justify going multi-region, and the useful thing about them is that each one points at a *different* topology. Naming your reason is therefore most of the design work. Figure 5 encodes this as the decision tree I actually walk in reviews.

![Decision tree starting from whether multi-region is needed at all, branching by availability, latency, and whether data can merge, down to the recommended topology](/imgs/blogs/multi-region-and-geo-distribution-5.webp)

**Availability — survive a region outage.** Your SLO is tight enough that a region-wide event would blow your error budget. The cheapest topology that solves this is active-passive: one region serves all traffic, a second region holds a warm, continuously-replicated standby, and on a region outage you fail over to it. You do *not* need writes to be fast in the second region (nobody is using it until failover), so you do not take on conflict resolution. This is the most common legitimate multi-region posture, and the one teams skip past too fast on their way to active-active.

**Latency — fast for users far away.** Your users are spread across continents and the cross-ocean round trip is making the product feel slow for some of them. The fix depends on whether it is *reads* or *writes* that are slow. If it is reads, you do not need a write-active second region at all — you need read replicas near the far users, which is a much cheaper change than full active-active. If it is *writes* that must be fast on multiple continents (a user in Sydney must get a fast write acknowledgement, not a 200ms one), then you genuinely need writes accepted locally in multiple regions, and that is where active-active and its conflict problem become unavoidable. Most "we need low latency globally" requirements are actually read-latency requirements in disguise, and the senior move is to check which before signing up for active-active.

**Residency — the law pins data to a place.** GDPR, China's data localization rules, India's, Russia's, and a growing list of others require that certain personal data physically reside (and sometimes be processed) inside a jurisdiction. This is not a performance requirement and you cannot solve it with replicas-for-speed — in fact, naive replication is the thing that *violates* it, because copying EU personal data to a US replica for performance is exactly what the rule forbids. Residency forces a *data-partitioned* topology where each user's regulated data lives in (and does not leave) their jurisdiction's region. We will design this concretely in section 11.

The reason it matters to separate these three is that they have wildly different costs and the cheapest solution to one is overkill or wrong for another. Availability wants active-passive. Read-latency wants read replicas. Write-latency wants active-active. Residency wants data partitioning by jurisdiction. A team that names its reason as "we need to be global" and reaches for active-active because it covers everything ends up paying active-active's full conflict-resolution and egress cost to solve a problem that read replicas would have solved for a fraction of the complexity. Name the reason; the reason names the topology.

## 4. Active-passive: warm standby and the failover you must rehearse

Active-passive is the workhorse of multi-region availability, and it is worth understanding its variants precisely because they trade RTO and RPO against standby cost on a clean spectrum. Figure 8 lays the spectrum out; figure 2 contrasts the active-passive posture with active-active.

![Matrix showing recovery time, recovery point, standby cost, and failover risk for backup restore, pilot light, warm standby, and active-active sync strategies](/imgs/blogs/multi-region-and-geo-distribution-8.webp)

Two numbers govern every failover design, and a senior puts them on the table before anything else. **RTO (Recovery Time Objective)** is how long the system is down during a failover — the wall-clock time from "region is gone" to "users are served again." **RPO (Recovery Point Objective)** is how much data you can lose — the time window of acknowledged writes that may vanish because they had not yet replicated to the standby when the primary died. RTO is about *availability*; RPO is about *durability*. They are independent, and you buy each down separately by spending money: you buy a smaller RTO with warmer standby capacity (so there is less to start up), and you buy a smaller RPO with tighter replication (synchronous instead of async).

The active-passive variants, from cheapest to warmest:

**Backup-and-restore (cold).** The second region holds nothing running — just backups in object storage. On a disaster you provision infrastructure and restore from the last backup. RTO is hours (provisioning plus restore of a large dataset). RPO is the age of the last backup — if you back up nightly, you can lose up to a day. Cost is near zero (just storage). This is fine for systems where a day-old recovery point and an hours-long RTO are acceptable, which is more systems than people admit, but not for anything customer-facing with a tight SLO.

**Pilot light.** A minimal version of the system runs in the second region — the database is replicating continuously (so RPO is small), but the application tier is scaled to near zero. On failover you scale up the app tier and redirect traffic. RTO drops to tens of minutes (no data restore, just scale-up). RPO is small (continuous replication). Cost is low (you pay for the replicating database and a skeleton app tier). This is a strong default for cost-conscious teams with moderate SLOs.

**Warm standby.** A scaled-down but fully functional copy of the whole system runs in the second region, continuously replicated. On failover you scale it up to full capacity (or it is already at full capacity, in which case it is "hot standby") and redirect. RTO is single-digit minutes; RPO is your async replication lag, typically around a second. Cost is roughly the cost of a second region (you are running a real, if smaller, copy). This is the standard for systems that need region-failure survival with a tight RTO.

The thing every variant shares, and the thing teams get wrong, is that **failover is a runbook you have to rehearse, not a feature you turn on.** A standby that has never served real traffic is a hypothesis, not a backup. The first time you fail over for real — under stress, at 3am, with a region actually down — you will discover the DNS TTL nobody lowered, the IAM role that only existed in the primary region, the secret that was not replicated, the database connection string hard-coded to the primary's endpoint, the capacity that was never actually load-tested in the standby. Teams that run regular failover drills (GameDays) have failovers that take minutes; teams that have never drilled have failovers that take hours or fail outright. The RTO on your slide is fiction until you have measured it in a drill. Figure 4 shows where the time actually goes in a real region failover, and the surprise for most people is how little of it is the promotion itself.

## 5. Why automated failover is dangerous: detection, decision, and split-brain

The instinct, once you have a warm standby, is to automate the failover: detect the primary is down, promote the standby, redirect traffic, all without a human. This instinct is usually wrong for region failover, and understanding why is one of the more senior things in this whole post.

The problem is that *detecting a region outage is genuinely hard to distinguish from a network partition between you and the region.* When your health checker in region B cannot reach the primary in region A, there are two possibilities, and they require opposite responses. Either (a) region A is actually down, in which case you should promote B's standby, or (b) region A is fine but the network path from your health checker to it is broken — a partition — in which case region A is still serving users on its side, and if you promote B you now have *two* primaries accepting writes. That is split-brain, and it is the most dangerous failure in this entire domain because it does not error — both halves work, independently, and silently diverge. When the partition heals you have two divergent write histories and no automatic way to reconcile them, which is how a 40-second blip turns into a multi-day data-repair outage.

Look at the timeline in figure 4 again with this in mind. The 90 seconds of failed health checks and the five minutes before a human confirms are not wasted time — they are *exactly the buffer that prevents a transient blip from triggering an unnecessary, dangerous failover.* The slowest, most deliberate part of the runbook is the part that protects you. A failover is a high-stakes, low-frequency, hard-to-reverse action; those are precisely the actions you want a human to confirm. Automated failover optimizes the wrong thing — it shaves a few minutes off RTO in exchange for a meaningfully higher probability of a catastrophic split-brain. For most systems that is a terrible trade. GitHub's October 2018 incident is the canonical example: an automated orchestration layer promoted a database replica in a second region during a 43-second cross-region network blip, the original primary kept accepting writes, and reconciling the two diverged clusters took over 24 hours of degraded operation. The 43-second blip would have been a 43-second blip if a human had been the one to decide.

If you do automate any of it, the non-negotiable safeguard is **fencing**: a mechanism that guarantees the old primary *cannot* keep accepting writes once you have promoted a new one. The options are quorum-gated promotion (you can only promote if you can reach a majority of a coordination quorum — so a minority-side health checker physically cannot trigger promotion), STONITH ("shoot the other node in the head" — forcibly kill or network-isolate the old primary before promoting), or fencing tokens / leases (the old primary holds a time-limited lease and stops accepting writes when it cannot renew it, so it self-fences during a partition). Fencing is what makes promotion safe; without it, automated promotion is an automated split-brain generator. The [consensus and coordination](/blog/software-development/system-design/consensus-and-coordination-in-distributed-systems) sibling post covers how to wire leases and quorum leadership into a design, and [Raft from scratch](/blog/software-development/database/raft-consensus-from-scratch) covers the consensus primitive underneath quorum-gated promotion.

#### Worked example: sizing RTO and RPO for a payments API

You run a payments API with a 99.99% availability SLO (about 52 minutes of allowed downtime per year) and a hard requirement that no acknowledged payment is ever lost (RPO = 0 for the payment-write path). The product team wants to know: which topology, and what will the numbers be?

Start with RPO = 0. That requirement alone rules out pure asynchronous replication for the payment write path, because async replication's RPO equals the replication lag at the moment of failure, which is nonzero (typically around a second, worse under load). To get RPO = 0 you must replicate payment writes synchronously to at least one node in another failure domain before acknowledging. Within a region, synchronous replication across AZs costs you only a low-single-digit-millisecond round trip — affordable. Across regions, synchronous replication costs you ~90ms per payment write — a real tax, but for a payment (which is not a high-QPS hot-path read) that may be acceptable. So the design is: payment writes commit synchronously to a quorum that spans AZs (and possibly a nearby second region), giving RPO = 0; everything else (read traffic, telemetry) replicates async.

Now RTO. The SLO budget is 52 min/year. If you expect, say, one region event per year that affects you, your failover RTO must be well under 52 minutes to leave room for everything else. With a warm standby and a rehearsed runbook, an RTO of ~5–10 minutes is achievable. But notice: a human-in-the-loop confirmation adds ~5 minutes of deliberate delay (to avoid split-brain), and that is *inside* your RTO. So the math is roughly: 90s detection + 5 min human confirm + 2 min promote/fence + 1–2 min DNS drain ≈ 9–11 min RTO. One such event per year consumes ~10 of your 52 minutes — fine. Two events consume ~20 minutes — still fine. The arithmetic says active-passive with a warm standby, synchronous payment writes for RPO = 0, human-confirmed failover with fencing, meets the SLO with margin. You do *not* need active-active for this, and active-active would actually *hurt* the RPO = 0 requirement by introducing the possibility of conflicting concurrent payment writes. The senior recommendation is the simpler topology, justified with numbers.

## 6. Active-active: the hardest topology, and why conflict resolution is unavoidable

Active-active means every region accepts writes locally. This is the topology you adopt when you genuinely need fast *writes* on multiple continents — the Sydney user who cannot tolerate a 200ms write round trip to Virginia. The win is real: writes are local everywhere, reads are local everywhere, and the system survives any single region with no failover step at all (the other regions are already serving). It is also, by a wide margin, the hardest thing in this post, and the place where teams most consistently underestimate the difficulty by an order of magnitude. Figure 2 contrasts it directly with active-passive.

![Before and after comparison contrasting active-passive with one writable home region and async standby against active-active accepting local writes everywhere with conflict resolution](/imgs/blogs/multi-region-and-geo-distribution-2.webp)

Here is the unavoidable problem. If Frankfurt and Virginia both accept writes locally to stay fast, and replication between them is asynchronous (it has to be — synchronous would re-impose the WAN round trip on every write and defeat the purpose), then there is a window during which the same logical record can be written in both regions concurrently before either has seen the other's write. When the two writes meet during replication, they *conflict*, and the system must resolve the conflict. There is no way to avoid this by being careful, because the speed of light guarantees a replication window during which both regions are ignorant of each other's recent writes. Active-active *is* a multi-leader system, and [the multi-leader replication deep-dive](/blog/software-development/database/distributed-replication-leader-multi-leader-leaderless) covers the mechanics; here we care about the *menu of conflict-resolution policies and their costs*, because choosing the wrong one is how active-active silently corrupts data.

**Last-write-wins (LWW).** When two writes conflict, keep the one with the higher timestamp and discard the other. This is the default in many systems because it is simple and always produces an answer. It is also the policy that silently destroys data, and it is a trap. The problem is twofold: first, clocks across regions are not perfectly synchronized (clock skew of milliseconds to tens of milliseconds is normal), so "higher timestamp" does not reliably mean "happened later" — you can discard the write that actually happened second. Second, and worse, LWW is *only correct for data where overwriting is the right semantics.* For a user's display name, LWW is fine — if they set it in two places concurrently, keeping one is acceptable. For an inventory count, a bank balance, or a collaborative document, LWW is catastrophic, because the two concurrent writes both represent *real, distinct intent* (two real sales, two real deposits, two real edits) and overwriting one silently loses real work. The retailer whose inventory went negative because LWW discarded a concurrent decrement learned this the expensive way.

**CRDTs (Conflict-free Replicated Data Types).** For data that can be *merged* rather than overwritten, model it as a CRDT and conflicts disappear by construction. A counter that supports increment/decrement can be a PN-Counter: each region tracks its own increments and decrements, and the merged value is the sum across regions — so "Frankfurt sold 3, Virginia sold 4, start was 10" merges deterministically to 10 − 3 − 4 = 3, regardless of order, partition, or clock skew. Sets can be OR-Sets (observed-remove sets). Collaborative text can use sequence CRDTs. The win is that merge is automatic, commutative, associative, and idempotent — every region converges to the same value with no coordination. The cost is that not all data fits a CRDT (some operations genuinely require a single point of decision, like "is this username already taken?"), and CRDTs carry metadata overhead. But for the data that *does* fit — counters, sets, collaborative documents, shopping carts — CRDTs are the correct answer in active-active, and "we used LWW because it was the default" is the wrong one.

**Version vectors / surfacing the conflict.** For data that cannot be merged automatically and cannot be safely overwritten, the honest move is to *detect* the conflict (using version vectors, which track causality so you can tell concurrent writes from sequential ones) and *surface* it — store both versions and let the application or the user resolve it (the way a git merge conflict or an early Dynamo shopping cart works). This is more work but it never silently loses data. The cost is application complexity (you must handle the "here are two conflicting versions" case everywhere).

The senior discipline for active-active is to answer the conflict question *per dataset* before adopting it: which data is mergeable (CRDT), which can surface conflicts (version vectors), and which can do neither — and the data that can do neither *should not be in an active-active store at all.* That last category — data that needs a single global point of decision, like uniqueness constraints, sequential IDs, or strict ordering — either stays single-region/home-region-routed, or goes into a system built to provide global strong consistency at the cost of cross-region latency, like [Spanner](/blog/software-development/database/spanner-truetime-and-external-consistency) or [CockroachDB](/blog/software-development/database/cockroachdb-distributed-sql-deep-dive). Trying to force such data into LWW active-active is the single most common way active-active goes wrong.

## 7. The data layer is the whole problem: where the system of record lives

Everything above orbits one question that teams routinely fail to answer explicitly: *where does the system of record live?* The application tier is easy to make multi-region — it is stateless, you run copies everywhere. The hard part, the part that *is* the multi-region problem, is the stateful data layer. And the single most important sentence you can say in a multi-region design review is "the system of record for this data lives in region X, and here is how every other region reads and writes it." Figure 6 shows the optimization that makes this fast.

![Before and after comparison of synchronous cross-region writes paying a wide-area round trip per commit against asynchronous replication with local reads keeping the hot path fast](/imgs/blogs/multi-region-and-geo-distribution-6.webp)

There are three fundamental data patterns, and most real systems use a mix:

**Single write region, read replicas everywhere (read-local, write-home).** One region is the system of record — all writes go there. Every other region has read replicas that receive asynchronous replication. Reads are local everywhere (fast); writes hairpin to the home region (a cross-region round trip if the writer is far away, but writes are usually rarer than reads). This is the cheapest way to get global read latency, it has no conflict problem (single write region = single point of serialization, just like single-leader), and it handles the common "global users, mostly reading" case beautifully. The cost: writers far from the home region pay the WAN round trip on writes, and a home-region outage stops all writes until you fail over the home region (so you pair this with active-passive on the home region). This is the topology I reach for *first* when the requirement is read-latency, and it is dramatically simpler than active-active.

**Multi-master (active-active).** Every region writes locally; covered in section 6. Use only when write-latency genuinely requires it and you have done the per-dataset conflict analysis.

**Partitioned by home region (data sharding by geography).** Each user (or tenant) has a *home region* where their data is mastered, chosen by where they are. A Frankfurt user's data is mastered in Frankfurt; a Virginia user's data is mastered in Virginia. Writes to a user's data are routed to that user's home region (local for them, fast); other regions hold read replicas of it for cross-user reads. This is the elegant middle path: it gives local writes *for the common case* (you mostly write your own data) without the conflict problem of true multi-master (because each record has exactly one writer region — its home), and it is the natural fit for residency (a user's home region can be chosen to satisfy the law). The cost is the routing layer (you must know each record's home region to route its writes there) and the cross-region read latency or staleness for the rare case of reading another region's data. This pattern — sometimes called "geo-partitioning" or "regional sharding" — is how a lot of large global systems actually work, and it is underused because it requires thinking of your data as partitioned by user rather than as one global blob. [Partitioning and sharding without downtime](/blog/software-development/system-design/partitioning-and-sharding-without-downtime) covers the mechanics of the routing and rebalancing this requires.

The optimization principle across all three, illustrated in figure 6, is the same: **keep reads local, route writes to wherever the record is mastered, replicate asynchronously, and pick the weakest consistency the data can tolerate across regions.** Synchronous cross-region writes are the thing to avoid unless a specific dataset truly needs RPO = 0 or global strong consistency, because they impose the WAN round trip on the hot path. Async replication with local reads keeps p99 write latency at single-region levels and pushes the cross-region cost onto background replication, where users never feel it directly — they only feel it as *staleness*, which we can bound and reason about.

## 8. Computing the stale-read window

When you replicate asynchronously, far regions are behind the system of record by the replication lag, and a read from a far region can return data that is stale by up to that lag. This is not a bug to be eliminated (you cannot eliminate it without paying the synchronous WAN tax); it is a property to be *measured and bounded*, because the size of the stale-read window determines whether async replication is acceptable for a given read. A senior puts a number on it.

The stale-read window has two components. First, the *replication lag* itself: how long it takes a write committed in the home region to be applied in the far region. This is dominated by the cross-region network RTT (you cannot apply a write before its bytes arrive — ~90ms minimum for Virginia-Frankfurt) plus any queueing in the replication pipeline (which grows under write load). At rest, replication lag might be ~100–200ms; under heavy write load, replication queues build and lag can climb to seconds. Second, the *consistency model* you expose: if you serve reads from the far replica with no freshness guarantee, a reader can see data up to the current lag old. The window the user actually experiences is the replication lag at the moment of their read.

The reason this matters for design is that *different reads tolerate different staleness*, and you route them differently:

- **Reads that tolerate seconds of staleness** (a product catalog, another user's profile, a news feed, analytics dashboards) go to the local replica with no special handling. The async lag is invisible because the data does not need to be fresh.
- **Reads that must reflect the user's own recent write** (the read-your-writes case — you just updated your profile, you must see the new value) cannot go to a lagging local replica, because the user will see their old value and think the write was lost. You route these to the home region, or you pin the user's reads for a short window, exactly as in the [replication strategies post](/blog/software-development/system-design/replication-strategies-and-their-failure-modes).
- **Reads that must be globally fresh** (rare — a balance check before a high-stakes transaction, a uniqueness check) must go to the system of record and pay the cross-region round trip, or use a system that provides global strong consistency.

#### Worked example: bounding the stale-read window for a global feed

You run a social product. Posts are mastered in us-east. EU users read their feed from an eu-west read replica that receives async replication. A product manager asks: "if I post something, how long until my EU followers can see it, and is that a problem?"

Compute the lag. A post commits in us-east. The replication stream ships it to eu-west. The floor is the one-way cross-region propagation, ~45ms. Add the replication pipeline's batching and apply time; at moderate load this might be another 50–150ms, so the typical lag is ~100–200ms. Under a traffic spike (a viral moment, peak hours), the replication queue backs up and lag can climb — measure your lag p99 *under peak load*, not at rest, because at rest it lies. Say your measured eu-west lag p99 under peak is 1.2 seconds. That means: when a US user posts, their EU followers see it within ~200ms typically, and within ~1.2s in the worst case at peak. **Is that a problem?** For a social feed, no — a follower seeing a post 1.2 seconds after it was posted is completely invisible; nobody perceives sub-two-second feed propagation as "broken." So async replication with local EU reads is correct here, and the stale-read window (≤1.2s p99) is well within tolerance.

Now change the data. Suppose instead this is a *chat* product and the "read" is the recipient seeing a message. A 1.2-second worst-case delay for a chat message is borderline — users notice multi-second message delays. And suppose it is a *trading* product and the read is a price quote: a 1.2-second stale price is unacceptable and dangerous. *Same replication lag, three different verdicts*, because the tolerance for staleness is a property of the use case, not the infrastructure. The senior deliverable is not "our lag is 1.2s" — it is "our lag p99 under peak is 1.2s, the feed tolerates seconds so this is fine, but the read-your-writes path for the poster's own view is pinned to us-east so they never see their own post lag." That sentence is the design.

## 9. Routing: GeoDNS, anycast, and latency-based steering

Once you have regions, you need to get each user to the right one, and the routing layer is where a lot of the actual latency win is captured or lost. There are three mechanisms, and they operate at different layers. Figure 3 shows latency-based routing steering users to their nearest healthy region.

![Graph showing EU and US users hitting a latency-based geo resolver that routes each to the nearest healthy region while a far region sits at higher latency](/imgs/blogs/multi-region-and-geo-distribution-3.webp)

**GeoDNS / latency-based DNS.** When a user resolves your domain, the DNS layer returns the IP of the region nearest to them (by geography, or better, by *measured latency*). Latency-based routing (as in AWS Route 53's latency policy or equivalent) is superior to pure geo-routing because it routes by actual measured network latency, which does not always match geographic distance (a user geographically near one region might have a faster network path to another). DNS-based routing is simple and works for any protocol, but it has one important weakness for failover: **DNS is cached**, and the cache respects the record's TTL. If your TTL is 300 seconds, a client that resolved to a now-dead region may keep hitting it for up to 5 minutes after you change the DNS to point elsewhere. This is why failover runbooks lower DNS TTLs in advance (to 30–60 seconds) — your DNS-based failover RTO has a floor equal to your TTL plus client cache behavior, and some clients ignore TTLs entirely. DNS failover is real but it is *slow and imprecise*, which is a reason to not rely on it alone for fast failover.

**Anycast.** The same IP address is announced from multiple regions via BGP, and the internet's routing fabric delivers a user's packets to the *topologically nearest* region announcing that IP. The user does not resolve different IPs; there is one IP, and the network routes it locally. Anycast is how CDNs and large DNS providers route, and its failover is beautiful: if a region stops announcing the anycast IP (because it died), BGP reconverges and traffic flows to the next-nearest region automatically, in seconds, with no DNS cache to wait out. The cost is that anycast is operationally heavier (you need BGP, your own IP space or a provider that does this), and it is best for stateless edge layers (CDN, edge proxies, DNS) rather than for routing to a specific stateful region, because BGP can re-route mid-connection in ways that break long-lived stateful sessions. The common pattern is anycast at the edge (for the fast, failover-friendly front door) handing off to region-pinned backends.

**Application-layer / client-side routing.** The smartest routing is often in your own code or a global load balancer that knows your topology: the client or a routing tier knows each user's *home region* (for geo-partitioned data) and routes their write requests there directly, while routing reads to the nearest replica. This is the routing that geo-partitioning (section 7) requires, and it is more precise than DNS or anycast because it routes per-request with full knowledge of where each record is mastered. A global load balancer (like a cloud global LB or an Envoy mesh) can do latency-based routing for reads and home-region routing for writes in the same request path. This is where the [load balancing from L4 to L7](/blog/software-development/system-design/load-balancing-from-l4-to-l7) decisions intersect with geo-distribution.

The practical architecture most large systems converge on layers these: anycast at the edge for a fast, failover-resilient front door; latency-based routing to send reads to the nearest region; and application-aware routing to send each write to its record's home region. No single mechanism does everything, and the senior move is to use each where it is strong.

## 10. Cell-based architecture and shuffle-sharding: capping the blast radius

Multi-region buys you survival of a whole-region failure, but it does nothing for the failure that is just as common and far sneakier: a *poison* failure that affects all regions at once because it is in your code or data, not your infrastructure. A bad deploy, a poison-pill request that crashes every server that processes it, a corrupt config pushed everywhere, a single hot tenant that overwhelms shared capacity — these failures do not respect region boundaries. They hit every region, because every region runs the same code. Multi-region is not a defense against them. Cell-based architecture is. Figure 7 shows the idea.

![Grid showing a cell router shuffle-sharding users across independent cells where one cell is degraded while the others keep serving their share of users](/imgs/blogs/multi-region-and-geo-distribution-7.webp)

A **cell** is a complete, independent instance of your stack — its own servers, its own data partition, its own everything — that serves a *subset* of your users. Instead of one big region serving all users, you partition each region into, say, ten cells, each serving 10% of users, with no shared fate between cells. The payoff is **blast-radius isolation**: when a poison request or a bad deploy takes down a cell, it takes down *that cell's 10% of users*, not everyone. Your worst-case incident goes from "100% of users down" to "10% of users down," which is the difference between a company-ending outage and a contained incident. You also get a safe deploy strategy for free: deploy to one cell, watch it, then roll forward cell by cell, so a bad deploy is caught at 10% blast radius instead of 100%. AWS uses cell-based architecture extensively for exactly this reason, and it is one of the highest-leverage availability patterns that has nothing to do with adding regions.

**Shuffle-sharding** is a refinement that dramatically improves the isolation math for a given number of cells. In plain cell-sharding, if you have 8 cells and assign each customer to 1 cell, a customer is fully exposed to their one cell's failures. In shuffle-sharding, you assign each customer to a *random pair* (or small subset) of cells, and the customer's requests can be served by either. The magic is combinatorial: with 8 cells and customers assigned to random *pairs*, there are 28 distinct pairs, so two customers share *both* their cells only rarely. A poison customer that takes down their 2 cells only fully overlaps with customers who happen to share both of those exact cells — a small fraction. Most other customers share at most one cell with the poison customer and so retain a healthy cell to be served from. Shuffle-sharding turns "one bad tenant takes down everyone in their cell" into "one bad tenant degrades only the small set of tenants who share their exact shard," which for realistic numbers can mean a single abusive customer affects well under 1% of others instead of one-Nth. AWS published the canonical analysis of this; the takeaway for an architect is that shuffle-sharding gives you the isolation of having far more cells than you actually run.

The relationship between cells and regions: they are *orthogonal* and you want both. Regions protect against infrastructure failure of a whole geography; cells protect against poison failures that cross regions. A mature large system is multi-region (for geographic survival) *and* cell-based within each region (for blast-radius isolation), because the two defend against different failure classes. A team that has gone multi-region but runs one giant cell per region has spent enormous effort on the rarer failure (whole-region outage) while remaining fully exposed to the more common one (a bad deploy taking down everyone). Cell-based architecture is frequently the higher-leverage investment, and it does not require a second region.

## 11. Data residency: pinning data to a jurisdiction

Residency is the reason for multi-region that has nothing to do with performance and cannot be solved by replicating-for-speed — in fact, naive replication *violates* it. The requirement is legal: certain personal data must physically reside (and sometimes be processed) inside a specific jurisdiction. GDPR governs EU residents' personal data, China's PIPL and data-localization rules govern data in China, and a growing list of countries have similar rules. The penalty for getting it wrong is regulatory (GDPR fines reach into the tens of millions of euros or a percentage of global revenue), so this is a correctness requirement with teeth, not a nice-to-have. Figure 9 shows the routing structure.

![Decision tree classifying a user record by jurisdiction, pinning EU personal data to an EU region while allowing US records to replicate to global read replicas](/imgs/blogs/multi-region-and-geo-distribution-9.webp)

The architecture residency forces is *data partitioning by jurisdiction*, and it interacts with everything above. Each user's regulated data is assigned a home region chosen to satisfy the law (an EU resident's personal data is mastered in an EU region and *does not leave it*). This is the geo-partitioning pattern from section 7, but now the partition key is legal jurisdiction rather than performance, and the constraint is one-directional: EU personal data may not be replicated to a US region *even for read performance*. That single constraint breaks the naive "read replicas everywhere" optimization — you cannot put EU personal data on a US replica, full stop.

The design moves that make this tractable:

**Classify data by sensitivity.** Not all of a user's data is regulated personal data. The user's name, email, and address are personal data (pinned). Aggregate, anonymized, or non-personal metadata often is not, and *may* be replicated globally. The architecture in figure 9 splits the record: PII is pinned to the home region; non-PII metadata can replicate to other regions for performance. Getting this split right (with legal counsel, not just engineering judgment) lets you keep most of the performance benefits of replication for the data that is allowed to move, while pinning only what the law actually requires.

**Route by the data's home region, not the request's origin.** A residency-correct system routes every access to a user's regulated data to that user's home region, regardless of where the request came from. An EU user traveling in the US still has their personal data served from the EU region (it never moved). This requires the same application-aware routing as geo-partitioning: the system must know each record's jurisdiction and route accordingly.

**Keep a global "directory" that is itself non-personal.** You need to know which region masters a given user without that lookup itself leaking personal data. A common pattern is a globally-replicated directory mapping user ID → home region, where the user ID is an opaque token (not itself PII), so the directory can live everywhere while the actual personal data stays pinned.

**Beware the accidental copies.** The violations that get teams in trouble are rarely the primary database — that part people get right. It is the *accidental* copies: logs that contain personal data shipped to a central US logging system, backups replicated cross-region for durability, a data warehouse that ETLs everything into one place for analytics, a cache in another region that holds personal data, a third-party service (analytics, email, support tooling) that receives the data. Residency compliance is mostly about hunting down every place data flows and ensuring none of them moves regulated data out of its jurisdiction. The senior move is to treat residency as a *data-flow audit* problem (where does this data go?) not just a *database-placement* problem (where is the primary?).

#### Worked example: a residency-compliant design for an EU-and-US product

You have a product with EU and US users and a GDPR requirement that EU users' personal data stay in the EU. The naive design — one us-east primary with global read replicas — is non-compliant because EU personal data lands on US replicas. Design the compliant version.

Partition users by jurisdiction at signup. An EU resident's personal data (name, email, address, anything identifying) is mastered in eu-central and *does not replicate outside the EU*. A US resident's data is mastered in us-east. Maintain a globally-replicated directory mapping the opaque user ID to home region (`user_8f2a → eu-central`) — this directory holds no PII, so it can live everywhere and routing can resolve a user's home region from any region in one local lookup. Application servers in every region, on any request touching a user's personal data, look up the home region and route the data access there: an EU user's profile read served from eu-central even if they are physically in the US (a small latency cost for that user when traveling, accepted for compliance). Non-personal data — product catalog, aggregate metrics, the directory itself — replicates globally for performance, because it is not regulated. Logs are scrubbed of PII before leaving their region, or shipped to a region-local logging stack. Backups of EU data stay in the EU. The data warehouse either stays EU-local for EU data or ingests only anonymized EU data.

The result: EU personal data physically never leaves the EU (compliant), US users get fast local access, the directory lookup is cheap and local everywhere, and only the rare case of a traveling EU user pays a cross-region hop for their own personal data. The thing that makes this work is that you classified data into "regulated, pinned" and "non-personal, free to move," and routed accordingly — rather than treating all data as one blob that either does or does not replicate. That classification, done with legal counsel, is the whole design.

## 12. Trade-offs: the topology decision matrix

Here is the decision laid out as a table — the heart of thinking like a senior about this, because no topology is "best"; each wins a column and pays in another. This is figure 1 in prose, with the costs named explicitly.

| Topology | Read latency | Write latency | Availability | Consistency | Relative cost | Ops complexity | When it wins |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Single-region (multi-AZ)** | Local for nearby users, cross-ocean for far users | Low (one DC) | Survives an AZ; a region event is full downtime | Strong, cheap | 1.0x | Low | Default. SLO fits a single region's availability; users mostly in one geography |
| **Active-passive (warm standby)** | Same as single-region (far users still cross-ocean) | Low (home DC) | Survives a full region outage via failover | Strong on the home region | ~1.7x | Medium | You need region-outage survival; an availability SLO a single region can't meet |
| **Read replicas (multi-region reads)** | Local everywhere | Writes hairpin to home region | Reads survive region loss; writes need home-region failover | Reads eventually consistent (bounded lag) | ~1.5x | Medium | Far users are slow on *reads*; writes are rarer and can route home |
| **Geo-partitioned (home-region sharding)** | Local for own data | Local for own data, cross-region for others' | Survives a region (only that region's users affected) | Strong per-record (single home writer) | ~2.0x | High | Global users writing their *own* data; residency requirements |
| **Active-active (multi-master)** | Local everywhere | Local everywhere | Survives any region, no failover step | Eventual; conflicts need CRDT/version vectors | ~2.2x + egress | Highest | Fast *writes* required on multiple continents and data is mergeable |

The senior reading of this table: complexity and cost climb steadily down the rows, and each row buys a specific property the row above lacks. You move down only when you have a *named requirement* that the cheaper row cannot meet. The single most common mistake is jumping to the bottom row (active-active) because it has the most green-looking cells, without noticing that it pays the most in consistency (you inherit conflict resolution) and cost (full second-region capacity plus cross-region egress) and ops complexity (the hardest topology to operate correctly). A senior climbs *down* this table one row at a time, stopping at the first row that meets the requirement, and can name what the next row down would cost if the requirement changed. "We use active-passive because our reason is availability and our writes are home-region; we'd move to geo-partitioned if write-latency for far users became a requirement, which would cost us a routing layer and per-record home tracking" — that sentence is the deliverable of a design review.

## 13. The economics: cross-region egress is the silent budget killer

There is a cost dimension that does not show up in latency or availability discussions and routinely surprises teams: **cross-region data transfer is expensive, and it is charged per gigabyte, forever.** Within a region, traffic between AZs is cheap or free. The moment data crosses a region boundary — which is exactly what replication, cross-region reads, and active-active conflict traffic do constantly — you pay egress, typically around \$0.02 per GB between regions on the major clouds (and \$0.05–\$0.09+ per GB to the public internet). That sounds small until you multiply by the volume a busy system replicates.

The mechanism that makes this a budget killer is that *active-active and full cross-region replication move every write to every region, continuously, all year.* If your system writes 10 TB/day of data that must replicate to two other regions, that is 20 TB/day of cross-region egress (each write to two destinations), or about 600 TB/month, at ~\$0.02/GB ≈ \$12,000/month in egress alone — and that is *just* the replication traffic, not the cross-region reads, not the failover drills, not the analytics ETL. Egress is the line item that makes active-active's "~2.2x + egress" cost real. It is also asymmetric and easy to under-budget because it does not appear in a latency test or a load test — it appears on the bill at the end of the month, and by then the architecture is shipped.

The optimization moves a senior reaches for here, which is where this connects to [cost as a design constraint](/blog/software-development/system-design/cost-as-a-design-constraint-finops):

- **Replicate less.** Do not replicate data that does not need to be in the far region. The most common waste is replicating everything (logs, derived data, caches) when only the system-of-record data needs to cross. Replicate the minimum.
- **Compress and batch replication.** Replication streams compress well; turning on compression can cut egress volume substantially. Batching reduces per-message overhead.
- **Keep reads local.** A cross-region read pays egress for the response *and* the latency. The read-local design (section 7) is a cost optimization as much as a latency one — every read you serve from a local replica is a cross-region transfer you did not pay for.
- **Prefer geo-partitioning over full active-active when you can.** Geo-partitioning replicates each record to fewer places (its home region plus selective read replicas) rather than every record to every region, which can dramatically cut egress versus full multi-master.

The senior framing of cost here mirrors the rest of the post: cross-region egress is the price of data mobility, and the optimization is to *move less data across regions* — keep reads local, replicate selectively, compress, and do not pay to move data that no far region needs. Measure it: put cross-region egress on a dashboard *before* you ship multi-region, not after the first surprising bill.

## 14. Stress-testing the design: three scenarios a review must walk

A multi-region design is not done until it has survived, on paper, the three failures it exists to handle. A senior walks each one and states the pass condition with a number. This is the "what breaks at a region failure / a partition / a residency requirement?" discipline.

**Stress test 1: a full region outage.** The home region (or an active region) goes completely dark. What happens, and what are the RTO and RPO? For single-region, the answer is "full downtime until the region recovers" — and if that is unacceptable, the stress test has just justified multi-region. For active-passive, the pass condition is a *number*: "RTO is ~9 minutes (90s detection + 5 min human confirm + 2 min fenced promotion + 1–2 min DNS drain), RPO is ~1 second for async paths and 0 for the synchronous money path." If you cannot state those numbers, you have not designed the failover, you have hoped for it. For active-active, the answer is the best one — "no failover step, the surviving regions are already serving, RTO ≈ 0" — but it is only true if you have actually provisioned the surviving regions to *absorb the dead region's load*, which is the hidden cost: active-active with two regions each running at 60% utilization cannot absorb a region loss (the survivor would need 120%), so true region-outage survival in active-active requires running each region with enough headroom to take the others' traffic, which is more capacity (and cost) than people budget. The pass condition: "can the survivors absorb the load? show me the headroom math," which connects directly to [capacity planning and autoscaling](/blog/software-development/system-design/capacity-planning-and-autoscaling).

**Stress test 2: a network partition between regions.** The regions are all alive but cannot talk to each other. This is the split-brain trap. For active-passive with *automated* failover, the pass condition is "show me the fencing mechanism (quorum-gated promotion, STONITH, or leases) that prevents the standby from promoting while the primary is still alive on the other side of the partition" — if you cannot name it, the design fails, because it will generate a split-brain exactly like GitHub 2018. For active-active, a partition means both sides keep accepting writes (that is the whole point — high availability) and you *bank* a pile of conflicts to resolve on heal, so the pass condition is "show me the conflict-resolution policy per dataset and prove it does not silently lose a user's work" — which means CRDTs or version vectors, never bare LWW, for any data where concurrent writes both represent real intent. This is the [CAP](/blog/software-development/system-design/articulating-tradeoffs-cap-pacelc-and-beyond) choice made concrete: under a partition, active-passive (with fencing) chooses consistency (the minority side stops writing), while active-active chooses availability (both sides write, and you pay in conflicts).

**Stress test 3: a residency requirement lands mid-design.** Legal informs you that EU personal data must stay in the EU, after you have already built read replicas everywhere. What breaks? The naive "replicas everywhere" design is now non-compliant, and the pass condition is "show me which data is regulated, that it is pinned to its jurisdiction, that no logs/backups/caches/ETL move it out, and that routing sends each access to the data's home region." This is the test that catches the *accidental copies* — the team that gets this right is the team that audited every data flow, not just the primary database. Walking this stress test even when residency is not yet a requirement is cheap insurance, because residency requirements have a way of landing late (a big EU customer, a new market, a regulatory change), and a design that has already classified its data by sensitivity can comply by changing routing rather than re-architecting.

The discipline across all three: name the failure, name the topology's response, state the pass condition, and put a number on it. A review that has walked these three is a review that will not be surprised at 3am.

## 15. Code: routing reads local and writes home

The concrete shape of the read-local/write-home optimization is a routing layer that knows each record's home region and steers requests accordingly. Here is the core idea in application code — a router that sends writes to the home region's primary and reads to the local replica, falling back across regions only when needed.

```python
import os

LOCAL_REGION = os.environ["AWS_REGION"]  # e.g. "eu-west-1"

# A small, globally-replicated directory: opaque user id -> home region.
# Holds no PII, so it can live in every region and be read locally.
HOME_REGION = {
    "user_8f2a": "eu-central-1",   # EU resident: data mastered in the EU
    "user_31bc": "us-east-1",      # US resident: data mastered in us-east
}

def home_region_for(user_id: str) -> str:
    # Local directory lookup; no cross-region hop just to find the home.
    return HOME_REGION.get(user_id, LOCAL_REGION)

def read_profile(user_id: str):
    home = home_region_for(user_id)
    if home == LOCAL_REGION:
        # Best case: the record is mastered here. Read the local primary or replica.
        return db_read(region=LOCAL_REGION, key=user_id)
    # Record mastered elsewhere. Prefer a *local replica* if one is allowed
    # to exist (non-regulated data); otherwise hairpin to the home region.
    if replica_allowed(user_id):                 # residency check
        return db_read(region=LOCAL_REGION, key=user_id, replica=True)  # stale-tolerant
    return db_read(region=home, key=user_id)     # cross-region read for pinned data

def write_profile(user_id: str, patch: dict):
    home = home_region_for(user_id)
    # Writes ALWAYS go to the record's home region: single writer => no conflicts.
    # If we are far from home, this pays a cross-region round trip — accepted,
    # because writes are rarer than reads and this avoids multi-master conflicts.
    return db_write(region=home, key=user_id, patch=patch)
```

The properties this encodes are the whole optimization: reads of your own (local) data are local and fast; reads of remote data prefer a local replica when residency allows (stale-tolerant, cheap) and only hairpin cross-region when the data is pinned; and *every write for a record goes to that record's single home region*, which is what eliminates the conflict problem without paying for synchronous multi-master. A health-check and failover layer (not shown) updates the `HOME_REGION` directory during a planned or unplanned home-region failover, after fencing the old home.

For the infrastructure side, here is the shape of latency-based DNS with health checks and a low TTL for failover responsiveness, in Terraform-style config:

```hcl
# Latency-based routing: each resolver gets the region nearest to them by
# MEASURED latency, with a health check that pulls a dead region out of rotation.
resource "aws_route53_record" "api_eu" {
  zone_id = var.zone_id
  name    = "api.example.com"
  type    = "A"
  ttl     = 60                       # low TTL: failover propagates in ~1 min, not 5
  set_identifier = "eu-west-1"
  latency_routing_policy { region = "eu-west-1" }
  health_check_id = aws_route53_health_check.eu.id   # dead region -> removed
  records = [var.eu_alb_ip]
}

resource "aws_route53_record" "api_us" {
  zone_id = var.zone_id
  name    = "api.example.com"
  type    = "A"
  ttl     = 60
  set_identifier = "us-east-1"
  latency_routing_policy { region = "us-east-1" }
  health_check_id = aws_route53_health_check.us.id
  records = [var.us_alb_ip]
}
```

The low TTL is the load-bearing detail: DNS-based failover cannot be faster than the TTL plus client cache behavior, so a 60-second TTL is a deliberate trade of slightly more DNS query volume for a meaningfully faster failover. Note also that DNS health-check-based removal handles *region* failover, but it is not the fast-and-precise mechanism — for that you layer anycast at the edge (section 9), which reconverges in seconds via BGP rather than waiting out DNS caches.

## 16. Case studies: how this goes right and wrong in production

Abstract trade-offs land harder as named incidents. Four teach the signature failures and wins of multi-region.

**GitHub, October 2018 — automated cross-region failover causes split-brain.** A routine network maintenance event caused a 43-second connectivity loss between GitHub's US East and US West data centers. The MySQL orchestration software, seeing the primary as unreachable from the other coast, automatically promoted a replica in the second region — but the original primary was still alive and still accepting writes from clients on its side of the partition. For those seconds, both regions had a writable primary, and the cross-region replication left the two clusters diverged. When connectivity returned, the system could not safely reconcile the two write histories automatically, and restoring data integrity required taking the site into a degraded state for over 24 hours. The lesson is section 5 exactly: **automated failover across a network boundary, without fencing strong enough to stop the old primary from accepting writes, is a split-brain generator.** The 43-second blip would have been a 43-second blip with a human in the loop; the automation turned it into a multi-day data-repair incident. The fix space is fencing (quorum-gated promotion, leases) and human confirmation for region-level failover.

**An active-active inventory mess — last-write-wins eats real sales.** A composite of a recurring pattern: a retailer runs inventory active-active across two regions with LWW conflict resolution, because LWW was the default and "worked in testing." During a brief inter-region partition, both regional warehouses decrement the same SKU's stock count concurrently: Region A sets it from 10 to 7 (sold 3), Region B sets it from 10 to 6 (sold 4). On heal, LWW compares timestamps and keeps the higher-clock write — say Region B's "6" — silently discarding Region A's "7." But Region A's three sales *actually happened*; those units left the building. The system now believes 6 are in stock when 3 are, and oversells. The deeper bug, from section 6: **LWW resolved by overwrite a value that should have been merged.** Inventory decrements are increments on a counter — a CRDT operation (10 − 3 − 4 = 3) — not a last-write-wins string. The fix was to model inventory as a PN-Counter CRDT, after which the merge held regardless of partition or clock skew. The general lesson: in active-active, the conflict policy is not an afterthought, and LWW is the trap that looks free and silently corrupts.

**A GDPR residency finding — replicas-for-speed violate the law.** A composite of a common audit failure: a team with EU and US users runs a us-east primary with read replicas in three US regions for read performance, and replicates EU users' personal data to all of them. An audit flags it: EU personal data is being copied to and stored in US regions, which GDPR forbids. Worse, the team discovers the data is *also* in their centralized US logging system and their US data warehouse, because logs and ETL copied it too — the *accidental copies* of section 11. The remediation was exactly the section 11 design: classify data into regulated PII (pinned to an EU region, never replicated out) and non-personal data (free to move), maintain a non-PII directory for home-region routing, scrub PII from logs before they leave the EU, and keep EU backups in the EU. The lesson: **residency is a data-flow audit problem, not a database-placement problem** — the primary is the easy part; the accidental copies in logs, backups, caches, and analytics are what get you fined.

**A read-local, write-home design that scaled cleanly.** The positive case, drawn from the common shape of well-run global SaaS: a team with users on multiple continents had a read-latency problem (far users slow on reads) but home-region writes (users mostly wrote their own data). Rather than reach for active-active, they used read replicas plus geo-partitioning: each tenant mastered in a home region, read replicas of non-sensitive data in other regions, writes routed home. Reads went local everywhere (single-digit ms), writes for a tenant's own data were local for that tenant, and the only cross-region cost was the rare cross-tenant read and the async replication stream — which they kept cheap by replicating selectively and compressing. They avoided conflict resolution entirely (single home writer per record), avoided the active-active egress bill, and met their latency SLO at ~1.5x single-region cost instead of ~2.2x+. The lesson, from sections 7 and 13: **most "we need to be global" requirements are read-latency requirements that read-local/write-home solves far more cheaply than active-active** — climb down the topology table only as far as the requirement forces you.

## 17. When to reach for each topology (and when not to)

A decisive recommendation, because "it depends" is only useful when you say what it depends on.

**Default to a single multi-AZ region.** For the overwhelming majority of systems, one region across three AZs with synchronous intra-region replication and automatic AZ failover is correct, and the burden of proof is on anyone proposing more. It survives any single data center, gives strong consistency cheaply, and is operable by a small team. Go multi-region only when you can name one of the three reasons — availability (SLO a single region can't meet), latency (far users genuinely slow), or residency (the law) — with a number attached. Vanity is not a reason.

**Reach for active-passive (warm standby) when the reason is availability.** If a region-wide outage would blow your error budget, the cheapest topology that fixes it is a warm standby in a second region with human-confirmed, fenced failover. Compute the RTO and RPO, rehearse the failover regularly (an un-drilled standby is a hypothesis), put RPO = 0 only on the write paths that truly need it (money, orders) via synchronous replication, and leave everything else async. Do not skip past this to active-active — active-passive solves the availability case at ~1.7x cost without taking on conflict resolution.

**Reach for read replicas or geo-partitioning when the reason is read or write latency.** If far users are slow on *reads*, add read replicas near them — much cheaper than active-active. If far users need fast *writes to their own data*, geo-partition: master each user in a home region, route their writes there, replicate selectively. This gives local writes for the common case without multi-master's conflict problem, because each record has exactly one home writer. It is the underused middle path and the right answer more often than active-active.

**Reach for active-active only when fast writes are genuinely required on multiple continents AND your data is mergeable.** This is the hardest topology; adopt it only when geo-partitioning cannot meet the write-latency requirement (e.g., truly global collaborative editing where any region writes any record). Before adopting, answer the conflict question per dataset: mergeable data → CRDT, surfaceable conflicts → version vectors, neither → it does not belong in active-active (route it home or put it in a globally-strong system like Spanner/CockroachDB). Never default to LWW for data where concurrent writes both represent real intent. And budget the cross-region egress and the headroom to absorb a region's load on the survivors.

**Across all topologies: never automate region failover without fencing, never put writes you cannot lose on pure async, never adopt a conflict policy without naming what it silently discards, and always cell-partition within each region for blast-radius isolation.** Those rules prevent the signature incidents above. And remember that cell-based architecture often buys you more availability per dollar than a second region, because it defends against the more common failure (a bad deploy crossing all regions) that multi-region does nothing for.

## 18. Key takeaways

- **Go multi-region for one of three reasons only — availability, latency, or residency — each with a number attached.** If you cannot name the reason, you have a single-region system someone wants to make four times harder. Vanity is not a reason.
- **The speed of light is a hard floor: ~90ms RTT Virginia-Frankfurt, ~200ms+ to Asia-Pacific.** You can have low cross-region write latency or strong cross-region consistency, but not both cheaply. Decide which the data needs.
- **The default is one multi-AZ region; climb down the topology table one row at a time, stopping at the first row that meets the requirement.** Single-region → active-passive → read-replicas → geo-partitioned → active-active, in increasing cost and complexity.
- **RTO and RPO are independent and bought separately with money.** Warmer standby buys smaller RTO; tighter (synchronous) replication buys smaller RPO. Put RPO = 0 only on the write paths that truly need it.
- **Automated region failover without fencing is an automated split-brain generator.** Detecting a region outage is hard to distinguish from a partition; a human-confirmed, fenced failover trades a few RTO minutes for not turning a 40-second blip into a multi-day data-repair outage.
- **Active-active is multi-leader, so conflict resolution is unavoidable.** Use CRDTs for mergeable data, version vectors to surface real conflicts, and never bare LWW for data where concurrent writes both represent real intent — LWW silently destroys data.
- **Keep reads local, route writes to the record's home region, replicate async, and bound the stale-read window with a measured number** (lag p99 under peak load, not at rest). Same lag is fine for a feed and unacceptable for a price quote — tolerance is a property of the use case.
- **Cell-based architecture and shuffle-sharding cap blast radius against poison failures that cross regions** (bad deploys, poison requests) — a failure class multi-region does nothing for. Often higher leverage per dollar than a second region.
- **Cross-region egress is the silent budget killer:** ~\$0.02/GB, charged forever, on every replicated write to every region. Replicate selectively, compress, keep reads local, and prefer geo-partitioning over full active-active to cut it.
- **Residency is a data-flow audit problem, not a database-placement one.** Pin regulated PII to its jurisdiction, classify non-personal data as free-to-move, and hunt down the accidental copies in logs, backups, caches, and analytics — that is what gets you fined.
