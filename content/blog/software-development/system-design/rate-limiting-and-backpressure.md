The most dangerous traffic your system will ever see is not an attack. It is a Tuesday where everything works — a product launch, a viral post, a marketing email that lands at 9:00 a.m. and brings 10x your normal load through the front door in ninety seconds. Nobody is being malicious. Every request is a real user who genuinely wants what you sell. And if you have not built flow control, that success is exactly what takes you down: the queues fill, the threads block, the database saturates, latency climbs past every timeout, clients retry, the retries become more load, and the system that was perfectly healthy at 1x falls over at 10x not because it ran out of capacity but because it had no way to say *no* gracefully. A senior engineer's instinct, the moment they see a system with no rate limiting and no backpressure, is the same instinct a structural engineer has looking at a bridge with no expansion joints: this will be fine until the day it is catastrophically not.

This post is about the two complementary halves of flow control, and the discipline of using them together. **Rate limiting** is how you reject excess at the edge — you decide, per client or per tenant or per IP, how much traffic you will accept, and you turn the rest away cheaply with a `429 Too Many Requests` before it ever touches your expensive internals. **Backpressure** is how you slow the *source* when your internals are already saturated — instead of accepting work into an unbounded queue and dying, you make the producer wait, or you shed the work you cannot do, so the queue never explodes. Rate limiting is a contract you publish in advance ("100 requests per minute, here is your budget"); backpressure is a runtime signal you send when reality diverges from the plan ("I am full right now, back off"). The two are not alternatives. A system with only rate limiting is brittle the moment one downstream dependency slows down and your published limit is suddenly too generous; a system with only backpressure has no way to be fair across tenants and will let one greedy client starve everyone else. You need both, and Figure 1 lays out the algorithm landscape we have to navigate first.

![Matrix comparing fixed window, sliding log, sliding counter, token bucket, and leaky bucket rate-limiting algorithms across burst handling, smoothing, memory, accuracy, and distributed cost](/imgs/blogs/rate-limiting-and-backpressure-1.webp)

I have watched all of this go wrong in production, which is why I care about it more than the topic deserves on paper. I have seen a fixed-window rate limiter let a client send 200 requests in a two-second span across a window boundary, because the math nobody checked allowed a full limit on each side of the seam. I have watched a retry storm turn a thirty-second blip into a forty-minute outage, because every client retried at the same instant on the same fixed delay and synchronized into a wave that hit the recovering service like a battering ram. I have watched a service with a beautiful unbounded in-memory queue absorb a spike "successfully" — accepting every request, queueing it, never dropping one — and then crash with an out-of-memory error four minutes later having served almost none of them, the textbook definition of accepting work you have no hope of completing. None of these were exotic failures. Each one is the documented, predictable failure mode of a flow-control decision somebody made (or failed to make) without costing the unhappy path. By the end of this post you should be able to size a token bucket for a real burst, design a distributed Redis limiter and state its per-request cost, decide where in your stack to enforce, and know when to shed load to protect your p99 instead of letting everyone's latency rot equally.

## 1. The two faces of flow control, and why you need both

Start with the cleanest possible mental split, because conflating these two is the root of most flow-control confusion. Rate limiting and backpressure both protect a system from too much work, but they act at opposite ends of the pipe and with opposite philosophies.

**Rate limiting acts at the entrance, proactively, against a published budget.** You decide in advance — based on capacity planning, fairness, or a pricing tier — that a given caller may send at most some rate, say 100 requests per minute. When they exceed it, you reject the excess immediately and cheaply, ideally at the very edge of your system before the request consumes a thread, a database connection, or a downstream call. The decision is *local to the request* and *stateless about your current health*: a rate limiter says "you are over your budget" regardless of whether the system is idle or melting. This is its strength (predictable, fair, cheap to evaluate) and its weakness (it does not know that *right now* your database is on fire, so a budget that was safe yesterday may be dangerous today).

**Backpressure acts from the inside, reactively, against your current real capacity.** When your internal queues are filling faster than you can drain them, backpressure is the signal that propagates *upstream* — back toward the producer or the client — telling them to slow down because you, the consumer, cannot keep up *right now*. The canonical example is TCP flow control: a receiver advertises a window telling the sender exactly how many more bytes it has room for, and the sender literally cannot transmit more until the receiver acknowledges and reopens the window. The decision is *global to your health* and *dynamic*: backpressure does not care what budget you published; it cares whether the work in flight is more than you can finish.

Here is the senior framing that ties them together. Rate limiting is a *coarse, fair, predictable* gate that you set from capacity planning and tier pricing. Backpressure is a *fine, dynamic, self-correcting* gate that responds to the actual moment-to-moment state of your system. Rate limiting protects you from the *known* shape of abuse (one client hammering you, a tier sending more than it paid for). Backpressure protects you from the *unknown* shape of overload (a downstream dependency slowing by 3x, a GC pause, a cache cold-start) that no static budget could have anticipated. A mature system layers them: rate limiting at the edge keeps the obvious excess out, and backpressure plus load shedding deeper in the stack catches the overload that slips through because your static budget happened to be wrong for today's conditions.

The cost of getting this wrong is asymmetric and worth internalizing. A system with *no* flow control does not degrade gracefully under overload — it collapses, and it collapses in the worst possible way, by accepting more work than it can do and then failing to do any of it. This is the failure mode that turns a capacity problem (you have 1x capacity and 10x demand, so 9x of requests should fail) into an availability problem (you have 1x capacity and 10x demand, so you serve 0x and everyone fails, including the 1x you could have served). Flow control's entire job is to keep an overload from converting your capacity into zero. A system that sheds 90% of a 10x spike and cleanly serves the remaining 10% is *up*. A system that tries to serve all of it and dies is *down*. That distinction — degrade versus collapse — is the whole game.

## 2. Fixed window: the simplest limiter, and its boundary burst bug

The simplest rate-limiting algorithm is the fixed window counter, and its simplicity is exactly why it is the most commonly deployed and the most commonly wrong. The idea is trivial: divide time into fixed windows (say, one-minute buckets aligned to the clock), keep a counter per client per window, increment on each request, and reject once the counter exceeds the limit. In Redis it is two operations: `INCR ratelimit:user123:minute42` and a `EXPIRE` to clean up, and you reject when the returned count exceeds your limit. Memory is O(1) per key — just a single integer — and the evaluation is a single atomic increment. For a lot of use cases, this is genuinely good enough, and a senior should not over-engineer past it without a reason.

But the fixed window has a bug that bites the moment a client is even slightly adversarial, and it is a bug of *math*, not of implementation. Consider a limit of 100 requests per minute. A client sends 100 requests in the last second of one window — at 0:59 — all of which are allowed because the counter for that minute only just reached 100. Then the clock ticks to 1:00, a new window starts, the counter resets to zero, and the client immediately sends another 100 requests in the first second of the new window. All 100 are allowed. The client has just sent **200 requests in a two-second span**, while you believed you were enforcing 100 per minute. The effective peak rate is double your configured limit, concentrated exactly at the window boundary, which is the worst place for it because boundaries are aligned across all clients — everyone's window resets at :00, so every client's burst lands at the same instant.

This is not a theoretical concern. If your downstream can handle 100 requests per minute spread out but not 200 in two seconds, the fixed window has handed you the exact spike you were trying to prevent, and handed it to you synchronized across every client at once. I have seen this take down a database that was provisioned with comfortable headroom for the *average* rate but no headroom for a 2x boundary spike multiplied across thousands of clients all resetting simultaneously at the top of the minute. The fix is not to patch the fixed window; the fix is to stop pretending a fixed window enforces a smooth rate. It enforces a count *per window*, which is a different and weaker thing.

There is a subtler version of the same problem even without an adversary: legitimate diurnal traffic that happens to cluster near window boundaries (cron jobs, scheduled syncs, mobile apps that all refresh on the minute) will naturally pile up at the seam. The fixed window does not need a malicious client to produce a boundary burst; it only needs correlated arrival times, which real traffic has in abundance. The algorithm's accuracy is fundamentally poor on any timescale shorter than the window, and "shorter than the window" is exactly where overload lives.

#### Worked example: the boundary burst in numbers

Suppose you run an API gateway in front of a service whose database comfortably handles 5,000 queries per second sustained but begins to queue and lag past 8,000 QPS. You have 10,000 active clients, each rate-limited to 60 requests per minute (1 per second average) on a fixed window. On paper your worst-case load is 10,000 clients × 60 req/min = 600,000 req/min = 10,000 QPS average, already at your ceiling but survivable if smooth.

Now the boundary. Each client is allowed to spend its entire 60-request budget in the last second of a window and another 60 in the first second of the next. If even 20% of your clients are mobile apps that refresh on a clock-aligned schedule — 2,000 clients — and they each fire 60 requests at 0:59 and 60 more at 1:00, you get 2,000 × 60 = 120,000 requests in the last second of minute one, then 120,000 more in the first second of minute two: **120,000 QPS for two consecutive seconds**, a 24x spike over your 5,000 QPS sustained capacity, concentrated exactly where your fixed window believes it is enforcing 1 req/sec/client. The database that was "fine on average" is now 15x past its lag threshold for two full seconds, every second on the minute, forever. The fixed window did not protect you; it manufactured a synchronized weapon. This is precisely the seam that the sliding window in the next section closes, and Figure 3 shows the before and after.

## 3. Sliding window log and sliding window counter: closing the seam

The sliding window log fixes the boundary problem by refusing to have boundaries at all. Instead of counting requests per fixed window, you store the *timestamp of every request* in a per-client log (a sorted set in Redis works well), and on each new request you evaluate "how many requests has this client made in the *last 60 seconds*, measured backward from *now*?" You drop timestamps older than 60 seconds, count what remains, and reject if it is at or above the limit. Because the window slides continuously with the current time, there is no seam to exploit: any 60-second span, no matter where it starts, is capped at the limit. This is the *exact*, correct enforcement of a rate, and it is what you reach for when correctness matters more than cost — billing-sensitive limits, security-sensitive limits, anything where "double the rate at the boundary" is unacceptable.

The price is memory and computation. The sliding window log stores one entry per request in the window, so a client allowed 1,000 requests per minute costs you up to 1,000 timestamps in memory, per client, all the time. With a million active clients that is potentially a billion timestamps resident — gigabytes of Redis memory spent purely on bookkeeping. The per-request cost is also higher: you must remove expired entries (`ZREMRANGEBYSCORE`), add the new one (`ZADD`), and count (`ZCARD`), three operations on a structure whose size grows with the limit. For a high limit on many clients, the sliding window log's O(n) memory makes it the most expensive algorithm in the family, and that cost is the reason it is not the default despite being the most correct.

The sliding window *counter* is the brilliant compromise that buys you ~99% of the accuracy for O(1) memory. Instead of storing every timestamp, you keep two fixed-window counters — the current window and the previous one — and you *interpolate*. The estimate for the rolling window is: the current window's count, plus the previous window's count weighted by how much of the previous window still overlaps the rolling 60-second view. Concretely, if you are 25% of the way into the current minute, then 75% of the previous minute is still inside your rolling 60-second window, so your estimate is `current_count + previous_count × 0.75`. You reject when that weighted estimate exceeds the limit.

```python
import time

def sliding_window_counter_allowed(redis, key, limit, window_seconds):
    now = time.time()
    current_window = int(now // window_seconds)
    previous_window = current_window - 1
    # how far we are into the current window, as a fraction [0, 1)
    elapsed = (now % window_seconds) / window_seconds
    prev_weight = 1.0 - elapsed

    cur_count = int(redis.get(f"{key}:{current_window}") or 0)
    prev_count = int(redis.get(f"{key}:{previous_window}") or 0)

    estimate = cur_count + prev_count * prev_weight
    if estimate >= limit:
        return False  # reject, over the rolling limit

    # admit: increment current window and set a 2-window TTL so it self-cleans
    pipe = redis.pipeline()
    pipe.incr(f"{key}:{current_window}")
    pipe.expire(f"{key}:{current_window}", window_seconds * 2)
    pipe.execute()
    return True
```

The approximation assumes traffic was uniformly distributed within the previous window, which is not exactly true, so the estimate can be slightly off — but Cloudflare published an analysis of this exact algorithm running on real traffic and found the error was under 0.003% of requests misclassified, with memory dropping to two counters per key instead of a full log. That is a stunning trade: you give up a fraction of a percent of accuracy and you get O(1) memory and O(1) evaluation. For the overwhelming majority of rate-limiting use cases, the sliding window counter is the right default, and the sliding window log is reserved for the rare case where you cannot tolerate even 0.003% error. Figure 3 contrasts the fixed window's boundary burst against the sliding window's smooth enforcement.

## 4. Token bucket: bursts allowed, the algorithm everyone actually wants

Here is the uncomfortable truth about sliding windows: enforcing a *perfectly smooth* rate is often not what you want. Real users are bursty. A user opens your app and it fires twelve API calls in the first half-second to render the home screen, then sits idle for thirty seconds reading. A perfectly smooth limiter that allows one request per second would reject eleven of those twelve startup calls and ruin the experience, even though the user's *average* rate over any minute is trivially under budget. What you actually want is a limiter that allows short bursts as long as the long-run average stays within bounds — and that is exactly what the token bucket gives you.

The token bucket holds a number of tokens, up to a maximum capacity (the bucket size). Tokens are added at a fixed refill rate — say 10 tokens per second. Each request consumes one token (or N tokens, for weighted requests). If a token is available, the request is allowed and a token is removed; if the bucket is empty, the request is rejected. The two parameters encode exactly the two things you care about: the **refill rate** is your sustained, long-run allowed rate, and the **bucket capacity** is your maximum burst — the most requests you will allow in an instant when the bucket is full. A user who has been idle accumulates tokens up to the cap, so their app-startup burst of twelve requests is allowed instantly (the bucket was full), and then they refill at the sustained rate for subsequent use. Figure 2 traces the refill-consume-allow loop.

![Pipeline diagram of a token bucket showing a refill clock adding tokens, a bucket with a capacity, a request consuming one token, and an allow-or-reject decision](/imgs/blogs/rate-limiting-and-backpressure-2.webp)

The implementation is delightfully simple and, crucially, stores only two numbers per key — the current token count and the timestamp of the last refill — so it is O(1) memory like the sliding counter but with first-class burst support. The trick is *lazy refill*: you do not run a background clock adding tokens; you compute how many tokens *should* have been added since the last request, based on elapsed time, at read time.

```python
import time

class TokenBucket:
    def __init__(self, capacity, refill_rate):
        self.capacity = capacity          # max burst, e.g. 500 tokens
        self.refill_rate = refill_rate    # sustained rate, e.g. 100 tokens/sec
        self.tokens = capacity            # start full
        self.last_refill = time.monotonic()

    def allow(self, cost=1):
        now = time.monotonic()
        # lazily add tokens earned since the last call, capped at capacity
        elapsed = now - self.last_refill
        self.tokens = min(self.capacity, self.tokens + elapsed * self.refill_rate)
        self.last_refill = now

        if self.tokens >= cost:
            self.tokens -= cost
            return True       # allowed
        return False          # rejected: bucket empty
```

The token bucket is, in my experience, the algorithm most production systems actually want, and it is the one that AWS, Stripe, and most API gateways default to. It models the real-world contract cleanly: "you may send at a sustained rate of R, and we will tolerate a burst of up to B above that when you have been quiet." It is cheap (two numbers per key), it handles bursts gracefully, and the two parameters map directly onto capacity-planning conversations: set the refill rate from your sustained downstream capacity, set the bucket size from the largest burst your downstream can absorb without lagging. The next section's worked example is precisely how a senior derives those two numbers from real constraints rather than guessing.

The one thing to watch is the `cost` parameter, which is how you handle *weighted* endpoints. Not all requests are equal — a search query that fans out to ten shards costs more than a cache-served read. The token bucket handles this naturally: charge a cheap endpoint 1 token and an expensive one 10 tokens, and your bucket now meters *work* rather than *request count*, which is what you usually actually care about. This is a real advantage over window counters, which can only count requests, not weight them.

#### Worked example: sizing a token bucket for a real burst

You operate a service whose downstream database sustains 2,000 writes per second indefinitely but begins to queue past 5,000 writes/second, and queued writes past that point push your p99 latency over your 200ms SLO. You want to admit legitimate bursts — your largest customer does a bulk import that fires up to 8,000 writes in a tight burst at the start of an hourly sync — without ever sustaining a rate that hurts the database. How do you size the token bucket?

Set the **refill rate** to your sustained safe capacity: 2,000 tokens/second. This is the long-run rate you will allow, and it sits comfortably under the 5,000/sec lag threshold. Now the **bucket capacity** controls the burst. If you set the cap to 8,000 tokens, an idle client who has accumulated a full bucket can fire all 8,000 writes instantly — which momentarily slams the database at 8,000/sec, past its 5,000 lag threshold, violating your p99. That is too generous. If you set the cap to 1,000, you reject most of the legitimate bulk import, which is too stingy. The senior move is to size the cap so that the *burst plus the steady drain* stays under the threshold for the duration the database can absorb it.

The database can absorb a queue of, say, 3,000 excess writes (the 5,000/sec capacity minus the 2,000/sec sustained, sustained for one second) before lag bites. So set the bucket capacity to **3,000 tokens**. Now an idle client can burst 3,000 writes instantly (5,000 total in that first second when you add the 2,000/sec refill — exactly at the threshold, not past it), then is throttled to the 2,000/sec refill rate, so the database queue drains rather than grows. The 8,000-write bulk import is admitted as: 3,000 instantly, then 2,000/sec for the remaining 5,000, completing in 2.5 seconds total — fast enough that the customer barely notices, smooth enough that the database never crosses its lag threshold. You sized the burst to the database's actual absorption capacity, not to a number that felt nice. That is the difference between a token bucket that protects p99 and one that violates it.

## 5. Leaky bucket: smoothing instead of bursting

The leaky bucket is the token bucket's mirror image, and the distinction is worth getting precise because they are often confused. Where the token bucket allows bursts (a full bucket can be spent all at once), the leaky bucket *forbids* them and instead enforces a perfectly smooth output rate. The physical picture is a bucket with a hole in the bottom that drains at a fixed rate; incoming requests are water poured in. The bucket drains at a constant rate regardless of how fast water comes in, so the *output* is always smooth even when the *input* is bursty. If water comes in faster than it drains, the bucket fills; if it overflows the bucket's capacity, the excess spills (requests are rejected).

The practical implementation is a queue that is serviced at a fixed rate. Requests arrive and are enqueued; a processor pulls from the queue at exactly the leak rate and forwards each one downstream. If the queue is full when a request arrives, the request is rejected. The defining property is that the downstream sees a *perfectly constant* request rate no matter how spiky the arrivals are — the leaky bucket has converted a bursty input into a smooth output, at the cost of latency (requests wait in the queue) and the cost of forbidding bursts entirely.

When do you want this over a token bucket? When your downstream genuinely cannot tolerate bursts at all. Some systems — a legacy payment processor that locks a row per transaction, a third-party API with a hard concurrency cap, a hardware device — have a fixed maximum *instantaneous* rate and will fail or lock up if you exceed it even briefly. For those, the token bucket's burst allowance is a liability: you do not want to let an idle client suddenly fire its accumulated burst into a downstream that cannot take it. The leaky bucket guarantees the downstream never sees more than the leak rate, ever, which is exactly the protection a burst-intolerant dependency needs. The trade is added latency (queueing delay) and no burst tolerance, which makes it the wrong choice for interactive user traffic that benefits from snappy bursts.

The senior summary of token versus leaky is one sentence: **token bucket protects the *caller's* experience by allowing bursts; leaky bucket protects the *downstream's* stability by forbidding them.** Choose based on which side of the connection is the fragile one. Most internet-facing APIs use token bucket because the fragile thing is the user's perception of responsiveness; some internal integrations use leaky bucket because the fragile thing is a downstream that cannot be bursted. Figure 1's matrix captures this directly: leaky bucket is the only algorithm with "smooth output: yes," and that single property is its entire reason to exist.

## 6. The trade-off matrix: choosing the algorithm

Now we can assemble the decision the way a senior assembles it in a design review — not "which is best" (there is no best) but "which property does this workload care about most, and which algorithm wins that property at an acceptable cost in the others." Figure 1 is the matrix; here is the prose that defends each cell, because a matrix without reasoning is a cheat sheet, not a decision.

| Algorithm | Bursts | Smoothing | Memory/key | Accuracy | Distributed cost | Reach for it when |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Fixed window | Allows 2x at seam | None | O(1) | Poor (boundary bug) | Low (one INCR) | You need dead-simple and can tolerate boundary spikes |
| Sliding window log | Forbids | None | O(n) per limit | Exact | High (sorted set ops) | Correctness is non-negotiable (billing, security) |
| Sliding window counter | Forbids | None | O(1) (2 counters) | ~99.997% | Low | You want sliding-window correctness cheaply (the default) |
| Token bucket | Allows up to cap | None | O(1) (2 numbers) | Good | Medium | You want graceful bursts for real users (most APIs) |
| Leaky bucket | Forbids | Perfect | O(queue) | Good | Medium | Downstream cannot tolerate bursts at all |

The decision tree in Figure 5 collapses this matrix into the two questions that actually drive the choice: do you need to allow bursts, and do you need exact counts? If bursts are fine (interactive user traffic, app-startup fan-out), token bucket. If bursts are not fine and you need a perfectly smooth output for a fragile downstream, leaky bucket. If bursts are not fine and you just need accurate windowed counting, then the question is whether you need *exact* counts — sliding window log if you do (and can pay the memory), sliding window counter if ~99.997% accuracy is fine (almost always). The fixed window is deliberately not a leaf in that tree, because its boundary bug makes it a poor choice the moment correctness matters, and when correctness does not matter you usually do not need a limiter at all.

![Decision tree for choosing a rate-limiting algorithm based on whether bursts are allowed and whether exact counts are required](/imgs/blogs/rate-limiting-and-backpressure-5.webp)

The optimization lens on this table is about *cost at scale*, and it is where the sliding window log loses despite being the most correct. With a million active keys and a 1,000-request limit, the log can resident gigabytes of timestamps; the sliding counter and token bucket both stay at two numbers per key — kilobytes total for the same million keys. At a million keys, that is the difference between needing a 64GB Redis instance and a 256MB one, which is a real money decision. The senior default — token bucket for burst-tolerant user traffic, sliding window counter for burst-intolerant accurate counting — is the default precisely because it wins the cost column without giving up much accuracy.

## 7. Where to enforce: edge, gateway, service, per-user, per-IP, per-tenant

Choosing the algorithm is half the decision; the other half is *where* you enforce it, and this is where senior engineers and junior engineers visibly diverge. A junior asks "what is the rate limit?" A senior asks "rate limit *of what, against what key, at which layer*?" because the same nominal limit means completely different things depending on the answers. Figure 8 stacks the enforcement layers.

![Stack diagram of rate-limit enforcement layers from edge and CDN down through API gateway, service, and concurrency limit](/imgs/blogs/rate-limiting-and-backpressure-8.webp)

**The enforcement layer.** Enforce as early as possible for the cheap, coarse limits, and as late as necessary for the fine, fair ones. At the **edge / CDN** (Cloudflare, Fastly, a global load balancer), you can drop obvious abuse — per-IP floods, known-bad patterns — before they consume any of your origin's resources at all. This is the cheapest possible rejection: the request dies thousands of miles from your servers. But the edge does not know your application semantics; it cannot tell that this IP is a corporate NAT carrying ten thousand legitimate users, or that this API key belongs to your most valuable enterprise tenant. So the edge handles the coarse, IP-shaped abuse, and the finer limits move inward. At the **API gateway**, you have the authenticated identity (API key, user ID, tenant ID) and can enforce per-key global limits — this is where most "100 requests per minute per API key" limits live. At the **service** layer, you can enforce limits that depend on application state the gateway does not have — per-tenant fairness weighted by plan, per-resource limits ("no more than 5 concurrent exports per account"). Each layer catches what the layer above could not see.

**The key dimension.** *What you count against* matters as much as how much. **Per-IP** is the bluntest: cheap to enforce, available at the edge, but wrong in both directions — a corporate NAT or a mobile carrier puts thousands of distinct users behind one IP (so a per-IP limit punishes them collectively), while an attacker with a botnet has thousands of IPs (so a per-IP limit barely slows them). Per-IP is a coarse first line, never the whole defense. **Per-user / per-API-key** is the workhorse: it requires authentication, so it lives at the gateway or service, but it correctly attributes usage to the actual caller and is fair across users. **Per-tenant** is the one that matters most for multi-tenant SaaS, and it is the one most often gotten wrong: in a system serving many customers, you must limit each *tenant* (customer account) so that one tenant's spike cannot starve the others — the noisy-neighbor problem. A per-user limit does not save you here, because one tenant might have ten thousand users; you need a per-tenant aggregate limit that caps the *account's* total footprint regardless of how it is distributed across that account's users.

The fairness problem hiding in this is real and worth a senior's attention. If you only enforce a *global* limit on your service (say, the service handles 50,000 QPS total), then a single greedy tenant can consume the entire global budget and starve every other tenant — they all get rejected because the global counter is full, even though they individually sent almost nothing. The fix is *per-tenant* limits that reserve fairness, possibly with a global ceiling on top: each tenant gets a guaranteed share, and the global limit catches the aggregate. This is the same fairness logic as fair queueing in networking, and getting it wrong is how one customer's bad day becomes everyone's bad day. We will stress-test exactly this noisy-tenant scenario in section 12.

## 8. The distributed counter problem: rate limiting across many nodes

Everything so far quietly assumed a single counter. The moment you run more than one gateway node — and you always do, for availability and scale — rate limiting becomes a distributed-state problem, and that is where it gets genuinely hard. If you have ten gateway nodes behind a load balancer and you naively keep the rate-limit counter *in memory on each node*, then each node enforces the limit independently, and a client whose requests are spread across all ten nodes gets *ten times* their intended limit. A "100 requests per minute" limit becomes "1,000 requests per minute" because each of the ten nodes independently allows 100. The local counter is wrong by a factor of your node count, and your node count changes with autoscaling, so the error is not even constant.

The standard fix is a shared, authoritative counter that all nodes consult — almost always Redis, because it is fast, atomic, and centralized. Every gateway node, on every request, asks Redis "increment this client's counter and tell me if they are over the limit." Because all nodes hit the same Redis key, the count is global and correct regardless of which node a request lands on. Figure 7 shows the topology: many gateway nodes converging on one Redis counter, which then decides allow or deny.

![Graph diagram showing three gateway nodes consulting a single shared Redis counter that allows requests to backends or returns 429 when over the limit](/imgs/blogs/rate-limiting-and-backpressure-7.webp)

This correctness has a cost, and naming that cost is the senior move. **Every rate-limited request now incurs a network round trip to Redis** before it can be admitted. If Redis is in the same datacenter, that is perhaps 0.5–1ms of added latency on the hot path of *every single request*, plus the load on Redis itself: if your gateway fleet handles 100,000 QPS, your Redis is now doing 100,000+ operations per second purely for rate limiting, before it does anything else. Redis can handle that — a single instance does hundreds of thousands of simple ops per second — but it is now a critical dependency in your request path, which raises the question every senior must answer: *what happens when Redis is slow or down?* If rate limiting fails closed (reject everything when Redis is unavailable), a Redis outage becomes a total outage. If it fails open (allow everything when Redis is unavailable), a Redis outage removes all your rate limiting at exactly the moment you might need it most. Neither is free; most teams fail open with a local fallback limiter, accepting that a Redis outage degrades to per-node local limits (imperfect but not catastrophic).

The atomicity requirement is the other subtlety. A naive "GET the count, check it, INCR if under limit" has a race: two nodes can both GET 99, both see they are under 100, and both INCR to make it 101, allowing one request too many. Under high concurrency these races compound. The fix is to make the check-and-increment atomic, which Redis supports via a Lua script that runs server-side as a single atomic unit. Here is a sliding-window-counter limiter as an atomic Lua script — this is close to what production systems actually deploy:

```lua
-- KEYS[1] = rate limit key prefix
-- ARGV[1] = window size in seconds
-- ARGV[2] = limit
-- ARGV[3] = current unix time (seconds, float)
local window = tonumber(ARGV[1])
local limit  = tonumber(ARGV[2])
local now    = tonumber(ARGV[3])

local cur_win  = math.floor(now / window)
local prev_win = cur_win - 1
local elapsed  = (now % window) / window
local prev_w   = 1.0 - elapsed

local cur_key  = KEYS[1] .. ":" .. cur_win
local prev_key = KEYS[1] .. ":" .. prev_win

local cur  = tonumber(redis.call("GET", cur_key)  or "0")
local prev = tonumber(redis.call("GET", prev_key) or "0")

local estimate = cur + prev * prev_w
if estimate >= limit then
  return 0   -- denied
end

-- admit: increment current window, set 2-window TTL
redis.call("INCR", cur_key)
redis.call("EXPIRE", cur_key, window * 2)
return 1     -- allowed
```

Because the entire read-decide-increment sequence runs inside Redis as one atomic script, there is no check-then-act race no matter how many gateway nodes call it simultaneously. The cost is one round trip and one script execution per request, which is the irreducible price of a correct distributed limit.

For very high scale, even one Redis round trip per request is too much, and this is where **approximate / batched** counting earns its place. The idea: each gateway node maintains a *local* token allowance, and periodically (every 100ms, say) syncs with Redis to reconcile its local count against the global one and fetch a fresh local budget. Between syncs, the node decides locally with zero Redis calls. This trades exactness for throughput — a node might briefly allow slightly over its share between syncs — but it cuts Redis load by orders of magnitude (one sync per node per 100ms instead of one call per request). The trade is the same as everywhere in distributed systems: you give up a little consistency to buy a lot of availability and throughput, and you choose the point on that curve based on how much over-limit you can tolerate. This is the same fundamental tension as choosing your data partitioning scheme, which the [consistent hashing and data partitioning deep-dive](/blog/software-development/database/consistent-hashing-and-data-partitioning) covers when you need to shard the rate-limit counters themselves across a Redis cluster because one instance can no longer hold all the keys.

The most elegant production approach to this is the **Generic Cell Rate Algorithm (GCRA)**, which is what powers limiters like `redis-cell` and many gateways. Instead of storing a count, GCRA stores a single value — the "theoretical arrival time" of the next allowed request — and computes admission from the gap between now and that value. It is a token bucket reimagined to need only one stored number and one atomic compare-and-set, which makes it cheap to run distributed. The detail is beyond this post, but the senior takeaway is: when per-request Redis cost becomes your bottleneck, GCRA and batched local counting are the two levers, and both trade a sliver of exactness for a large cut in coordination cost.

## 9. The 429 contract: Retry-After, backoff, jitter, and retry storms

Rejecting a request is only half a polite limiter; *how* you reject determines whether the client behaves well or makes everything worse. The HTTP standard answer is to return **`429 Too Many Requests`** with a **`Retry-After`** header telling the client exactly how long to wait before trying again. This is a contract: you are not just saying "no," you are saying "no, and here is precisely when to ask again." A well-behaved client reads `Retry-After`, waits that long, and retries. The header can be a number of seconds or an HTTP date, and a senior always sends it, because the alternative — a bare 429 with no guidance — leaves the client guessing, and guessing clients retry immediately, which is exactly the behavior that turns a brief overload into a sustained one.

The dangerous failure mode here is the **retry storm**, and it is one of the most common ways a recoverable blip becomes an outage. The mechanism: your service has a momentary problem and starts returning 429s (or 503s) to, say, half your clients. Those clients, written naively, retry *immediately* and *all at the same time*. The retries hit your already-struggling service as a *synchronized wave*, adding load exactly when it can least handle it. The service returns even more 429s, more clients retry in the next synchronized wave, and the load *amplifies* instead of decaying. The retries themselves become the load that prevents recovery — a positive feedback loop where the cure (retrying) is the disease. I have watched a thirty-second database hiccup turn into a forty-minute outage entirely through retry amplification, where the original problem was long gone but the synchronized retry waves kept the service pinned. Figure 6 traces this timeline.

![Timeline diagram of a retry storm showing synchronized immediate retries amplifying load versus exponential backoff with jitter spreading load so the service recovers](/imgs/blogs/rate-limiting-and-backpressure-6.webp)

The two-part fix is **exponential backoff** plus **jitter**, and both parts are essential — backoff alone is not enough. Exponential backoff means each successive retry waits longer: 1 second, then 2, then 4, then 8, doubling each time up to a cap. This decays the retry load over time so a struggling service gets breathing room. But backoff *alone* still synchronizes: if all clients failed at the same instant and all use the same backoff schedule, they all retry at 1s together, then at 2s together, then 4s together — the waves are spaced out but still synchronized into spikes. **Jitter** breaks the synchronization by randomizing each client's wait. The most robust variant, which AWS published a well-known analysis on, is *full jitter*: each client waits a random duration between zero and the current backoff ceiling. This spreads retries uniformly across the interval instead of clustering them at the ceiling, smoothing the retry load into a flat trickle instead of a series of spikes.

```python
import random
import time

def retry_with_backoff(call, max_retries=6, base=1.0, cap=60.0):
    for attempt in range(max_retries):
        try:
            return call()
        except RateLimited as e:
            if attempt == max_retries - 1:
                raise
            # exponential ceiling, capped
            ceiling = min(cap, base * (2 ** attempt))
            # full jitter: sleep a uniform random in [0, ceiling]
            delay = random.uniform(0, ceiling)
            # honor server's Retry-After if present and longer
            if e.retry_after is not None:
                delay = max(delay, e.retry_after)
            time.sleep(delay)
```

Two senior refinements on top of backoff-plus-jitter. First, **respect the server's `Retry-After`** when it is present and longer than your computed backoff — the server knows when it expects to recover, and you should not retry before then. Second, add a **retry budget** (or "retry token bucket") on the *client* side: cap the total fraction of requests that are retries, so that even with perfect backoff, a sustained failure cannot make retries dominate your traffic. A common rule is "retries may be at most 10% of total requests"; once you exceed that, you stop retrying and fail fast, because if 10% of your traffic is already retries, the downstream is clearly not recovering and piling on more retries only delays its recovery. This caps the amplification factor regardless of how the backoff math plays out. The combination — exponential backoff, full jitter, honor Retry-After, retry budget — is the complete client-side contract that turns a retry from a weapon into a courtesy.

The interplay with [idempotency](/blog/software-development/system-design/idempotency-and-exactly-once-by-design) matters here too: retries are only safe if the operation is idempotent, because a retry after a 429 is fine (the request never executed) but a retry after a *timeout* is dangerous (the request may have executed and you would do it twice). A senior pairs every retry policy with an idempotency key so that retrying a possibly-executed write does not double-charge a customer. Rate limiting, retries, and idempotency are three sides of the same reliability story.

## 10. Backpressure: bounded queues, blocking versus dropping

Now we cross from the entrance to the interior, from rate limiting to backpressure. Rate limiting rejects excess *before* it enters; backpressure manages excess that is *already inside* your system, in your queues and buffers, when your consumers cannot drain them fast enough. The single most important backpressure decision — and the one most systems get wrong by default — is whether your internal queues are *bounded* or *unbounded*.

An **unbounded queue** is the default in far too many systems, and it is a trap. A Java `LinkedBlockingQueue` with no capacity argument, a Go channel that is buffered too generously, a list you `append` to without limit — these accept work forever, and that is precisely the problem. When work arrives faster than you can process it, an unbounded queue does not reject anything; it grows. And grows. Memory climbs, and two terrible things happen simultaneously: latency climbs without bound (a request enqueued behind a million others waits a million dequeue-times, far past any timeout, so by the time you process it the client has long given up and the work is wasted), and eventually you run out of memory and crash, having accepted enormous amounts of work and completed almost none of it. The unbounded queue is the mechanism by which a system "successfully" accepts a spike and then dies — it converts an overload into an out-of-memory crash. Figure 4 shows this directly.

![Before and after diagram contrasting an unbounded queue that grows until out-of-memory crash against a bounded queue that sheds over capacity to keep p99 latency finite](/imgs/blogs/rate-limiting-and-backpressure-4.webp)

A **bounded queue** is the foundation of every backpressure strategy, because the bound is what forces a decision when the queue is full. The moment you cap the queue at, say, 1,000 items, you have to answer: *what happens when the 1,001st item arrives and the queue is full?* And that question has exactly two good answers, which are the two faces of backpressure.

**Block the producer (true backpressure).** The producer trying to enqueue *waits* until there is room. This propagates the slowdown upstream: the producer is now slower because it is blocked, so *its* producer slows, and the pressure walks all the way back to the original source — ideally, the client, who experiences a slower response and (if well-behaved) sends less. This is "real" backpressure: the signal flows upstream and the source actually slows down. It is the right answer when the producer *can* slow down without harm — an internal pipeline stage, a consumer pulling from a Kafka topic (it just polls slower), a batch job. The [backpressure and flow control mechanism deep-dive](/blog/software-development/message-queue/backpressure-and-flow-control) covers how brokers implement exactly this propagation at the protocol level.

**Drop the work (load shedding).** When you *cannot* make the producer wait — because the producer is a user holding an HTTP connection who will not tolerate blocking, or because blocking would propagate into a deadlock — you instead *reject* the excess work, returning a `503 Service Unavailable` and shedding it. This keeps your latency bounded (the queue never grows past its cap, so wait time is capped) at the cost of failing some requests *now* rather than failing all of them *later*. Load shedding is backpressure for systems that cannot block, which is most user-facing systems. The senior insight is that *shedding early is a feature, not a failure*: a request you shed in 1ms with a clean 503 is infinitely better than a request you accept, queue for 30 seconds, and then fail on a timeout having burned 30 seconds of capacity on work nobody is waiting for anymore.

The decision between blocking and dropping comes down to one question: *can the producer afford to wait?* If yes (internal pipeline, async consumer), block — true backpressure is the cleanest, because no work is lost, the system just runs at its real throughput. If no (user-facing request, real-time path), drop — load shedding, because a fast clean rejection beats a slow eventual failure. Many real systems do both at different layers: block on internal pipeline stages where waiting is fine, shed at the user-facing edge where waiting is not.

## 11. Reactive streams, credit-based flow control, and TCP as the canonical example

The most elegant form of backpressure is *credit-based flow control*, where the consumer explicitly tells the producer how much it is allowed to send, and the producer literally cannot exceed it. This is not a metaphor — it is how the most reliable flow-control system on earth works, the one running underneath every connection you have ever made: TCP.

TCP flow control works by the receiver advertising a *receive window* — a number of bytes it currently has buffer space for — in every acknowledgement it sends back to the sender. The sender may have at most that many unacknowledged bytes in flight. As the receiving application reads data out of the buffer, the window reopens and the receiver advertises a larger window, granting the sender permission to send more. If the receiving application stops reading (it is slow, or busy), the buffer fills, the advertised window shrinks toward zero, and the sender *stops transmitting entirely* until the window reopens. The sender cannot overwhelm the receiver because the receiver hands out *credits* (window space) and the sender spends them. This is perfect backpressure: the slow side controls the fast side's rate, automatically, with no configuration, scaling smoothly from one byte to gigabits. Every senior should hold TCP as the reference implementation of how backpressure *should* feel — invisible when there is capacity, firmly limiting when there is not.

The **Reactive Streams** specification (implemented in Project Reactor, RxJava, Akka Streams, and baked into many modern async runtimes) brings exactly this credit model to application-level data pipelines. The core of the spec is the `request(n)` signal: a subscriber tells the publisher "I am ready for `n` more items," and the publisher may emit at most `n` items before waiting for the next `request`. The subscriber requests more only as fast as it can process, so a slow subscriber automatically slows a fast publisher — credit-based backpressure, at the level of objects flowing through a stream rather than bytes flowing through a socket. This is why Reactive Streams matters as an architecture pattern: it makes backpressure *the default* rather than something you bolt on. In a pull-based reactive pipeline, an unbounded queue cannot form, because nothing is produced until it is requested. The producer is structurally incapable of overwhelming the consumer.

The contrast with naive push-based code is the whole point. In push-based code — a callback that fires whenever data is available, a producer that writes to a queue as fast as it can — the producer's rate is decoupled from the consumer's rate, and the only thing absorbing the mismatch is a buffer that can overflow. In pull-based, credit-based code, the consumer's `request(n)` *is* the rate limit, dynamically, and the mismatch is absorbed by the producer simply not producing. The architectural lesson is to prefer pull-based, demand-driven designs for any pipeline where the producer can outrun the consumer, because they make backpressure a property of the structure rather than a feature you remember to add. When you are wiring [queues and event streaming into an architecture](/blog/software-development/system-design/queues-and-event-streaming-for-architects), this is the property to look for: does the consumer control its own intake rate, or can the producer flood it?

## 12. Load shedding, admission control, and the brownout pattern

Load shedding becomes powerful — not just survivable — when you make it *selective* rather than random. A system under overload that sheds requests *at random* keeps itself alive but degrades every user equally and, worse, sheds your most valuable traffic at the same rate as your least valuable. The senior pattern is **priority-based load shedding**: when you must shed, shed the *low-value* work first and protect the *high-value* work. This is the difference between a brownout (some features degrade, the core keeps working) and a blackout (everything fails together). Figure 9 contrasts the two.

![Before and after diagram contrasting random load shedding that drops checkout traffic against priority shedding that drops analytics and batch work first to keep checkout admitted](/imgs/blogs/rate-limiting-and-backpressure-9.webp)

The mechanism is **admission control with request priorities**. You classify incoming work into priority tiers — for an e-commerce system, perhaps: tier 0 is checkout and payment (revenue, never shed), tier 1 is browsing and search (important, shed reluctantly), tier 2 is recommendations and analytics (nice to have, shed first), tier 3 is background batch jobs (shed immediately under any pressure). When your admission controller detects overload — queue depth past a threshold, latency past your SLO, concurrency past a limit — it starts shedding from the bottom: tier 3 first, then tier 2, then tier 1 if it must, and tier 0 only in the most extreme last resort. The result is a *graceful* degradation curve: as load climbs from 1x toward 10x, you shed progressively more low-value work, but checkout keeps working the entire time. Your revenue path is protected by sacrificing your analytics path, which is exactly the trade a business wants.

The **brownout pattern** generalizes this: instead of an all-or-nothing decision per request, the service *reduces its own work* under pressure. A product page under load might skip the personalized recommendations (an expensive call), serve a generic page, and still complete — degraded but functional. A search under load might return fewer results, or skip the expensive re-ranking step, or serve a slightly stale cached result. The service voluntarily does *less work per request* so it can do *more requests*, trading quality for availability. This is brownout: like a power grid dimming the lights to avoid a blackout, the service dims its features to avoid a total failure. The senior design move is to identify, *in advance*, which parts of each request are essential and which are optional, and to wire in a way to drop the optional parts under pressure. A request that *can* shed its own optional work is far more resilient than one that is all-or-nothing.

#### Worked example: computing when to shed to protect p99

You run a service with a target p99 latency of 200ms. Each request takes 20ms of CPU-bound work on average (so one worker processes 50 requests/second), and you run 40 workers, giving a total capacity of 40 × 50 = 2,000 requests/second. By Little's Law, the number of requests in the system equals the arrival rate times the time each spends in the system: L = λ × W. At steady state with λ = 2,000/sec and W ≈ 20ms service time, you have L = 2,000 × 0.02 = 40 requests in service — exactly your 40 workers, fully utilized with no queue. So far, so good: at 2,000 QPS you are at the edge of capacity with near-zero queueing and a tight p99.

Now a spike pushes arrivals to 2,400/sec, 1.2x capacity. The excess 400/sec has nowhere to go but the queue, and the queue grows by 400 items every second. After just one second, a newly arriving request sits behind 400 queued requests; at 2,000/sec drain rate that is 400 / 2,000 = 0.2 seconds = 200ms of pure queueing delay *on top of* the 20ms of work — your p99 has already blown past 200ms after a single second of 1.2x overload. After five seconds of sustained 1.2x, the queue holds 2,000 items and the queueing delay is a full second; your p99 is now 1,020ms, five times your SLO, and climbing. The lesson is brutal and precise: **a queue at over 100% utilization grows without bound, and queueing delay is what destroys your p99 long before you crash.**

The shedding math falls out of this directly. To hold p99 under 200ms, you must keep the queue depth under (200ms − 20ms service) × 2,000/sec drain = 0.18 × 2,000 = 360 requests. So set your admission controller to start shedding when the queue depth exceeds ~360, and shed *exactly enough* to hold the queue at that bound. At 2,400/sec arrival and 2,000/sec capacity, you must shed 400/sec — and if you shed those 400 from your lowest-priority tier (analytics, batch), you protect the p99 of your high-priority traffic completely: checkout requests never see a queue deeper than 360, so their p99 stays under 200ms even as you reject 400 analytics requests every second. You computed the shed threshold from Little's Law and your SLO, and you shed early — at queue depth 360, not at the point of collapse. That is the entire discipline: *shed before the queue eats your p99, and shed the cheap work first.* This is the optimization that converts an overload from a latency catastrophe into a controlled, prioritized degradation.

## 13. Concurrency limits, Little's Law, and adaptive concurrency

There is a third lever beyond rate limiting (requests per time) and queue bounds (items in buffer): the **concurrency limit** — the maximum number of requests you allow *in flight simultaneously*. This is often the most directly useful limit, because it maps to the actual scarce resource. A service does not run out of "requests per second" in the abstract; it runs out of threads, connections, memory, or downstream capacity, all of which are about *concurrent* in-flight work, not rate. Limiting concurrency directly caps the resource that actually runs out.

Little's Law ties concurrency, rate, and latency together, and a senior keeps it in their head: **L = λ × W**, where L is the number of requests in the system, λ is the throughput (arrival rate), and W is the latency (time in system). This means concurrency, throughput, and latency are not three independent knobs — fix any two and the third is determined. If your service can hold L = 100 concurrent requests and each takes W = 50ms, then your maximum throughput is λ = L / W = 100 / 0.05 = 2,000 requests/second, full stop. You cannot do more without either more concurrency (more workers) or less latency (faster work). And critically, the relationship reverses under overload: if you push arrivals past 2,000/sec, you cannot increase L (it is capped at 100 by your worker count), so W *must* rise — latency inflates to absorb the excess, which is exactly the p99 destruction from the previous section, expressed as a law. Little's Law is *why* shedding protects latency: by capping L (concurrency) and λ (admitted rate), you cap W (latency) by arithmetic.

A *static* concurrency limit works but requires you to know the right number, which is hard because the right number changes — a downstream slowdown means each request holds a connection longer, so the same concurrency limit now corresponds to a lower safe throughput. This is where **adaptive concurrency limits** shine. Netflix open-sourced their `concurrency-limits` library, which applies the same algorithm TCP uses for congestion control (additive-increase / multiplicative-decrease, AIMD) to *application-level* concurrency. The library measures request latency continuously; when latency is stable and low, it gradually *increases* the concurrency limit (additive increase, probing for more capacity); when latency starts to climb — the signal that you are approaching saturation — it sharply *decreases* the limit (multiplicative decrease, backing off fast). The limit self-tunes to sit right at the knee of the latency curve, automatically finding the maximum concurrency that does not blow up latency, and re-finding it whenever conditions change. It is backpressure that configures itself.

This is the optimization endgame for flow control: rather than a human guessing static limits that are wrong the moment conditions change, the system *measures its own latency and adapts its admitted concurrency in real time* to hold latency at target. You stop tuning magic numbers and start declaring an SLO ("keep p99 under 200ms") and letting the controller find the concurrency that achieves it. The same AIMD logic that keeps the internet from collapsing under congestion keeps your service from collapsing under overload, and it is one of the highest-leverage resilience patterns a senior can introduce because it removes the brittle, hand-tuned constant that was going to be wrong eventually anyway.

## 14. The interplay with autoscaling and circuit breakers

Flow control does not live alone; it sits in a triangle with **autoscaling** and **circuit breakers**, and the senior skill is making the three cooperate rather than fight. Each handles a different timescale of overload, and confusing their roles is a common design error.

**Autoscaling** is the *slow* response: when sustained load rises, you add capacity (more instances) to meet it. But autoscaling takes minutes — new instances must boot, warm caches, join the load balancer, pass health checks. A 90-second traffic spike is *over* before autoscaling can react to it. So autoscaling handles the *sustained* shift in load (your traffic genuinely grew 3x this quarter), and it is useless for the *spike* (10x in 90 seconds). The senior insight: **rate limiting and load shedding are what protect you during the minutes autoscaling needs to catch up.** They are not alternatives to autoscaling; they are the bridge that keeps you alive while autoscaling works. A system with autoscaling but no shedding falls over during every fast spike, because the spike outruns the scaling. A system with shedding but no autoscaling survives spikes but stays under-provisioned for real growth. You need both, on their respective timescales.

**Circuit breakers** are the *downstream-failure* response: when a dependency you call starts failing or timing out, the circuit breaker "opens" and fails fast — it stops calling the broken dependency for a cooldown period, returning an immediate error instead of waiting for a timeout on every request. This is backpressure aimed at a *downstream*: it is how you avoid piling load onto a dependency that is already down, and how you avoid having your own threads all blocked waiting on timeouts to a dead service. The interplay with rate limiting and shedding: when a circuit breaker opens, the work it was doing has to go somewhere — either it is shed (you fail those requests fast), or it queues (and now you need backpressure on that queue), or it falls back to a degraded path (brownout). A circuit breaker without a shedding or fallback plan just moves the pile-up from "blocked on a dead dependency" to "blocked in a queue waiting for the dependency to come back," which is no better.

The clean mental separation: **rate limiting** protects you from *too many callers*; **backpressure and shedding** protect you from *too much in-flight work*; **circuit breakers** protect you from *a failing dependency*; **autoscaling** adjusts *your capacity to sustained demand*. They operate on different causes and different timescales, and a resilient system deploys all four, each doing its job, with clear hand-offs: the circuit breaker decides a dependency is down, shedding decides which work to drop while it is down, autoscaling adds capacity if the elevated load persists, and rate limiting ensures no single caller can monopolize whatever capacity remains. Getting these to cooperate — rather than, say, autoscaling adding capacity that immediately gets consumed by a retry storm that better rate limiting would have prevented — is the systems-thinking that separates a senior design from a pile of individually-correct components.

## 15. Stress-testing the design: spike, retry storm, noisy tenant

A design is not done until you have stress-tested it against the three failures that actually happen. Let me walk the rate-limiting-plus-backpressure design through each, the way I would in a review.

**The 10x spike.** A marketing email lands and brings 10x normal traffic in 90 seconds. What happens? At the edge, per-IP limits catch nothing useful (this is real distributed traffic, not one IP). At the gateway, per-key token buckets admit each client's legitimate burst and reject sustained excess — but with 10x the *clients* active, the aggregate admitted load is still well above capacity. So the inner defense engages: the concurrency limiter caps in-flight work at the level that holds p99, the bounded queues fill to their shed threshold, and the admission controller starts shedding low-priority work (analytics, recommendations) while protecting checkout. Autoscaling notices the sustained elevation and begins adding capacity, which lands in two to three minutes. **Net result:** during the spike, low-priority features brown out and a fraction of requests get a clean 429 or 503, but checkout stays up and p99 on the critical path stays under SLO. The system *degrades* instead of collapsing — the spike is survived, not by having 10x capacity, but by having a graceful way to serve the most important 1x and shed the rest. Without the layered defense, the same spike fills an unbounded queue and OOM-crashes the whole thing in minutes, serving nobody.

**The retry storm.** A downstream dependency hiccups for thirty seconds and the service returns 429s/503s to a chunk of traffic. Do the retries amplify into an outage? Only if clients retry naively. With the client contract from section 9 — exponential backoff, full jitter, honor Retry-After, retry budget capping retries at 10% of traffic — the retries spread out over time instead of synchronizing into waves, decay as the backoff grows, and are capped in volume by the retry budget. The thirty-second hiccup stays a thirty-second blip: the service recovers, the backed-off clients trickle back in at staggered times, and load returns to normal without the amplification spiral. **Net result:** a recoverable blip stays recoverable. But this defense depends on clients you may not control. For third-party clients that retry badly, your *own* rate limiter is the backstop — it caps how much retry traffic any single bad client can throw at you, so even a misbehaving client's retry storm is bounded by its rate-limit budget. This is why server-side rate limiting and client-side backoff are complementary: the client backoff prevents storms from well-behaved clients, and the server limit caps the damage from badly-behaved ones.

**The noisy tenant.** One customer runs a runaway script that fires 100x their normal traffic. Does it take down the other tenants? Only if you enforced limits at the wrong granularity. With *per-tenant* limits (section 7), the runaway tenant hits *their own* limit and gets throttled, while every other tenant's budget is untouched — the noisy neighbor is isolated to their own apartment. With only a *global* limit, the runaway tenant would consume the entire global budget and every other tenant would start getting rejected, turning one customer's bad script into an everyone-outage. **Net result with per-tenant limits:** the blast radius is exactly one tenant — the one who caused it. This is the single most important reason multi-tenant systems must rate-limit per tenant, not just globally: it converts a shared-fate failure into an isolated one. The fairness you build with per-tenant limits is what guarantees one tenant's worst day is not everyone's worst day.

Three failures, three defenses, all tested before the incident rather than during it. That rehearsal — walking the design through spike, storm, and noisy neighbor and naming exactly what saves you in each — is the design-review discipline that the [tradeoffs and CAP/PACELC framing](/blog/software-development/system-design/articulating-tradeoffs-cap-pacelc-and-beyond) trains you to make explicit rather than implicit.

## 16. Case studies: Stripe, Cloudflare, and two production incidents

**Stripe's rate limiting.** Stripe published a clear account of running four distinct kinds of limiters, and the lesson is that "rate limiting" is plural, not singular. They run a **request rate limiter** (the classic per-key requests-per-second cap), a **concurrent requests limiter** (capping how many requests a given user can have in flight at once — the concurrency limit from section 13, because some users send fewer but heavier requests), a **fleet usage load shedder** (reserving a fraction of total capacity for the most critical request types so that a flood of one type cannot starve another), and a **worker utilization load shedder** (shedding by priority when the workers themselves approach saturation, protecting critical traffic). The senior lesson: a mature system does not have *a* rate limiter; it has a *family* of limiters operating on different dimensions (rate, concurrency, capacity reservation, worker saturation), because no single dimension catches every abuse shape. Stripe also chose the token bucket as their core algorithm precisely for its burst tolerance, matching the principle from section 4 that real API traffic is bursty and a smooth limiter would feel broken to legitimate callers.

**Cloudflare's distributed rate limiting.** Cloudflare operates rate limiting across hundreds of edge data centers, which is the distributed-counter problem (section 8) at planetary scale — a client's requests can hit any of hundreds of locations, and the limit must hold globally. They published their analysis of the sliding window counter algorithm running on production traffic, reporting that the approximation misclassified well under 0.01% of requests while using a fraction of the memory of a sliding window log, which is the empirical validation of the section 3 trade-off. At their scale, doing exact global counting on every request through a single coordinator is impossible (the coordination cost would dwarf the work), so they accept a sliver of approximation in exchange for being able to enforce limits at the edge with local state and periodic reconciliation — the approximate/batched approach from section 8, chosen because at planetary scale exactness is simply too expensive. The lesson: the right algorithm depends on scale, and at extreme scale the approximate algorithms win not because exactness is undesirable but because it is unaffordable.

**A retry-storm outage.** Numerous published post-mortems describe the same shape: a brief dependency degradation triggers retries, the retries are naive (immediate, synchronized, no jitter), and the retry load amplifies the original problem into a sustained outage far longer than the trigger. The common thread across these incidents is that *the original fault was minor and recovered quickly, but the retry behavior turned a blip into an outage.* The fix in every case is the same: exponential backoff with jitter, plus a retry budget to cap the amplification factor. The lesson a senior takes is that **your client retry policy is part of your reliability design, not an afterthought** — a service with perfect server-side flow control can still be taken down by its own clients retrying badly, so the client contract (section 9) is as much a part of the system as the server limiter.

**A load-shedding brownout save.** The positive case studies are quieter because the whole point is that nothing dramatic happened. Teams that built priority-based shedding report the same outcome during major spikes: the system *browned out* — recommendations went generic, search dropped re-ranking, analytics events were sampled or dropped — but the revenue path stayed up and the incident was a graph that dipped and recovered rather than a page and a war room. The lesson is that **the win from load shedding is an outage that did not happen**, which makes it underappreciated precisely because its success is invisible. A senior advocates for shedding not by pointing to a save (there is no dramatic save to point to) but by pointing to the *avoided* collapse — the difference between a brownout graph and a blackout page, which is the entire return on the investment.

## 17. When to reach for this (and when not to)

Flow control is not free — every limiter is code to maintain, a counter to store, a latency to add, and a source of false rejections to debug — so a senior applies it where it earns its keep and skips it where it does not.

**Reach for rate limiting when** you have multiple callers (especially untrusted or multi-tenant ones) sharing a finite resource, when you sell tiered access (the limit *is* the product differentiation), when you need to protect a fragile downstream from being overrun, or when you have any public-facing API at all (the internet *will* find you, and abuse is a when not an if). For a public API, rate limiting is not optional; it is table stakes, and shipping without it is shipping a denial-of-service vulnerability.

**Reach for backpressure and load shedding when** your system has any path where work can arrive faster than you can process it — which is almost every system under a spike. The specific trigger is: do you have *bounded* queues and a *defined behavior* when they fill? If your answer is "we use unbounded queues and hope," you have a latent OOM-crash waiting for its spike. Bound your queues and decide block-versus-shed *before* the incident, not during it. Priority shedding specifically earns its keep the moment you have requests of clearly different business value (checkout versus analytics), which is most real products.

**Be cautious / skip it when** you are a single-tenant internal tool with a handful of trusted callers and no spike risk — the operational complexity of a distributed limiter is not worth it for ten internal users, and a simple bounded queue may be all the flow control you need. Be cautious about *over-limiting*: a limit set too aggressively rejects legitimate traffic and shows up as mysterious 429s in your customers' logs, which is its own kind of outage (a self-inflicted one). And do not reach for a *distributed* (Redis-backed) limiter when a *local* per-node limiter would do — if you have one node, or if approximate per-node limits are acceptable, you save yourself a critical dependency in your request path. The distributed counter is necessary only when correctness across nodes genuinely matters and the per-node error is unacceptable.

The meta-rule: **match the flow-control mechanism to the actual failure you are protecting against.** Rate limiting for too-many-callers, backpressure for too-much-in-flight-work, shedding for can-not-block overload, circuit breakers for failing-dependencies. Deploying a mechanism that does not match your real failure mode adds complexity without adding protection, which is the over-engineering a senior is paid to avoid as much as the under-engineering.

## 18. Key takeaways

- **Rate limiting and backpressure are complementary, not alternatives.** Rate limiting rejects excess at the entrance against a published budget; backpressure slows the source from the inside against your real-time capacity. A mature system layers both — the static budget catches known abuse, the dynamic signal catches the overload no budget anticipated.
- **The fixed window has a boundary bug that lets a client send 2x the limit across the seam.** Use a sliding window counter for cheap, nearly-exact enforcement, or a sliding window log only when you cannot tolerate even 0.003% error. Never trust a fixed window to enforce a smooth rate.
- **Token bucket is what most APIs actually want** because real traffic is bursty; size the refill rate from sustained downstream capacity and the bucket cap from the burst your downstream can absorb without lagging. Leaky bucket is the mirror image — choose it only when the downstream cannot tolerate bursts at all.
- **The distributed counter problem is real:** per-node counters multiply your limit by your node count. A shared Redis counter with an atomic Lua check fixes correctness but adds a round trip per request and a critical dependency; at extreme scale, approximate batched counting trades a sliver of exactness for an order-of-magnitude cut in coordination cost.
- **Return 429 with Retry-After, and require clients to use exponential backoff with full jitter plus a retry budget.** Naive synchronized retries amplify a blip into an outage; jitter breaks the synchronization and the budget caps the amplification. Your client retry policy is part of your reliability design.
- **Bound every queue.** An unbounded queue converts an overload into an OOM crash by accepting work it cannot complete. A bounded queue forces the block-versus-shed decision, which is the foundation of all backpressure.
- **Shed early, shed by priority.** A request shed cleanly in 1ms beats one accepted, queued 30 seconds, then timed out. Drop low-value work first (the brownout pattern) so the revenue path survives while analytics degrades — that is degradation instead of collapse.
- **Little's Law (L = λ × W) is the math behind all of it:** cap concurrency and admitted rate to cap latency. Adaptive concurrency limits (Netflix's AIMD approach) self-tune the cap to hold p99 at target, removing the brittle hand-tuned constant.
- **Per-tenant limits isolate the noisy neighbor.** Limiting only globally lets one runaway tenant starve everyone; per-tenant limits convert a shared-fate failure into an isolated one. This is the single most important granularity decision in a multi-tenant system.
