Here is the moment that separates a junior's news-feed design from a senior's. You have just sketched a clean architecture on the whiteboard: when someone posts, you write a copy of that post id into every follower's timeline, so a feed read becomes a single fast lookup. The interviewer nods, lets you finish, and then says four words that detonate the whole thing: "A celebrity just posted." You do the arithmetic out loud and your stomach drops — that one post has to be written into a hundred million timelines, and you have ten thousand celebrities, and they post several times a day. Your elegant precompute-everything design has just turned a single user action into a hundred-million-write storm, and it does this thousands of times an hour. The clean design was correct for ninety-nine percent of your users and catastrophically wrong for the one percent who generate most of the load.

A news feed looks deceptively simple from the outside. You follow people; you open the app; you see their recent posts, freshest or most relevant first; you scroll and more loads. That is the entire product surface, and it is the most-read surface on the internet — the home feed of a large social product is served on the order of a million times a second. The difficulty is not in any one piece. It is that the read path and the write path pull in opposite directions, the follower graph is wildly skewed (most people have a few hundred followers; a few have a hundred million), and the thing users actually want — the *right* posts, not merely the *recent* posts — turns a storage problem into a machine-learning ranking problem with a hard latency budget. Figure 1 frames the central fork that the rest of this post lives inside: do you pay the cost at write time or at read time?

This post is the architect's decision layer for the news-feed problem. We are not going to re-derive how Redis stores a list or how a B-tree index works — those mechanisms are deep-dived elsewhere on this blog, and I will cross-link them where they matter. My job here is the reasoning a senior brings to a design review: how to *choose* between fan-out on write and fan-out on read from the shape of your follower graph, how to *solve* the celebrity problem with a hybrid instead of pretending it does not exist, how to *size* the fan-out storm and the timeline storage with back-of-the-envelope math, how a chronological feed becomes a ranked feed without blowing the latency budget, and how to keep the whole thing standing when a post goes viral and the read path catches fire.

By the end you should be able to do five concrete things: derive the write-amplification and read-amplification of each fan-out strategy from the follower graph and pick the right one for a given workload; explain and defend the hybrid push/pull design that every large social product actually runs; estimate posts per day, fan-out writes per day, timeline storage in terabytes, and peak read QPS well enough to size a fleet; design a ranking pipeline — candidate generation, feature store, scoring, ranking — that fits inside a 200-millisecond read budget; and stress-test the design against the three things that actually break it in production, the celebrity fan-out storm, the viral hot read, and the ranking latency tail. We will reason from numbers throughout, because the entire news-feed design is a numbers argument and the numbers are what win or lose the review.

## 1. Framing and scope: what a feed actually has to do

Before any architecture, a senior pins down the requirements, because the right design for "show me the 30 most recent posts from people I follow" is wildly different from "show me the posts I am most likely to engage with, freshest first, deduplicated, with ads interleaved." Scope discipline is the first thing the interviewer is grading, and it is the first thing that saves you in real life. Let us be precise.

**Functional requirements.** A user can publish a post. A user can follow and unfollow other users. A user can open their home feed and see posts from accounts they follow, ordered either chronologically (most recent first) or by relevance (ranked). The feed paginates — the user scrolls and loads more, infinitely, without seeing duplicates or skipping posts. A user can view another user's profile feed (just *that* user's posts, which is a strictly easier problem we will mostly set aside). That is the core. Everything else — likes, comments, reshares, notifications, stories, ads — is adjacent and we will scope it out except where it touches the feed directly, which is mostly through ranking signals.

**Non-functional requirements — the ones that actually drive the design.** The home feed read must be fast: a p99 well under a few hundred milliseconds, because this is the most latency-sensitive surface in the product and every hundred milliseconds measurably costs engagement. The system must be highly available for reads — a feed that fails to load is a worse experience than a feed that is slightly stale, which gives us our most important consistency lever. The system must scale to hundreds of millions of daily active users and the read volume that implies. And critically: **the feed can be eventually consistent.** If you post and your follower sees it 1 or 2 seconds later instead of instantly, nobody notices and nobody cares. If a follower sees the post in a slightly different order than another follower, nobody notices. This is not a bank ledger. We will lean on this relentlessly, because trading consistency for availability and latency is exactly what makes the whole design tractable — and it is precisely the kind of decision the [consistency models guide for architects](/blog/software-development/system-design/consistency-models-a-practical-guide-for-architects) exists to help you make deliberately rather than by accident.

The single most important property of this problem, and the one a junior misses, is the **read/write ratio and the graph skew.** Reads vastly outnumber writes — a typical user opens their feed many times a day and posts rarely, so reads outnumber writes by something like 100:1. That ratio is what tempts you toward precomputation: if reads dominate, do the work once at write time so every read is cheap. And the graph is power-law skewed: the median follower count is a few hundred, but the maximum is a hundred million, and a tiny number of accounts produce a wildly outsized share of the fan-out cost. Hold those two facts — reads dominate, the graph is brutally skewed — in your head, because every decision in this post falls out of them.

![A side-by-side comparison of fan-out on write versus fan-out on read showing where each strategy pays its cost in the request lifecycle](/imgs/blogs/design-a-news-feed-and-timeline-1.webp)

Figure 1 states the fork the whole design turns on. Fan-out on write — "push" — does the expensive work when a post is created: it writes a copy of the post into a precomputed timeline for every follower, so a read is a single cheap lookup. Fan-out on read — "pull" — does almost nothing at write time and pays the cost at read time, gathering posts from everyone you follow and merging them when you open the app. Each strategy is correct for a different part of the workload, and the senior move, which we build toward, is to not choose one globally but to apply each where it wins. Hold the picture; the next sections fill in the math that decides where the line falls.

## 2. Estimation: size the problem before you design it

A senior never proposes an architecture for a feed without first doing the napkin math, because the numbers decide which designs are even viable. Fan-out on write is a beautiful idea right up until the storage and write-throughput numbers tell you it cannot be done naively, and you only learn that by estimating. Let us do it properly; this is the same discipline the [back-of-the-envelope estimation post](/blog/software-development/system-design/back-of-the-envelope-estimation-for-system-design) drills, applied to this specific problem.

Start with the user base. Assume **500 million daily active users (DAU).** Assume each posts, on average, modestly — say each active user produces 0.2 posts per day (most people post rarely; a minority post a lot). That gives roughly **100 million posts per day.** Spread over 86,400 seconds, that is about **1,200 posts per second on average,** and peak traffic runs perhaps 3x the average, so call it **~3,600 posts/second at peak.** Posts per second is small and unalarming. It is the fan-out multiplier that turns it terrifying.

Assume the **average follower count is 500.** (The median is lower, but the mean is dragged up by the skew, and for write-volume estimation the mean is what matters.) Under pure fan-out on write, each post produces, on average, 500 timeline writes. So fan-out writes per day are 100M posts × 500 followers = **50 billion timeline writes per day.** That is roughly **600,000 timeline writes per second on average,** and at peak, with the 3x multiplier, **~1.8 million timeline writes per second.** That is a serious write fleet — orders of magnitude more than the post rate — and it is the dominant cost of the push strategy. The asymmetry is the whole story: you turned 1,200 posts/second into 600,000 writes/second by precomputing.

Now read QPS. With 500M DAU each opening the feed several times a day and each open paginating a few times, you are easily at **billions of feed reads per day,** which lands around **1 million read QPS at peak** for the feed endpoint, with each read touching a timeline plus hydration of ~30 posts. Reads dominate writes in *request count* even though writes dominate in *fan-out work* — both numbers are large, and they pull the design in different directions.

![A matrix mapping daily users, posts per day, average followers, fan-out writes per day, timeline storage, and peak read QPS to their architectural implications](/imgs/blogs/design-a-news-feed-and-timeline-6.webp)

Figure 6 collects these numbers in one place so the design has a consistent quantitative backbone. The line that matters most is "fan-out writes/day = 50 billion," because that is the number that kills the naive design and forces the hybrid. Every subsequent decision — storage layout, sharding, the celebrity carve-out — exists to make that 50-billion-writes-a-day number survivable, or to avoid paying it where it is not worth paying.

#### Worked example: timeline storage cost

Let us size the storage for fan-out on write, because the answer dictates the data model. We do **not** store full posts in each follower's timeline — that would be insane duplication. We store **post ids only,** roughly 8 bytes for the post id plus 8 bytes for a sort key (timestamp or score), so call it **16 bytes per timeline entry.** We cap each timeline at the most recent **800 entries** — nobody scrolls past a few hundred posts, and an uncapped timeline grows without bound. So each user's timeline costs at most 800 × 16 bytes = **12.8 KB.** Across 500M users that is 500M × 12.8 KB ≈ **6.4 TB** of timeline data, and with replication (say 3x for durability and read scaling) and Redis overhead, round up to roughly **10–20 TB** of in-memory timeline storage. That fits in a sharded Redis fleet of a few hundred nodes — expensive, but tractable. The discipline here is what makes push viable: store ids not bodies, cap the length, and hydrate the full posts on read. Had we stored full 1 KB posts per entry, the same calculation gives 500M × 800 × 1 KB = 400 TB, which is roughly 30x more expensive and pushes you off RAM entirely. The data-model choice — ids plus a cap — is what turns an impossible 400 TB into a manageable ~10 TB.

#### Worked example: sizing the read fleet and the fan-out fleet

Now size the compute, because the napkin numbers should produce a fleet count you could defend. Start with reads: ~1 million feed reads/second at peak, each read doing a timeline lookup, a small VIP merge, ranking, and hydration of ~30 posts. Suppose a single feed-service instance, with caching, handles ~2,000 feed reads/second at the target p99 (ranking and hydration are the cost). Then you need on the order of 1,000,000 / 2,000 = **~500 feed-service instances** at peak, plus headroom for the 3x burst and for instance failure, so provision closer to **~800–1,000 instances.** That is a large but unremarkable stateless fleet behind a load balancer — exactly the kind of horizontally scalable tier the [load balancing from L4 to L7](/blog/software-development/system-design/load-balancing-from-l4-to-l7) post sizes.

Now the fan-out fleet. Average fan-out is ~600k timeline writes/second, peaking near ~1.8M/second. Suppose one fan-out worker, pipelining writes, sustains ~20,000 timeline writes/second (network-bound on the Redis round trips, batched per shard). Then steady state needs 600,000 / 20,000 = **~30 workers,** and peak needs ~90 — but the queue is what saves you here. Because fan-out is decoupled behind a queue, you do *not* provision for the instantaneous peak; you provision for the *average* plus enough margin to drain bursts within your freshness SLO (say, fan-out completes within a few seconds). So you might run **~50 fan-out workers** and let the queue absorb spikes, with autoscaling adding workers when queue depth climbs. The contrast is the lesson: the read fleet must be sized for peak (reads cannot wait), but the write/fan-out fleet can be sized for average because the queue buffers the peak. That asymmetry — size synchronous paths for peak, asynchronous paths for average — is a reusable capacity-planning principle, and it is why the queue-decoupled fan-out is not just about retries but about *fleet cost.*

## 3. The API: keep the contract small

A feed has a small, stable API surface, and pinning it down early forces clarity about pagination and ranking. Here is the contract a senior would put on the board. The write side:

```http
POST /v1/posts
Authorization: Bearer <token>
Content-Type: application/json

{ "text": "shipping the hybrid fan-out today", "media_ids": ["m_8123"] }

201 Created
{ "post_id": "p_99c1", "created_at": "2026-06-15T10:02:11Z" }
```

```http
POST   /v1/users/{user_id}/follow      -> 204
DELETE /v1/users/{user_id}/follow      -> 204
```

The read side is where the interesting decision lives — **pagination must be cursor-based, not offset-based:**

```http
GET /v1/feed?limit=30&cursor=<opaque>
Authorization: Bearer <token>

200 OK
{
  "items": [ { "post_id": "p_99c1", "author": "u_77", "score": 0.91, ... }, ... ],
  "next_cursor": "eyJzIjowLjgxLCJpZCI6InBfNGEifQ"
}
```

Why cursor-based and never offset-based? Because the feed is a live, mutating stream. With offset pagination (`?page=3&size=30`, i.e. `OFFSET 90 LIMIT 30`), new posts arriving at the top shift everything down, so by the time the user scrolls to "page 3" the offset points to different content — they see duplicates at the page boundary or skip posts entirely. A cursor encodes *where you were* — the score and id of the last item you saw — so the next page resumes exactly after that point regardless of what got inserted at the top. The cursor is an opaque, base64-encoded token; making it opaque means you can change its internals (from a timestamp to a `(score, post_id)` tuple when you add ranking) without breaking clients. This is a small decision that prevents a whole class of "why am I seeing the same post twice" bugs, and getting it right early is pure senior hygiene — the kind of stable-contract thinking the [API design post](/blog/software-development/system-design/api-design-rest-grpc-graphql) treats as a first-class concern.

## 4. High-level architecture: the two paths

Strip the feed to its essence and you have two independent paths that meet at a shared timeline store. The **write path:** a post arrives, gets persisted durably, and then is fanned out — the system decides, per author, whether to push the post id into follower timelines now or to leave it to be pulled at read time. The **read path:** a feed request arrives, reads the user's precomputed timeline, merges in any live pulls (for celebrities the user follows), ranks the merged set, slices a page, hydrates the post bodies, and returns. These two paths scale independently, fail independently, and have completely different bottlenecks — the write path is bottlenecked on fan-out throughput, the read path on merge-and-rank latency. Keeping them separate is the first structural decision.

![A directed architecture graph showing the write path routing posts to a fan-out service or a celebrity bypass, and the read path merging precomputed timelines with celebrity pulls](/imgs/blogs/design-a-news-feed-and-timeline-2.webp)

Figure 2 shows the hybrid architecture we are building toward, so you can see the whole board before we justify each piece. On the write side, a **post service** persists the post and hands it to a **fan-out router** that checks the author's type. Normal users go to **fan-out workers** that push the post id into each follower's timeline in the **timeline store** (sharded Redis). Celebrities are *not* fanned out — their posts sit in their own author feed, flagged for pull. On the read side, a **feed service** reads the user's precomputed timeline, pulls the recent posts of any celebrities the user follows, and **merges and ranks** the union before returning a page. The fan-out workers are decoupled from the post service by a queue, because fan-out is bursty, retryable, and must not block the user's `201 Created` — this is a textbook use of the [queues and event-streaming patterns for architects](/blog/software-development/system-design/queues-and-event-streaming-for-architects), where the queue absorbs the fan-out storm and lets workers drain it at their own pace.

The reason fan-out goes through a queue and not a synchronous call deserves emphasis. When you post, the API should return `201 Created` the instant the post is durably stored — within tens of milliseconds. The fan-out to 500 (or 100 million) timelines happens *after,* asynchronously, by workers consuming from a queue. This decoupling does three things: it keeps post latency low and predictable regardless of follower count; it makes fan-out *retryable* without the user waiting (a worker that crashes mid-fan-out just re-reads the message); and it lets you absorb bursts by buffering in the queue instead of overwhelming the timeline store. The cost is that the feed becomes eventually consistent — there is now a lag, usually sub-second, between posting and the post appearing in followers' feeds — and we already decided that is fine. This is the single most important structural choice in the write path.

## 5. Fan-out on write (push): make reads trivial

Let us fully develop the push strategy, because it is the right answer for the overwhelming majority of users and the foundation the hybrid builds on. The idea: when a normal user posts, a fan-out worker looks up that user's follower list and writes the new post id into the precomputed timeline of every follower. The timeline is a per-user, length-capped list of post ids, kept hot in Redis. When a follower opens their feed, the read is almost free: read the list of ids, hydrate the bodies, return. All the work happened at write time, amortized across the many reads that follow.

```python
# Fan-out worker: push a post id into every follower's timeline
def fan_out_on_write(post_id, author_id, created_at):
    followers = follower_store.get_followers(author_id)   # e.g. 500 ids
    score = created_at                                    # chronological sort key
    pipe = redis.pipeline()
    for fid in followers:
        key = f"feed:{fid}"
        pipe.zadd(key, {post_id: score})                  # sorted set, score = time
        pipe.zremrangebyrank(key, 0, -801)                # cap at newest 800
    pipe.execute()                                        # one round trip, batched
```

Two implementation details make or break this. First, **use a sorted set (`ZADD`), not a plain list,** with the score being the timestamp (later, the ranking score). A sorted set keeps the timeline ordered and lets `ZREVRANGE` page it efficiently, and `ZREMRANGEBYRANK` trims it to the cap in one cheap operation. Second, **pipeline the writes** so 500 follower updates cost one network round trip per shard, not 500 — without pipelining, the per-write network latency dominates and a worker can fan out only a handful of posts per second.

![A layered stack showing a feed request reading a capped Redis list of post ids, hydrating bodies from a post store, and rebuilding from a durable log on a miss](/imgs/blogs/design-a-news-feed-and-timeline-7.webp)

Figure 7 shows the storage layout that makes the read trivial. The timeline is a Redis sorted set per user, `feed:{uid}`, capped at ~800 entries, holding **post ids only** (~16 bytes each). On read, you take the top N ids from the set and **batch-hydrate** the full post bodies with a single `MGET` against the post store (itself cached). The bodies live once, in the post store; the timelines hold only references. And because the timeline is a *cache* — derivable from posts plus the follow graph — a cold or evicted timeline can be rebuilt by reading the user's followees' recent posts from a durable log. That rebuildability is what lets you keep timelines in volatile RAM without risking data loss: the source of truth is the durable post store, the timeline is a precomputed view.

The push strategy's profile is exactly what the workload wants for normal users: **read amplification is O(1)** — one timeline read plus one batched hydration, p99 in the single-digit milliseconds — and the cost is **write amplification of O(F)** where F is the follower count. For a user with 500 followers that is 500 cheap writes, fully acceptable. The strategy reads beautifully and writes cheaply *as long as F stays small.* The entire celebrity problem is what happens when F is not small.

There is one more piece of fan-out-worker mechanics worth getting right, because it is where a naive implementation silently loses or duplicates posts. Fan-out is consumed from a queue, and queues deliver at-least-once — a worker can crash after writing some timelines but before acknowledging the message, so the message is redelivered and those timelines get written *again.* For a feed this is mostly harmless because a sorted set is idempotent under re-insertion: `ZADD feed:{fid} {score} {post_id}` with the same id and score just overwrites the same member, so a redelivered fan-out produces no duplicate. That idempotency is not an accident; it is why a sorted set keyed by post id is the right structure rather than a plain list (where a re-push would append a second copy). The discipline of making the fan-out write idempotent so at-least-once delivery is safe is exactly the [idempotency and deduplication](/blog/software-development/system-design/idempotency-and-exactly-once-by-design) discipline applied to the feed: do not fight at-least-once delivery, design the write so repeating it is a no-op. A worker that fans out a 500-follower post, crashes at follower 300, and is redelivered simply re-writes followers 0–299 (idempotent no-ops) and continues — no duplicates, no losses, no coordination required.

A second worker subtlety: **chunk large follower lists.** A worker should not hold one message while it writes a million timelines; if it does, a crash loses all the in-progress work and redelivery redoes a million writes. Instead, for any author above a few thousand followers, the fan-out is *chunked* — the follower list is split into batches of, say, 1,000, and each batch is its own queue message. A worker processes one 1,000-follower chunk, acknowledges it, and moves on; a crash loses at most one chunk's progress, and the chunks parallelize across the whole worker fleet. Chunking is what lets fan-out scale horizontally and recover cheaply, and it is the bridge between the clean per-post fan-out of normal users and the (eventually unworkable) fan-out of near-celebrities just below the pull threshold.

## 6. Fan-out on read (pull): make writes trivial

Now the opposite strategy, because understanding why pure pull also fails is what motivates the hybrid. In fan-out on read, posting is trivial: the post is written once, into the author's own feed, and that is it — no fan-out. The work happens at read time. When you open your feed, the system looks up everyone you follow, reads each of their recent posts, merges those streams in time order, and returns the top page. Nothing is precomputed; everything is computed live.

```python
# Pull / fan-out on read: merge followees' recent posts at query time
def fan_out_on_read(user_id, limit=30):
    followees = follow_store.get_followees(user_id)       # could be thousands
    streams = []
    for fid in followees:
        streams.append(post_store.recent_posts(fid, k=limit))  # K queries!
    merged = heapq.merge(*streams, key=lambda p: -p.score)     # k-way merge
    return list(itertools.islice(merged, limit))
```

The profile is the mirror image of push: **write amplification is O(1)** — one write per post, no matter how many followers — and **read amplification is O(K)** where K is the number of accounts you follow. If you follow 1,000 accounts, every feed open fans out to ~1,000 reads, gathers their recent posts, and does a k-way merge. That read is expensive and its latency is dominated by the slowest of the K queries (tail latency amplification — with 1,000 parallel reads, the chance that *at least one* is slow approaches certainty, so your p99 read is governed by your p99.9 backend). For a user who follows thousands of accounts and opens their feed dozens of times a day, you are doing the same expensive merge over and over, recomputing a result that barely changed. Pull wastes work on the read-heavy workload exactly where push shines.

So why would anyone pull? Because pull has one property push cannot match: it costs **nothing extra per follower at write time.** A post by an account with 100 million followers costs the same one write as a post by an account with three followers. Pull does not care about the follower count at all — the cost lives entirely on the read side, paid only by users who actually open their feed, and only for the accounts they actually follow. That single property is the key to the celebrity problem, because the celebrity problem is precisely "an account whose follower count makes push explode." If you pull *those* accounts and push everyone else, you get push's cheap reads for the common case and pull's follower-count-independence for the expensive tail.

## 7. The celebrity problem and the hybrid resolution

Here is the crux of the entire design, the thing the opening scenario was about. Under pure fan-out on write, a post's write cost is O(F), the follower count. For the median user, F is a few hundred and the cost is trivial. For a celebrity, F is tens of millions to a hundred million, and the cost is catastrophic.

#### Worked example: the celebrity fan-out storm

Take a celebrity with **100 million followers.** Under pure push, a single post by that account must write the post id into **100 million timelines.** Even if each timeline write is a cheap 50-microsecond Redis operation and you pipeline aggressively, 100 million writes is enormous: at, say, 1 million timeline writes per second of dedicated fan-out throughput, that one post takes **100 seconds** to fully fan out. During those 100 seconds, the post is missing from most followers' feeds — the freshness guarantee is shot. Worse, you have ~10,000 such mega-accounts, and they post several times a day; the aggregate fan-out from celebrities alone can rival or exceed the fan-out from all 500 million normal users combined. The fan-out queue backs up, workers fall behind, and a single popular event — a major sports moment, a breaking-news post — sends a synchronized burst of celebrity posts that produces a **fan-out storm** measured in *billions* of writes in minutes. Pure push does not just cost more for celebrities; it falls over.

![A side-by-side comparison showing a celebrity post under push generating a hundred million timeline writes versus pull-for-celebrity storing a single write merged live at read time](/imgs/blogs/design-a-news-feed-and-timeline-4.webp)

Figure 4 makes the explosion visceral: on the left, one celebrity post fans out into 100 million timeline writes and a backed-up queue; on the right, the same post is stored once in the author's feed and merged live at read time, costing zero fan-out writes. The math is brutal and one-directional — push's cost scales with follower count, and follower count is exactly what a celebrity has in pathological quantity.

The resolution is the **hybrid:** push for the 99%, pull for the 1%. Concretely, you draw a threshold on follower count — say, accounts above 100,000 followers are "celebrities" and bypass fan-out on write. When a normal user posts, you push as before. When a celebrity posts, you do *not* fan out; the post sits in the celebrity's own feed, flagged. On the read path, after reading a user's precomputed timeline, the feed service also looks at the (small) list of celebrities that user follows, pulls those celebrities' recent posts live, and merges them into the timeline before ranking. The merge is cheap because the number of celebrities any one user follows is small — you might follow a thousand normal accounts but only a handful of mega-celebrities. So you pay a tiny per-read pull cost (merging a handful of celebrity feeds) to avoid the catastrophic per-write push cost (fanning out to a hundred million timelines). That is the trade, and it is overwhelmingly worth it.

![A decision tree branching on author follower count to choose push fan-out, live pull, or a split of push for active followers and pull for dormant ones](/imgs/blogs/design-a-news-feed-and-timeline-8.webp)

Figure 8 is the decision tree a senior keeps in their head when a post arrives: the decisive fork is the *author's* follower count, not the reader's. A normal user's post takes the push branch — fan out to follower timelines. A celebrity's post takes the pull branch — store once, merge live on read. And there is a refinement worth naming: for a celebrity whose followers are *mostly active,* you can do a finer split — push to the active followers (who will actually read soon, so precomputing is not wasted) and pull for the dormant ones (who may never open the app, so pushing would be pure waste). That active/dormant split is how the very largest systems shave the fan-out bill further, and it is the kind of second-order optimization that distinguishes a staff-level answer.

Where exactly do you draw the threshold? It is a tuning knob, not a constant, and reasoning about it is the kind of quantitative argument a design review rewards. Set the threshold too high (say, only accounts above 10 million followers pull) and you still push to accounts with millions of followers — most of the fan-out-storm cost remains. Set it too low (say, anyone above 1,000 followers pulls) and you push the read-side merge work onto nearly everyone, eroding push's cheap-read advantage and bloating every feed read with many live pulls. The sweet spot is where the *marginal cost* of pushing one more follower exceeds the *marginal cost* of pulling that author live at read time. Empirically that lands around the tens-of-thousands to ~100k-follower range for most products, because below it the fan-out is cheap and the author posts rarely enough that pushing is clearly cheaper, and above it the fan-out cost dominates. You can even make the threshold *dynamic* — factor in the author's posting frequency (a high-follower account that posts rarely is cheaper to push than a medium-follower account that posts constantly) and their followers' activity rate. The point is that the threshold is a measured, tunable boundary you revisit as the graph and posting behavior shift, not a magic number.

The hybrid introduces real complexity — a follower-count threshold to tune, two code paths to maintain, a merge step on every read, and the awkward boundary case of an account that crosses the threshold (a user who goes viral overnight and jumps from 5,000 to 5 million followers, whose old posts were pushed but whose new posts should be pulled). That transition is genuinely fiddly: the moment an account is reclassified from "push" to "pull," you stop fanning out its new posts, but its already-pushed posts still sit in follower timelines — so for a window you must both read those pushed posts *and* pull the new ones, deduplicating where they overlap, until the old pushed posts age off the capped timelines. Handling that crossover cleanly (and the reverse, a celebrity whose follower count drops back below threshold) is where most of the hybrid's real bugs live. These are real costs, and a senior names them. But the alternative is a design that cannot serve celebrities at all, so the complexity buys correctness, not mere elegance.

## 8. Read vs write amplification: the core trade-off, quantified

Step back and look at the trade-off through the lens that actually governs the decision: amplification. Every fan-out strategy is a choice about *where to spend* — the same logical work (delivering N posts to M followers) can be paid at write time or read time, and amplification is the multiplier on each.

| Strategy | Write amplification | Read amplification | Storage | Celebrity behavior |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Push (fan-out on write) | O(F) — F = followers | O(1) — single lookup | High (per-follower copies) | Explodes at large F |
| Pull (fan-out on read) | O(1) — one write | O(K) — K = followees | Minimal (no copies) | Native (cost is follower-independent) |
| Hybrid (push + pull-for-VIP) | O(F) for normal, O(1) for celeb | O(1) + small VIP merge | Medium (copies for normal only) | Solved (pull the few VIPs) |

The senior reading of this table: **push optimizes the read path at the expense of the write path; pull does the reverse; the hybrid pays a small, bounded read-side merge to escape the unbounded write-side cost that push incurs for celebrities.** Because reads outnumber writes ~100:1 in request count, you *want* most of the system to be push — cheap reads, paid for once at write — and you only break that rule precisely where push's write cost goes nonlinear, which is celebrities. The hybrid is not a compromise that is worse than both; it is strictly better than either alone across the real, skewed workload, at the cost of complexity.

![A matrix scoring push, pull, and hybrid across write cost, read latency, storage cost, celebrity handling, and freshness](/imgs/blogs/design-a-news-feed-and-timeline-3.webp)

Figure 3 is the trade-off matrix in full, the artifact you would put on the whiteboard in the design review. Read it column by column. Push wins read latency (~5ms) and freshness-once-delivered but loses on write cost (O(F)), storage, and celebrity handling (explodes). Pull wins write cost (O(1)), storage (minimal), and celebrity handling (native) but loses badly on read latency (~200ms for a big k-way merge). Hybrid takes the green cells from both: cheap reads (~30ms including the small VIP merge), medium write cost, medium storage, and *solved* celebrity handling. No row is a clean sweep, which is the entire point — the matrix forces you to admit that the hybrid *pays* in complexity and a per-read merge for what it *gains* in escaping the celebrity explosion. Never present a design without naming what it costs; this matrix is how you do that honestly.

The freshness row deserves a note because it is subtle. Push has a **freshness lag**: between the moment a post is created and the moment fan-out finishes writing it to all timelines, the post is missing from feeds — usually sub-second for normal users, but seconds-to-minutes for accounts with huge follower lists (which is another reason to pull them). Pull is always perfectly fresh because it reads live. The hybrid is fresh for celebrities (pulled live) and near-fresh for normal users (push lag is small when F is small). So the hybrid's freshness profile is actually *better* than pure push, because the accounts with the worst push-lag — celebrities — are exactly the ones it pulls live. The optimization compounds: pulling celebrities fixes both the write-cost explosion *and* the freshness lag in one move.

## 9. From chronological to ranked: the ranking pipeline

So far the feed has been chronological — newest first. Every large social product long ago moved to a **ranked feed:** instead of strict reverse-chronological order, posts are scored by predicted relevance (how likely you are to engage) and shown best-first. Ranking is what turns the feed from a storage problem into a machine-learning problem, and it imposes a hard latency budget on the read path because the scoring must happen *while the user waits.*

The ranked read path is a funnel. You start with a large pool of candidate posts (everything the user could possibly see), narrow it with cheap filters, score the survivors with an expensive model, and return the top slice. The funnel shape matters because the model is too expensive to run on everything — you cannot score a million candidates per request in 50 milliseconds — so candidate generation's job is to get the pool down to a scoreable size (a couple thousand) cheaply, and the model's job is to rank that pool accurately.

![A pipeline showing candidate generation producing two thousand posts, a feature store hydrating features, a scoring model, and a ranking and diversification step returning the top thirty](/imgs/blogs/design-a-news-feed-and-timeline-5.webp)

Figure 5 shows the four stages. **Candidate generation** assembles the pool — for a feed, that is the posts in your precomputed timeline plus the live-pulled celebrity posts plus, often, some out-of-network candidates (posts from accounts you do not follow but might like), typically a couple thousand posts total. **Feature hydration** looks up the features the model needs: *user* features (your interests, recent activity), *post* features (age, media type, the author's typical engagement), and *cross* features (your past interaction with this author, topical affinity). These come from a **feature store** — a low-latency key-value system holding precomputed features — because computing features on the fly would blow the budget. **Scoring** runs the candidates through a model that predicts, per candidate, the probability you will engage (like, comment, dwell, reshare), combining those into a single score; this must complete in p99 under ~50ms, which constrains model size and demands batched inference. **Ranking and diversification** sorts by score, then applies business rules — don't show three posts from the same author in a row, interleave ads, ensure topic diversity — and returns the top ~30.

```python
# Ranked read path, sketched
def ranked_feed(user_id, cursor, limit=30):
    # 1. Candidate generation: timeline + live VIP pulls + out-of-network
    candidates = timeline.read(user_id, n=1500)
    candidates += pull_celebrity_posts(user_id)            # live merge
    candidates += out_of_network_candidates(user_id, n=200)

    # 2. Feature hydration (batched lookups against the feature store)
    feats = feature_store.batch_get(user_id, [c.id for c in candidates])

    # 3. Scoring: predict P(engage) per candidate, p99 < 50ms
    scores = ranker.predict(user_id, candidates, feats)    # batched inference

    # 4. Rank + diversify + paginate by cursor
    ranked = diversify(sorted(zip(candidates, scores), key=lambda x: -x[1]))
    return paginate(ranked, cursor, limit)
```

A word on the signals, because "predict engagement" is doing a lot of work and a senior should know what the model actually consumes. The score is typically a blend of several predicted probabilities — probability of a like, a comment, a reshare, a long dwell, a click — each predicted by the model and combined with business-chosen weights (a reshare might be weighted far more than a like because it signals stronger value). The features feeding those predictions split into three families, which is why the feature store is keyed three ways. *User* features capture the reader: their topical interests, their recent engagement history, their session context (time of day, device). *Post* features capture the item: its age (freshness decays fast — a 12-hour-old post is heavily penalized regardless of quality), its media type, its author's baseline engagement rate, early engagement velocity (a post getting likes fast is a strong signal). *Cross* features capture the reader-item pair: has this reader engaged with this author before, does the post's topic match the reader's affinities, is the author in the reader's close-friends cluster. Cross features are the most predictive and the most expensive — there are O(users × authors) of them — so they are computed lazily and cached hard. The reason this matters architecturally: the *cross* features are why you cannot precompute a single global ranking; the ranking is personalized per reader, which is exactly why scoring must run on the read path, per request, under budget, rather than once at write time like the fan-out.

The architectural consequence of ranking is that the read path's latency budget is now dominated by candidate generation and scoring, not by the timeline lookup. A senior allocates the budget explicitly: if your p99 read target is 200ms, you might spend ~10ms reading the timeline and pulling VIPs, ~30ms hydrating features, ~50ms scoring, ~20ms ranking and diversification, and leave the rest for network and hydration. Every stage has a budget, and blowing any one blows the whole read. This is also where the [caching strategies that bite](/blog/software-development/system-design/caching-strategies-and-the-pitfalls-that-bite) earn their keep — feature lookups, candidate sets, and even fully-ranked feed pages are cached aggressively, because re-running the full funnel on every single feed open (and a user opens their feed many times an hour) would be ruinously expensive. The common pattern is to compute a ranked feed, cache it for a short TTL (tens of seconds to a couple of minutes), and serve subsequent opens from that cache, only re-ranking when the cache expires or significant new content arrives.

## 10. The read-path merge in detail

Let us zoom into the read path of the hybrid, because the merge is where the two strategies physically meet and where the read latency budget is won or lost. When a feed request arrives, the feed service does several things, ideally in parallel, then combines them.

![A directed graph of the read path showing a feed request fan out to the precomputed timeline and parallel celebrity pulls, merging into a dedup, rank, page, and hydrate sequence](/imgs/blogs/design-a-news-feed-and-timeline-9.webp)

Figure 9 traces it. The request fans out to two sources in parallel: the **precomputed timeline** (a single Redis read of the user's `feed:{uid}` sorted set) and the **celebrity feeds** the user follows (a small number of parallel pulls, one per followed celebrity). Those two streams **merge** into a deduplicated union of post ids — dedup matters because a post could theoretically arrive via both paths during a threshold-crossing transition. The merged set is **ranked** (the funnel from the previous section) and **paged** by the cursor, then the top page is **hydrated** — a single batched `MGET` fetches the full post bodies from the post store. The parallelism is the key latency move: the timeline read and the celebrity pulls happen concurrently, so the read latency is the *max* of the two, not the sum.

Two failure modes to design around. First, the celebrity pull is the riskier leg — it is K parallel reads against the post store, and tail-latency amplification means one slow celebrity feed can stall the whole merge. The fix is a **timeout with graceful degradation:** if a celebrity pull does not return within, say, 50ms, drop it and serve the feed without that celebrity's latest post. A feed missing one celebrity's most recent post for one refresh is invisible to the user; a feed that hangs for 2 seconds waiting on a slow pull is a disaster. This is exactly the [reliability and graceful-degradation discipline](/blog/software-development/system-design/reliability-slos-error-budgets-and-graceful-degradation) — degrade the feature, never the page. Second, a cold timeline (cache miss — the user has not opened the app in a while and their timeline was evicted) requires a rebuild: read the user's followees' recent posts and reconstruct the timeline on the fly. The rebuild is expensive (it is essentially a one-time pull), so you cache the rebuilt result immediately and warm timelines proactively for users likely to return.

## 11. Data model and sharding

The data model is small but the sharding decisions are load-bearing. There are four logical stores.

**The post store** holds the durable, canonical post bodies, keyed by `post_id`. It is the source of truth and the thing timelines reference. Because posts are immutable once created and accessed by id, this is an ideal key-value workload, sharded by `post_id`. The recent posts are heavily cached (a viral post is read millions of times; we will return to this in the stress test).

**The follow graph** holds, for each user, their followers and their followees. This is the hottest lookup in the write path — fan-out reads the follower list on every post — so it is denormalized into two adjacency lists: `followers:{uid}` (who follows me, read on fan-out) and `followees:{uid}` (who I follow, read on pull-merge). It is sharded by `uid`. For celebrities, the follower list is enormous (100M entries), which is another reason not to read it on every post under push — yet another nudge toward pulling celebrities.

**The timeline store** holds the precomputed timelines, `feed:{uid}` sorted sets in Redis, capped at ~800 entries. **Shard by `uid`** so each user's timeline lives entirely on one shard and a feed read is a single-shard operation. This is the central sharding decision and it follows directly from the access pattern: feed reads and fan-out writes are both keyed by the *target* user, so sharding by target user makes both single-shard. The [partitioning and sharding without downtime](/blog/software-development/system-design/partitioning-and-sharding-without-downtime) post covers how to grow this fleet as you add users without a migration outage — relevant here because a successful feed only ever needs *more* timeline shards, never fewer.

**The feature store** (for ranking) holds precomputed user, post, and cross features for low-latency hydration, sharded for parallel batch reads.

A subtle but important point: shard the timeline store by `uid`, and a single user's feed read never fans out across shards — it is one `ZREVRANGE`. But fan-out *writes* do scatter across shards (a post's 500 followers live on many different shards), which is fine because each write is independent and the pipeline batches per shard. Contrast this with sharding timelines by `post_id` or `author_id`, which would make a feed read a scatter-gather across many shards — far worse for the dominant read path. The rule: **shard by the key of your most frequent, most latency-sensitive access,** which for a feed is the reading user. Get this wrong and every feed read becomes a fan-in across the cluster.

### Follow, unfollow, and the backfill problem

Follow and unfollow look trivial — flip an edge in the graph — but under fan-out on write they create a real backfill question that a senior anticipates. When user A follows user B, A's timeline currently contains nothing from B. Do you *backfill* — read B's recent posts and inject them into A's precomputed timeline so A immediately sees B's content — or do you let the timeline fill naturally from B's next post forward? Backfill is a nice experience (you follow someone and their recent posts appear) but it is a burst of writes at follow time, and at scale (a celebrity gaining millions of followers during a viral moment, each follow triggering a backfill) it can be its own small fan-out storm. The pragmatic answer most systems land on: backfill a *small* number of recent posts synchronously at follow time (so the feed is not empty), and rely on forward fan-out for the rest. The cost is named and bounded.

Unfollow is the trickier direction. When A unfollows B, B's posts are still sitting in A's precomputed timeline. Do you scrub them out immediately (a burst of deletes across A's timeline) or let them age out naturally as the capped timeline rolls forward? Immediate scrubbing is correct but expensive; lazy filtering — keeping the ids but filtering at read time against the *current* follow set — is cheaper and is what many systems do, accepting that an unfollowed user's posts linger briefly in the precomputed list until they scroll off. This is another place the feed's eventual-consistency tolerance pays off: a just-unfollowed user's post appearing for one more refresh is not a correctness bug, it is acceptable lag, and recognizing which "bugs" are actually acceptable lag is a core senior skill. The same logic covers blocks and privacy changes — filter at read against current state rather than trying to synchronously scrub every precomputed timeline.

## 12. Consistency: why eventual is exactly right

A news feed is one of the cleanest examples of a system where strong consistency would be a *mistake* — not merely unnecessary but actively harmful, because it would cost availability and latency for a guarantee nobody needs. Let us be precise about what consistency model the feed actually requires, because saying "eventual is fine" without understanding *why* is hand-waving, and a senior is expected to defend it.

The user-visible guarantees that matter are weak. **Followers may see a new post with a lag** — sub-second under normal push, seconds for large fan-outs — and this is fine; nobody perceives a 1-second delay between someone posting and it appearing in their feed. **Different followers may see posts in slightly different orders or with slightly different freshness** — fine; no two users compare feeds. **A post you make is not guaranteed to appear in your own feed instantly** — though here you often *do* want read-your-writes for the author's own profile view, which you get cheaply by reading the author's own feed directly rather than waiting for fan-out. The one consistency property you genuinely want is **monotonicity within a session:** as a user scrolls and paginates, they should not see posts jump around or reappear, which the cursor (encoding the exact `(score, id)` position) provides without any global consistency.

Contrast this with what strong consistency would cost. To guarantee that every follower sees a post the instant it is created, you would have to make fan-out synchronous and block the post until all timelines are updated — which is impossible for a celebrity (you cannot block a post for the 100 seconds it takes to fan out to 100M timelines) and pointless for everyone else. Eventual consistency is not a compromise the feed tolerates; it is the *correct model* for the feed, and it is what unlocks asynchronous fan-out, the read/write path split, and high availability. This is the kind of deliberate, requirement-driven consistency choice the [consistency models guide](/blog/software-development/system-design/consistency-models-a-practical-guide-for-architects) argues every architect should make explicitly rather than inheriting by accident. The feed gets to be eventually consistent because the *product* is eventually consistent, and recognizing that is half the design.

## 13. Stress test: what breaks at scale

A design is only as good as its behavior under stress, so let us deliberately break this one. A senior does not present a design as finished until they have walked it through its worst days. There are three failure scenarios that actually happen to feeds, and the hybrid is built specifically to survive them.

**Scenario 1: a celebrity posts (the fan-out storm).** We covered the arithmetic — 100M followers means a pure-push design tries to do 100M writes for one post. Under the hybrid, this is a non-event: the celebrity's post is *not* fanned out at all; it is stored once and pulled live by readers. The fan-out storm simply does not happen because we removed the cause. This is the headline benefit of the hybrid: the worst-case write amplification is bounded by the celebrity threshold (say 100k), not by the maximum follower count (100M). What breaks at 10x scale? If celebrity *count* grows 10x, the read-side merge sees more candidate celebrities per user, but since each user still follows only a handful of celebrities, the per-read cost barely moves. The hybrid degrades gracefully as the graph skews harder, which is exactly the direction real graphs skew.

**Scenario 2: a viral post (the hot read / thundering herd).** A single post — by a celebrity or a normal user whose post catches fire — gets read tens of millions of times in minutes. Now the bottleneck is not fan-out; it is the *post store hydration* for that one post id. Tens of millions of reads for the same `post_id` is a textbook hot key. The fix is layered caching: the post body for a hot post is cached in Redis and, ideally, in the feed service's in-process cache, so the vast majority of those reads never touch the post store. But caching a hot key has its own trap — the **thundering herd / cache stampede:** when the hot post's cache entry expires, thousands of concurrent reads all miss simultaneously and slam the post store at once. The fix is **request coalescing (single-flight):** the first miss fetches and populates the cache while all concurrent misses for the same key *wait* on that single in-flight fetch rather than each issuing their own. This is precisely the stampede pattern the [caching pitfalls post](/blog/software-development/system-design/caching-strategies-and-the-pitfalls-that-bite) dissects — a viral post is the canonical hot key, and single-flight plus a small jittered TTL is the canonical fix.

#### Worked example: surviving a viral post

A post goes viral: **20 million reads in 5 minutes,** which is ~67,000 reads/second for that single post id. Without caching, that is 67k QPS hammering one post-store shard — it falls over. With a Redis cache holding the post body, those 67k reads hit Redis (which handles it easily) and the post store sees ~0. Now the TTL expires. Without single-flight, at the expiry instant, potentially thousands of in-flight requests all miss and issue concurrent reads to the post store — a 67k-QPS spike against one shard for the duration of one cache fill. With single-flight, exactly **one** request fetches from the post store on the miss; the other ~thousands wait microseconds for it to populate the cache, then read from cache. The post store sees **one** read instead of thousands per TTL cycle. Add a small random jitter to the TTL so not every hot post expires on the same boundary, and the post store's view of a viral post is a trickle — a few reads per minute — instead of a periodic stampede. The viral post, which looks like it should be the scariest load, becomes the *easiest* to serve because its extreme read concentration is exactly what caching is best at.

**Scenario 3: the ranking latency tail.** The ranked read path has a hard budget, and the scoring stage is the most likely to blow it. At 10x candidate volume, or when the feature store is slow, the model scoring can balloon past its 50ms budget and drag the whole p99 read over the SLO. The fixes are budget enforcement and degradation: cap the candidate set size (score 1,500, not 15,000); time-box the scoring stage and, on timeout, fall back to a cheaper ranker or even to chronological order for that request; and cache ranked feed pages so most opens skip scoring entirely. The principle is that **ranking is a best-effort enhancement, not a hard dependency** — a slightly-worse-ranked feed served fast beats a perfectly-ranked feed served slow, and the system must be able to shed ranking work under pressure. Building that fallback in from the start is the difference between a feed that degrades and one that times out.

## 14. Optimization: where the real money and latency go

Let us pull the optimization thread together explicitly, because "the optimization" in a feed is a specific, defensible set of moves, each with a measurable win.

**Optimization 1: precompute for the 99%, compute-on-read for the 1%.** This is the headline. Push for normal users (cheap reads, bounded write cost) and pull for celebrities (avoid the unbounded write cost). The win is measured in fan-out writes avoided: removing celebrity fan-out can cut total fan-out volume by *half or more* depending on the graph, because mega-accounts contribute a wildly disproportionate share of fan-out. You measure it as fan-out writes/second before and after the celebrity carve-out — a direct, dollar-denominated reduction in your fan-out fleet and timeline-store write load.

**Optimization 2: store ids, not bodies; cap the timeline.** Storing 16-byte ids instead of ~1KB bodies and capping at 800 entries takes timeline storage from ~400TB to ~10TB — a ~30x reduction that moves timelines from "impossible on RAM" to "comfortably in a Redis fleet." You measure it as bytes-per-timeline and total fleet RAM.

**Optimization 3: cache aggressively at every read layer.** Cache post bodies (kills the viral-post hot read), cache ranked feed pages (kills repeated re-ranking for frequent openers — a user who opens the app 20 times an hour should pay for ranking once, not 20 times), and cache features. The win is measured as the read-path's cache hit rate and the resulting reduction in post-store QPS and scoring invocations. A 95% feed-page cache hit rate means you run the ranking funnel 20x less often.

**Optimization 4: batch and pipeline everything.** Pipeline fan-out writes (500 writes → 1 round trip per shard), batch-hydrate post bodies (`MGET` not N `GET`s), batch feature lookups, and batch model inference. Each batching move turns per-item network latency into per-batch latency, often a 10–100x throughput gain on the affected stage. You measure it as items-per-round-trip and the stage's p99.

**Optimization 5: the active/dormant split for celebrities.** Even within the pull-for-celebrity rule, push to a celebrity's *active* followers (who will read soon) and pull for *dormant* ones (who may never open the app). This recovers cheap reads for the followers who matter while still avoiding the wasted writes to dormant accounts. The win is measured as wasted fan-out writes eliminated — writes to timelines that are never read are pure cost, and the dormant fraction of a celebrity's followers can be the majority.

The meta-point: the feed's optimizations all flow from the same two facts we started with — reads dominate writes, and the graph is brutally skewed. Precompute because reads dominate; carve out celebrities because the graph is skewed; cache because reads repeat. Every optimization is a direct consequence of the workload's shape, which is why a senior derives them rather than memorizes them.

## 15. Case studies: how real systems do this

The hybrid fan-out design is not theoretical — it is, in broad strokes, how the major social products actually run their feeds. The specifics below are at the level of publicly discussed architecture; I will keep the numbers at order-of-magnitude where exact figures are not public.

**Twitter's timeline.** Twitter's home timeline is the canonical public example of fan-out on write at massive scale. For most users, when you tweet, the tweet id is fanned out into the precomputed home timelines (historically held in a Redis-based store) of your followers, so reading your timeline is a fast lookup. The team discovered the same celebrity problem this post centers on — fanning out a tweet from an account with tens of millions of followers is prohibitively expensive — and the publicly discussed resolution is exactly the hybrid: high-follower accounts are *not* fully fanned out on write; their tweets are merged into followers' timelines at read time. Twitter's architecture is where much of the industry's vocabulary for this problem ("fan-out," the "timeline service," the celebrity carve-out) comes from. The concrete lesson: at scale, the question is never push *or* pull, it is *where you draw the line between them.*

**Instagram's feed.** Instagram similarly precomputes feeds and has spoken publicly about ranking and caching their feed heavily, and about the engineering discipline of keeping the feed read fast under enormous scale on a (historically Postgres- and Cassandra-backed) stack. The relevant lesson for our design is their emphasis on caching and on the feed as a *ranked,* not merely chronological, product — the move from "recent posts" to "predicted-relevant posts" with a machine-learning ranking layer, run within a tight latency budget, mirrors the ranking pipeline in section 9.

**Facebook's feed and the feature store.** Facebook's News Feed is the archetype of a *ranked* feed at scale, with a candidate-generation-then-scoring funnel and a heavy reliance on precomputed features served from low-latency stores. The lesson here is the one about budgets: ranking is run on the read path under a hard latency ceiling, which forces the candidate set to be narrowed cheaply before the expensive model runs, and forces features to be precomputed rather than calculated on the fly. The architecture of "narrow first, score second, within a budget" is industry-standard for any large ranked feed.

**The common thread — the queue-decoupled fan-out.** Across these systems, the structural pattern is the same: posting is a fast, durable write that returns immediately; fan-out happens asynchronously via a queue of workers; the queue absorbs bursts and makes fan-out retryable; timelines are precomputed caches rebuildable from durable post logs; celebrities bypass fan-out and are pulled live; and the read path caches and ranks. If you understand that pattern and the celebrity carve-out, you understand how every large feed works. The differences between products are in the *thresholds and the ranking,* not in the fundamental shape.

## 16. When to reach for each strategy (and when not to)

A decisive recommendation, because the point of all this is to choose well under real constraints.

**Use pure fan-out on write (push) when** your follower graph has a bounded maximum follower count — an internal corporate social tool, a niche community, any system where no account has more than, say, tens of thousands of followers. With a bounded max, push's O(F) write cost never explodes, you get the cheapest possible reads, and you avoid the hybrid's complexity entirely. Push is the right *default* for any feed where you can guarantee the graph stays un-skewed. Do not add pull complexity you do not need.

**Use pure fan-out on read (pull) when** writes are extremely cheap and reads are rare or the graph is small — a low-traffic feed, an MVP where you have not yet hit scale, a system where users follow few accounts and open the app rarely. Pull is also the right *first* implementation: it is dramatically simpler (no fan-out infrastructure, no timeline store, no fan-out workers), and you should not build push until your read volume justifies the precompute. Many feeds start as pull and migrate to push/hybrid only when reads outgrow the live-merge cost. Starting with pull and evolving is the [evolutionary-architecture](/blog/software-development/system-design/evolutionary-architecture-designing-for-change) move — do not build the celebrity carve-out on day one for a product with no celebrities.

**Use the hybrid when** your graph is power-law skewed — any public social product where some accounts have orders of magnitude more followers than the median. This is the only viable design at large public scale, and the complexity is mandatory, not optional, because neither pure strategy survives the skew. The moment you have celebrities, you need the hybrid; before that, you do not.

**Reach for ranking when** the volume of candidate posts exceeds what a user can reasonably scroll, so that *ordering* becomes the product — which is essentially every feed past a certain follow-count. **Do not reach for ranking when** chronological order is genuinely what users want (a strict timeline, a chat-like feed) or when you lack the engagement data to train a useful ranker; a chronological feed is simpler, debuggable, and perfectly good until relevance becomes the bottleneck. Premature ranking adds a whole ML system and a latency budget you do not yet need.

The senior summary: **start simple (pull, chronological), evolve to push when reads dominate, add the celebrity carve-out when the graph skews, add ranking when ordering becomes the product.** Each step is forced by a specific scaling pressure, and adding any of them before its pressure arrives is over-engineering. The architecture should grow into the hybrid, not be born as it.

## 17. Forward: the messaging-system cousin

It is worth noting how this design relates to its sibling, the [chat and messaging system](/blog/software-development/system-design/design-a-chat-and-messaging-system), because they look superficially similar (both deliver messages from authors to recipients) but pull apart on requirements in an instructive way. A feed is *read-heavy, eventually consistent, fan-out-to-many, and best-effort* — a follower missing the latest post for a second is invisible. A chat system is *delivery-guaranteed, ordered-per-conversation, fan-out-to-few, and latency-critical in both directions* — a dropped or reordered message is a bug, and the recipient expects near-instant delivery with read receipts. The same fan-out vocabulary applies (you "fan out" a group message to its members), but chat's small fan-out (a group has dozens of members, not millions) means the celebrity problem largely disappears, while its strong delivery and ordering guarantees introduce problems the feed never has. Seeing where the two diverge sharpens the understanding of both: the feed's freedom to be eventually consistent and best-effort is precisely what makes its hard problems (fan-out scale, ranking) tractable, and chat trades that freedom for guarantees that create different hard problems.

## 18. Key takeaways

- **The fork is where you pay, not what you compute.** Fan-out on write pays at post time for cheap reads; fan-out on read pays at read time for cheap posts. The same delivery work, billed at opposite ends. Choose based on your read/write ratio and graph skew, never by default.
- **Push optimizes reads (O(1)) and pays O(F) writes; pull inverts it.** Because reads outnumber writes ~100:1 in a feed, you *want* push for the common case — pay once at write, amortize across many reads.
- **The celebrity problem is push's O(F) going nonlinear.** A 100M-follower post is 100M timeline writes. The hybrid resolves it: push the 99% of normal users, pull the 1% of celebrities live at read time. Bounded write cost, solved freshness, small per-read merge.
- **Store ids, cap the timeline, hydrate on read.** Timelines hold 16-byte post ids, capped at ~800 entries — ~10TB across 500M users instead of ~400TB if you stored bodies. The data-model choice is what makes push affordable.
- **Eventual consistency is correct, not a compromise.** A feed can lag a second and reorder freely because the product can; that freedom is what unlocks async fan-out, the read/write split, and high availability. Defend it as a deliberate choice.
- **Cursor pagination, never offset.** A live stream shifts under offset pagination, causing duplicates and skips. A cursor encodes the exact `(score, id)` position and resumes cleanly regardless of inserts.
- **Ranking is a best-effort funnel under a hard budget.** Generate candidates cheaply, score the survivors with a budgeted model, degrade to chronological on timeout. A fast okay-ranked feed beats a slow perfect one.
- **The viral post is a caching problem, not a fan-out problem.** Tens of millions of reads for one post id is a hot key; layered caching plus single-flight turns a stampede into a trickle. The scariest-looking load is the easiest to serve.
- **Shard by the reader.** Timelines sharded by user id make the dominant access — the feed read — a single-shard operation. Shard by the key of your most frequent, most latency-sensitive access.
- **Grow into the hybrid; don't be born as it.** Start pull-and-chronological, evolve to push when reads dominate, add the celebrity carve-out when the graph skews, add ranking when ordering becomes the product. Each step is forced by a specific pressure.
