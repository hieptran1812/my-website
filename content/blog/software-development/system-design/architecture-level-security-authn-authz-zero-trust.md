The cheapest security bug I ever shipped cost a single line of code and four hours of incident response, and it taught me the thesis of this entire post: security is not a feature you add at the end, it is a property of the architecture you either have or do not. The bug was an authorization check that asked "is this user logged in?" but forgot to ask "does this user own *this* object?" — so any authenticated customer could increment the ID in a URL and read another customer's invoice. The login worked perfectly. The token was valid, the signature verified, the session was real. Every layer of *authentication* did its job. And the system still leaked every customer's data to every other customer, because authentication answers "who are you" and we had never built the layer that answers "what are you allowed to do." That gap — between proving identity and enforcing permission — is where most real breaches live, and a senior engineer feels its absence in a design the way a structural engineer feels a missing load path.

This post is about treating security as an architectural concern: a set of properties you design in from the first whiteboard sketch, not a checklist a security team bolts on the week before launch. We will work through the two questions every request must answer — **authentication** (who are you: sessions, tokens, JWT and its sharp edges, OAuth2/OIDC, mTLS, API keys) and **authorization** (what may you do: RBAC, ABAC, ReBAC, policy engines, the centralize-versus-embed decision) — and then the architectural disciplines that contain damage when, not if, something is compromised: **defense in depth** and the trust boundary, **zero trust** versus the old perimeter model, **secrets management** with short-lived credentials, and the **principle of least privilege** as a blast-radius tool. Figure 1 starts us at the front door with the OIDC flow that most modern systems use to answer "who are you," because authentication is where the request begins and where the most subtle protocol mistakes hide.

![Pipeline showing the OIDC authorization code flow from user browser through the app server to the identity provider and back with a short-lived JWT](/imgs/blogs/architecture-level-security-authn-authz-zero-trust-1.webp)

The reason security belongs in the architecture and not in a feature ticket is that the expensive mistakes are structural, not local. A missing input-sanitization call is a bug you fix in one place. A system that *trusts the network* — that assumes any caller inside the firewall is friendly — is a structure, and you cannot patch your way out of it; you have to redesign the trust model. The same is true of a system where every service runs as one over-permissioned superuser, where secrets live in plaintext environment variables baked into the deploy, where a leaked token is valid for a year because nobody built revocation. These are not bugs. They are architectural choices, usually made by *not* making a choice, and they decide how bad your worst day is. By the end of this post you should be able to design the authentication and authorization for a multi-tenant API, reason about whether to centralize or embed your authorization decisions, place your trust boundaries deliberately, and compute the blast-radius reduction that short-lived credentials buy you — and defend every one of those choices in a review when someone asks "what happens when this token leaks?"

A note on scope. This is the architect's decision layer, not a cryptography textbook. I will explain how a JWT is structured well enough to show you where it bites, but I will not re-derive elliptic-curve signatures; when a mechanism needs depth I will point you at it and stay focused on the question a senior actually owns: *which* approach, *where* enforced, and *what it costs*. Security has more genuinely adversarial failure modes than any other topic in this series, so the stress-testing discipline — "what breaks at 10x?" becomes "what does an attacker do with this?" — matters more here than anywhere.

## 1. The two questions every request must answer

Before any architecture, fix the vocabulary, because conflating these two words is the single most common source of security bugs I have reviewed. Every request that reaches your system must be evaluated against two distinct questions, in order, and they are not the same question.

**Authentication (authN) answers "who are you?"** It is the act of proving identity — verifying that the caller is who they claim to be. A username and password, a signed token, a client certificate, an API key: all are mechanisms for establishing identity. Authentication produces a *principal*: a verified statement of who is making this request. It is binary in spirit — either you have proven your identity or you have not — and it happens once, early, at the edge of your system.

**Authorization (authZ) answers "what are you allowed to do?"** Given a proven identity, authorization decides whether *this* principal may perform *this* action on *this* resource. It is not binary across the system; it is per-action, per-resource, and it must be evaluated on every operation that has access rules, not just at login. Authorization produces a *decision*: allow or deny, for a specific request against a specific object.

The invoice bug from the intro was a pure authorization failure stacked on a perfect authentication layer, and that pattern is so common it has a name in the OWASP Top 10: **broken object-level authorization**, also called IDOR (insecure direct object reference). The system authenticated the user flawlessly — valid token, real session — and then failed to check that the authenticated user was allowed to touch the *specific object* they requested. Authentication tells you the request comes from a real, logged-in user. It tells you *nothing* about whether that user owns the row they are reading. Treating "authenticated" as "authorized" is the conceptual error behind a huge fraction of real-world data leaks, and the first discipline of architecture-level security is to keep these two questions rigorously separate in your design and in your code.

There is a third question that quietly rides along with both: **auditing** — "what did you do?" Every meaningful action should leave a tamper-resistant record of which principal did what to which resource and when. Auditing is not a security control that prevents anything; it is the control that lets you *detect* and *reconstruct* a breach after the fact, and it is the difference between "we were breached, and here is exactly what the attacker touched" and "we were breached, and we have no idea what they accessed, so we must assume everything." We will return to audit logging as a first-class architectural concern, because a senior designs for the incident they will eventually have, not just the happy path they hope for.

## 2. Authentication: sessions versus tokens

The first architectural fork in authentication is how you remember that a user proved their identity, because HTTP is stateless and proving identity on every single request by re-sending a password is both slow and dangerous. There are two dominant answers, and they sit at opposite ends of a statefulness trade-off.

**Server-side sessions** keep the state on your side. When a user logs in, you create a session record in a shared store (Redis, a database) holding the user's identity and metadata, and you hand the client an opaque, random session ID in a cookie. On every subsequent request, the client sends the cookie, and your server looks up the session ID in the store to recover who they are. The session ID itself carries no information — it is just a random key into your server-side state. The defining property is that *the source of truth lives on your server*, which makes one capability trivial that tokens make hard: **instant revocation**. To log a user out everywhere, or to kill a compromised session, you delete one row. The session is gone on the next request. The cost is that every authenticated request requires a lookup in the shared session store, which is a network round trip and a dependency that must scale with your traffic and stay available — if the session store is down, nobody is authenticated.

**Tokens (typically JWTs)** push the state to the client. Instead of a random ID pointing at server state, you hand the client a *self-contained* token that carries the identity claims directly, cryptographically signed so the client cannot tamper with it. On each request the client sends the token; your server *verifies the signature* and reads the claims straight out of the token — no lookup, no shared store, no round trip. The defining property is **statelessness**: any server with the verification key can validate the token in isolation, which is wonderful for horizontal scaling and for distributed systems where the request bounces across many services. The cost is the mirror image of the session's strength: because the token is self-contained and valid until it expires, **you cannot easily revoke it**. You issued a bearer of authority that lives on the client, and short of maintaining a denylist (which reintroduces the very server-side state you were trying to avoid), a stolen token is valid until expiry.

This is the core trade-off, and it is worth stating as a senior would in a review: **sessions trade a per-request lookup for instant revocation; tokens trade revocation for statelessness.** Neither is "more secure" in the abstract. A high-security application that must be able to kill access instantly — a banking back office, an admin console — often *wants* the server-side control of sessions and accepts the lookup cost. A horizontally scaled API serving millions of requests across dozens of stateless services often *wants* the no-lookup scalability of tokens and mitigates the revocation gap with short expiry and refresh tokens. The mature answer is frequently a hybrid: short-lived stateless access tokens for the hot path, plus a stateful, revocable refresh token checked only at refresh time, which we will design shortly. The point is that you choose deliberately, knowing exactly which property you are giving up.

## 3. JWT and its sharp edges

Because JWTs are so common, every senior must know not just how to use them but precisely how they bite, because the failure modes are subtle and have caused real breaches. A JWT is three base64url-encoded parts joined by dots: a **header** (which signing algorithm), a **payload** (the claims — who, what scopes, when it expires), and a **signature** (a cryptographic check over the header and payload). The signature is the whole game: it lets any holder of the verification key confirm the token was issued by your authority and has not been altered, without contacting that authority. Here is the honest minimum for verifying one correctly.

```python
import jwt  # PyJWT
from jwt import InvalidTokenError

PUBLIC_KEY = load_public_key()  # RS256 public key from your IdP's JWKS

def verify_access_token(token: str) -> dict:
    try:
        claims = jwt.decode(
            token,
            PUBLIC_KEY,
            algorithms=["RS256"],          # PIN the algorithm — never trust the header
            audience="api.example.com",    # this token was minted FOR us
            issuer="https://auth.example.com",
            options={"require": ["exp", "iat", "aud", "iss", "sub"]},
        )
    except InvalidTokenError as e:
        # expired, bad signature, wrong audience, wrong issuer, missing claim
        raise Unauthorized(str(e))
    return claims  # has sub, scope, tenant_id, exp...
```

The single most important line is `algorithms=["RS256"]`, and the bug it prevents is the infamous **`alg: none` attack**. Early JWT libraries trusted the *header* to declare the signing algorithm. An attacker could take a valid token, change the header to `{"alg": "none"}`, strip the signature, and send it — and a naive library would see "algorithm: none, so no signature to verify" and *accept the unsigned token as valid*. Total authentication bypass: the attacker forges any identity they like. The fix is to never let the token tell you how to verify it; you pin the expected algorithm server-side and reject anything else. A close cousin is the **RS256-to-HS256 confusion attack**: if your code accepts both, an attacker can take your *public* RSA key (which is public by design), use it as the *secret* for HMAC-SHA256, sign a forged token with it, and a library that picks the algorithm from the header will verify the HS256 signature using the public key as the secret — again forging any identity. Pinning the algorithm kills both.

The second sharp edge is **revocation**, the structural weakness from section 2. A JWT is valid until its `exp` claim says it is expired, full stop. If a token is stolen at minute one of a one-hour expiry, the attacker has fifty-nine minutes of valid access and *you cannot stop them* without building extra machinery. This drives a hard architectural rule: **keep access-token lifetimes short** — fifteen minutes is a common sweet spot — so that the maximum exposure of a stolen token is bounded by the expiry, not by how long it takes you to notice. Short expiry is your primary revocation mechanism for stateless tokens, and it is why the OIDC flow pairs a short access token with a longer-lived, *revocable* refresh token: the access token is fast and stateless on the hot path, and the refresh token is checked against server state only every fifteen minutes, giving you a revocation point without a per-request lookup. The third edge is simpler but bites constantly: **do not put secrets in the payload.** The payload is base64-encoded, not encrypted — anyone holding the token can decode and read every claim. Sensitive data in a JWT is sensitive data handed to the client in readable form.

## 4. OAuth2 and OIDC: delegating identity

When the user does not authenticate directly to your app but through an identity provider — "Log in with Google," or a corporate SSO, or your own central auth service — you are in OAuth2/OIDC territory, and Figure 1 above showed the flow you should reach for. The distinction worth keeping straight: **OAuth2 is an *authorization* framework** for granting an app delegated access to resources (it answers "may this app act on the user's behalf?"), while **OIDC (OpenID Connect) is an authentication layer on top of OAuth2** that adds a standardized identity token (it answers "who is this user?"). In practice you usually want OIDC, because you usually want to know who the user *is*, and OIDC gives you an **ID token** (a JWT describing the user) alongside the **access token** (the credential for calling APIs).

The flow you should use for a web or mobile app is the **authorization code flow with PKCE**, and its central security property is that the user's password and the powerful tokens never pass through the app's front channel where they could be intercepted. The user is redirected to the identity provider, authenticates *there* (your app never sees the password), and the provider redirects back with a short-lived, single-use **authorization code**. Your app's backend then exchanges that code — over a direct, server-to-server back channel — for the actual tokens. PKCE (Proof Key for Code Exchange) closes a specific attack: the app generates a random secret at the start, sends only its hash to the provider, and must present the original secret to redeem the code, so an attacker who intercepts the authorization code in the redirect cannot redeem it without the secret. The architectural lesson is that the flow is *designed around trust boundaries*: the password stays at the IdP (the only place it should be), the code crosses the untrusted browser but is useless without the back-channel exchange and the PKCE secret, and the real tokens only ever travel server-to-server.

The flows you should *not* reach for in modern designs are worth naming so you can veto them in a review. The **implicit flow** (tokens returned directly in the redirect URL fragment) was a workaround for old browsers and is now discouraged because it exposes tokens in the front channel and in browser history; the authorization code flow with PKCE replaces it. The **resource owner password credentials flow** (the app collects the user's password and sends it to the IdP) defeats the entire point of delegation — the app sees the password — and should be used only for legacy migration, never new designs. When someone proposes "we will just collect the password and call the auth API," the senior answer is that this collapses the trust boundary the whole protocol exists to preserve.

## 5. Service-to-service: mTLS and workload identity

Authentication is not only about humans. Inside a distributed system, services call other services constantly, and "who are you" has to be answered for *machines* too — a question the old perimeter model answered with a dangerous shrug: "you're inside the network, so you're trusted." That shrug is exactly what zero trust rejects, and the mechanism that replaces it for service-to-service calls is **mutual TLS (mTLS)**. In ordinary TLS, the *server* presents a certificate so the client knows it is talking to the real server; in mutual TLS, the *client* also presents a certificate, so both ends cryptographically prove their identity to each other. Each service gets its own short-lived certificate tied to its workload identity (issued by an internal certificate authority, often automated by a service mesh), and a service refuses connections from any caller that cannot present a valid, current certificate for an allowed identity.

The architectural payoff is large: with mTLS, the *network* stops being a trust boundary and *identity* becomes the trust boundary. It no longer matters that an attacker is "inside" — if they cannot present a valid service certificate, they cannot make a call that any service will answer. This is the concrete mechanism that makes zero trust real for the east-west traffic between services, and it is why service meshes (Istio, Linkerd) make automatic mTLS one of their headline features: they issue, rotate, and verify the certificates so individual services do not have to. The certificates are deliberately short-lived (hours, not years) and auto-rotated, which means a compromised service's credential expires fast — the same short-lived-credential discipline we apply to humans, applied to machines.

For simpler cases or external partners, **API keys** are the low-end of machine authentication: a long random string that identifies a calling application. They are fine for coarse identification and rate-limiting by client, but they are *static bearer credentials* — anyone who has the key is the client — so they share every weakness of a long-lived secret and must be treated as one: stored in a vault, never in code, rotatable, and scoped to the minimum the integration needs. A senior reaches for mTLS for internal service-to-service trust, reserves API keys for coarse external identification where mTLS is impractical, and never treats an API key as if it were a real, revocable, short-lived identity.

## 6. Where the token lives: storage and the XSS/CSRF squeeze

Before leaving authentication, there is an architectural decision that quietly decides whether your token scheme is safe or a liability: **where does the client store the token, and how is it sent?** This is the kind of detail juniors treat as a frontend concern and seniors recognize as a security-architecture decision, because the storage choice trades two different attacks against each other and you must choose which one you are defending against more.

The first option is **local storage (or memory) plus an `Authorization: Bearer` header.** JavaScript reads the token and attaches it to each request. The advantage is that this is immune to CSRF (cross-site request forgery) — a malicious site cannot make the browser attach your token, because the token is not sent automatically; your code has to attach it deliberately. The disadvantage is that it is exposed to XSS (cross-site scripting): if an attacker can run *any* JavaScript on your page — through a vulnerable dependency, an unsanitized input, a compromised third-party script — that script can read the token straight out of local storage and exfiltrate it. You have traded CSRF immunity for XSS exposure.

The second option is an **httpOnly, Secure, SameSite cookie.** The browser stores the token in a cookie that JavaScript *cannot read* (`httpOnly`), only sends over HTTPS (`Secure`), and only attaches on same-site requests or per your `SameSite` policy. The advantage is XSS resistance: even if an attacker runs JavaScript on your page, they cannot read an httpOnly cookie, so they cannot steal the token to use elsewhere. The disadvantage is CSRF exposure: because the browser attaches the cookie *automatically* to requests to your domain, a malicious site can trick the browser into making an authenticated request — which is why cookie-based auth needs CSRF defenses (the `SameSite` attribute, which largely closes this, plus anti-CSRF tokens for the cases it does not).

The senior framing is that **there is no storage location that defends against both XSS and CSRF for free; you pick your defense and then close the other gap deliberately.** The modern consensus for browser apps leans toward httpOnly + `SameSite=Strict`-or-`Lax` cookies, because XSS is the more dangerous and more common attack (it gives the attacker arbitrary code execution in your origin, which is game-over for almost any token scheme), and `SameSite` has matured enough to neutralize most CSRF. The deeper point for this post is that the token storage decision is *part of the authentication architecture*, not an afterthought — a perfectly verified JWT stored where any XSS can read it is a credential waiting to be stolen, and the strongest verification logic in section 3 protects nothing if the token is trivially exfiltrated from the client. This is defense in depth reaching all the way to the browser: the token's confidentiality on the client is as architectural as its signature on the server.

## 7. Authorization models: RBAC, ABAC, ReBAC

Once you know *who* the caller is, you must decide *what they may do*, and there are three dominant models for expressing access rules. Choosing among them is one of the highest-leverage architectural decisions in security, because the wrong model makes your permission logic either too rigid to express real rules or too sprawling to audit. Figure 2 reframes the larger shift these models live inside — from trusting the network to trusting verified identity — which is the backdrop for why per-request authorization matters at all.

![Before and after comparison contrasting the perimeter trust model where the firewall is the only gate against zero trust where every hop is re-authenticated](/imgs/blogs/architecture-level-security-authn-authz-zero-trust-2.webp)

**RBAC (role-based access control)** assigns permissions to *roles*, and users to roles. An "admin" role may do everything; an "editor" may write content; a "viewer" may only read. You check authorization by asking "does this user's role include the required permission?" RBAC's strength is simplicity and auditability: roles are coarse, there are few of them, and you can look at a role and immediately understand what it grants. Its weakness is that it cannot express rules that depend on *context* or on the *specific object*. "An editor may edit, but only documents in their own department, and only during business hours" is awkward-to-impossible in pure RBAC — you end up exploding into hundreds of hyper-specific roles ("editor-department-finance-businesshours") that defeat the simplicity that made RBAC attractive. This role explosion is the classic sign that you have outgrown RBAC.

**ABAC (attribute-based access control)** decides based on *attributes* of the principal, the resource, the action, and the environment, evaluated by a policy. "Allow if `user.department == resource.department AND action == 'read' AND time in business_hours`." ABAC is far more expressive than RBAC — it can encode arbitrary contextual rules — but it pays for that expressiveness in **auditability**: answering "who can access this document?" is no longer a lookup, it is a question you must *evaluate the policy* to answer, potentially across every user, because access depends on runtime attributes. ABAC's flexibility is its strength and its murkiness is its cost.

**ReBAC (relationship-based access control)** decides based on the *relationships* between principals and resources, modeled as a graph — the model Google's Zanzibar (the system behind Google Docs sharing) popularized. "You may view this document if you are its owner, OR a member of a group it was shared with, OR you can view a folder that contains it." Permissions flow through relationship edges: sharing a folder grants access to everything inside it because the *contains* and *member-of* edges connect you. ReBAC is the right model for sharing-heavy products (documents, repos, photo albums) where access is defined by who-is-connected-to-what rather than by static roles or attributes. Its cost is the most complex evaluation of the three: answering a check can require traversing the relationship graph, which is why systems like Zanzibar are sophisticated distributed graph databases under the surface. Figure 4 lays these three side by side against the properties that actually decide between them.

![Matrix comparing RBAC, ABAC, and ReBAC across coarse roles, context rules, relationship rules, audit clarity, and per-check latency](/imgs/blogs/architecture-level-security-authn-authz-zero-trust-4.webp)

The honest senior take is that most systems start with RBAC because it is simple and sufficient, and reach for ABAC or ReBAC only when a real requirement forces it: ABAC when context (department, time, location, data classification) genuinely drives access, ReBAC when sharing and group membership define access. The mistake is to reach for the powerful model prematurely — to build a Zanzibar clone for an app that has three roles — and pay its complexity tax for expressiveness you do not need. ## 8. Centralize or embed: the policy decision

Whatever model you choose, you face an architectural fork that matters as much as the model itself: where does the authorization *decision* get made? Do you embed the policy logic inside each service, or centralize it in a dedicated policy engine that services call? This is the centralize-versus-embed decision, and it is a genuine trade-off with no universally right answer.

**Embedding** the authorization logic in each service — the service has the role checks and policy rules in its own code — is fast (no network call to decide) and simple to start. Its weakness is consistency and auditability across a fleet: when the same permission logic lives in twenty services in slightly different forms, a policy change means twenty deployments, and a subtle divergence between two services' copies of "who may delete an order" is exactly the kind of gap an attacker finds. Embedded policy drifts.

**Centralizing** the decision in a policy engine — the canonical open-source choice is **OPA (Open Policy Agent)**, which evaluates declarative policies written in a language called Rego — gives you one place to define, version, test, and audit your authorization rules, and every service asks the same engine the same way. The classic pattern runs OPA as a sidecar next to each service so the policy *decision* is local (a fast in-process or localhost call) while the policy *definition* is centrally managed and distributed. Here is a small Rego policy enforcing multi-tenant object-level authorization — exactly the rule whose absence caused the intro's invoice leak.

```rego
package authz

import future.keywords.if

default allow := false

# Allow if the caller's tenant matches the resource's tenant
# AND the caller's role grants the requested action.
allow if {
    input.principal.tenant_id == input.resource.tenant_id
    some role in input.principal.roles
    action_allowed[role][input.action]
}

action_allowed := {
    "admin":  {"read": true, "write": true, "delete": true},
    "editor": {"read": true, "write": true},
    "viewer": {"read": true},
}
```

The senior framing: **centralize policy *definition*, but keep the *enforcement point* close to the resource.** OPA-as-a-sidecar achieves both — one source of truth for rules, decisions made locally without a cross-network hop. The deeper principle, though, is that the authorization decision must be made *where the resource lives*, by code that has the actual object in hand, because only there can it check object-level ownership. A centralized engine can decide "may an editor write?" but the service holding the order must still confirm "is *this* order in the caller's tenant?" — which is why the policy above takes both the principal's `tenant_id` and the resource's `tenant_id` as input. Centralizing the rule does not relieve you of enforcing it at the object. This is the architectural heart of preventing IDOR: every read and write checks the specific object against the caller, every time, at the service that owns the data.

## 9. The confused deputy and the trust boundary

A subtle and dangerous class of authorization failure is the **confused deputy** problem, and understanding it is what separates an engineer who *uses* permissions from one who *reasons about* them. A confused deputy is a privileged component that is tricked into misusing its authority on behalf of a less-privileged caller. The component has legitimate, broad permissions; an attacker who cannot do something directly persuades the privileged component to do it *for* them, and the system sees the privileged component's identity, not the attacker's, so the check passes.

The textbook modern instance is **server-side request forgery (SSRF)**. Suppose your service has a feature that fetches a URL the user provides — a webhook tester, an image-from-URL importer, a link preview generator. The *service* runs inside your network with the network's privileges. An attacker supplies not a public URL but an *internal* one — say, the cloud metadata endpoint at `169.254.169.254`, which on many cloud platforms returns the instance's credentials to anything that asks from inside. Your fetch service, the confused deputy, dutifully makes the request *with its own network position*, retrieves the credentials, and hands them back to the attacker. The attacker could not reach that endpoint directly — it is internal — but they reached it *through* your service, which had the access they lacked. Figure 7 shows the attack path and the fix side by side.

![Graph showing an SSRF attack path where a fetch service reaches the internal metadata endpoint and the egress allowlist fix that blocks the pivot](/imgs/blogs/architecture-level-security-authn-authz-zero-trust-7.webp)

The fix is architectural, and it has two parts that a senior applies together. First, **validate at the trust boundary**: the fetch service must treat the user-supplied URL as untrusted input and refuse to fetch internal addresses — an *egress allowlist* of permitted destinations, or at minimum a denylist of internal ranges (link-local, private, loopback) resolved *after* DNS to defeat rebinding tricks. Second, **least privilege removes the prize**: if the service has no need to reach the metadata endpoint, its network egress should not allow it at all, and the credentials it would have leaked should be short-lived and narrowly scoped so they are worth little even if exfiltrated. The confused deputy works because a privileged component faces untrusted input without re-validating at the boundary; the defense is to re-validate every input at every boundary and to ensure the deputy's privilege is the minimum it needs, so even a successful confusion yields little. This is why **every trust boundary re-validates** — a principle worth its own section.

## 10. Defense in depth and where to validate

The deepest architectural principle in security is **defense in depth**: no single control is trusted to be sufficient, so you layer independent controls such that bypassing one does not bypass them all. The opposite — a single perfect gate with soft, trusting internals — is the perimeter model, and it fails the moment that one gate is bypassed, because there is nothing behind it. Defense in depth assumes every layer *will* eventually be bypassed and arranges that the next layer still stops the attacker. Figure 3 shows the layered controls a request crosses.

![Stack diagram showing defense in depth layers from edge WAF and TLS through gateway authentication and service authorization down to scoped data access with encryption at rest](/imgs/blogs/architecture-level-security-authn-authz-zero-trust-3.webp)

Concretely, the layers are: at the **edge**, a WAF (web application firewall) filters known attack patterns and TLS encrypts the transport and rate limiting absorbs floods; at the **gateway**, authentication verifies the token and establishes the principal; at the **service**, authorization checks that this principal may perform this action on this object; at the **data** layer, queries are scoped to the caller's tenant and data is encrypted at rest. Each layer is an independent control with its own assumption, and the architectural rule is that **a layer never trusts that an earlier layer did its job perfectly.** The service re-checks authorization even though the gateway authenticated, because authentication is not authorization and because a bug or bypass at the gateway must not become a free pass to the data. This is why a senior is suspicious of any design where validation happens "once, at the edge" — the edge protects against external attackers, but it does nothing against a compromised internal service or a bug that lets a request skip the edge, and the layers behind it must each hold on their own.

This connects directly to the trust boundary, which deserves a precise definition: **a trust boundary is any point where data crosses from a less-trusted zone to a more-trusted zone, and it is exactly where you must re-validate.** The boundary between the internet and your edge is obvious. Less obvious — and more often missed — are the *internal* boundaries: between two of your own services, between your service and your database, between your application and a third-party API. The senior discipline is to enumerate the boundaries explicitly and to validate *at each one*, because "the caller is one of our own services" is precisely the assumption a compromised service exploits to move laterally. Figure 5 maps these boundaries as a tree so you can see that every edge in it is a place input must be re-checked.

![Tree of trust boundaries showing the request crossing the network edge with WAF and authentication and the app tier with authorization and a scoped data store](/imgs/blogs/architecture-level-security-authn-authz-zero-trust-5.webp)

There is a genuine tension here with performance, and a senior names it rather than hand-waving it. Re-validating at *every* boundary costs latency and CPU — re-parsing, re-checking signatures, re-authorizing. The optimization is not to *skip* validation but to make it *cheap and to validate at the right granularity*: verify a JWT signature once at the gateway and pass a trusted, in-process principal object downstream (so services do not each re-verify the signature, but they *do* each re-check authorization against the object they hold). Validate *structure* once at the boundary and *authorization* at every object access. The wrong optimization is "we validate at the edge so internal calls skip it"; the right one is "we verify cryptographic identity once and re-check object-level permission everywhere, because the cheap check that prevents IDOR is the one you can never skip." Section 14 returns to this performance-versus-security tuning with numbers.

## 11. Zero trust versus the perimeter model

The shift Figure 2 framed deserves its own treatment, because it is the defining architectural change in security thinking of the last decade. The **perimeter model** (often called "castle and moat") draws a hard boundary — the firewall — and trusts everything inside it. Authenticate at the front door, and once you are in, you can talk to any service, reach any database, move freely. It is simple and it was the default for decades, and it has one catastrophic failure mode: **once an attacker is inside, there is nothing stopping lateral movement.** A single compromised laptop, a phished employee, one exploited public-facing service, and the attacker is "inside the perimeter," which the model treats as trusted — so they pivot from service to service, escalate, and reach the crown jewels, because the architecture assumed inside equals safe.

**Zero trust** rejects the premise. Its mantra is "never trust, always verify," and its operational meaning is precise: **the network location of a caller grants no trust; every request is authenticated and authorized regardless of where it comes from.** A call from inside the data center is treated with exactly the same suspicion as a call from the public internet — it must present a valid identity (mTLS certificate, token) and pass authorization, every hop, every time. There is no "inside" that is automatically trusted. The practical consequence is the one that matters most: zero trust **contains the blast radius of a compromise.** When every hop re-authenticates and re-authorizes, a single compromised service cannot freely pivot — to reach the next service it must present credentials it may not have, and to reach the data it must pass authorization it may not hold. The breach is contained to what that one identity could legitimately do, instead of expanding to everything inside the moat.

The cost of zero trust is real and a senior accounts for it: it requires identity for every workload (the mTLS and workload-identity machinery from section 5), authorization checks on internal calls (latency and complexity), and the operational overhead of issuing and rotating short-lived certificates everywhere — which is why service meshes exist, to make that overhead manageable. You do not get zero trust for free; you pay it in infrastructure and per-hop verification cost. But the alternative — the perimeter model — pays its cost all at once, on the day of a breach, when one foothold becomes total compromise. The senior judgment is that for any system where a breach is consequential, the steady per-hop cost of zero trust is cheaper than the rare catastrophic cost of perimeter collapse, and the trade-off only gets more favorable as the tooling matures.

It is worth being precise that zero trust is a *destination*, not a switch you flip, and a senior plans the migration in stages rather than promising it overnight. The pragmatic path is to start by making identity strong everywhere (every workload gets a verifiable identity, even if you do not yet enforce on it), then to turn on *monitoring* of internal calls (log what talks to what, so you learn the real call graph before you start denying), then to enforce authorization on the highest-value paths first (the data tier, the admin surfaces), and only then to push enforcement outward to every hop. The order matters because flipping enforcement on blind — denying internal calls before you have mapped which services legitimately talk to which — is how a zero-trust rollout causes its own outage, denying traffic that was always legitimate but undocumented. The senior who has done this once knows that the hardest part is not the cryptography or the mesh; it is discovering the undocumented dependencies that the perimeter model let accumulate precisely because nothing ever checked them. Zero trust forces you to make the implicit call graph explicit, and that discovery — uncomfortable, revealing, occasionally alarming — is itself a large part of the security value, because a dependency nobody knew about is a dependency nobody secured.

## 12. Secrets management and the secret-zero problem

Secrets — database passwords, API keys, signing keys, encryption keys — are the credentials that let your services do their privileged work, and how you manage them is one of the loudest signals of a system's security maturity. The naive approach, which is depressingly common, is to put secrets directly in code or in plaintext environment variables baked into the deployment. Both are architectural failures. **Secrets in code** end up in version control, where they live forever in history even after you "remove" them, and where anyone with repo access — including a compromised CI system or a leaked clone — has them. **Secrets in plaintext environment variables** are readable by anything that can inspect the process, leak into logs and crash dumps and debugging tools, and are visible to every co-located process. The first rule of secrets management is therefore blunt: **secrets never live in code or in plaintext config, period.**

The architectural answer is a **secrets manager / vault** (HashiCorp Vault, AWS Secrets Manager, GCP Secret Manager) — a dedicated, access-controlled, audited service that stores secrets encrypted and hands them out only to authenticated, authorized callers, logging every access. But the vault's real power is not just secure storage; it is **dynamic, short-lived credentials.** Instead of storing a static database password that every service shares forever, the vault *generates a fresh, unique, short-lived credential on demand* — a database username and password valid for fifteen minutes, scoped to exactly the permissions that service needs, automatically revoked when the lease expires. Figure 9 shows that flow: the service proves its workload identity to the vault, receives a short-lived credential, uses it, and lets it expire.

![Pipeline showing a service authenticating to a vault with its workload identity and receiving a short-lived scoped database credential with a fifteen minute lease](/imgs/blogs/architecture-level-security-authn-authz-zero-trust-9.webp)

This solves the right problem in the right way, but it raises the puzzle every secrets system must answer: **the secret-zero problem.** If all your secrets live in the vault, and the vault requires a credential to authenticate, then *how does a service get the credential to authenticate to the vault in the first place?* You cannot store *that* secret in the vault — it is the bootstrap. Naively you would put a vault token in an environment variable, and now you are back to a static secret in plaintext config, having gained nothing. The mature answer is **workload identity backed by the platform**: the service proves who it is using an identity the platform vouches for and that is *not a stored secret* — a cloud instance identity document signed by the cloud provider, a Kubernetes service account token the cluster mints and rotates, a signed attestation from the orchestrator. The service presents this platform-issued identity to the vault, the vault verifies it with the platform, and only then issues the real credentials. Secret-zero is solved by anchoring trust in the *platform's* attestation rather than in any secret a human placed there, which is the only way to break the bootstrap regress. When you design a secrets architecture, the question to answer first is "how does the very first identity get established without a stored secret?" — and if the answer is "we put a token in an env var," you have not solved it, you have hidden it.

There is a second discipline that travels with the vault and is easy to forget: **rotation and revocation must actually be exercised, not merely possible.** A vault that *can* rotate credentials but never does is barely better than static secrets, because the long-lived credential is still long-lived in practice. The architectural commitment is to make rotation *automatic and routine* — credentials that rotate on a schedule the system enforces, leases that expire whether or not anyone remembers them, and a tested break-glass path to revoke a specific credential immediately when a compromise is suspected. A senior treats "can we revoke this credential in under a minute, and have we tested that we can?" as a design requirement, because the day you need to revoke a leaked credential is the worst possible day to discover the revocation path does not work. The whole value of short-lived, vault-issued credentials is that the leak self-heals on a clock; the whole value of a tested revocation path is that you do not have to wait for the clock when you already know.

## 13. Least privilege and blast-radius reduction

Running through every section so far is one principle that a senior treats as the organizing idea of architectural security: **the principle of least privilege.** Every component — every service, every credential, every user — should have the *minimum* permissions required to do its job, and nothing more. Not "admin because it's easier," not "all tables because we might need them," not "the whole network because debugging is simpler." The minimum. The reason is not pedantry; it is **blast-radius reduction**: the damage a compromise can do is bounded by the permissions the compromised thing held, so minimizing permissions minimizes the worst case.

The mental shift a senior makes is to stop asking "what permissions does this service need to work?" and start *also* asking "what is the worst this credential can do if it leaks?" — because both questions have the same answer, and the second one makes the stakes vivid. An over-permissioned service is a latent disaster: it works perfectly until it is compromised, and then the attacker inherits every permission you granted out of convenience. A service that only needs to read one table but was granted full database admin "to be safe" hands an attacker the entire database on compromise. The same service scoped to read-only on one table hands the attacker a read of one table. Same breach, wildly different outcome, decided entirely by a permission grant you made months earlier. This is why least privilege is an *architectural* property: it is a decision you bake into how you provision identities and scope credentials, not a thing you can retrofit after a breach.

Blast-radius thinking also drives **segmentation**: partitioning the system so a compromise in one zone cannot reach another. Separate the tenant data so one tenant's breach cannot read another's (the multi-tenant isolation we will design). Separate the network so a compromised frontend cannot directly reach the database. Separate credentials so the service that sends email cannot also read user passwords. Each boundary is a firebreak, and the architecture's resilience to compromise is measured by how small you can make the largest blast radius. A senior reviewing a design asks, for each component, "if an attacker fully owned *this*, what could they reach?" — and treats any answer of "basically everything" as a structural flaw to fix before launch, not a risk to accept.

## 14. Worked example: authN and authZ for a multi-tenant API

#### Worked example: designing the auth for a multi-tenant API

Let us design the authentication and authorization for a multi-tenant SaaS API — the kind where many customer organizations (tenants) share infrastructure but must never see each other's data — because it forces every principle in this post to become concrete, and because tenant isolation is where most multi-tenant breaches actually happen.

**Authentication.** A user logs in via OIDC (section 4) to our central identity provider, which issues a short-lived access token: a JWT with a fifteen-minute expiry, signed RS256, carrying `sub` (the user ID), `tenant_id` (which organization they belong to — the single most important claim in the whole design), `roles` (their roles within that tenant), and the standard `exp`, `iss`, `aud`. The gateway verifies this token on every request using the pinned-algorithm verification from section 3, rejecting `alg: none` and any token whose audience or issuer is wrong. Alongside the access token, the user holds a longer-lived refresh token (say seven days) that is *stateful and revocable* — stored server-side — so that disabling a user takes effect within fifteen minutes (the next refresh fails) without a per-request lookup. This is the hybrid from section 2: stateless on the hot path, revocable at the refresh point.

**Authorization — the two checks that prevent cross-tenant leaks.** Every request that touches a resource must pass *two* authorization checks, and missing either one is a breach. First, the **tenant check**: the `tenant_id` in the token must match the `tenant_id` of the resource being accessed. This is the wall between tenants, and it must be enforced *in the data layer*, not just in application logic — the safest design scopes *every query* to the caller's tenant automatically (a tenant filter injected into every database query, or row-level security in the database that the application cannot forget to apply), so that even a buggy handler physically cannot return another tenant's row. Second, the **role/object check**: within the tenant, does this user's role permit this action on this object? This is the RBAC (or finer) check, and for object-level operations it must confirm the specific object belongs to the user or their tenant — the exact check whose absence is IDOR.

**The flow, concretely.** A request arrives: `GET /api/v1/invoices/inv_8821`. The gateway verifies the JWT signature, audience, issuer, and expiry, extracting `tenant_id=acme`, `roles=[viewer]`. It passes a trusted principal object to the invoices service. The service loads invoice `inv_8821`, reads its `tenant_id`, and runs the OPA policy from section 8: is `acme == invoice.tenant_id`? If the invoice belongs to a different tenant, **deny** — and critically, return a `404 Not Found`, not a `403 Forbidden`, because a 403 leaks the existence of an object in another tenant. Is `viewer` allowed to `read`? Yes. Allow. Now stress-test it. An attacker in tenant `acme` changes the URL to `inv_9999`, an invoice belonging to tenant `globex`. The tenant check fires: `acme != globex`, deny, 404. The IDOR is blocked not because the attacker's token was invalid — it was perfectly valid — but because the architecture re-checks *object ownership against the authenticated tenant on every access.* That single check, enforced at the data layer so no handler can forget it, is the difference between a multi-tenant system and a data breach. Cross-link the data-layer enforcement to how you actually [partition and shard tenant data](/blog/software-development/database/database-partitioning-and-sharding) so the isolation is physical, not just logical, and to [load balancing from L4 to L7](/blog/software-development/system-design/load-balancing-from-l4-to-l7) for where the gateway that verifies the token physically sits.

## 15. The optimization lens: validate once at the right boundary

Security and performance are usually framed as opponents, but the senior framing is sharper: **the optimization is not to do less validation, it is to do each validation exactly once, at the right boundary, and make it cheap.** Sprinkling redundant cryptographic checks at every layer is slow *and* no more secure; skipping checks to go fast is fast *and* a breach. The real bottleneck in an auth-heavy system is almost always one of two things — repeated expensive cryptographic verification, and per-request round trips to a central auth store — and both have clean optimizations that cost nothing in security.

**Verify the signature once, authorize everywhere.** A JWT signature verification (RS256) costs real CPU — on the order of tens of microseconds to a fraction of a millisecond depending on key size — and if every one of a dozen internal services re-verifies the same token's signature on a single user request, you have paid that cost a dozen times for no security gain, because the *signature* does not change as the request moves internally. The optimization: verify the cryptographic signature *once* at the gateway, then pass a small, trusted, in-process principal object to downstream services (over mTLS-protected internal links, so the channel itself is authenticated). Downstream services trust the principal *because the channel is mTLS-authenticated*, and they spend their cycles on the check that actually varies per service — *authorization against the specific object they hold* — not on re-verifying a signature that has not changed. You verify identity once and re-check permission everywhere, which is both correct (section 10's rule) and fast.

**Cache the decision, not the credential — carefully.** For authorization decisions that are expensive (a ReBAC graph traversal, an ABAC policy over many attributes), caching the *decision* for a short TTL can cut p99 dramatically, but it trades freshness for speed: a cached "allow" survives a permission revocation until the TTL expires. The senior tunes this deliberately — a short TTL (seconds) for decisions that change rarely, no caching for high-stakes operations, and explicit cache invalidation on permission changes for the middle ground. Lean on [caching strategies and the pitfalls that bite](/blog/software-development/system-design/caching-strategies-and-the-pitfalls-that-bite) for the staleness math, because a stale authorization cache is a security bug, not just a correctness one.

#### Worked example: blast-radius reduction from short-lived credentials

Now the optimization that matters most for *security*, with numbers. Compare two credential strategies for a service's database access, and compute the blast-radius reduction Figure 6 claims.

![Before and after comparison contrasting a long-lived static database key valid for ninety days against a short-lived vault-issued credential valid for fifteen minutes](/imgs/blogs/architecture-level-security-authn-authz-zero-trust-6.webp)

**Long-lived credential.** The service uses a static database password stored in config, rotated every 90 days (a generous real-world cadence). Suppose the credential leaks — committed to a repo, captured in a log, exfiltrated via the SSRF of section 9. The exposure window is the time until the next rotation, *up to 90 days*, during which the attacker has full, valid database access. Even with diligent monitoring, a leak discovered "fast" might still mean days of exposure. The blast radius in time is enormous, and in scope it is whatever that static credential could do.

**Short-lived credential.** The service authenticates to the vault with its workload identity (section 12) and receives a database credential with a 15-minute lease, scoped to exactly the tables and operations it needs (least privilege, section 13), auto-renewed while the service runs and auto-revoked when it stops. Now suppose *this* credential leaks. The exposure window is at most **15 minutes** — after that the lease expires and the credential is dead, no human action required. Compute the reduction: 90 days is 129,600 minutes; 15 minutes is 15. The exposure window shrinks by a factor of **129,600 / 15 = 8,640x**. The attacker who steals a short-lived credential gets fifteen minutes of *scoped* access instead of three months of *broad* access — and because the credential is scoped to least privilege, even those fifteen minutes are limited to a few tables, not the whole database. The blast radius collapses on both axes, time and scope, and the only cost is the vault infrastructure and a sub-millisecond credential fetch the service does at startup and renewal. That 8,640x is the kind of number a senior brings to a design review to justify the vault: it is not "vaults are best practice," it is "vaults cut our worst-case credential exposure from three months to fifteen minutes."

## 16. Encryption, rate limiting, WAF, and audit logging

A few more controls round out the architectural security toolkit, each earning its place by the specific failure it prevents. **Encryption in transit** (TLS everywhere, including internal service-to-service via mTLS) protects against an attacker who can observe network traffic — and in a zero-trust world that includes the internal network, so "it's internal, plaintext is fine" is exactly the assumption zero trust rejects. **Encryption at rest** protects against an attacker who gets the disk or the backup — a stolen snapshot, a misconfigured storage bucket — and is cheap insurance against a whole class of exfiltration. The architectural subtlety is **key management**: encryption is only as strong as the protection of the keys, so keys live in a KMS (key management service) with their own access control and audit, encryption keys are themselves encrypted by a master key (envelope encryption), and keys are rotated. Encrypting data with a key stored next to the data in plaintext is theater; the key has to be protected by a different boundary than the data it protects.

**Rate limiting and the WAF are security controls, not just reliability controls.** Rate limiting (covered in depth in [rate limiting and backpressure](/blog/software-development/system-design/rate-limiting-and-backpressure)) caps how fast an attacker can guess passwords, enumerate object IDs (slowing the IDOR-by-brute-force attack), or exfiltrate data — it turns a smash-and-grab into a slow crawl that monitoring can catch. A WAF filters known attack signatures (SQL injection payloads, common exploit patterns) at the edge, which is a *layer*, not a *solution*: it catches the obvious and the automated, but a senior never treats it as a substitute for fixing the injection at the trust boundary, because a WAF is bypassed by anything novel. Both are early layers in defense in depth — they reduce the volume and the obviousness of attacks reaching your real controls, buying time and signal, while the real enforcement still happens deeper.

**Audit logging** is the control you will be most grateful for during the incident you have not had yet. Every security-relevant action — every authentication, every authorization decision (especially denials), every privileged operation, every secret access — should produce an immutable, tamper-resistant log entry recording the principal, the action, the resource, the decision, and the time. The architectural requirements are that the logs are *append-only* (an attacker who breaches the system must not be able to erase their tracks, so logs ship immediately to a separate, write-once store the breached system cannot rewrite) and *complete enough to reconstruct the breach* (what did this identity touch?). The difference between "we were breached and here is the exact list of records the attacker accessed" and "we were breached and have no logs, so we must notify every customer that their data *may* have been accessed" is, in regulatory and reputational terms, enormous — and it is decided entirely by whether you treated audit logging as an architectural requirement up front. Tie this to the broader [observability by design](/blog/software-development/system-design/observability-metrics-logs-traces-by-design) discipline, since security audit logs and operational telemetry share infrastructure but have stricter integrity requirements.

## 17. Case studies: four ways this goes wrong in production

The lessons land harder as incidents than as principles, so here are four failure shapes, each mapping to a section above, drawn from the well-documented public record of how these bugs actually bite.

**The JWT `alg: none` / signature-confusion bug.** Multiple JWT libraries across languages shipped, years ago, with the `alg: none` acceptance and the RS256-to-HS256 confusion described in section 3 — flaws serious enough that they prompted security advisories and library rewrites across the ecosystem. The shape is always the same: the library trusted the token's header to decide how to verify the token, which is logically circular (the thing being verified tells you how to verify it), and the result was full authentication bypass — forge any identity, no signature needed. The senior lesson is twofold: **never let untrusted input decide how it is validated** (pin the algorithm server-side), and **the most dangerous bugs are in the verification logic, not the business logic**, because a flaw there silently defeats every downstream control. A perfect authorization layer behind a forgeable token protects nothing.

**The IDOR / broken-object-authorization breach.** Broken object-level authorization sits at or near the top of the OWASP API Security Top 10 because it is so common and so quietly catastrophic. The recurring public shape: an API authenticates users correctly but checks only "are you logged in?" not "do you own this object," so incrementing or guessing an ID in a URL or request body exposes other users' records — orders, messages, documents, personal data — at scale. The breaches are large precisely because they are *automatable*: one valid account plus a loop over IDs dumps the whole dataset. The senior lesson is the one from section 14: **authentication is not authorization, and object-level ownership must be re-checked on every single access, enforced at the data layer so no handler can forget it.** The fix is not clever; it is disciplined, applied everywhere, every time.

**The over-permissioned service and lateral movement.** A repeated post-mortem shape: a single component is compromised (a phished credential, an exploited dependency, an SSRF), and because the system trusted the network (perimeter model, section 11) and the compromised component held broad permissions (no least privilege, section 13), the attacker pivots freely — from the foothold to internal services that asked no questions of an "internal" caller, escalating until they reach the crown-jewel data. The trigger is small; the damage is total, and the amplifier is always the same two architectural choices: implicit network trust plus over-provisioned permissions. The senior lesson is that **zero trust plus least privilege is what converts a foothold into a contained incident instead of a total compromise** — if every hop had re-authenticated and every credential had been scoped, the attacker's pivot would have died at the first boundary that asked "prove you may do this."

**The secrets-in-git leak.** The most preventable breach of all: a credential — an API key, a cloud access key, a database password — committed to a git repository, public or private, and then found, often within minutes, by automated scanners that crawl public repos and trade leaked keys. Because git history is permanent, "deleting" the secret in a later commit does *nothing*; it lives in history forever, so the only real remediation is to *rotate the secret* (invalidate it) and scrub history, in that order. The shape repeats across companies of every size because it is so easy to do and so hard to undo. The senior lesson maps to section 12: **secrets never touch source control**, full stop — they live in a vault, they are short-lived where possible so a leak self-expires, and you run automated secret-scanning in CI to catch the commit *before* it lands. The 8,640x blast-radius reduction from short-lived credentials in section 15 is exactly what turns this from a five-alarm fire into a fifteen-minute exposure.

## 18. When to reach for this (and when not to)

Security controls are not free — each is code to maintain, latency to pay, and a source of friction for legitimate users — so a senior applies them proportionally to the actual risk, neither under-securing a sensitive system nor over-engineering a low-stakes one.

**Always, non-negotiably, for any system handling real user data or money:** separate authentication from authorization and enforce object-level authorization at the data layer; never trust the network for anything consequential; keep secrets out of code and config; and audit-log security-relevant actions. These are table stakes — shipping a multi-tenant or financial system without them is shipping a breach with a delay, and a senior treats their absence as a launch blocker, not a backlog item.

**Reach for the heavier machinery when the stakes justify it.** Full zero trust with mTLS everywhere earns its operational cost when you have a non-trivial internal service mesh and a breach would be consequential — for a three-service internal tool with ten trusted users, the per-hop verification overhead may exceed the risk it mitigates, and a simpler model with strong edge auth and least privilege is the right altitude. A dynamic-secrets vault earns its complexity when you have many services and credentials whose leak would be expensive; for a single app with one database, a well-protected secrets manager without full dynamic credentials may be proportionate. ABAC and ReBAC earn their complexity when context or sharing genuinely drives access; reaching for Zanzibar to express three roles is the over-engineering a senior avoids as deliberately as the under-engineering.

**The meta-rule is to match the control to the threat and the blast radius.** For every component, ask "what is the worst an attacker who owns this could do?" and invest in proportion to that answer. A control that does not reduce a real blast radius is complexity without security; a missing control on a path that could leak every customer's data is a structural flaw regardless of how unlikely the trigger feels. Security is risk management, not maximization — the goal is not the most controls, it is the *right* controls placed where the damage would otherwise be largest, which is exactly the blast-radius reasoning this whole post is built on. And as you weigh those controls, remember they are not the only architectural constraint competing for the budget — the same blast-radius and proportionality reasoning applies to spend, which is why [cost as a design constraint and FinOps](/blog/software-development/system-design/cost-as-a-design-constraint-finops) is the natural companion to this post.

## 19. Key takeaways

- **Authentication and authorization are different questions, and conflating them is the most common breach.** AuthN proves *who you are* once at the edge; authZ checks *what you may do* on *every action against the specific object*. Broken object-level authorization (IDOR) is what you get when you treat "authenticated" as "authorized" — re-check object ownership on every access, at the data layer.
- **Sessions trade a per-request lookup for instant revocation; tokens trade revocation for statelessness.** Neither is inherently more secure. The mature pattern is a hybrid: short-lived stateless access tokens on the hot path plus a revocable, stateful refresh token checked only at refresh.
- **JWTs bite in three places: pin the algorithm (kill `alg: none` and RS256/HS256 confusion), keep expiry short because you cannot revoke them, and never put secrets in the readable payload.** The most dangerous bugs live in verification logic, not business logic.
- **Choose your authorization model from the shape of your rules, not their count.** RBAC for coarse roles, ABAC when context drives access, ReBAC when a sharing graph does. Start with RBAC; reach for the powerful model only when a real requirement forces it, and centralize policy *definition* while keeping enforcement at the object.
- **Every trust boundary re-validates — including the internal ones.** Defense in depth assumes each layer will be bypassed and arranges that the next still stops the attacker. The confused-deputy and SSRF classes exist precisely because a privileged component faced untrusted input without re-validating at the boundary.
- **Zero trust replaces network trust with identity trust, and its whole payoff is blast-radius containment.** When every hop re-authenticates and re-authorizes, a compromised service cannot pivot freely — the breach is contained to one identity instead of expanding through the perimeter.
- **Secrets never live in code or plaintext config; the vault's real power is short-lived dynamic credentials.** Solve the secret-zero problem by anchoring the first identity in the platform's attestation, not a stored token. Short-lived credentials cut a leaked credential's exposure window from months to minutes — an 8,640x blast-radius reduction in the worked example.
- **Least privilege is blast-radius reduction made architectural.** Ask not just "what does this need?" but "what is the worst this can do if it leaks?" — the answers match, and the second makes the over-permissioned service the latent disaster it is.
- **Audit logging is the control you build for the incident you have not had yet.** Append-only, shipped to a separate write-once store, complete enough to answer "what did the attacker touch?" — the difference between a precise breach report and notifying every customer that their data *may* have leaked.
