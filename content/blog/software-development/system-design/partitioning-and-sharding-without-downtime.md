Every team that grows past a single database arrives at the same fork in the road, usually at 2 a.m., usually with a pager going off. The primary is at 90% CPU, writes are queuing, and adding a read replica did nothing because the problem is writes, not reads. Someone says the word: "we need to shard." And then the room goes quiet, because everyone knows that sharding is the architectural decision you can least afford to get wrong and the one that is hardest to undo. Pick the wrong partition key and you do not find out for six months — you find out when one shard is on fire and the other nineteen are idle, and the only fix is to re-partition the entire dataset while it is serving live traffic.

I want to be precise about what this post is and is not. The `database/` folder on this blog already deep-dives the *mechanisms*: how [partitioning and sharding actually divide a dataset](/blog/software-development/database/database-partitioning-and-sharding), how [consistent hashing maps keys to nodes with minimal movement](/blog/software-development/database/consistent-hashing-and-data-partitioning), and how [you reshard and rebalance a live system without downtime](/blog/software-development/database/live-resharding-and-rebalancing-without-downtime). This post is the *architect's decision layer* on top of those. Not how a hash ring is implemented, but how a senior *chooses* the scheme, *chooses* the key, *detects* a hot partition from raw load numbers, and *plans* a zero-downtime reshard so that when the design review happens the answer is already decided and defensible. The single most consequential decision in this entire domain — more than the scheme, more than the technology — is the partition key, and figure 1 frames why: the key sits between your dataset and every scheme, and it determines what stays cheap.

![A layered diagram showing a dataset feeding into a partition key, which in turn feeds the three partitioning schemes of hash, range, and directory](/imgs/blogs/partitioning-and-sharding-without-downtime-1.webp)

By the end of this post you should be able to do four concrete things. First, choose among hash, range, and directory partitioning by starting from the query you run most, not from the scheme that sounds most scalable. Second, pick a partition key that co-locates your common query on one shard, keeps shards even, and avoids cross-shard fan-out — and explain the trade-off when those three goals conflict. Third, look at a per-shard load dashboard, identify a hot partition, and apply the right mitigation (salting, splitting, a dedicated shard, or request coalescing) without guessing. Fourth, write down the exact steps and the time-and-cost of a zero-downtime reshard: dual-write, backfill, verify, cutover, with a rollback at every step. Let us build to all four.

## 1. Why we partition at all (and the line you cross)

Partitioning is what you do when one machine can no longer hold the data or serve the load, and replication has stopped helping. The distinction matters because the two scaling axes solve different problems and people reach for the wrong one constantly. Replication (covered in depth in [replication strategies and their failure modes](/blog/software-development/system-design/replication-strategies-and-their-failure-modes)) puts copies of the *same* data on multiple machines: it scales reads, gives you failover, and survives a node dying. It does nothing for write throughput — every write still goes to one leader — and it does nothing for storage, because every replica holds the whole dataset. Partitioning (sharding) puts *different* data on different machines: each shard holds a slice, so writes spread across shards and storage divides across them. You partition when you have outgrown a single machine's *write* capacity or its *storage* capacity, not its read capacity.

The honest version of "we need to shard" is a number. A single well-tuned Postgres or MySQL instance on modern hardware comfortably handles a few thousand to low tens of thousands of write transactions per second and a few terabytes of hot data before you are fighting the machine instead of using it. Those numbers move with hardware and workload, but the shape is stable: there is a ceiling, and below it sharding is a liability, not an asset. The first senior instinct is therefore *delay*. Sharding multiplies your operational surface area — every backup, every schema migration, every failover, every "find this user's data" debugging session now happens N times and sometimes across shards. A vertical bump (bigger box), a read replica, a cache in front of the hot read path, archiving cold rows to cheaper storage, and moving heavy analytics off the transactional database will buy you a year or two and cost a fraction of what sharding costs. Shard when you have *measured* that you are within striking distance of the single-node write or storage ceiling and the cheaper levers are exhausted — not because a diagram in a design doc said "scale horizontally."

When you do cross the line, partitioning splits along one of two dimensions, and conflating them causes real pain. *Horizontal* partitioning splits rows: shard 1 holds users A–M, shard 2 holds users N–Z; same schema, different rows. This is what "sharding" almost always means. *Vertical* partitioning splits columns or tables: the user's profile lives in one store, their billing in another, their activity log in a third. Vertical partitioning is really service decomposition wearing a database hat, and it is often the right first move — separating the high-write activity log from the low-write profile lets each scale on its own axis without the full cost of horizontal sharding. The rest of this post is about horizontal sharding, because that is the decision that demands a key.

## 2. The three schemes, and what each one costs

There are exactly three ways to map a row to a shard, and every sharding system is one of them or a hybrid. The choice is not about scale — all three scale — it is about *which query you keep cheap and which you make expensive*. Figure 1 stacked them; let us take each one seriously, because picking the scheme is the second decision after picking the key, and the two are entangled.

**Hash partitioning** computes a hash of the partition key and assigns the row to `hash(key) mod N` shards (or, better, to a position on a hash ring — more on that in section 5). The defining property is *even spread*: a good hash function scatters keys uniformly, so 200 million users land roughly 200/N million per shard with no clustering, regardless of whether user IDs are sequential, clumped, or adversarial. Even spread is the single most valuable property in sharding because it is what keeps every shard at roughly the same load, which is what lets you provision for the average instead of the peak. The price you pay is steep and specific: hashing *destroys order*. Adjacent keys (user 1000 and user 1001, or all events from last Tuesday) land on different shards by design, so a *range scan* — "give me all orders from January" or "the next 50 rows after this cursor" — becomes a query against every shard. Hash partitioning makes point lookups by key trivially cheap and range scans catastrophically expensive.

**Range partitioning** assigns rows to shards by contiguous key ranges: shard 1 holds timestamps Jan–Mar, shard 2 holds Apr–Jun, or shard 1 holds user IDs 1–10M, shard 2 holds 10M–20M. The defining property is the mirror image of hash: *range scans are cheap* because adjacent keys live together, so "all orders in January" hits exactly one shard, and ordered pagination is a single-shard sequential read. The price is the mirror image too: range partitioning is a *hot-spot factory*. If your key is a timestamp and your workload is "mostly recent data" (which describes nearly every real system — feeds, logs, orders, messages), then *all current writes* land on the single shard holding the latest range, and the other shards are cold archives. The newest range is always the hottest, so range-on-time guarantees a moving hot shard. Range partitioning wins when you genuinely need ordered scans and your access is *not* concentrated at one end of the range.

**Directory (lookup) partitioning** keeps an explicit map from key (or key-range) to shard — a lookup table you consult before every routed request. The defining property is *flexibility*: because the mapping is data, not a formula, you can place any key on any shard, move a single hot key to its own shard, split a range that got too big, and rebalance precisely without re-hashing the world. This is the model Vitess and many large-scale systems converge on because it gives operators surgical control. The price is an *extra hop and a critical dependency*: every request consults the directory first, so the directory must be fast (it lives in memory, heavily cached) and highly available (if it is down, you cannot route anything). You are trading a stateless formula for a stateful, must-always-be-up lookup service. Directory partitioning wins when you need operational control over placement and can afford to run a rock-solid routing tier.

Most mature systems are hybrids. A common and excellent pattern is *hash to a partition, range within it* — or, more precisely, hash the dataset into a fixed large number of logical partitions (say 4096) for even spread, then use a directory to map those logical partitions onto physical shards so you can rebalance by moving whole partitions. You get hash's even spread, directory's placement control, and you avoid range's hot-spot trap. We will return to this "fixed partitions" pattern in section 9 because it is the rebalancing strategy a senior reaches for first.

## 3. The partition key is the decision (everything else is detail)

Here is the claim I will defend for the rest of the post: the partition key is the most consequential decision in your system's data architecture, more consequential than the database product, the cloud, or the scheme. The reason is asymmetry. Almost everything else is reversible at moderate cost — you can change databases, add caches, swap hash for range — but changing the partition key means *moving every row to a different shard*, which is the single most expensive operation in distributed data. The key is the load-bearing wall. You can repaint the house, but you do not move that wall without a project.

A partition key has to satisfy three goals simultaneously, and the art is that they often conflict, so you are picking which one to compromise. Figure 2 will show what happens when you get the first goal wrong; for now, name the three.

![A before-and-after comparison showing a single hot celebrity key overwhelming one shard on the left and the same key salted across sixteen buckets to spread load on the right](/imgs/blogs/partitioning-and-sharding-without-downtime-2.webp)

**Goal 1: high cardinality and even distribution.** The key must have enough distinct values to spread across shards, and those values must be roughly uniform in *traffic*, not just in count. Cardinality alone is not enough — a `country` column has high enough cardinality but is wildly skewed (the US and India dwarf San Marino), so it makes a terrible key. `user_id` for a consumer app is usually excellent: millions of distinct values, and while some users are heavier than others, the distribution is broad. `tenant_id` for a B2B SaaS is dangerous, because tenant sizes follow a power law — one enterprise customer can be a thousand times bigger than the median, and that customer will melt a shard.

**Goal 2: co-location of the common query.** The key should be the value your most frequent query already filters on, so that query lands on a single shard. If 90% of your reads are "load everything for user X" and you shard by `user_id`, those reads are single-shard and fast. If you shard by `post_id` instead, "load everything for user X" becomes a scatter-gather across all shards. The senior move is to enumerate your top three queries by volume *before* choosing a key, and pick the key that makes the heaviest query single-shard. This is the optimization lens applied to sharding: co-locate the hot query.

**Goal 3: bounded partition size and isolation.** No single value of the key should grow without limit or attract unbounded traffic, because a partition is the unit you move during rebalancing, and an enormous partition cannot be split or moved gracefully. If you shard a chat app by `channel_id`, a channel with 5 million members and 10k messages/second is a single partition that cannot be divided — it will always live entirely on one shard and there is no key you can add to spread it. The fix is to choose a key with a natural ceiling, or to make a composite key (`channel_id + message_bucket`) so a hot channel can spread.

When the three goals conflict, you compromise deliberately. The classic conflict: even spread (goal 1) wants a hash key, but co-location (goal 2) wants the entity you query by, and isolation (goal 3) wants no unbounded value. A frequent resolution is a *composite key*: `(tenant_id, entity_id)` hashed together gives you spread across entities while keeping a tenant's data co-located when you query within a tenant. There is no key that is best on all three axes for all workloads — if there were, this would not be a senior decision. Figure 6 later in the post turns these goals into a decision tree you can walk.

A worked tension makes the compromise concrete. Suppose you are building the orders service for a B2B platform. Goal 2 screams "shard by `tenant_id`" because your heaviest query is "show this tenant's recent orders," and sharding by tenant makes it single-shard. But goal 1 and goal 3 both object: tenant sizes follow a brutal power law, so your three largest enterprise customers might each generate more orders than the bottom 10,000 tenants combined, and sharding by `tenant_id` puts each of those whales entirely on one shard — three melting shards and a fleet of idle ones. The senior resolution is not to abandon `tenant_id` (that would sacrifice your hot query) but to make it composite: shard by `(tenant_id, order_bucket)` where `order_bucket = hash(order_id) % 16`. A normal tenant's orders still cluster on a small number of shards (so "this tenant's orders" fans out to at most 16 buckets, cheaply), while a whale tenant's orders spread across 16 buckets and never melt a single shard. You traded a perfectly-single-shard read (one shard) for a bounded small fan-out (16 buckets) in exchange for surviving the whale. That is the shape of every good key decision: you do not get all three goals, so you find the composite that loses the *least* on the goal that matters least for your workload.

There is one more subtlety that separates seniors from the rest: the partition key and the *primary key* are not the same thing, and conflating them causes grief. The partition key decides *which shard*; the primary key decides *which row within that shard*. They often share a prefix (the partition key is the first component of the primary key) but the primary key usually carries more — a sort key for ordering within the shard, a uniqueness component. DynamoDB makes this explicit with its partition-key/sort-key split; Cassandra with its partition-key/clustering-key split. The architectural consequence: you choose the partition key for *distribution* and the sort/clustering key for *in-shard ordering and range scans*, which lets you have both even cross-shard spread (via the partition key) and cheap ordered scans *within* a partition (via the sort key). This is how a single design gets hash-like spread across shards and range-like scans within a shard — the hybrid from section 2, expressed as a composite primary key.

## 4. The hot-key problem: one viral item, twenty idle shards

The failure mode that humbles every sharding design is the *hot key* — also called the celebrity problem, the hot partition, or skew. The math of why it is so dangerous is simple and worth internalizing. If you have 20 shards and load is perfectly even, each shard sees 5% of traffic. But traffic is never perfectly even. A single viral tweet, a celebrity account, a product that goes on sale, a "thundering herd" of clients all reading the same configuration key — any of these concentrates a large fraction of total traffic onto the *one* key, which lives on *one* shard. Now that shard is at 90% CPU serving one key while the other 19 shards are at 3%. You cannot solve this by adding shards, because adding shards does not move the hot key off its single home. You have a capacity problem on one machine in a fleet that is 95% idle.

I have lived this incident more than once, and the shape is always the same. A dashboard shows aggregate QPS comfortably within fleet capacity, so nobody believes there is a capacity problem — but p99 latency is spiking and a fraction of requests are timing out. The tell is the *per-shard* dashboard: one shard's CPU and queue depth are pinned while the fleet average looks fine. Aggregate metrics hide hot keys completely. The first lesson of hot keys is operational: you must have per-shard, ideally per-key, load visibility, or you will be blind to the most common sharding failure.

There are four mitigations, and a senior reaches for them in roughly this order depending on the situation. Figure 2 above showed the first one.

**Key salting (spreading a hot key).** Append a small random or bucketed suffix to the hot key so it maps to many shards: instead of writing all of celebrity `user_123`'s data under `user_123`, write under `user_123#0` through `user_123#15`, spreading 16 ways. Writes now scatter across up to 16 shards, so no single shard takes the full load. The cost is on reads: to read the celebrity's full data you must now scatter-gather across all 16 salt buckets and merge. Salting trades a write hot-spot for a read fan-out, which is the right trade when the hot key is *write*-heavy (a counter, an append-only feed) and reads can tolerate the fan-out. You typically salt *only* the keys you have identified as hot, not every key — salting everything destroys co-location for the 99.99% of keys that were never hot.

**Splitting the hot partition.** If your system supports dynamic splitting (DynamoDB adaptive capacity, a directory-based system, Vitess), you split the partition containing the hot key so its neighbors move elsewhere and the hot key gets more of a shard to itself. This helps when the heat is spread across a *range* of keys (a popular range, like "today's orders") rather than concentrated on a single literal key — splitting cannot help a single key that is already alone on a shard, because there is nothing to split off.

**A dedicated shard for the whale.** When one key is permanently, predictably enormous — a single enterprise tenant that is 1000x the median, or a handful of celebrity accounts — the cleanest answer is to give it its own shard (or its own small fleet) and route its traffic there explicitly via the directory. This is isolation as a deliberate placement decision: the whale gets dedicated capacity, the long tail shares the general pool, and a whale's spike never touches a normal tenant. This is exactly why directory partitioning exists.

**Request coalescing and caching.** When the hot key is *read*-heavy and the value changes slowly (a popular product page, a viral post's content, a config value), the cheapest fix is to not hit the shard at all for most reads. Put a cache in front, and *coalesce* concurrent misses so that when 10,000 requests for the same key arrive in the same millisecond during a cache miss, exactly one of them hits the database and the other 9,999 wait for that single result. Coalescing (also called request collapsing or single-flight) turns a 10,000-QPS read hot-spot into a 1-QPS database read plus 9,999 cache hits. For read-heavy hot keys this is by far the highest-leverage mitigation, and it is covered more deeply in the [caching strategies post](/blog/software-development/system-design/caching-strategies-and-the-pitfalls-that-bite).

#### Worked example: detecting and fixing a hot partition from load numbers

You run a social feed sharded by `author_id` across 16 shards. Total write load is 80,000 writes/second, which should be 5,000 per shard. Your per-shard dashboard shows shards 0–14 at roughly 3,500–4,000 writes/second each, but shard 11 is at 38,000 writes/second and its CPU is pinned at 96% with a growing write queue and p99 latency climbing past 400ms while the fleet p99 is 25ms. Fifteen shards are healthy; one is melting.

Step one is detection, and the numbers already gave it to you: a single shard at roughly 8x the expected per-shard load with a queue building is the textbook signature of a hot key. Aggregate write load (80k) is well within a 16-shard fleet's capacity (16 shards could handle, say, 16 × 15k = 240k), so anyone looking only at the aggregate would see 33% utilization and conclude there is no problem. The per-shard view is what exposes it.

Step two is finding the key. You sample the writes hitting shard 11 (a `pg_stat_statements`-style top-keys query, or a sampled tap on the write path) and discover that 34,000 of the 38,000 writes/second are for a single `author_id` — a celebrity account that just posted something viral, and millions of fans are generating activity (likes, comments, reshares) all attributed to that author. One key, one shard, 34k writes/second.

Step three is the fix. Because this is a write-heavy hot key, salting is the right tool. You change the write path for *this author only* (identified by a hot-key list maintained from the per-key metrics) to write under `author_id#bucket` where `bucket = hash(activity_id) mod 32`, spreading the 34k writes/second across up to 32 buckets, roughly 1,060 writes/second each — comfortably absorbed by the fleet because the 32 buckets hash to many different shards. The read path for that author's aggregate activity now fans out to 32 buckets and merges, which adds maybe 5–10ms to those specific reads but eliminates the write hot-spot. You keep the salt active while the author is hot and can remove it (re-consolidate) once traffic normalizes. Result: shard 11 drops from 96% CPU to roughly 8%, fleet p99 returns to 25ms, and you did it with a targeted change to one author's keys, not a fleet-wide reshard.

The senior lesson buried here: you fixed a single melting shard by changing how *one key* is written, not by adding hardware and not by re-partitioning. Hot keys are a *key-placement* problem, and the cheapest fixes operate on keys, not on the fleet.

## 5. Consistent hashing: rebalancing with minimal movement

The naive way to assign a key to one of N shards is `hash(key) mod N`. It spreads evenly and it is dead simple, and it has a fatal flaw the moment you change N: going from 4 shards to 5 changes the modulus, which changes the assignment of *almost every key* — roughly N-1 out of N keys move. Adding one machine triggers a near-total reshuffle, which means a flood of data movement and cache invalidation precisely when you are trying to add capacity (usually because you are already under load). `mod N` makes the one operation you need — adding a shard — maximally expensive.

Consistent hashing fixes this. The full mechanism lives in the [consistent hashing deep-dive](/blog/software-development/database/consistent-hashing-and-data-partitioning); here is the architect's version of why it matters. Place both shards and keys on a ring of hash values (0 to 2³²). A key belongs to the first shard you reach going clockwise from the key's position. When you add a shard, it claims one arc of the ring, and *only the keys in that arc* move — roughly 1/N of the keys, not N-1/N. When you remove a shard, only its arc's keys move, to the next shard clockwise. Adding or removing a node touches a small slice instead of the whole dataset. Figure 4 shows the ring with virtual nodes.

![A ring diagram showing a key hashing onto a hash ring where shards A and B own arcs through virtual nodes, while shard C is leaving and only its arcs get reassigned](/imgs/blogs/partitioning-and-sharding-without-downtime-4.webp)

There is one more refinement that figure 4 includes and that you must not skip in practice: *virtual nodes*. A plain consistent-hashing ring with one point per physical shard distributes arcs unevenly — by luck, one shard might own a 15% arc and another a 3% arc, recreating skew. The fix is to give each physical shard many positions on the ring (commonly 100–256 virtual nodes per physical shard). With 256 virtual nodes each, the law of large numbers smooths the arc sizes so every physical shard owns close to its fair share, and when a shard leaves, its load redistributes *evenly* across all remaining shards (because its 256 arcs are scattered all around the ring) rather than dumping entirely onto its single clockwise neighbor. Virtual nodes are what make consistent hashing both even and minimal-movement; a ring without them is neither reliably.

The architect's takeaway: consistent hashing (with virtual nodes) is the default rebalancing mechanism for hash-partitioned systems because it bounds data movement to roughly 1/N per node change instead of nearly all of it. The competing strategy — a *fixed* large number of logical partitions mapped to physical shards via a directory — achieves the same goal differently (move whole partitions, never re-hash) and is what Vitess, Kafka, and many others actually use; section 9 weighs the two.

#### Worked example: cost of adding a shard, mod-N vs consistent hashing

You run a 10-shard cache holding 500 GB of data total (50 GB per shard), and you need to add a shard to bring it to 11 because you are running low on memory. Each shard is connected at 10 Gbps (1.25 GB/s) but you cap rebalance traffic at 20% of that — 250 MB/s — to avoid starving live traffic.

With `hash(key) mod N`, going from 10 to 11 shards changes the modulus from 10 to 11, and the fraction of keys whose assignment changes is roughly (N-1)/N relative to the new layout — empirically about 90% of keys move. That is 0.9 × 500 GB = 450 GB of data movement, at 250 MB/s that is 1,800 seconds, *30 minutes* of heavy cross-fleet copying, during which cache hit rates crater (the moved keys miss until they land), so your database behind the cache takes a brutal load spike for half an hour. Adding capacity *caused* an outage-grade event.

With consistent hashing, the new shard claims roughly 1/11 of the ring, so about 500 GB / 11 ≈ 45 GB moves — only from the neighbors whose arcs the new shard now bisects. At 250 MB/s that is 180 seconds, *3 minutes*, and only the keys in the moved arcs miss, so the hit-rate dip and database spike are an order of magnitude smaller. Same goal, 10x less data moved, 10x faster, 10x less collateral damage. This 10x is the entire reason consistent hashing exists, and it is why no serious system uses bare `mod N` for a fleet that changes size.

## 6. Choosing the key in practice: a decision you can walk

Let me make the key-choice operational. The starting point is always the same and it is counterintuitive to juniors: *do not start from the data model, start from the query workload.* List your top queries by volume and by latency-sensitivity, and let the heaviest, most latency-critical query dictate the key, because that is the query you most need to keep single-shard. Figure 6 turns this into a decision tree.

![A decision tree that starts from the dominant query and branches to a range key for scans, a hash key for even point lookups, or a composite key when one identifier is skewed](/imgs/blogs/partitioning-and-sharding-without-downtime-6.webp)

Walk the tree. **Is your dominant query a range or time scan?** If your bread-and-butter access is "give me the events between two timestamps" or "paginate this ordered list," you need order preserved, which points to a range key — but the tree's right child warns you to *watch the hot tail*, because a time range key concentrates writes on the newest shard (section 2). The mitigation is to make the range key composite: `(shard_bucket, timestamp)` where `shard_bucket` is a small hash, so recent writes spread across a handful of buckets while you keep most of the range-scan benefit within a bucket.

**Is your dominant query a point lookup by an entity id?** Most transactional workloads are here: "load user X's profile and their orders," "get this tenant's settings." Then you want a hash key on that entity id for even spread — *unless* one id is skewed. **Is one id skewed?** If entity sizes are uniform-ish (consumer `user_id`), a plain hash key is perfect: even spread and the common query is single-shard. If one id can be 100–1000x the others (a B2B `tenant_id`, a celebrity `author_id`), a plain hash key still puts that whale entirely on one shard, so you need a *composite key* — `(entity_id, sub_bucket)` — that lets a hot entity spread across buckets while normal entities stay co-located. The composite key is the senior's answer to "I need co-location *and* I have whales."

Two more rules that the tree does not show but that experienced engineers apply reflexively. First, *prefer a key the application already has at request time.* If routing requires a database lookup to find the partition key, you have added a hop to every request; the best keys are ones the client already carries (the logged-in `user_id`, the `tenant_id` from the subdomain). Second, *immutability.* The partition key should never change for a row, because changing it means moving the row to a different shard — an expensive, error-prone operation that most sharding layers do not support transactionally. Never shard by a mutable attribute like `status` or `region` that a row can transition between; shard by an immutable identity.

```sql
-- BAD partition key: low cardinality, skewed, and 'mostly recent' writes
-- all land on the newest shard. This is a hot-spot generator.
CREATE TABLE events (
  created_at  timestamptz,   -- range key on time = newest shard is always hot
  region      text,          -- low cardinality, wildly skewed
  payload     jsonb
) PARTITION BY RANGE (created_at);

-- BETTER: composite key spreads writes across buckets while keeping
-- per-entity reads single-shard. shard_bucket is a small hash of the
-- entity, so recent writes scatter and 'load entity X' stays local.
CREATE TABLE events_v2 (
  entity_id    bigint,                 -- the value the common query filters on
  shard_bucket smallint,               -- hash(entity_id) % 64, immutable
  created_at   timestamptz,
  payload      jsonb,
  PRIMARY KEY (shard_bucket, entity_id, created_at)
) PARTITION BY HASH (shard_bucket);    -- even spread across physical shards
```

The senior framing of key choice in one sentence: pick the immutable identifier your heaviest query already filters on, hash it for even spread, and add a sub-bucket only where you have whales — and if your heaviest query is a range scan, accept the hot-tail risk and mitigate it with a bucketed range key rather than pretending the risk is not there.

## 7. Cross-shard queries: the scatter-gather tax

The moment you shard, a category of query that used to be free becomes expensive: any query that does not filter on the partition key must touch *every* shard. This is scatter-gather (also called fan-out/fan-in), and its cost is the second great tax of sharding after hot keys. Figure 7 shows the shape and the trap.

![A fan-out graph where a coordinator sends a query to three shards in parallel, two return in roughly fourteen milliseconds while one straggler takes ninety-five, and the merge step must wait for the slowest](/imgs/blogs/partitioning-and-sharding-without-downtime-7.webp)

The trap that figure 7 highlights is *tail amplification*. When a coordinator fans out to N shards and must wait for all of them before merging and returning, the query's latency is the latency of the *slowest* shard, not the average. If each shard has a p99 of, say, 100ms (and a p50 of 15ms), then a single-shard query is fast 99% of the time — but a scatter-gather to 10 shards is fast only if *all 10* are fast, and the probability of all 10 being under their p50 is 0.5¹⁰ ≈ 0.1%. The math is unforgiving: with 10 shards each having a 1% chance of a slow response, the chance that *at least one* is slow is 1 − 0.99¹⁰ ≈ 9.6%, so nearly 10% of your scatter-gather queries hit a straggler and run slow. Fan-out *multiplies your exposure to the tail*. This is why a sharded system can have great single-shard p99 and terrible cross-shard p99 — they are different distributions.

There are three architect-level responses to the scatter-gather tax, in order of preference. The first and best is *avoid it by key design*: choose the partition key so your heaviest queries filter on it and stay single-shard (section 6). This is the optimization that pays forever. The second is *denormalize and pre-aggregate*: if you frequently need a cross-shard view (a global leaderboard, a per-user feed that aggregates many authors), maintain a separate, purpose-built copy partitioned the *other* way, or a materialized aggregate, so the cross-shard query becomes a single-shard read of the pre-built result. This is fundamentally CQRS — a read model shaped for the query — and it costs you write amplification and eventual-consistency lag in exchange for killing the fan-out. The third, when you genuinely must scatter, is to *bound the damage*: cap fan-out width, use a hedged-request or partial-result strategy (return after 9 of 10 shards respond, fill the straggler asynchronously), and set aggressive per-shard timeouts so one slow shard cannot hold the whole query hostage.

```python
import asyncio

async def scatter_gather(query, shards, per_shard_timeout=0.05, min_responses=None):
    """Fan out to all shards, but do not let one straggler hold the result hostage.
    Returns partial results if some shards time out (min_responses satisfied)."""
    min_responses = min_responses or len(shards)
    tasks = [asyncio.create_task(s.execute(query)) for s in shards]
    results, done_count = [], 0
    # gather with a hard per-shard timeout; a slow shard is dropped, not awaited forever
    for fut in asyncio.as_completed(tasks, timeout=per_shard_timeout):
        try:
            results.append(await fut)
            done_count += 1
            if done_count >= min_responses:   # enough shards answered; stop waiting
                break
        except (asyncio.TimeoutError, Exception):
            continue   # straggler dropped; reconcile asynchronously if needed
    for t in tasks:
        t.cancel()     # cancel the laggards so they do not pile up
    return merge(results)
```

The number to carry from this section: every shard you fan out to multiplies your probability of hitting a tail-latency event, so a senior treats *fan-out width as a cost to minimize*, not a free parallelism win. Size partitions and choose keys so the common path is single-shard, and pay the scatter-gather tax only on rare or asynchronous queries.

## 8. Cross-shard transactions: why you design them away

If scatter-gather *reads* are expensive, cross-shard *writes that must be atomic* are where sharded systems go to die. A single-shard transaction is a normal local ACID transaction — fast, atomic, isolated, with all the [isolation guarantees](/blog/software-development/database/isolation-levels-and-the-anomalies-they-prevent) the engine gives you. A transaction that must atomically modify rows on two different shards needs a distributed-commit protocol, and every option is worse than the local case. Figure 8 contrasts them.

![A before-and-after diagram contrasting a cross-shard transaction needing a two-phase-commit coordinator and held locks against a co-located write that commits as one local atomic transaction](/imgs/blogs/partitioning-and-sharding-without-downtime-8.webp)

The classic mechanism is *two-phase commit* (2PC): a coordinator asks every participating shard to *prepare* (do the work, hold the locks, promise you can commit), and once all vote yes, tells them all to *commit*. It is correct, but it has two properties that make seniors avoid it for high-throughput paths. First, it holds locks across a network round trip — every participant locks its rows from the prepare until the final commit, so a slow participant or a slow network stretches lock-hold time and tanks throughput under contention. Second, and worse, 2PC is *blocking*: if the coordinator crashes after participants have prepared but before they are told the outcome, the participants are stuck holding locks, unable to commit or abort, until the coordinator recovers. A blocking protocol on your write path is a latent availability hazard. 2PC is acceptable for low-frequency operations where correctness dominates and you can tolerate the latency and the rare stall, but it is the wrong tool for the hot path.

The senior responses, in order:

**Design the transaction onto one shard.** This is the real answer 90% of the time. If two rows must change atomically, *make them share a partition key* so they live on the same shard and the transaction is local. The order and its line-items, the account and its ledger entries, the user and their settings — co-locate them under the entity's key and the cross-shard transaction disappears. This is why goal 2 (co-location) in section 3 is not just about read performance; it is also about keeping transactions single-shard. Figure 8's "after" is exactly this move.

**Use a saga for cross-entity workflows.** When the operation genuinely spans entities that cannot be co-located (transfer money from user A on shard 1 to user B on shard 7), do not force atomicity across shards — instead model the workflow as a *saga*: a sequence of local transactions, each on one shard, with compensating transactions to undo earlier steps if a later one fails. Debit A (local txn, with an idempotency key), credit B (local txn), and if the credit fails, run the compensation that re-credits A. The saga gives up atomic isolation (there is a window where the money has left A but not arrived at B) in exchange for never blocking and never needing a distributed lock. You make that window safe with idempotency and a clear state machine. The full mechanism is in the [saga pattern deep-dive](/blog/software-development/database/saga-pattern-distributed-transactions); the architect's job is to recognize when a cross-shard operation should be a saga rather than a 2PC and to design the compensations.

**Use a NewSQL database if you truly need distributed ACID at scale.** Sometimes the workload genuinely requires many cross-shard atomic transactions and you cannot co-locate or saga your way out — a global financial ledger, an inventory system with cross-region atomic moves. That is precisely the workload [Spanner](/blog/software-development/database/spanner-truetime-and-external-consistency) and [CockroachDB](/blog/software-development/database/cockroachdb-distributed-sql-deep-dive) were built for: they implement distributed transactions with tuned, non-blocking-ish commit protocols and pay the latency cost so you do not implement 2PC yourself. The trade-off is that distributed-transaction latency (cross-region especially) is fundamentally higher than local, and you are buying a more complex, more expensive system. Reach for NewSQL when distributed ACID is a *core requirement*, not a convenience — and revisit whether better key design could have kept the transactions local first.

The rule: cross-shard transactions are a smell, and the first fix is almost always a better partition key that co-locates the rows. Only when entities genuinely cannot be co-located do you choose between a saga (give up atomicity, stay available) and distributed ACID (keep atomicity, pay latency and cost). Never reach for 2PC on a hot path by reflex.

## 9. Rebalancing strategies: fixed partitions vs dynamic splitting

Shards drift out of balance over time — data grows unevenly, some keys get hotter, you add hardware. *Rebalancing* is moving data between shards to restore balance, and how you do it depends on a choice you make at design time. Figure 9 lays out the strategies.

![A matrix comparing fixed partition count, dynamic splitting, and hash-mod-N rebalancing across how much moves when you add a node, balance quality, and operational complexity](/imgs/blogs/partitioning-and-sharding-without-downtime-9.webp)

**Hash mod N — the strategy to avoid.** As section 5 showed, `hash(key) mod N` rebalancing remaps almost every key when N changes. Figure 9's bottom row marks it `danger` for "move on add" because adding a node moves nearly everything. It is even and simple but brittle the instant the fleet resizes. Do not build new systems on it.

**Fixed partition count.** Decide up front on a large, fixed number of *logical* partitions — far more than you will ever have physical shards (e.g. 4096 partitions for a fleet that might grow to 100 shards). Hash keys into the 4096 partitions (this never changes), then map partitions to physical shards via a directory. To rebalance, you move *whole partitions* between shards — never re-hashing a key, just reassigning which shard owns a partition. Adding a shard means handing it some partitions from the busy shards; the data movement is exactly those partitions and nothing else. This is the strategy Vitess, Elasticsearch, Kafka (with its fixed partition count per topic), and many others use, and it is what a senior reaches for first because the ops are simple (move bucket X from shard 3 to shard 9) and balance is good as long as you picked enough partitions. The one rule: choose the partition count generously at the start, because *changing the partition count* is the expensive operation (it does require re-hashing) — so set it high enough that you never have to.

**Dynamic splitting.** Start with a few partitions and let the system *split* a partition automatically when it grows past a size or load threshold (and optionally *merge* small ones). This is the [Spanner](/blog/software-development/database/spanner-truetime-and-external-consistency)/[CockroachDB](/blog/software-development/database/cockroachdb-distributed-sql-deep-dive)/HBase/DynamoDB model: a hot or large range splits in two, and one half moves to a less-loaded node. The benefit is that balance is *automatic and adaptive* — you do not pre-guess the partition count, and the system responds to where the heat actually is by splitting that exact range. The cost is operational complexity: the split/merge machinery is intricate, splits have to happen online without dropping writes, and a poorly-tuned splitter can thrash (split, merge, split). Figure 9 marks dynamic splitting `best` for balance quality and `high` for ops complexity — that is exactly the trade. Reach for it when you cannot predict your hot spots and want the system to self-balance; accept that you are running a more complex engine.

The architect's default: *fixed partitions* (hash into many logical partitions, map to shards via directory, rebalance by moving whole partitions) is the right starting point for most teams because it gives you consistent-hashing-grade movement bounds with dead-simple operations. *Dynamic splitting* is what you adopt (or what your NewSQL database gives you) when hot spots are unpredictable and worth the operational sophistication to self-heal. Bare `mod N` is a teaching example, not a production strategy.

## 10. Resharding without downtime: the dual-write, backfill, cutover playbook

Eventually you will have to do the thing everyone fears: change the sharding of a *live* system — split shards, change the key, migrate from one scheme to another — while it serves traffic, with no maintenance window. This is the capstone skill, and it is entirely mechanical if you follow the playbook. The full operational mechanism is in [live resharding and rebalancing without downtime](/blog/software-development/database/live-resharding-and-rebalancing-without-downtime); here is the architect's plan and why each step is shaped the way it is. Figure 5 shows the sequence.

![A timeline of a zero-downtime reshard moving through deploy dual-write, backfill history, verify row parity, flip reads from one percent to one hundred, then stop old writes](/imgs/blogs/partitioning-and-sharding-without-downtime-5.webp)

The governing principle is *reversibility at every step*. You never do an irreversible operation while you are uncertain, and you always keep the old layout fully functional until the new one has proven itself in production. That is what makes it zero-downtime *and* zero-data-loss. The sequence:

**Step 1 — Dual-write.** Deploy code that writes every change to *both* the old layout and the new layout, synchronously or via an outbox, while continuing to *read only from the old* layout. From this moment, the new layout is kept current for all *new* writes — it just does not have the *historical* data yet, and nobody reads it. Dual-write is reversible: if anything looks wrong, you stop writing to the new layout and you have lost nothing, because reads never touched it. The one subtlety is making the dual-write reliable — if the new-layout write can fail independently, use the [transactional outbox / CDC pattern](/blog/software-development/database/change-data-capture-and-the-outbox-pattern) so the new layout is fed from the old layout's change stream and can never silently drift.

**Step 2 — Backfill.** Copy the *historical* data from the old layout into the new layout, in the background, throttled so it does not starve live traffic. This is the long step — it can run for hours or days on a large dataset — and the dual-write from step 1 is what makes it safe: while the backfill copies old rows, any row that changes is *also* being written live to the new layout, so you do not have to freeze writes. You backfill in batches with checkpoints so you can resume after an interruption, and you throttle to a fraction of capacity (the same 20%-of-bandwidth discipline from section 5). Backfill is reversible: it only writes to the not-yet-read new layout.

**Step 3 — Verify.** Before you let a single user read from the new layout, *prove it is correct*. Run a reconciliation that compares the old and new layouts row-by-row (or by checksums per partition) and reports any mismatch. This is the step teams skip and then regret, because a subtle dual-write bug (a field not copied, a type coercion, a missed delete) is invisible until a user sees wrong data — and verification catches it while you can still fix it with reads still on the old layout. You do not proceed until parity is clean. Verify is the gate.

**Step 4 — Cutover (the careful read flip).** Now, and only now, you move *reads* to the new layout — and you do it *gradually*. Flip 1% of reads to the new layout, watch error rates and latency and correctness, then 5%, 25%, 100%. At every percentage you are still dual-writing, so the old layout is current and the flip is instantly reversible: if the new layout misbehaves at 5%, you flip reads back to 0% with a config change and you are safe, because the old layout never stopped being correct. The gradual flip is what converts "big scary migration" into "a series of small reversible config changes." This is exactly the discipline of a feature flag applied to the data layer.

**Step 5 — Decommission.** After reads have run at 100% on the new layout long enough to trust it (hours to days, through at least one peak), stop dual-writing, stop writing to the old layout, and eventually retire it. This is the *only* irreversible step, and you take it last, deliberately, after the new layout has carried full production read+write traffic and proven itself.

```python
# Cutover read routing as a flag-controlled gradual flip. Dual-write stays ON
# at every step, so flipping read_pct back to 0 is an instant, safe rollback.
class ShardRouter:
    def __init__(self, old, new, read_pct_to_new=0.0):
        self.old, self.new = old, new
        self.read_pct = read_pct_to_new   # 0.0 -> 1.0, raised gradually

    def write(self, key, value):
        self.old.write(key, value)        # always write old (current until step 5)
        self.new.write(key, value)        # dual-write to new layout

    def read(self, key):
        # deterministic per-key routing so a given key reads consistently
        if (hash(key) % 10000) / 10000.0 < self.read_pct:
            return self.new.read(key)     # routed to NEW layout
        return self.old.read(key)         # still on OLD layout
```

#### Worked example: planning a zero-downtime reshard during peak season

You run an e-commerce orders database sharded by `order_id` (hash) across 8 shards holding 4 TB total (500 GB/shard). It is growing 30% per quarter, and you have realized the key is wrong: your heaviest query is "show this customer's order history," which currently scatter-gathers across all 8 shards (because orders are sharded by `order_id`, not `customer_id`), and that query is your p99 latency problem. You need to reshard from `order_id` to `customer_id` across 16 shards (250 GB/shard target) — and you cannot take downtime because it is the run-up to your peak season.

First, the time-and-cost estimate (this is where [back-of-the-envelope estimation](/blog/software-development/system-design/back-of-the-envelope-estimation-for-system-design) earns its keep). The backfill must copy 4 TB from the old layout to the new. If you throttle the backfill to 200 MB/s aggregate (to stay out of live traffic's way during the peak run-up), 4 TB / 200 MB/s = 20,000 seconds ≈ 5.5 hours of pure copy time — but with batching overhead, checkpointing, and verification reads interleaved, budget 2–3x, so plan for roughly 12–16 hours of backfill, comfortably an overnight job. The verify pass re-reads both layouts; checksumming 4 TB at, say, 400 MB/s read is another ~3 hours. Total elapsed: dual-write deploy (minutes), backfill (overnight), verify (a few hours), then the gradual read flip over a day of watching dashboards. Call it a 3-day operation end to end, with the risky steps overnight when traffic is low.

The cost is dominated by *temporary double storage and double writes*: during the migration you are storing the data twice (old 4 TB + new growing to 4 TB = ~8 TB) and every write hits both layouts (doubling write IOPS). So provision the new 16-shard fleet at full size before you start, budget for ~2x storage cost for the migration window, and ~2x write capacity. At cloud prices, 4 TB of extra fast storage for a week and double write IOPS is a few thousand dollars — trivial against the cost of doing it wrong.

Now the plan, mapped to the playbook. (1) Deploy dual-write: every order write goes to both the `order_id`-sharded old fleet and the `customer_id`-sharded new fleet; reads stay on old. (2) Backfill overnight: stream the 4 TB historical orders into the new fleet, re-keyed by `customer_id`, throttled, checkpointed. (3) Verify: reconcile order counts and checksums per customer between old and new; fix any dual-write gaps; do not proceed until clean. (4) Gradual cutover: flip the customer-history read to the new fleet at 1% → 5% → 25% → 100% over a day, watching that the single-shard customer-history query on the new layout shows the expected p99 drop (it should fall from a 10-shard scatter-gather tail to a single-shard read — the whole point of the migration). Other queries that filter by `order_id` now scatter-gather on the new layout, so confirm those are acceptable or keep them on the old layout's read path until you have a secondary index. (5) After a peak-traffic day at 100% with clean dashboards, stop dual-writing and decommission the old 8-shard fleet.

The senior judgment in this example: you would *not* start this migration *during* the peak — you would do it in the run-up, with the irreversible decommission deferred until *after* peak, so that if anything is even slightly off you still have the fully-functional old layout to fall back to during your highest-revenue window. The reversibility of every step is what lets you run a risky migration against a deadline. And note the payoff is measurable: a specific p99 drop on a specific query, which is how you justify the entire 3-day operation in the design review.

## 11. The optimization lens: co-locate, even out, parallelize

Step back from the individual decisions and the senior optimization framework for sharding is three objectives, often in tension, that you balance for *your* workload:

**Co-locate the common query.** The single highest-leverage optimization is choosing a key so your heaviest query lands on one shard (sections 3, 6, 7). A single-shard query is 5–50x cheaper in tail latency than a scatter-gather (section 7's tail-amplification math), and it keeps related transactions local (section 8). Measure this directly: the fraction of your top-N queries that are single-shard is the headline sharding-efficiency metric. If your hottest query is a scatter-gather, your key is wrong, full stop.

**Keep shards even.** A perfectly co-located design that puts a whale on one shard still melts (section 4). Evenness is what lets you provision for the average instead of the peak, which is a direct cost lever: if your shards are even within ±15%, you provision each at roughly fleet-average × 1.3 for headroom; if one shard is 8x the others, you must provision *every* shard for the hot one's peak or accept that one shard fails — either way you pay multiples more. Measure evenness as the ratio of max-shard load to mean-shard load; a healthy fleet keeps it under ~1.5, and anything over ~3 is a hot-key incident waiting to page you.

**Size partitions for parallelism.** Partition granularity is a tuning knob. Too few large partitions and you cannot spread load or rebalance smoothly (you cannot move a 500 GB partition gracefully, and you cannot parallelize work within it). Too many tiny partitions and you pay coordination overhead — more connections, more fan-out width, more metadata. The sweet spot is enough partitions that (a) the largest is small enough to move in minutes during rebalancing and (b) you have several partitions per physical shard so adding a shard can claim a few without splitting anything. The fixed-partition strategy (section 9) operationalizes this: pick a partition count that gives you, say, 20–50 partitions per shard at target scale, each sized to move quickly.

The measurement discipline ties it together. The four numbers a senior watches on a sharded fleet: *single-shard query fraction* (co-location — want it high for your hot queries), *max/mean shard load ratio* (evenness — want it under 1.5), *scatter-gather p99* (fan-out tail — watch it diverge from single-shard p99), and *rebalance data-movement per node change* (want it bounded to ~1/N, the consistent-hashing or fixed-partition guarantee). When any of these drifts, it points at a specific decision to revisit.

## 12. Stress-testing the design: what breaks at 10×, at a hot key, at a reshard

A design is only as good as how it fails, so a senior stress-tests every sharding plan against the three scenarios that actually break sharded systems.

**A hot key (one viral item).** You stress-tested this in section 4's worked example. The failure signature is a single shard pinned while the fleet is idle, invisible in aggregate metrics. The design must include: per-shard and per-key load visibility (you cannot fix what you cannot see), a hot-key detection mechanism (a top-keys sampler), and a *prepared mitigation* — typically a salting path that can be enabled per-key for write hot-spots and a request-coalescing cache for read hot-spots. The test: can you go from "shard 11 is on fire" to "fixed" in minutes by enabling a per-key mitigation, without a fleet reshard? If the only answer to a hot key is "reshard the whole fleet," the design fails the stress test.

**A shard hitting capacity (the 10× question).** Your fleet is at 40% utilization, and then a marketing campaign or organic growth pushes one shard — or the whole fleet — toward capacity. The design must answer: *how do I add capacity, and how much moves when I do?* If you are on bare `mod N`, adding a shard moves 90% of the data and triggers an outage-grade cache-miss storm (section 5's worked example) — the design fails the 10× test because growth itself is dangerous. If you are on consistent hashing with virtual nodes or fixed partitions, adding a shard moves ~1/N of the data in minutes, and the fleet scales smoothly — the design passes. The 10× stress test for sharding is fundamentally "is adding a shard cheap?", and the answer is determined entirely by your rebalancing strategy (section 9).

**A resharding during peak.** The hardest scenario: you must reshard *while* under high load, perhaps because you cannot wait for a quiet window. The design must support the dual-write/backfill/verify/cutover playbook (section 10) with *throttling* (so the backfill does not starve live traffic) and *reversibility* (so a problem at 5% read-flip rolls back instantly). The stress test: if the new layout shows a problem at the 25% read flip during peak, can you return to a fully-healthy state with a single config change in seconds? If yes (because dual-write kept the old layout current), the design passes. If the migration has any irreversible mid-step, it fails — you have bet your peak on a migration with no undo.

The meta-lesson across all three: a sharded design is *judged by its operability under failure*, not its elegance under sunshine. The three questions — can I fix a hot key without resharding? is adding a shard cheap? can I reshard reversibly under load? — are the design review you should run on yourself before someone runs it on you.

## 13. Case studies

**Instagram and the sharded ID.** Instagram's early scaling is the canonical lesson in *building sharding into the data model from the start*. They sharded their Postgres data across many logical shards (mapped onto fewer physical machines, so they could move logical shards between physical hosts as they grew — the fixed-partition pattern). The clever part was the ID scheme: rather than relying on a central ID generator (a bottleneck and a single point of failure) or random UUIDs (which destroy locality and bloat indexes), they generated 64-bit IDs that *encode the shard*: a timestamp in the high bits (for rough sortability and time-ordering), a shard id in the middle bits, and a per-shard auto-increment sequence in the low bits. Because the shard is baked into the ID, any service holding an object's ID can route directly to its shard with no lookup — co-location and routing solved in the identifier itself. The lesson for an architect: when you can encode the shard into your primary key, you eliminate the directory hop for the common case and you get time-ordering for free; design the ID scheme as part of the sharding decision, not after it.

**A hot-key incident (the celebrity write storm).** The pattern recurs across every large social and commerce platform: a single account or item goes viral, and the shard hosting its key saturates while the fleet is idle — exactly section 4's scenario. The publicly-discussed shape (across companies like Twitter/X with celebrity tweets, and any flash-sale commerce system) is that aggregate dashboards looked healthy while a single partition's queue grew and tail latency spiked, and the fix was per-key mitigation — salting the hot key's writes across buckets and/or fronting its reads with a coalescing cache — *not* adding fleet capacity, which would not have moved the hot key. The lesson: hot keys are a permanent operational reality at scale, not a bug to be fixed once; you build the detection and the per-key mitigation into the platform and treat hot-key handling as standing infrastructure.

**Vitess and resharding MySQL at scale (YouTube, Slack).** [Vitess](/blog/software-development/database/vitess-sharding-mysql-at-scale) is the sharding layer YouTube built on MySQL and that Slack (and many others) later adopted, and it is the production embodiment of nearly everything in this post. It uses the fixed-logical-partition model (keyspaces divided into shards by a sharding key, with a routing layer — VTGate — that knows the map), and its `VReplication`/`Reshard` workflow *is* the dual-write/backfill/verify/cutover playbook, automated: it sets up replication streams from source shards to target shards, copies historical data, continuously catches up live changes (the dual-write equivalent via MySQL's binlog), runs a `VDiff` consistency check (the verify step), and then switches traffic shard-by-shard with the ability to reverse. Slack's well-documented migration of its core databases onto Vitess took years precisely because they did it shard-by-shard, reversibly, with no downtime — the gradual, reversible cutover discipline applied at company scale. The lesson: you do not have to invent the resharding playbook; the mature tools encode it, and your job as an architect is to understand the playbook so you can operate the tool, reason about its time-and-cost, and know what to watch during the cutover.

**DynamoDB adaptive capacity (the platform that handles hot keys for you).** DynamoDB is interesting because Amazon spent years engineering away the very problems in this post so users would not have to. Early DynamoDB had a hard hot-partition problem: throughput was divided evenly across partitions, so a hot key throttled at its partition's fraction even if the table had spare capacity overall (section 4's math, enforced by the system). Amazon's response was *adaptive capacity* and later *automatic splitting of hot partitions* — the platform detects a hot partition and gives it more of the underlying capacity, and splits partitions that are large or hot, all transparently (section 9's dynamic-splitting strategy as a managed feature). The lesson for an architect choosing a datastore: managed platforms increasingly absorb hot-key and rebalancing complexity, which is a real reason to choose them — but the absorption is not magic, it has limits (a single literal key still cannot exceed one partition's ceiling), so you must still design a key with enough cardinality and avoid single-key whales even on a platform that "handles" hot partitions.

## 14. Trade-offs: the decision matrix

Every choice in this post comes down to naming what you gain, what you pay, and when it wins. Figure 3 is the scheme-level matrix; the table below makes the full decision explicit, because the heart of thinking like a senior is never recommending anything without naming its cost.

![A matrix comparing hash, range, and directory partitioning across even spread, range scans, hot-key resistance, rebalancing cost, and cross-shard cost](/imgs/blogs/partitioning-and-sharding-without-downtime-3.webp)

| Decision | You GAIN | You PAY | WHEN it wins |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hash partitioning** | Even spread, hot-key resistance, cheap point lookups | Range scans become scatter-gather; order destroyed | Point-lookup workloads (load entity X) with no range-scan need |
| **Range partitioning** | Cheap range/time scans, ordered pagination | The newest range is always hot; hot-spot factory | Range-scan workloads where access is NOT concentrated at one end |
| **Directory partitioning** | Surgical placement, easy rebalance, isolate whales | Extra hop; directory must be fast and always-up | You need operational control over placement (whales, hot ranges) |
| **Plain hash key** | Simplest even spread | A whale still lands entirely on one shard | Entity sizes are uniform-ish (consumer user_id) |
| **Composite key** | Co-location plus the ability to spread whales | More complex queries; partial-key reads fan out | You need co-location AND you have skewed/whale entities |
| **Salting a hot key** | Spreads a write hot-spot across buckets | Reads of that key now scatter-gather and merge | Write-heavy hot key; reads tolerate fan-out |
| **Request coalescing / cache** | Collapses a read hot-spot to ~1 DB read | Adds a cache tier; staleness window | Read-heavy hot key with slowly-changing value |
| **Co-locate (single-shard txn)** | Local ACID, no distributed commit | Requires rows to share a partition key | Related rows you can key together |
| **Saga (cross-entity)** | Never blocks, no distributed lock | Gives up atomic isolation; needs compensations | Entities that genuinely cannot be co-located |
| **2PC** | True cross-shard atomicity | Blocking; holds locks across the network | Low-frequency operations where correctness dominates |
| **Consistent hashing** | ~1/N data moves on node change | Needs virtual nodes to stay even | Hash-partitioned fleets that resize often |
| **Fixed partitions** | Move whole buckets; simple ops; bounded movement | Must pick partition count generously up front | Most teams' default rebalancing strategy |
| **Dynamic splitting** | Automatic, adaptive balance | High operational complexity; split/merge machinery | Unpredictable hot spots; willing to run a complex engine |

The single most important row in this table is the one you build everything else around: choosing the *key* (plain hash vs composite) determines co-location, which determines whether your common query is single-shard, which determines your tail latency and whether your transactions are local. Get the key right and most of the other rows become easy. Get it wrong and you are looking at the reshard playbook in section 10.

## 15. When to reach for sharding (and when not to)

Sharding is a powerful, expensive, hard-to-reverse tool, so the decision of *whether* to shard is as senior as the decision of *how*.

**Reach for sharding when** you have *measured* that you are approaching a single node's write-throughput ceiling (tens of thousands of write TPS) or storage ceiling (single-digit TB of hot data) *and* the cheaper levers are exhausted: you have already added read replicas, fronted hot reads with a cache, archived cold data, and moved analytics off the transactional path. Shard when the bottleneck is genuinely *writes or storage on one machine*, because that is the only thing sharding fixes that replication and caching cannot.

**Do not reach for sharding when** your bottleneck is reads (add replicas and a cache first — far cheaper, far less operational pain), when you are below the single-node ceiling (a bigger box buys you a year for a fraction of the cost and complexity), or when you have not yet enumerated your query workload (sharding before you know your hot query means you will pick the wrong key). And be especially wary of sharding *prematurely* because a design doc said to "build for scale" — premature sharding multiplies your operational surface (N-times the backups, migrations, failovers, and cross-shard debugging) for scale you do not have, and the wrong-key decision you make under no real pressure is the one you will pay to undo later. The cheapest shard is the one you delayed until you actually needed it and chose the key with real workload data in hand.

**Choose your scheme by the query, not the buzzword.** Hash for point lookups, range only when you truly need ordered scans and access is not concentrated at one end, directory when you need placement control. Choose your key to co-locate the heaviest query, spread evenly, and avoid whales — composite when those conflict. Choose your rebalancing strategy for cheap node addition — fixed partitions as the default, dynamic splitting when hot spots are unpredictable, never bare `mod N`. And when you must reshard, follow the reversible dual-write/backfill/verify/cutover playbook and defer the only irreversible step until the new layout has carried full production traffic through a peak.

## 16. Key takeaways

- **The partition key is the most consequential decision in your data architecture** because changing it means moving every row. Choose the immutable identifier your heaviest query filters on, hash it for even spread, and add a sub-bucket only where you have whales.
- **Start from the query workload, not the data model.** Enumerate your top queries by volume before choosing a key, and pick the key that makes the heaviest, most latency-sensitive query single-shard.
- **Hash buys even spread and kills range scans; range buys scans and breeds hot spots; directory buys placement control and costs a hop.** No scheme wins every axis — choose by which query you must keep cheap.
- **Hot keys are a permanent operational reality, not a one-time bug.** Aggregate metrics hide them; you need per-shard and per-key visibility. Fix write hot-spots with salting, read hot-spots with coalescing, predictable whales with a dedicated shard — never by adding fleet capacity.
- **Fan-out is a cost to minimize.** Every shard a query touches multiplies its exposure to the tail, so a non-key query's p99 tracks the slowest shard. Co-locate the common query so it stays single-shard, and pay the scatter-gather tax only on rare or asynchronous queries.
- **Cross-shard atomic transactions are a smell.** The first fix is a better key that co-locates the rows; the second is a saga (give up atomicity, stay available); 2PC is for rare correctness-critical operations only, never the hot path.
- **Never resize with bare `hash mod N`** — it moves ~90% of the data per node change. Use consistent hashing with virtual nodes or fixed logical partitions so adding a shard moves ~1/N.
- **Reshard with the reversible playbook: dual-write → backfill → verify → gradual cutover → decommission.** Keep the old layout current and reads reversible until the new layout has proven itself in production; take the one irreversible step last.
- **Judge a sharded design by its operability under failure:** can you fix a hot key without resharding, is adding a shard cheap, can you reshard reversibly under load? If any answer is no, the design is not done.
