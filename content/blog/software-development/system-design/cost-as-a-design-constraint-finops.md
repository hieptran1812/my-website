There is a design review I have sat in more than once, and it always goes the same way. An engineer presents an architecture that is, by every functional measure, excellent. The latency numbers are good. The availability story is airtight. The data model is clean, the failure modes are handled, the diagram is beautiful. Then someone — usually the person who has been on call when the cloud bill arrived — asks one question: "What does this cost to run at the traffic we're forecasting?" And the room goes quiet, because nobody computed it. The design works. It just might cost four hundred thousand dollars a year to operate a feature that generates eighty thousand dollars of revenue, and nobody noticed, because cost was not on the list of things the design had to satisfy.

That silence is the gap this post closes. A senior engineer treats dollars the same way they treat latency or availability: as a non-functional requirement with a number attached, a budget the design must live inside, and a metric you can measure, defend, and optimize. "It works" and "it works at a cost the business can bear" are two different bars, and the second one is the one that keeps the feature alive past the next budget review. The discipline that puts a dollar figure next to every architecture decision has a name now — FinOps — but you do not need the framework to practice the mindset. You need to understand the cloud cost model well enough to predict the bill before you build, and to know which knobs actually move it.

This post is the architect's decision layer on cost. We are not going to walk through the AWS pricing console line by line; prices change and that is what calculators are for. We are going to build the mental machinery: the three line items that make up almost every cloud bill (compute, storage, and the silent killer, data transfer), the metric that turns cost into something you can reason about on a whiteboard (cost per request, and its bigger sibling, unit economics), and the levers a senior reaches for when a bill is too high — knowing the savings, the latency or reliability you pay, the effort, and the risk of each. Figure 1 is where we start, because it makes the single most important point in the whole post: the line item you assume dominates the bill usually is not the one that does.

By the end you should be able to do four concrete things. Estimate the monthly bill for a service from its traffic, storage footprint, and data flow, and find the dominant line item. Compute cost per request and unit economics, and reason about whether your design makes those numbers scale sub-linearly or super-linearly as you grow. Pick the right cost lever for the dominant line item — right-sizing, spot, tiering, caching, co-location — and name what each one costs you. And defend the spend in a review the way you would defend a p99 budget, with numbers, a stress test, and an honest account of the trade-off. We will stress-test every design the way a finance review does: what happens to this bill at ten times the users, and is the answer "ten times" or "a hundred times"?

## 1. Cost is a non-functional requirement, not an afterthought

The cleanest way to internalize the FinOps mindset is to put cost on the same shelf as the other non-functional requirements you already respect. You would never ship a design without asking about its p99 latency, its availability target, or its blast radius under a region failure. Those are constraints the design must satisfy, and you reason about them up front, not after the system is live. Cost belongs on that shelf. A design that hits every latency SLO but blows the operating budget is exactly as broken as a design that is cheap but slow — it just fails a different review, often a quarter later, when someone finally reads the bill.

The reason cost gets treated as an afterthought is that the cloud removed the moment where you used to confront it. In the data-center era, capacity cost was a capital expense you negotiated months ahead: you bought servers, you racked them, the money left the building visibly, and someone with a budget signed for it. The cloud turned that into a variable operating expense that accrues silently by the second, and the bill arrives a month after the decision that caused it. The decoupling is the danger. The engineer who flips a config flag at 2 p.m. on a Tuesday does not see a dollar leave; the consequence shows up four weeks later as a line on a finance spreadsheet that nobody can attribute back to that flag. The whole practice of FinOps exists to re-couple the decision to the dollar — to put the cost signal close enough in time and ownership to the engineer making the choice that the choice gets made with cost in mind.

For an architect, that means cost enters the design at the same moment latency does. When you choose between a managed database and a self-hosted one, you are choosing a cost structure, not just an ops model. When you decide a request fans out to twelve microservices across availability zones, you have signed up for a data-transfer bill on every internal hop, whether or not you wrote it down. When you set log retention to "keep everything forever" because it was the default, you committed to a storage line item that grows without bound. None of these are accidents you discover later; they are decisions you can price at design time, if you know the model. The senior move is to do back-of-the-envelope cost math in the same breath as the back-of-the-envelope capacity math — and if you want the capacity half of that skill, the [back-of-the-envelope estimation post](/blog/software-development/system-design/back-of-the-envelope-estimation-for-system-design) is the companion to this one.

Let me be precise about what "cost as a requirement" looks like in practice, because vague good intentions do not survive a design review. It looks like a target: this service must cost no more than a fixed number of cents per thousand requests, or this feature's infrastructure must stay under a monthly cap, or our unit cost per active user must fall as we grow rather than rise. A target you can measure is a constraint the design must satisfy. A vibe — "let's be cost-conscious" — is not, and it will lose every argument against a shiny feature. The rest of this post is about how to set those targets and how to design to hit them.

## 2. The cloud cost model: three line items that explain almost everything

Strip away the hundreds of SKUs on a cloud bill and almost every dollar falls into one of three buckets: compute (the CPU, memory, and time you rent), storage (the bytes you keep, priced per gigabyte-month by how fast you can read them), and data transfer, usually called egress (the bytes you move, priced per gigabyte by how far they travel). Almost everything else — load balancers, managed databases, queues, serverless functions — is a repackaging of these three with a markup for the management. If you can estimate compute, storage, and egress for a design, you can estimate its bill to within a factor that is good enough to make decisions.

![A cost breakdown matrix showing four components with their compute, storage, and egress spend, where egress dominates for the object store row](/imgs/blogs/cost-as-a-design-constraint-finops-1.webp)

Figure 1 shows a realistic monthly breakdown for a modest service, and it makes the point that drives the whole post: the dominant line item is rarely the one you would have guessed. Look at the object-store row. Its storage cost is a tame six hundred dollars, but its egress — the cost of serving those bytes out to users and across regions — is thirty-four hundred, almost six times the storage it is built on. Look at the database row: its compute is the headline, but its egress, the cost of shipping query results and replication traffic across zones, is larger than the storage it manages. If you walked into this bill assuming "storage is cheap, compute is the cost," you would optimize the wrong thing and save almost nothing. The first discipline of FinOps is to break the bill down by line item before you touch any lever, because the breakdown tells you where the leverage is.

Let me walk each line item, because the pricing logic of each is different and the levers that move each are different.

**Compute** is priced by the resource-second: a virtual machine of a given size costs some number of dollars per hour it runs, and you pay for it whether or not it is doing useful work. The two facts that matter for an architect are that idle compute costs exactly as much as busy compute (an instance at five percent CPU bills the same as one at ninety-five), and that the price you pay per core-hour depends heavily on the *commitment* you make. On-demand is the most expensive way to buy a core; reserved capacity (committing to a one- or three-year term) is forty to sixty percent cheaper; spot capacity (renting the provider's spare cycles that can be reclaimed with little notice) is sixty to ninety percent cheaper. The compute line item is therefore as much about *how* you buy as how much you use, and we will spend a whole section on that.

**Storage** is priced per gigabyte-month, and the price varies by roughly two orders of magnitude depending on how fast you need to read the data back. Block storage on fast SSD attached to a running instance is the most expensive. Standard object storage is about ten times cheaper per gigabyte. Infrequent-access and cold tiers are cheaper still, and archive tiers — where retrieval takes minutes to hours — are cheaper than that by another order of magnitude. The architect's lever here is *lifecycle*: data is hot when it is new and cools as it ages, and the storage tier should cool with it. A bill that keeps three-year-old logs on hot storage is paying SSD prices for bytes nobody has read since the quarter they were written.

**Egress** is the silent killer, and it deserves the rest of this post's attention more than the other two combined, because it is the line item engineers most consistently fail to model. Data transfer is priced by where the bytes go. Traffic *into* the cloud is almost always free. Traffic *out to the internet* — serving responses to users, video, downloads — costs real money per gigabyte, often eight to twelve cents. Traffic *between regions* costs money. And the one that bites hardest because it is invisible in the architecture diagram: traffic *between availability zones within the same region* costs money too, typically a cent or two per gigabyte each way. That cross-AZ charge sounds trivial until you remember that a chatty microservice mesh moves the same request's data across zone boundaries dozens of times, and at scale a single cent per gigabyte multiplied by petabytes of internal chatter becomes a five-figure line item that no feature owner can explain.

The reason egress is the silent killer is structural. Compute and storage show up as resources you provisioned — you can see the instances and the buckets. Egress is a side effect of how your components *talk to each other*, and nothing in the architecture diagram has a price tag on the arrows. The bytes flowing between your service and your database, between your service and the one next to it, between your region and the user — those arrows are where the money quietly leaks, and they are exactly the part of the design that gets the least scrutiny. We will come back to this repeatedly, because more cost-optimization wins for an architect live in the arrows than in the boxes.

## 3. Cost per request: the metric that makes cost reasonable

You cannot optimize what you cannot measure, and a total monthly bill is too coarse to optimize against. The metric that turns cost into something you can reason about at a whiteboard is **cost per request** — the total cost to operate a service divided by the number of requests it served. It is the dollar analog of p99 latency: a single number per unit of work, comparable across designs, that you can attribute, trend, and attack. Once you have cost per request, a thousand abstract dollars becomes a concrete "this endpoint costs four-tenths of a cent to serve, and that one costs three cents, and here is why."

The power of cost per request is that it makes the *shape* of your cost curve visible. A total bill that doubled tells you nothing on its own — maybe traffic doubled and the cost is fine, or maybe traffic grew ten percent and something is badly wrong. Cost per request normalizes that away. If your traffic grew tenfold and your cost per request held flat, your design scales linearly and you are fine. If cost per request *fell* as traffic grew, you have a design with fixed overhead amortizing across more requests, which is the healthiest possible signal. If cost per request *rose* as traffic grew, you have a super-linear cost structure hiding in the architecture, and it is going to become a crisis precisely when you succeed.

#### Worked example: computing the monthly bill and finding the dominant line item

Let me make this concrete with a service we can fully price. Suppose we run an API that serves 500 requests per second on average, which is about 1.3 billion requests per month. Each request triggers a little compute, reads from a database, and returns a 40 KB JSON response to the user over the internet. We want the monthly bill and, more importantly, the dominant line item.

Compute first. Say we run 20 instances to handle the load with headroom, each costing \$0.20 per hour on-demand. That is 20 instances times \$0.20 times 720 hours per month, which is \$2,880 per month for compute. Add the database: a managed Postgres primary plus one replica, call it \$1,400 per month all-in. Compute-ish total: about \$4,300.

Storage next. The database holds 800 GB on fast storage at roughly \$0.10 per GB-month, which is \$80. We also keep 15 TB of object storage (uploaded files, backups) at \$0.023 per GB-month, which is 15,000 times \$0.023, about \$345. Storage total: roughly \$425. Notice how small storage is — this is typical, and it is why "storage is cheap" is a true instinct that leads you to ignore the line item that is *not* cheap.

Now egress, the one people forget. Each request returns 40 KB to the user over the internet. That is 1.3 billion requests times 40 KB, which is 52,000 GB — 52 TB — of internet egress per month. At \$0.09 per GB, that is 52,000 times \$0.09, which is **\$4,680 per month**, just to ship the responses out. The egress line item alone is *larger than all of compute*, and it was invisible until we did the byte math. Add cross-AZ chatter — say the service-to-database and service-to-service traffic moves another 30 TB across zone boundaries at \$0.01 per GB each way, about \$600 — and egress is comfortably the largest single category on the bill.

Total: roughly \$4,300 compute + \$425 storage + \$5,280 egress, about **\$10,000 per month**, with egress the dominant line item at over half. Cost per request: \$10,000 divided by 1.3 billion, about **\$0.0000077**, or 0.77 cents per thousand requests. Now we know what to attack. Shaving 20 percent off compute saves \$860. Shaving 20 percent off egress — by compressing responses, or putting a CDN in front so repeat bytes never leave the origin — saves over \$1,000 and is usually easier. The breakdown told us where the leverage is, and it was not where instinct pointed.

That worked example is the entire method in miniature: estimate each line item from the design's traffic and byte flow, find the dominant one, and aim the optimization there. Do this *before* you build, on the whiteboard, and the architecture changes you would make are cheap; do it after the bill arrives and they are a migration.

## 4. Unit economics: why cost must scale sub-linearly

Cost per request is the operational metric; **unit economics** is the business metric that decides whether the company survives your design. Unit economics is cost per *business unit* — cost per active user, cost per transaction, cost per gigabyte stored on a customer's behalf — and the rule that governs it is simple and unforgiving: as the business grows, the cost per unit must stay flat or fall. If it rises, you have built a business that gets *less* profitable the more it succeeds, and that is the kind of problem that does not announce itself until you are too big to fix it cheaply.

The intuition is about what grows with scale and what does not. Some costs are fixed — the control plane, the monitoring stack, the standby replica that exists whether you have one user or a million. Those costs amortize: spread across more users, the per-user share falls, which pushes unit cost *down* as you grow. Other costs are variable and scale with usage — the egress to serve each user, the compute per request. Those hold unit cost flat if they scale linearly. The danger is the third category: costs that scale *super-linearly*, growing faster than the user base, which push unit cost *up*. The most common super-linear cost in distributed systems is coordination overhead — the chatter between components that grows not with the number of users but with the number of *interactions between components*, which can grow quadratically as you add both users and services.

![A branching graph showing 10x user growth forking into a sub-linear path that falls and a super-linear path that rises sharply](/imgs/blogs/cost-as-a-design-constraint-finops-6.webp)

Figure 2 shows the fork that the same growth event produces depending on which side of this you are on. Start at the same place: ten times the users, with the same per-user traffic shape. If your cost is dominated by fixed overhead that amortizes, your cost per request *falls* — from four-tenths of a cent to two-tenths in the figure — and your total bill grows only sixfold for tenfold the users. That is the dream: you got cheaper per unit as you grew. But if your cost is dominated by per-call cross-AZ chatter that grows with the interaction count, your cost per request *rises* — to over a cent — and your bill grows twenty-eight-fold for the same tenfold user growth. Same growth, same traffic, wildly different bill, decided entirely by whether the architecture's dominant cost amortizes or compounds.

This is why "does cost grow ten times or a hundred times at ten-times scale" is the single most important stress test in this post, and we will apply it to every design. The answer is not a property of the cloud or the traffic — it is a property of *your architecture's cost structure*, and you choose it. A design where each user's requests are independent and served from cache scales sub-linearly. A design where serving each user requires a fan-out that touches a growing fraction of your services scales super-linearly. The senior instinct is to find the super-linear term in the cost model and either cap it, cache it, or co-locate it away before it becomes the whole bill.

## 5. The big cost levers: what each one saves and what it costs

Once you have broken the bill down and found the dominant line item, you reach for a lever. There are a handful of big ones, and a senior knows not just what each saves but what each *costs* — in latency, in reliability, in engineering effort, and in risk. A lever that saves money but adds a failure mode is not free, and presenting one without naming its cost is how you lose a design review to someone who has been burned by it.

![A tree of cost levers organized under the three bill line items of compute, storage, and egress](/imgs/blogs/cost-as-a-design-constraint-finops-3.webp)

Figure 3 organizes the levers under the three line items, and the structure is the point: each lever attaches to a line item, so the dominant line item chooses your first lever for you. If egress dominates, right-sizing your compute is the wrong first move no matter how satisfying — you reach for caching and co-location, the levers under egress. Let me walk the major ones.

**Right-sizing** is the most boring and most reliable lever: match the instance size to the actual resource usage instead of the size someone guessed during the initial deploy. Most fleets are over-provisioned because the original sizing was a cautious estimate that nobody ever revisited, and CPU and memory utilization graphs routinely show instances running at fifteen to thirty percent. Right-sizing to fit the real load typically cuts twenty to forty percent off compute, costs essentially nothing in latency or reliability if you leave sane headroom, takes low effort (read the utilization graph, change the instance type), and carries low risk. It is the first lever you pull because it is almost pure upside. The only way it bites is if you cut headroom so close that a traffic spike has no room, which is why right-sizing pairs with autoscaling.

**Autoscaling and scale-to-zero** attack the "idle compute costs the same as busy compute" problem by removing capacity when it is not needed. Autoscaling adds and removes instances to track load, so you are not paying for peak capacity twenty-four hours a day when peak only happens four hours a day. Scale-to-zero — common in serverless and increasingly in container platforms — takes it further: a service that gets no traffic runs no instances and costs nothing. The savings are large for spiky or low-duty-cycle workloads, thirty to seventy percent. The cost you pay is the cold start: when a scaled-to-zero service gets its first request, that request waits for an instance to spin up, which can add hundreds of milliseconds to seconds of latency. For a batch job nobody is waiting on, that is free money; for a user-facing API with a tight p99 budget, the cold-start tax may be unacceptable, and you keep a warm floor of instances. The lever's cost is latency variance, and you spend it where users will not feel it. The mechanics of *how* to autoscale safely — the signals, the lag, the thundering-herd traps — are the subject of the [capacity planning and autoscaling post](/blog/software-development/system-design/capacity-planning-and-autoscaling); here the point is just that autoscaling is a cost lever with a latency price tag.

**Spot instances** are the biggest single discount available — sixty to ninety percent off on-demand — and the biggest risk, because the provider can reclaim them with little notice. Spot capacity is the cloud's spare cycles, sold cheap on the condition that you give them back when a paying on-demand customer wants them. For fault-tolerant, interruptible work — batch processing, CI, stateless workers that can be killed and restarted, anything that checkpoints — spot is enormous savings for a manageable engineering cost (you must handle the reclaim signal gracefully). For stateful or latency-critical work that cannot tolerate a node vanishing mid-request, spot is a reliability risk you should not take. The lever's cost is interruption risk, and the discipline is to put only genuinely interruptible workloads on it.

**Caching** is a cost lever as much as a latency lever, and it attacks egress and database load simultaneously. A cache hit is a response that never queried the database (saving database compute and the cross-AZ egress to reach it) and, at the CDN tier, a byte that never left your origin (saving internet egress). A CDN in front of a content-heavy service can move ninety percent of the egress bill off your origin and onto the CDN's cheaper bulk-transfer pricing. The cost you pay is staleness and the operational complexity of invalidation — the cache is a second copy of your data with its own consistency problems. The full treatment of those problems lives in the [caching strategies post](/blog/software-development/system-design/caching-strategies-and-the-pitfalls-that-bite); from the cost angle, the headline is that caching is often the highest-leverage lever when egress or database load dominates, and it pays for itself many times over.

**Storage tiering** attacks the storage line item by moving cold data to cheaper tiers as it ages, and it is almost pure savings for data with a clear access-frequency decay. We will give it its own section because the tier ladder is worth seeing.

**Managed versus self-hosted** is the lever with the most expensive mistake on either side, because the cost it trades is not just dollars but engineer-time, and that gets its own section too.

![A matrix scoring six cost levers across savings, latency or reliability cost, effort, and risk](/imgs/blogs/cost-as-a-design-constraint-finops-5.webp)

Figure 4 scores these levers across the four dimensions a senior actually weighs: savings, the latency or reliability cost, the engineering effort, and the risk. The shape of the table is the lesson. Right-sizing is all green — high savings, no cost, low effort, low risk — which is why it is always the first move. Spot is the highest savings but carries real reliability cost and high risk, so it goes only where the workload can absorb interruption. Self-hosting can save money but pays in ops burden, high effort, and high risk, which is why "we'll run it ourselves to save money" so often turns out to be the most expensive decision in the room. Read the table as a priority queue: start with the high-savings, low-risk levers, and only descend to the high-risk ones when the dominant line item demands it.

## 6. Storage tiering: let data cool, and pay accordingly

Storage tiering deserves a close look because it is one of the rare levers that is almost pure savings if you get the access pattern right, and because the price gradient across tiers is steep enough that getting it wrong is expensive in the other direction. Data has a temperature. When a record is written, it is hot — likely to be read soon, and reads should be fast. As it ages, it cools — read less often, until eventually it is touched only by a compliance audit or a rare investigation. The storage tier should track the temperature, because the cloud charges roughly an order of magnitude more per gigabyte for each step up in retrieval speed.

![A five-layer storage tier stack from hot SSD to deep archive with prices falling roughly tenfold per tier](/imgs/blogs/cost-as-a-design-constraint-finops-4.webp)

Figure 5 shows the ladder with representative prices. Hot SSD block storage sits at the top at around twenty-three cents per gigabyte-month, with millisecond reads — this is where your live database lives, and you pay the premium for instant access. Standard object storage is roughly ten times cheaper at a couple of cents per gigabyte, still with millisecond access, which is where most application data and recent files belong. The infrequent-access tier is cheaper again, around a cent and a quarter, with the same fast reads but a small per-retrieval fee that makes it a bad deal for hot data and a great deal for data you read occasionally. Archive tiers drop to under half a cent, with retrieval measured in minutes; deep archive bottoms out near a tenth of a cent per gigabyte, with retrieval in hours. From top to bottom, that is more than a two-hundred-fold price spread for the same byte, decided entirely by how fast you need it back.

The architect's lever is the lifecycle policy: a rule that automatically moves objects down the ladder as they age. Keep the last thirty days on standard, move thirty-to-ninety-day data to infrequent access, push anything older than a year to archive, and let deep archive hold the compliance tail. The savings compound because most data is cold — in a typical system, the data written in the last week serves the overwhelming majority of reads, and everything older is dead weight paying hot prices for no reason. A logging or analytics store with no lifecycle policy is the single most common avoidable line item I see: terabytes of year-old logs sitting on standard storage that nobody will ever read, costing ten to fifty times what they would in archive.

#### Worked example: a logging store with and without lifecycle

Suppose a service generates 2 TB of logs per month and we retain them for three years. Without a lifecycle policy, all of it sits on standard object storage at \$0.023 per GB-month. At steady state after three years, we hold 72 TB (2 TB times 36 months), and the monthly storage bill is 72,000 GB times \$0.023, which is **\$1,656 per month**, growing until retention caps it.

Now add a lifecycle policy. The last month — 2 TB — stays hot on standard for fast querying during incidents: 2,000 times \$0.023, about \$46. The next two months (4 TB) go to infrequent access at \$0.0125: about \$50. Everything older than three months — 66 TB — goes to deep archive at \$0.001 per GB-month, where it is searchable for audits but slow to retrieve: 66,000 times \$0.001, which is **\$66**. Total with tiering: about **\$162 per month** versus \$1,656 without. That is a tenfold reduction, and it costs nothing in functionality — the recent logs you actually query during an incident are still hot and fast, and the old logs nobody reads are simply cheap. The only thing you gave up is fast retrieval of three-year-old data, which is a trade you will happily make, and which most systems make without anyone deciding to. The lesson: a missing lifecycle policy is a recurring monthly tax for a feature you do not use.

## 7. The arrows, not the boxes: data transfer is where the money leaks

I keep returning to egress because it is the line item where an architect has the most leverage and the least visibility, and the single highest-value habit you can build is to price the arrows in your diagram, not just the boxes. The compute and storage in a design are the boxes — you provisioned them, you can see them, they have obvious price tags. The egress is the arrows — the data flowing between components — and nothing in a standard architecture diagram tells you those arrows cost money. They do, and the cost depends entirely on which boundaries the bytes cross.

The boundary that bites hardest is the availability-zone boundary, because it is the most invisible and the most frequently crossed. Within a single region, a cloud provider runs multiple availability zones for fault isolation, and traffic between them costs money — typically a cent or two per gigabyte, often charged in both directions. The reason this becomes a huge bill is the modern microservice pattern: a single user request fans out to a dozen services, each of which calls a database and a couple of other services, and if those services are spread across zones for availability, every one of those internal hops crosses a billed boundary. The same request's data can cross zone lines twenty or thirty times, and you pay a cent per gigabyte each crossing. At a billion requests a month moving real payloads, that compounds into a five-figure line item that no single feature owner can explain, because no single feature is responsible — it is an emergent property of how the whole mesh is laid out.

![A before-and-after diagram contrasting a cross-zone chatty call chain against the same chain co-located in one zone](/imgs/blogs/cost-as-a-design-constraint-finops-2.webp)

Figure 6 shows the fix and its magnitude. On the left, a request chain of twenty internal hops is spread across availability zones, so each hop pays the cross-AZ tax both ways, and the internal egress alone runs nine thousand dollars a month. On the right, the same twenty-hop chain is *co-located* — the services that talk to each other most are pinned to the same availability zone, so the chatter stays intra-zone, where transfer is free. The internal egress for that chain drops to zero. We did not change the number of calls, the payload sizes, or the logic; we changed only *where the talking happens*, and we deleted the entire line item. This is the kind of win that lives in the arrows: invisible until you price them, and enormous once you do.

The architectural principle is **locality of expensive communication**: keep the components that talk to each other most either in the same process, the same host, or at least the same availability zone, so the chatter does not cross a billed boundary. This is in tension with availability — spreading services across zones is exactly how you survive a zone failure — which is why the real design is *zone-aware*: replicate for availability across zones, but route a given request's hot path to stay within one zone whenever possible, only crossing zones for the replication and failover paths that genuinely need it. Modern service meshes support topology-aware routing precisely so you can keep the common case local and pay the cross-zone cost only on failover. The senior move is to make zone-locality a routing default, not an accident.

Here is the kind of zone-aware routing configuration that turns the principle into a setting, using a Kubernetes service with topology-aware hints:

```yaml
apiVersion: v1
kind: Service
metadata:
  name: payments-api
  annotations:
    # prefer endpoints in the caller's own zone; cross-zone only on failover
    service.kubernetes.io/topology-mode: Auto
spec:
  selector:
    app: payments-api
  ports:
    - port: 8080
      targetPort: 8080
  # internalTrafficPolicy keeps node-local traffic on-node when possible
  internalTrafficPolicy: Cluster
```

That one annotation tells the platform to route a caller to a same-zone instance of `payments-api` when one is healthy, falling back to another zone only when the local one is unavailable. The common case stays intra-zone (free); the failover case crosses zones (paid, but rare). You have made locality the default and pushed the cross-zone cost onto the exception path, which is exactly where you want it. The same idea applies to databases: read from a same-zone replica rather than the cross-zone primary when the read can tolerate it, and you cut both latency and the cross-AZ transfer bill at once.

## 8. The decisions that quietly cost the most

Some architecture decisions cost a fortune not because they are dramatic but because they are defaults nobody revisited. These are the line items that show up on the bill with no owner, because the decision that caused them was never a decision — it was an unexamined assumption. A senior learns to hunt these out, because they are the cheapest wins available: pure waste, no functionality given up.

**Chatty cross-AZ microservices** we just covered — the arrows crossing zone boundaries dozens of times per request. This is the biggest of the quiet costs, and it is invisible because no diagram prices the arrows.

**Unbounded log and telemetry retention** is the second. Logs and metrics default to "keep them," and "keep them" with no lifecycle policy means hot storage forever. A system that logs verbosely and retains everything on standard storage accumulates terabytes that nobody queries, paying hot prices for cold data. The fix is a retention policy and a tiering policy, and the worked example in the tiering section shows the tenfold savings sitting on the table.

**Over-replication** is the third, and it is subtle because replication is *good* — you replicate for durability and availability. But every replica is a full copy of the data, paying full storage cost, plus the egress to keep it in sync. Three replicas across three zones is the standard for a reason, but five replicas because someone was nervous, or replicas in regions you do not actually serve, or keeping every replica on hot storage when some could be on cheaper tiers — these are durability you are not using, paid for monthly. The discipline is to replicate exactly as much as your durability and availability SLOs require and not one copy more, and to know that each additional copy is a recurring cost, not a one-time safety. The replication topology choices themselves — how many, where, sync or async — are the subject of the [replication strategies post](/blog/software-development/system-design/replication-strategies-and-their-failure-modes); the cost lens here is that each copy has a price tag.

**Idle non-production environments** are the fourth, and the most embarrassing when you find it. Development, staging, and test environments are often full-size copies of production that run twenty-four hours a day, seven days a week, even though engineers use them eight hours a day, five days a week. That is an environment running at full cost for the seventy-five percent of the week nobody is touching it. A scheduled scale-down — turn non-prod environments off at night and on weekends, or scale them to zero and spin up on demand — routinely cuts non-prod compute by sixty to seventy percent with zero impact on anyone, because nobody is using those resources during the hours you turn them off. This is free money that sits uncollected in most organizations because turning things off requires someone to own the decision, and ownership is exactly what these quiet costs lack.

The thread connecting all four is *defaults that grew without a decision*. Cross-AZ chatter is the default of spreading services for availability without pricing the arrows. Unbounded retention is the default of "keep the logs." Over-replication is the default of "more copies feel safer." Idle non-prod is the default of "leave it running." None of them is a feature anyone would defend if asked to price it. The FinOps habit is to periodically audit the bill for line items nobody can attribute to a feature, because those are almost always defaults masquerading as decisions, and they are the easiest cost to cut precisely because cutting them gives up nothing.

## 9. Compute pricing: on-demand, reserved, and spot as a portfolio

The compute line item is as much about *how you buy* as how much you use, and the senior approach is to treat your fleet as a portfolio of pricing models matched to workload classes, not a monolith bought one way. Buying every core on-demand is paying the rack rate for everything, including the steady baseline you will run for years and the interruptible batch that could run on spare cycles for a tenth the price. The optimization is to segment the fleet by its tolerance for commitment and interruption, and buy each segment the cheapest way it can safely use.

![A before-and-after diagram contrasting an all-on-demand fleet with a reserved-plus-spot blend that costs a third as much](/imgs/blogs/cost-as-a-design-constraint-finops-7.webp)

Figure 7 shows the portfolio approach against the naive one. On the left, a fleet of a hundred cores — forty cores of steady baseline running around the clock, sixty cores of bursty batch — bought entirely on-demand, costs eighteen thousand dollars a month at the flat rate. On the right, the same hundred cores, segmented: the forty-core baseline goes on a three-year reserved commitment at sixty percent off, because we know we will run that baseline for years; the sixty-core bursty batch goes on spot at eighty percent off, because batch is interruptible and we handle the reclaim gracefully. The blended bill drops to sixty-four hundred dollars a month — a sixty-five percent cut — with no change to the work being done, only to the *contract under which the cores are rented*. The latency-critical baseline is untouched; the batch absorbs the occasional spot reclaim because it checkpoints.

![A decision tree mapping a workload to its cheapest safe pricing model based on interruptibility and commitment horizon](/imgs/blogs/cost-as-a-design-constraint-finops-8.webp)

Figure 8 turns this into a decision procedure you can run on any workload. The first fork is the decisive one: can the work be interrupted? If yes — it is batch, it checkpoints, a vanished node costs only a restart — it goes on spot, the cheapest tier, and you accept the reclaim risk because the workload absorbs it. If no — it is online, latency-critical, stateful — then the second question is whether it is a steady baseline you will run for a known term. If yes, reserved capacity or a savings plan locks in forty to sixty percent off in exchange for the commitment. If the load is spiky or idle-heavy rather than steady, serverless or scale-to-zero matches cost to actual use. And only the truly unpredictable, volatile, can't-commit workload stays on on-demand, the most expensive tier, which is correct precisely because on-demand's value is flexibility and you pay for flexibility you actually need. The tree's logic is interruptibility first, commitment horizon second, and the leaves name the cheapest *safe* option for each combination — never the cheapest option absolutely, because the cheapest option for latency-critical stateful work would be spot, and spot would cost you an outage.

The portfolio mindset is the senior framing. You are not choosing one pricing model for the company; you are matching each workload class to the cheapest model it can safely use, and the savings come from the *matching*. The common mistake is buying everything one way — all on-demand because it is simplest, or all reserved because someone mandated a commitment — and either over-paying for the flexible parts or over-committing the volatile ones. Segment first, then buy each segment right.

## 10. Build versus buy: engineer-time is a cost line item

The managed-versus-self-hosted decision is where the most expensive cost mistakes get made, because the dollar comparison people run is almost always wrong — it compares the cloud sticker prices and ignores the largest cost in the equation, which is engineer-time. A managed database costs more per month than the raw instances you would run it on yourself. That gap is real and visible, and it tempts engineers into self-hosting to "save money." But the gap is not the whole cost. Running the database yourself means *your engineers* handle the upgrades, the backups, the failover testing, the security patches, the 3 a.m. page when a replica falls over. That labor is a cost — a large one — and it does not appear on the cloud bill, which is exactly why it gets left out of the comparison and exactly why the comparison comes out wrong.

The correct comparison is **total cost of ownership**, and it includes engineer-time at a fully loaded rate. A senior engineer's fully loaded cost — salary, benefits, overhead — is on the order of two hundred thousand dollars a year, which is roughly a hundred dollars an hour, give or take. If self-hosting a database saves three thousand dollars a month on the cloud bill but consumes thirty engineer-hours a month in operational toil, you saved thirty-six thousand a year in cloud spend and spent thirty-six thousand a year in labor, and you broke even — except you also took on the *risk* of running critical infrastructure yourself, the opportunity cost of those engineers not building features, and the long-tail cost of the one bad outage that self-hosting eventually produces. The managed service was cheaper once you priced the labor, and it was cheaper still once you priced the risk.

#### Worked example: managed versus self-hosted database TCO

Let me put real numbers on it. We need a production Postgres with a primary, a replica, automated backups, and point-in-time recovery. Two options.

Self-hosted: three instances (primary, replica, backup coordinator) at \$0.30 per hour each, which is 3 times \$0.30 times 720 hours, about **\$650 per month** in compute, plus \$200 in storage and backup egress, so roughly **\$850 per month** in cloud cost. Now the labor. Patching, backup verification, replication monitoring, failover drills, and the occasional incident realistically consume an engineer for about twenty hours a month at steady state — and far more in the months something breaks. Twenty hours times \$100 per hour is **\$2,000 per month** in labor. Add an honest expected cost for the rare bad outage — say one serious incident a year costing forty engineer-hours plus reputational and SLA damage, amortized to a few hundred dollars a month. True monthly TCO: roughly **\$3,150**.

Managed: the equivalent managed Postgres with a replica, automated backups, and point-in-time recovery costs around **\$2,400 per month** all-in on the cloud bill — clearly more than the \$850 self-hosted *cloud* cost. But the managed service handles the patching, backups, replication, and failover, so the engineer-time drops to maybe two hours a month of oversight, \$200 in labor. True monthly TCO: roughly **\$2,600**.

The managed option costs nearly three times more on the line item people look at (\$2,400 versus \$850) and yet is *cheaper in total* (\$2,600 versus \$3,150) once engineer-time is priced — and it is dramatically cheaper in risk and in opportunity cost, because those engineer-hours are now building product instead of babysitting a database. The lesson is not "always buy managed"; at large enough scale, where you have a dedicated platform team and the self-hosted unit cost falls because the labor amortizes across a huge fleet, self-hosting can win decisively. The lesson is that the comparison must include engineer-time, and that the intuition "self-hosting is cheaper" is true only on the line item that is not the whole cost. The choice of datastore *family* is a separate decision — the [choosing a datastore post](/blog/software-development/system-design/choosing-a-datastore-sql-nosql-newsql) covers that — but whichever family you pick, the managed-versus-self question gets answered on TCO, not on sticker price.

## 11. Cost observability: you cannot optimize a bill you cannot attribute

Everything in this post assumes you can *see* your costs broken down by service, team, and feature. In practice that is the hard part, because the default cloud bill is one giant number with no attribution, and a number you cannot attribute is a number you cannot optimize — you do not know which team to talk to, which feature to question, or which line item to attack. Cost observability is the discipline of making the bill legible, and it rests on three pillars: tagging, showback or chargeback, and anomaly detection.

**Tagging** is the foundation, and it has to be enforced, not requested. Every resource — every instance, bucket, database, load balancer — carries tags identifying the team, the service, the environment, and ideally the feature that owns it. With consistent tags, the bill can be sliced by any of those dimensions: this team spent this much, this service costs this per month, production versus non-prod, feature A versus feature B. Without tags, the bill is an undifferentiated blob and every cost conversation starts with weeks of forensic accounting. The senior move is to make tagging a deploy-time requirement enforced by policy — an untagged resource fails to provision — because retroactive tagging never happens and untagged spend is un-attributable spend, which is the same as un-optimizable spend.

**Showback and chargeback** turn attribution into accountability. Showback means each team *sees* its own cloud spend, broken out, every month — the bill is no longer a central mystery but a number each team owns. Chargeback goes further and actually charges the spend back to the team's budget. The behavioral effect is the whole point: when a team sees its own cost, the engineer who flipped that config flag now gets the bill, closing the loop between the decision and the dollar that the cloud broke open. Showback alone, without any chargeback, changes behavior dramatically, because most over-spend is not malice — it is invisibility, and making it visible is most of the fix.

**Anomaly detection** is the safety net for the costs that escape the planning process — the runaway bills. A config change, a retry storm, a forgotten autoscaling ceiling, a CDN that got bypassed, can quietly multiply a line item, and without an alert the multiplication runs for the entire billing period before anyone notices. Cost anomaly detection watches each line item's trend and alerts when one spikes beyond its normal band, the way you alert on a latency spike. The senior framing is that cost deserves the same observability as latency and errors — a dashboard, a trend, and an alert — because the cost of *not* watching is a month of runaway spend before the bill arrives.

![A timeline of a runaway egress incident building silently over a month until finance flags the overage](/imgs/blogs/cost-as-a-design-constraint-finops-9.webp)

Figure 9 is what happens when all three pillars are missing, told as an incident timeline. On day zero, someone flips a flag that bypasses the CDN — a routine change, no alarm. On day one, origin egress jumps tenfold, but quietly, because there is no anomaly alert watching egress. Through day nine the elevated spend accrues unnoticed. On day twenty-one, even when someone glances at the bill, the spend is *un-taggable* — it cannot be attributed to a team or feature, so it cannot be chased down. Not until day thirty-one, when finance closes the billing period, does anyone notice the hundred-and-forty-thousand-dollar overage, and only on day thirty-three is the alert added, the resource tagged, and the CDN restored. A full billing cycle of runaway spend, caused by a one-line change, invisible the entire time because there was no anomaly alert to catch the spike, no tag to attribute it, and no team seeing its own egress trend. Every pillar that was missing maps directly to a point in that timeline where the bleeding could have been stopped. Cost observability is not bureaucracy; it is the monitoring that turns a month-long silent leak into a same-day page.

## 12. Trade-offs: choosing a lever like a senior

Now let me pull the levers together into the decision the way it actually happens in a review, because knowing the levers is not the same as knowing which to pull first. The senior approach is mechanical: break the bill down, find the dominant line item, and pick the lever that attacks it with the best ratio of savings to risk. You do not start with the lever that is most fun or most discussed; you start with the line item that is largest, because optimizing anything else is rounding error.

Here is the decision laid out as the trade-off table that should be on the whiteboard in any cost review:

| Lever | What you gain | What you pay | When it wins |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Right-sizing | 20–40% off compute | Almost nothing, if you keep headroom | Always first — fleets are routinely over-provisioned |
| Autoscaling / scale-to-zero | 30–70% off spiky workloads | Cold-start latency on first request | Variable or low-duty-cycle load where users tolerate variance |
| Spot instances | 60–90% off compute | Interruption risk — node can vanish | Fault-tolerant, interruptible, checkpointed batch |
| Caching (incl. CDN) | Cuts egress and DB load | Staleness, invalidation complexity | Egress or read-heavy database load dominates |
| Storage tiering | 50–90% off cold storage | Slow retrieval of cold data | Data with a clear access-frequency decay |
| Co-locate cross-AZ chatter | Deletes internal egress | Tighter coupling to zone topology | Chatty internal mesh crosses zone boundaries |
| Compression | 30–80% off egress and storage | CPU to compress and decompress | Large payloads, egress-dominated bills |
| Reserved / savings plan | 40–60% off steady compute | Multi-year commitment | Predictable baseline you will run for years |
| Managed over self-hosted | Saves engineer-time and risk | Higher cloud sticker price | Small/mid team where labor dominates TCO |

The table is the heart of thinking like a senior about cost: every lever names what it gains, what it costs, and the condition under which it wins. Never recommend a lever without naming its cost — "let's use spot" is incomplete; "let's use spot for the batch tier because it's interruptible and saves eighty percent, accepting the reclaim risk we already handle" is a senior recommendation. The condition column is what turns the table from a menu into a decision: you do not pull the lever you like, you pull the lever whose winning condition matches your dominant line item.

The optimization discipline that underlies the whole table is three steps, and it is worth stating plainly because it is the actual method this entire post teaches. First, **measure cost per unit** — break the bill into line items and compute cost per request or cost per user so you have a number to attack and a baseline to measure the win against. Second, **attack the biggest line item** — pull the lever whose winning condition matches the dominant cost, because a fifty-percent cut to the largest item beats a ninety-percent cut to a small one. Third, **know when cheaper-but-slower wins** — sometimes the right answer is the deliberately less performant option, because a batch job does not need spot's reliability, an archive does not need SSD's speed, and a non-prod environment does not need to run at night. The senior move is not always to make things faster; it is to spend money only where the business value justifies it, and to let everything else be cheap.

That last point deserves emphasis because it runs against an engineer's instinct. We are trained to optimize for performance, and "cheaper but slower" feels like losing. But a huge fraction of cost waste is *over-performance*: paying for SSD speed on data accessed once a year, paying for on-demand reliability on a job that does not care, paying for a warm fleet to serve traffic that does not exist at 3 a.m. Recognizing where slower-and-cheaper is *correct* — not a compromise but the right design — is as much a senior skill as recognizing where fast-and-expensive is worth it. The cost-optimal design is not the cheapest possible and not the fastest possible; it spends generously where business value is high and ruthlessly little everywhere else.

## 13. Stress-testing the design: 10× growth, runaway egress, spot reclamation

A design's cost story is only as good as its behavior under stress, and the three stresses that matter for cost are growth, runaway spend, and the reliability events that your cheap-tier choices expose you to. Let me run each, because the stress test is where a plausible-looking cost design either holds or reveals the super-linear term hiding inside it.

**The 10× growth test.** The question is the one from the unit-economics section: at ten times the users, does the bill grow ten times, or a hundred times? Run it against the architecture. If serving each user requires a fan-out that grows with the number of services, or cross-AZ chatter that compounds, the bill grows super-linearly and ten-times users becomes thirty-times cost — exactly when success should be rewarding you, it punishes you. If each user's requests are independent, served from cache, and kept zone-local, the fixed overhead amortizes and ten-times users becomes six-times cost, because cost per request *fell*. The test forces you to find the super-linear term *before* growth exposes it. The fix is always the same shape: cap the fan-out, cache the repeated work, co-locate the chatter — turn the compounding term into an amortizing one. Do this on the whiteboard at design time and it is a routing config; discover it at 10× scale and it is a re-architecture under a finance deadline.

**The runaway-egress test.** Suppose a config change accidentally bypasses the CDN, or a bug puts a large payload in a tight retry loop, or a new client starts pulling full datasets it should paginate. Egress multiplies overnight. Does anything catch it? If the answer is "finance, when the bill closes a month later," your design has no cost observability and you have signed up for the Figure 9 incident. The design must include an egress anomaly alert and a cost ceiling, the way it includes a latency alert and a rate limit. The senior framing is that *cost is a thing that can go runaway, so it needs the same circuit-breakers as load* — an alert on the spike, a budget ceiling that pages, and ideally a kill switch on the line item that can quadruple. A design that can produce a runaway bill but cannot detect one is as incomplete as a service that can be overloaded but has no rate limiter.

**The spot-reclamation test.** You moved the batch tier to spot to save eighty percent. Now the provider reclaims a third of your spot nodes at once — which it can, and will, exactly when on-demand demand spikes. Does the batch survive? If the work is checkpointed and the orchestrator reschedules the interrupted tasks, the batch absorbs the reclaim and finishes a little later, and you keep the eighty-percent savings. If the work is *not* checkpointed — if a reclaimed node loses an hour of un-saved progress — then spot was a false economy that trades dollars for re-done work and missed deadlines. The test forces the precondition that makes spot safe: spot savings are real *only if* the workload is genuinely interruptible, and "genuinely interruptible" means checkpointed, idempotent, and reschedulable. Putting non-interruptible work on spot to save money is the cost equivalent of removing the safety rail because it was in the way — the savings are visible and the cost shows up exactly when you least want it. The idempotency that makes spot-interrupted work safe to retry is itself a design property worth getting right, and the [idempotency and exactly-once post](/blog/software-development/system-design/idempotency-and-exactly-once-by-design) covers how to build it.

The common thread across all three stress tests is that a cost design is not just the steady-state bill — it is how the bill behaves under growth, under accident, and under the failure modes your cheap-tier choices introduce. A design that is cheap at steady state but super-linear under growth, undetectable under runaway, or fragile under reclamation is not actually a good cost design; it is a cheap demo that becomes an expensive incident. Stress-test the dollars the way you stress-test the latency.

## 14. Case studies: egress surprises, spot wins, and a managed-versus-self decision

Patterns are easier to trust when you have seen them play out in production, so here are three grounded cases — one each for the egress surprise, the spot win, and the managed-versus-self decision — with the lesson each teaches.

**The egress-bill surprise: a startup serving media from origin.** A common and well-documented pattern across many companies is the team that builds a content-heavy product — images, video, large downloads — and serves it directly from origin object storage or origin compute, without a CDN, because in development it worked fine and the egress was trivial. Then the product gets popular, the traffic is real, and the bill arrives: the bulk of it is internet egress, often the single largest line item, dwarfing the compute and storage that the team was actually thinking about. The dollar figures vary, but the shape is consistent across published cost post-mortems: egress was the dominant, unanticipated line item, and it was unanticipated precisely because the architecture diagram showed boxes (storage, compute) and not the arrow to the user that carried all the bytes. The fix is almost always a CDN in front of the origin — repeat bytes are served from the edge at bulk-transfer rates, the origin serves each object once, and the egress line item drops by the cache-hit fraction, often ninety percent. The lesson: for any content-heavy service, price the egress arrow to users *first*, and put a CDN in front before the traffic is real, not after the bill is.

**The spot-instance win: large-scale batch and CI on interruptible capacity.** Organizations running large batch fleets — data processing, machine-learning training, continuous-integration farms — have repeatedly demonstrated that moving interruptible work to spot capacity cuts the compute bill by the better part of an order of magnitude. The published patterns share a recipe: the work is broken into idempotent, checkpointed units; an orchestrator (a batch scheduler, a CI system, a training framework with checkpointing) detects reclaimed nodes and reschedules their work onto fresh capacity; and the fleet is diversified across instance types and zones so a reclaim of one pool does not stall everything. With that recipe, spot delivers sixty to ninety percent savings on the compute line item with the only cost being slightly longer and more variable completion times, which batch work tolerates by definition. The lesson: spot is a massive, reliable saving *for genuinely interruptible work that checkpoints*, and the engineering investment that makes it safe — idempotent units, checkpoint-and-resume, diversified pools — pays for itself many times over. The failure cases are equally instructive: teams that put latency-critical or non-checkpointed work on spot to chase the discount learned that the reclaim event becomes an outage, which is why the interruptibility precondition is non-negotiable.

**The managed-versus-self decision: when self-hosting actually won, and when it lost.** The honest case here cuts both ways, because the TCO answer genuinely depends on scale. Many small and mid-sized teams have found, often the expensive way, that self-hosting databases or message brokers to save on the cloud sticker price was a false economy: the engineer-time consumed by patching, backups, failover, and incidents exceeded the cloud savings, and the one bad outage erased years of it. For those teams, moving to managed services *raised* the cloud bill and *lowered* the total cost of ownership, freeing the engineers who had been babysitting infrastructure to build product. But the inverse case is real too: at very large scale, where the data footprint is enormous and a dedicated platform team already exists, the per-unit cost of self-hosting falls because the fixed labor amortizes across a huge fleet, and the managed-service markup on petabytes becomes the larger number. Several large engineering organizations have publicly described moving *off* managed services onto self-hosted infrastructure at scale for exactly this reason, and saving substantially. The lesson is not a universal answer but a universal *method*: compute the total cost of ownership including engineer-time at both your current and projected scale, and let the crossover point — not the sticker price and not the dogma — decide. Small team: managed almost always wins on TCO. Hyperscale with a platform team: self-hosting can win. The method is constant; the answer moves with scale.

## 15. When to reach for the cost lens (and when not to)

Cost is a real constraint, but like every constraint it can be over-applied, and a senior knows when to spend engineering effort on cost optimization and when premature optimization of the bill is just a different waste. Here is the decisive guidance.

**Reach for the cost lens hard when** you are designing a system whose unit economics determine whether the business is viable — anything where cost per user or per transaction must beat a revenue number to make sense. Reach for it when the bill is already large enough that a percentage improvement is real money: at a hundred thousand dollars a month, a twenty-percent cut is a quarter-million a year, which pays for the engineering many times over. Reach for it when a single line item dominates and is obviously waste — unbounded retention, idle non-prod, cross-AZ chatter — because those are pure savings with nothing given up. And reach for it at design time *always*, in the lightweight form: doing the back-of-the-envelope cost math alongside the capacity math costs an hour and prevents the four-hundred-thousand-dollar feature nobody priced.

**Do not over-invest in cost optimization when** the bill is small relative to the engineering time it would take to optimize. Spending two engineer-weeks (call it twenty thousand dollars of loaded labor) to shave two hundred dollars a month off a bill is a negative-return project that will take eight years to break even; the engineers should be building product. Do not optimize cost at the expense of the reliability or latency the business actually needs — a cheaper design that misses the latency SLO or adds an outage mode is not cheaper once you price the SLA penalties and the incident. Do not micro-optimize a line item that is a rounding error while ignoring the dominant one, which is the most common cost-optimization mistake: teams love to tune the compute they understand and ignore the egress they do not. And do not let cost discipline become a culture of saying no to every spend — the goal is to spend *deliberately*, generously where value is high and ruthlessly little where it is not, not to minimize the bill at the expense of the product.

The synthesis is that cost is a first-class non-functional requirement, which means it gets weighed *against* the other requirements, not above them. A design that hits its latency, availability, and cost targets together is better than one that minimizes any single dimension at the expense of the others. The senior skill is holding all of them at once: the cheapest design that still hits the latency SLO and the availability target and the cost budget — that is the optimum, and finding it is the whole job. When you grow into multiple regions, the cost dimension gets a new axis entirely, because cross-region transfer and replicated footprint multiply the bill in ways that interact with availability — the [multi-region and geo-distribution post](/blog/software-development/system-design/multi-region-and-geo-distribution) picks up the cost story where this one's single-region treatment leaves off.

## 16. Key takeaways

- **Cost is a non-functional requirement with a number, not an afterthought.** Put a dollar target on the design the way you put a p99 target on it, and reason about it at design time, on the whiteboard, in the same breath as capacity. "It works" and "it works at a cost the business can bear" are different bars, and the second one keeps the feature alive.
- **Three line items explain almost every bill: compute, storage, and egress.** Break the bill down before you touch a lever, because the dominant line item is rarely the one instinct points to — and egress, the cost of the arrows in your diagram, is the silent killer engineers most consistently fail to model.
- **Cost per request and unit economics are the metrics that make cost reasonable.** Normalize the bill per unit of work, and watch whether the per-unit number falls (sub-linear, healthy) or rises (super-linear, a crisis waiting for your success) as you grow. Find the super-linear term and cap, cache, or co-locate it away.
- **Every lever names what it gains, what it costs, and when it wins.** Right-sizing first (pure upside), then autoscaling, caching, tiering, co-location, spot — matched to the dominant line item, never recommended without its cost. The optimization discipline is three steps: measure cost per unit, attack the biggest line item, and know when cheaper-but-slower is the right answer, not a compromise.
- **Engineer-time is a cost line item, so build-versus-buy is a TCO decision.** The sticker-price comparison is always wrong because it omits the largest cost — the labor to operate what you self-host. Small teams: managed almost always wins on total cost. The method is constant; the crossover moves with scale.
- **Co-locate expensive communication and let data cool.** Keep chatty components zone-local so the bytes do not cross billed boundaries, and let a lifecycle policy move cold data down the storage ladder — both are near-pure savings that give up nothing you use.
- **Cost needs the same observability as latency: tag, show back, and alert.** An untagged bill is an un-optimizable bill; a bill with no anomaly alert is a month-long silent leak waiting to happen. Make tagging a deploy-time requirement, show each team its own spend, and alert on the spike.
- **Stress-test the dollars.** Run the design against 10× growth (does cost grow 10× or 100×?), a runaway-egress accident (does anything catch it before finance does?), and a spot reclamation (does the batch survive?). A cheap steady-state bill that is super-linear, undetectable, or fragile under stress is a cheap demo that becomes an expensive incident.
