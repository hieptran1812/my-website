Two people open the same document and start typing at the same instant. One adds a word at the start of a sentence; the other deletes a word three paragraphs down. Neither of them is waiting for the other — that is the whole point of a collaborative editor, the illusion that you are alone with the document while in fact you are sharing it with anyone from one colleague to a hundred strangers in a public link. The hard part is not the typing. The hard part is that the two edits were composed against *different* versions of the document, arrive at the server in an order neither client predicted, and must somehow leave every screen showing the *same* final text. Get that wrong and a character lands in the wrong place, a deletion eats the wrong word, and — in the worst and most common naive failure — one person's edit is silently thrown away because the other person's save arrived a few milliseconds later. The user never sees an error. They just lose a sentence and never trust your product again.

Here is the claim this whole post rests on: **a collaborative editor is a distributed-systems problem wearing a text-editor costume, and the central decision is how concurrent edits to shared state converge without lost updates.** Everything else — the WebSocket transport, the blinking cursors, the snapshots, the offline mode — is supporting cast around that one question. There are two serious answers to it. *Operational transformation* (OT) transforms each concurrent operation against the others so that applying them in any order yields the same result; it is powerful, it is what Google Docs runs, and it effectively requires a central server to be the referee. *Conflict-free replicated data types* (CRDTs) give each character enough identity that concurrent operations *commute* — they converge no matter what order they apply, with no central authority needed — at the cost of carrying metadata on every character. Figure 1 frames the stakes before we go anywhere near the mechanics: a naive last-write-wins merge that drops an edit, versus a convergent merge that keeps both.

![Before and after comparison of a last-write-wins merge that drops one of two concurrent edits versus a convergent merge that keeps both edits and reaches the same final document on every replica](/imgs/blogs/design-a-collaborative-editor-1.webp)

By the end of this post you should be able to do five concrete things. First, explain *why* last-write-wins is the wrong default and what "convergence" actually requires. Second, reason through OT and CRDT well enough to pick one in a design review and defend the choice against the obvious objections — "isn't OT too complex?" and "don't CRDTs blow up memory?" Third, sketch the full architecture: the per-document collab server, the WebSocket fan-out to collaborators, the op-log and snapshot store, and the deliberately separate ephemeral channel for presence. Fourth, do the capacity math — documents, concurrent editors, ops per second, op-log growth — and use it to set the snapshot interval and the presence budget. Fifth, handle the three things that break naive designs: a hundred people editing one document, a long offline edit that reconnects, and collaborative undo, which is genuinely the hardest part of the whole system. The mechanism deep-dives for consistency, replication, and event logs live in their own posts in this series and in the database and message-queue folders; this post is the architect's decision layer that sits on top of them.

## 1. The core problem: concurrent edits to shared state

Strip away the editor and you are left with a brutally simple distributed-systems problem. Multiple replicas of a shared mutable value. Each replica accepts local mutations immediately — it has to, because a text editor that lags behind your keystrokes is unusable, and "immediately" means before the server has heard about the edit. So at any instant the replicas disagree. The system's only job is to make them *agree again*, eventually, on a single value, no matter what concurrent mutations happened in the meantime. This is exactly **eventual convergence**, and it is the same property a [leaderless replicated database](/blog/software-development/system-design/replication-strategies-and-their-failure-modes) needs after a partition heals. The difference is that a database can often resolve a conflict by picking a winner — last-writer-wins on a key is an acceptable, if lossy, policy for a shopping-cart field. For text, picking a winner is a catastrophe, because the "loser" is a paragraph a human wrote and expects to still be there.

Why is last-write-wins so wrong here? Because a document is not a single value you replace; it is a *sequence of positions*, and two edits almost never touch the same position. If I insert "Hello " at the front while you fix a typo at the end, there is no conflict at all in any meaningful sense — both edits should simply apply. Last-write-wins models the whole document as one opaque blob: whoever's save lands last overwrites the entire thing, including the other person's untouched changes. The edits did not actually conflict; the *representation* forced them to. The entire field of collaborative editing exists to replace "the document is one value" with "the document is a structure of independently-addressable positions, and edits to different positions never conflict." Once you accept that reframing, the problem narrows to a single hard case: what happens when two edits *do* touch overlapping positions, like two people inserting a character at the same index at the same time?

That hard case is where OT and CRDT earn their keep, and it is subtler than it looks. Suppose the shared text is `AB`, and I insert `X` at index 1 to make `AXB` while, concurrently, you insert `Y` at index 1 to make `AYB`. Both of us computed "index 1" against the original `AB`. The server receives both. If it naively applies mine then yours, it inserts `X` at 1 (`AXB`), then `Y` at 1 (`AYXB`). If it applies yours then mine, it gets `AXYB`. Those are *different documents*. Worse, your client already optimistically rendered `AYB` and now has to be told the truth is `AXYB` or `AYXB` — and whichever it is, my client must agree. The two requirements are now sharp: **convergence** (every replica ends at the identical string) and **intention preservation** (the `X` and the `Y` both survive, neither overwrites the other, and they land somewhere reasonable). Last-write-wins fails both. OT and CRDT each guarantee the first and work hard at the second.

It helps to name the three properties a correct solution must hold, because they become the rubric you grade any design against. **Convergence**: given the same set of operations, all replicas reach the same state regardless of the order or timing in which they received them. **Intention preservation**: the effect each user intended is visible in the final result — an insert inserts, a delete deletes, and neither silently disappears. **Causality preservation**: if operation B was composed by a user who had already seen operation A, then every replica applies A before B (you cannot delete a character that, from your point of view, has not been typed yet). Causality is where vector clocks and version vectors enter the picture; the [consistency-models guide](/blog/software-development/system-design/consistency-models-a-practical-guide-for-architects) works out the causal-consistency machinery that a collaborative editor leans on. Hold these three in your head and the rest of this post is mostly about how OT and CRDT each satisfy them with different costs.

## 2. The architecture at a glance

Before the deep dives, the shape of the system. A collaborative editor is, at the architecture level, three planes that we deliberately keep separate: a **live edit plane** that moves operations between editors in tens of milliseconds, a **durability plane** that persists every operation and periodically snapshots the document, and an **ephemeral presence plane** that carries cursors and selections and is allowed to lose data freely. Conflating these planes is the single most common design mistake, because each has wildly different requirements — the edit plane must never lose an op, the presence plane should never *persist* one. Figure 2 lays out the components and shows the two paths (edit and durability) branching out of the collab server.

![Architecture graph showing editor clients connecting through an L7 load balancer to a per-document collab server that broadcasts to peers, appends to an op-log, snapshots periodically to an object store, and pushes cursors to a separate ephemeral presence bus](/imgs/blogs/design-a-collaborative-editor-2.webp)

Walk the request path. An editor client holds a persistent **WebSocket** to a collab server. The connection is sticky: an [L7 load balancer](/blog/software-development/system-design/load-balancing-from-l4-to-l7) routes every connection for a given document to the *same* server instance, because the convergence logic for a document needs to live in one place — that is the per-document "room." When you type, the client applies the edit locally and optimistically (zero perceived latency), then sends the operation up the socket. The collab server is the referee: it sequences the operation, transforms it (OT) or integrates it (CRDT), appends it to the **op-log**, and **broadcasts** the result to every other socket in the room. Those clients apply it, and now everyone's screen matches. Off the hot path, a background process compacts the op-log into a **snapshot** every N operations and persists the snapshot blob to object storage. Cursors and selections take a completely different route: they flow through an in-memory **presence channel** that fans out to the room and never touches the op-log or the snapshot store.

The thing to internalize is *why the planes are split*. The edit plane is correctness-critical and append-only — it is the source of truth, and it looks exactly like an [event-sourced log](/blog/software-development/message-queue/event-sourcing-and-cqrs-with-an-event-log): the document is a *projection* built by replaying operations. The durability plane exists so a server crash or a cold document load does not require replaying millions of operations from the dawn of the document — snapshots bound the replay. The presence plane exists because cursors update at 60 Hz when someone drags a selection, and persisting that would generate orders of magnitude more writes than the actual edits, for data that is worthless one second later. A senior who has run one of these systems will tell you the presence plane is where the cost and the incidents hide, precisely because people forget it is high-frequency. We will size all three planes in the estimation section, but keep the picture in figure 2 in mind — every deep dive that follows is zooming into one box of it.

## 3. Operational transformation: transform ops against each other

Operational transformation answers the hard concurrent-insert case with a function. Recall the `AB` example: I insert `X` at index 1, you insert `Y` at index 1, both against the original. OT defines a **transform** function `T(op_a, op_b)` that takes my operation and *adjusts its position* to account for the fact that yours has already been applied. The server picks a canonical order — say it applies yours first, producing `AYB` — and then, instead of applying my "insert X at 1" verbatim (which would land before your `Y`), it transforms my op against yours: because your insert at index 1 shifted everything from index 1 onward to the right by one character, my insert at index 1 becomes "insert X at index 2," yielding `AYXB`. Meanwhile my client, which already shows `AXB`, receives your op transformed against mine — your "insert Y at 1" becomes "insert Y at 1" still (my X went in after, so it does not shift your position) — wait, that gives my client `AYXB` too only if the transform is defined symmetrically and consistently. That last clause is the entire difficulty of OT.

The property OT must guarantee is called **transformation property 1 (TP1)**: applying `op_a` then `T(op_b, op_a)` must yield the same document as applying `op_b` then `T(op_a, op_b)`. In words: no matter which operation each replica saw first, after transforming the other operation against it, both replicas converge. For a single pair of operations this is tractable to define and test. The trouble is that real editing produces *streams* of operations, and an operation arriving late may need to be transformed against a whole *sequence* of operations it never saw — which requires the transforms to compose correctly, a property called **TP2**. TP2 is notoriously hard; a number of published OT algorithms shipped with subtle TP2 bugs that only surfaced under specific three-way concurrent edits. This is the honest reason OT has a reputation for being treacherous: the *idea* is simple, but a *correct* implementation that handles all the n-way concurrent cases is genuinely difficult, and the difficulty is the kind that hides until a particular race occurs in production.

OT's structural consequence is that it strongly prefers a **central server**. The clean way to dodge TP2 is to never let the general case arise: route every operation through one authoritative server that assigns a global sequence number, and have each client transform only against the operations it has not yet acknowledged. Google's Jupiter / Wave model — the lineage behind Google Docs — does exactly this. The client keeps a queue of unacknowledged local ops; when a remote op arrives, it is transformed against that queue; when the server acks a local op, it leaves the queue. Because every op is linearized by the server, no client ever has to solve the full n-way transform; it only ever transforms against its own outstanding ops. This is elegant and it is why Google Docs feels rock-solid — but notice what it costs: the server is now *mandatory and stateful*, the single point that orders the world. If the server is unreachable you can keep typing locally, but you cannot converge with anyone until it comes back. For an online-only product that constraint is free; for an offline-first or peer-to-peer product it is a wall.

```python
# OT insert-against-insert transform (the TP1 core, simplified)
def transform_insert(op_a, op_b):
    # op = {"type":"insert", "pos": int, "char": str, "site": int}
    # Return op_a adjusted to apply *after* op_b has been applied.
    if op_b["pos"] < op_a["pos"]:
        # b inserted to the left of a -> a shifts right by one
        return {**op_a, "pos": op_a["pos"] + 1}
    if op_b["pos"] > op_a["pos"]:
        # b inserted to the right -> a is unaffected
        return op_a
    # Tie: both inserted at the same position. Break the tie
    # deterministically by site id so BOTH replicas agree on order.
    if op_a["site"] < op_b["site"]:
        return op_a               # a stays; b will shift right
    return {**op_a, "pos": op_a["pos"] + 1}
```

The tie-break on `site` in that snippet is the heart of convergence: when two inserts land at the same position, *some* total order must be imposed, and it must be a property both replicas can compute independently without talking to each other. Site id (a stable per-client identifier) does that. Notice that this is already the same trick CRDTs use — a deterministic total order on concurrent operations — just expressed as a runtime transform instead of as a baked-in identity. That observation, that OT and CRDT are two encodings of "impose a deterministic order on concurrent edits," is the cleanest way to hold both in your head at once.

## 4. CRDTs: make the operations commute

CRDTs attack the same problem from the opposite side. Instead of *transforming* operations at apply time so they converge, CRDTs design the data structure so that operations **commute** by construction — applying them in any order, any number of times, yields the same result, with no transform step and no central referee. For a text editor the relevant family is the **sequence CRDT**: a list where every element carries a globally-unique, totally-ordered *position identifier* that fixes where it sits relative to its neighbors forever, independent of indices. The insight is that array indices are the enemy — they shift when neighbors are inserted or deleted, which is exactly what forces OT to recompute positions. If instead each character owns a stable identity that never shifts, then "insert a character between these two identities" is an operation that means the same thing on every replica regardless of what else happened. Figure 4 shows two concurrent inserts resolving by their position ids into one converged string.

![Graph showing two sites concurrently inserting characters with dense position identifiers between stable neighbors, then both replicas sorting by position id and converging to the identical string](/imgs/blogs/design-a-collaborative-editor-4.webp)

How do you generate a unique position *between* any two existing positions, infinitely? This is the design that distinguishes the sequence-CRDT variants. **LSEQ** and **Logoot** use a fractional / variable-length identifier: between positions `0.3` and `0.4` you can always mint `0.35`, and when you run out of room you add another digit — the identifiers form a dense tree so there is always space between any two. **RGA** (Replicated Growable Array) takes a different tack: each character references the id of the character it was inserted *after*, plus a unique `(timestamp, site)` tag; concurrent inserts after the same character are ordered by their tags. **Yjs**, the most widely deployed text CRDT in production today, uses a doubly-linked-list structure (its YATA algorithm) with a clever block-encoding optimization that we will get to in the optimization section. All of them share the core property: a character's identity is independent of its index, so concurrent inserts and deletes commute and converge by *sorting on identity*, with the math *proving* convergence rather than tests merely failing to find a counterexample.

There is a subtlety in fractional position ids worth understanding because it is where naive CRDT implementations quietly degrade. When two clients repeatedly insert at the *same* spot — say both keep typing at the very front of the document — the fractional identifiers between two fixed neighbors get longer and longer (0.5, then 0.25, then 0.125, then 0.0625…), because each insert must fit *between* the previous one and its neighbor. In a pathological interleaving (two users alternately inserting at the boundary), identifier length can grow linearly with the number of inserts, and since every character carries its identifier, the metadata can balloon faster than the text. LSEQ specifically addresses this by randomizing the allocation strategy per depth so adversarial interleavings do not always hit the worst case, and RGA sidesteps it entirely by using fixed-size `(timestamp, site)` tags plus an "inserted-after" reference rather than dense fractions. A senior evaluating a CRDT library asks exactly this question — "how does your position scheme behave under adversarial concurrent insertion at one spot?" — because the answer separates a library that stays compact from one that bloats on a hostile or just unlucky edit pattern.

The price CRDTs pay is **metadata and tombstones**, and a senior names this cost out loud before anyone in the review asks. Every character needs its position identifier — at minimum a `(site, clock)` pair, often more for fractional schemes — so a one-byte character can carry tens of bytes of metadata. Worse, you cannot truly *delete* a character, because some other replica might still reference its identity as the "insert after" anchor for one of *its* concurrent inserts. So a delete becomes a **tombstone**: the character is marked dead but its identity-bearing husk remains in the structure forever, or until a garbage-collection pass can prove no live reference exists. A document that has had a million characters typed and deleted over its lifetime can carry a million tombstones, and the in-memory structure can be many times the size of the visible text. This is the central CRDT trade-off: you bought decentralization and provable convergence, and you are paying for it in memory and in the engineering of tombstone garbage collection. The optimization section is largely about making that bill smaller.

```javascript
// Yjs: a CRDT text document, no transform logic, no central referee.
import * as Y from 'yjs'

const doc = new Y.Doc()
const ytext = doc.getText('body')

// Concurrent edits applied as binary updates that COMMUTE:
ytext.insert(0, 'AB')                 // doc is "AB"
const update = Y.encodeStateAsUpdate(doc)

// Replica 2 starts from the same update, edits independently:
const doc2 = new Y.Doc()
Y.applyUpdate(doc2, update)
doc2.getText('body').insert(1, 'Y')   // "AYB" on replica 2

// Replica 1 edits at the same index, concurrently:
ytext.insert(1, 'X')                  // "AXB" on replica 1

// Exchange updates in EITHER order -> both converge identically:
Y.applyUpdate(doc,  Y.encodeStateAsUpdate(doc2))
Y.applyUpdate(doc2, Y.encodeStateAsUpdate(doc))
// doc.toString() === doc2.toString()  // guaranteed by construction
```

The thing to feel in that snippet is what is *absent*: there is no server in it, no transform function, no sequence number assigned by an authority. Two replicas exchanged binary updates and converged. That is the CRDT superpower and the reason it has eaten the offline-first and peer-to-peer corners of the market. The server, if there is one, degrades from "the referee that orders the world" (OT) to "a relay and a place to store the bytes" (CRDT) — it forwards updates and persists them, but it does not need to *understand* them to keep replicas consistent. That demotion of the server is the architectural decision hiding inside "OT vs CRDT," and it is why the choice cross-links so directly to [consistency models](/blog/software-development/system-design/consistency-models-a-practical-guide-for-architects) and [replication strategies](/blog/software-development/system-design/replication-strategies-and-their-failure-modes): you are really choosing between a leader-based and a leaderless replication model for the document.

## 5. OT vs CRDT: the central design decision

This is the decision a senior gets paid to make, so make it explicitly rather than by vibe. The two approaches converge to the same user-visible outcome — no lost edits, everyone sees the same document — but they distribute the cost completely differently, and the right choice depends on three product constraints: do you have (or want) a central server, do clients need to edit offline or sync peer-to-peer, and how large can a single document get. Figure 3 lays the trade-offs side by side as a matrix; spend a moment on it because every row is a real argument someone will make in your design review.

![Decision matrix comparing operational transformation and CRDT across central-server dependence, per-character metadata, offline and peer-to-peer sync, convergence guarantee, and undo difficulty](/imgs/blogs/design-a-collaborative-editor-3.webp)

Read the matrix as a set of trades, not a scoreboard. OT wins **metadata cost**: operations are tiny (a position and a character), the in-memory document is just the text, and there are no tombstones — a server can hold enormous documents cheaply. OT also wins **undo simplicity**, for a subtle reason we will dig into later: because the server linearizes all operations, "undo my last op" has a well-defined meaning. CRDT wins **server-dependence** (it does not need one), **offline and peer-to-peer** (native — two clients can sync directly), and **convergence confidence** (proven by construction rather than validated by a test suite you hope is exhaustive). The crossover question is almost always: *is being able to edit offline or without a central authority a real product requirement?* If yes, CRDT, full stop — OT's central-server dependence is a wall you cannot climb. If no — if your product is online-only and you are happy to run a server — then OT's leanness becomes attractive, *especially* for very large documents where CRDT tombstone overhead bites.

That said, the industry has been drifting toward CRDTs even for server-backed products, and it is worth understanding why, because it is not fashion. First, the metadata cost has come *way* down: modern CRDTs like Yjs encode runs of contiguous characters as a single block, so a paragraph typed left-to-right (the common case) costs roughly one block's worth of metadata, not one per character — turning the worst case into a rare case. Second, the *engineering* cost asymmetry is real: a correct CRDT is a library you import and largely trust, whereas a correct OT implementation is something your team owns and must get TP2-right forever, including every time you add a new operation type (rich text, embedded objects, comments). Third, CRDTs compose better with [event sourcing](/blog/software-development/message-queue/event-sourcing-and-cqrs-with-an-event-log) and with multi-region replication, because they tolerate out-of-order and delayed delivery natively. The honest summary: OT is not obsolete — it powers the largest deployed collaborative editor on earth — but for a *new* system the burden of proof has shifted, and you should reach for a CRDT unless you have a specific reason (extreme document size, a team already expert in OT, a need to interoperate with an existing OT protocol) to do otherwise. Figure 7's decision tree encodes exactly this reasoning.

There is a third option people forget in reviews, and naming it makes you look senior: **differential synchronization** (Neil Fraser's diff-match-patch approach, the lineage behind some of Google's early sync). Instead of operations, each side periodically diffs its current text against a shadow copy, ships the diff, and patches fuzzily. It is dramatically simpler to implement and tolerant of unreliable transports, but it is *not* guaranteed to converge under high concurrency — patches can fail to apply and get dropped, so it trades correctness for simplicity. It is a legitimate choice for low-concurrency cases (a comment field, a form two people occasionally touch) and a *wrong* choice for a real-time multi-cursor editor. Knowing when the cheap option is acceptable is half of senior judgment, and we will put differential sync explicitly in the rejected-alternatives table later so it is a *considered* rejection, not an omission.

## 6. The real-time transport: WebSocket and the edit fan-out

Whatever convergence engine you pick, the operations have to *move*, and for a real-time editor that means a persistent bidirectional connection — a **WebSocket** (or WebTransport, increasingly). The requirement is sub-100ms perceived collaboration latency, which rules out HTTP polling (too much overhead per op) and even long-polling (a new connection per message). A WebSocket stays open, so an op is one frame on an established connection — single-digit milliseconds of framing overhead on top of network RTT. The architecture mirrors the chat-system pattern: a stateful connection tier holds the sockets, and operations fan out to every other socket in the document's room. The cross-cutting concern is that the *room* — the set of sockets editing one document plus the document's convergence state — must live on one server, which is why the load balancer is sticky-by-document and why scaling is per-document, not per-request.

The fan-out math is where this bites. A document with `E` concurrent editors generating `r` ops per second each means the server receives `E × r` ops per second and must broadcast each to `E − 1` other sockets, so outbound message rate is `E × r × (E − 1) ≈ E² × r`. That `E²` is the scaling villain. Ten editors at 2 ops/sec each is 10 × 2 × 9 = 180 outbound messages/sec — trivial. A hundred editors in a public document at 2 ops/sec is 100 × 2 × 99 ≈ 19,800 outbound messages/sec from a *single* document room, on a *single* server, because the room cannot be split. This is the stress-test we will return to: the quadratic fan-out, not the convergence algorithm, is what actually melts a collaborative editor under a flash crowd. The mitigations are operation **batching** (coalesce ops in a short window — say 50ms — into one frame, cutting message count without adding perceptible latency) and, at the extreme, switching a hot document from full fan-out to a *broadcast tree* or read-only follower mode. The transport layer borrows directly from the patterns in [queues and event streaming](/blog/software-development/system-design/queues-and-event-streaming-for-architects) for the fan-out and from the real-time-media patterns in the [LiveKit deep-dive](/blog/software-development/system-design/livekit-real-time-communication) for connection management at scale.

```javascript
// Server-side room: receive an op, sequence it, persist, fan out.
class DocRoom {
  constructor(docId, engine, oplog) {
    this.docId = docId
    this.engine = engine        // OT transformer or CRDT integrator
    this.oplog = oplog          // append-only durable log
    this.sockets = new Map()    // siteId -> WebSocket
    this.seq = 0
  }

  async onOp(fromSite, op) {
    // 1) Integrate against current doc state (OT transform / CRDT merge)
    const applied = this.engine.integrate(op)
    // 2) Assign a global sequence number (this is the linearization point)
    applied.seq = ++this.seq
    // 3) Durably append BEFORE acking, so a crash never loses an op
    await this.oplog.append(this.docId, applied)
    // 4) Ack the sender, then fan out to everyone else
    this.sockets.get(fromSite)?.send(ack(applied.seq))
    for (const [site, ws] of this.sockets) {
      if (site !== fromSite) ws.send(encode(applied))
    }
  }
}
```

The ordering in that handler is not arbitrary, and a reviewer should poke at it. We integrate, *then* durably append, *then* ack. If we acked before appending, a crash between ack and append would tell the client its op is safe when it is not — a lost edit, the exact failure we are designing against. If we fanned out before appending, peers could see an op that the source of truth never recorded, so a server restart would diverge the document from what users saw. Durability before visibility is the invariant, and it is the same invariant a [write-ahead log](/blog/software-development/system-design/storage-engines-btrees-vs-lsm-trees-for-architects) enforces in a database. The cost is one durable write on the critical path of every op, which is why the op-log must be fast (an append to a local log segment or a low-latency append-optimized store), and why batching helps here too — appending a batch of ops is one write, not N.

#### Worked example: batching a 20-editor room

Put numbers on why batching is the highest-leverage knob. A meeting document has **20 active editors**, each typing at ~3 ops/sec during a busy stretch. Without batching, the server receives 20 × 3 = 60 ops/sec and fans each to 19 peers: 60 × 19 = **1,140 outbound edit frames/sec**, each frame carrying ~100 bytes plus ~60 bytes of WebSocket and TCP framing overhead — so ~160 bytes/frame × 1,140 ≈ **182 KB/sec** of egress, of which more than a third is pure framing overhead, not payload. Now batch with a **50ms coalescing window**: each editor's ops within a 50ms window ship as one frame (so each editor contributes at most 20 frames/sec regardless of typing speed), and the server coalesces all ops destined for one peer in that window into a single combined frame. Outbound frame count drops to roughly 20 editors × 20 windows/sec = 400 frames/sec to *send*, each carrying multiple ops, and the framing overhead is now amortized across several ops per frame. Egress drops to ~**70 KB/sec** and frame count by ~2.5×, while the added latency is at most 50ms — below the ~100ms threshold where collaboration stops feeling instant. The measurable win: a 2.5× reduction in frame-processing CPU and a 60% cut in framing overhead, for a latency cost the user cannot perceive. Batching is free latency-budget arbitrage, and it is the first optimization to reach for, not the last.

The reason this matters more than algorithmic cleverness is that frame *count* — not byte volume — is what saturates a connection server, because each frame costs a syscall, a TLS record, and a trip through the event loop regardless of how few bytes it carries. A server that comfortably pushes hundreds of MB/sec of bulk data can choke on a few hundred thousand tiny frames/sec. This is why the op transport and the presence transport both fixate on *coalescing many small updates into fewer frames* rather than on compressing the bytes — the bytes were never the bottleneck. It is the same lesson the [backpressure-and-rate-limiting](/blog/software-development/system-design/rate-limiting-and-backpressure) work teaches: the unit of cost is the operation, not the byte, and you optimize by reducing operation count.

## 7. Presence and awareness: ephemeral by design

Now the part everyone underestimates. The colored cursors, the name labels floating beside them, the highlighted selections — collectively "presence" or "awareness" — are what make a collaborative editor *feel* alive, and they have completely different requirements from edits. Presence is **ephemeral, high-frequency, and lossy by design**: a cursor position is worthless the instant the cursor moves, so it must never be persisted, and dropping a presence update is invisible (the next one corrects it 16ms later). Treat presence like an edit — durably log every cursor move — and you will generate ten to a hundred times the write volume of the actual editing, all of it garbage within a second. This is the single most common way teams accidentally make a collaborative editor expensive. Figure 5 shows presence flowing through a separate in-memory channel that fans out and then forgets.

![Graph showing one editor's cursor move fanning out through an in-memory room channel with a thirty-second time to live to three other editors, never touching the durable op-log](/imgs/blogs/design-a-collaborative-editor-5.webp)

So presence gets its own plane with three properties. **No persistence**: presence lives in memory on the room server (or in a fast ephemeral store like Redis with a short TTL), and vanishes when the user disconnects or the TTL lapses — there is nothing to load when a document opens cold, because there is no "presence history." **Throttling**: the client coalesces cursor movement and sends at a capped rate, typically 20–30 Hz, not on every pixel of mouse movement — the human eye cannot perceive faster, so anything more is pure waste. **Last-write-wins is fine here**: presence is the one place where LWW is *correct*, because you only ever care about the latest cursor position; older positions are not data you want to preserve. The contrast with the edit plane is total — edits demand convergence and zero loss; presence demands cheapness and tolerates loss — and keeping them in separate planes is what lets each be optimized for its real requirement. Yjs builds this in as a separate "awareness" protocol that rides the same WebSocket but is explicitly not part of the document CRDT, which is the right factoring.

The subtle failure mode in presence is the **ghost cursor**: a user closes their laptop without a clean disconnect, the socket lingers, and their cursor hangs in the document long after they are gone. The fix is the TTL plus a heartbeat — each client refreshes its presence every few seconds, and presence with a stale heartbeat is reaped. The other failure mode is presence *amplifying* the quadratic fan-out: at 30 Hz, a hundred editors moving cursors is 100 × 30 × 99 ≈ 297,000 presence messages/sec, dwarfing the edit traffic. The mitigations are the same family — throttle harder under load, batch presence updates for the room into a single periodic frame ("here are all 100 cursors as of now"), and at the extreme cap the number of *rendered* remote cursors (you do not need to see all 100; the dozen near your viewport suffice). A senior sizes the presence plane for the flash-crowd case explicitly, because it is where the surprise bill arrives. We put concrete numbers on all of this in the estimation section.

## 8. Persistence: the op-log and periodic snapshots

The document has to survive a server crash, a redeploy, and being closed for a year and reopened. The persistence model is **event sourcing**, and it is worth saying so plainly because it gives you the whole pattern for free: the document is not stored as text, it is stored as the **ordered log of operations** that produced it, and the current text is a *projection* you build by replaying the log. The [event-sourcing-and-CQRS deep-dive](/blog/software-development/message-queue/event-sourcing-and-cqrs-with-an-event-log) works out the general pattern; here it maps cleanly: ops are events, the op-log is the event store, and the rendered document is the read model. This buys you time-travel (replay to any point = version history for free), auditability (who changed what, when), and a clean recovery story (rebuild from the log). It costs you the central event-sourcing problem: replaying a long log to load a document is slow, and the log grows without bound. The fix is the central event-sourcing fix: **snapshots**. Figure 6 shows ops appending to the log, periodic snapshots compacting it, and a load replaying only the tail after the latest snapshot.

![Pipeline showing edit operations appending to a durable op-log, periodic snapshots compacting the log every five hundred operations, and a document load replaying only the operations after the latest snapshot](/imgs/blogs/design-a-collaborative-editor-6.webp)

A **snapshot** is a materialized copy of the document's full state (the text plus, for a CRDT, the position metadata and live tombstones) at a known sequence number. Take one every `N` operations or every `T` minutes, persist it to object storage, and now loading a document is "fetch the latest snapshot, replay only the operations logged *after* its sequence number." Replay goes from "millions of ops" to "at most N ops," which bounds load latency to a constant you control. Snapshotting also enables **op-log truncation**: once a snapshot durably exists, the operations *before* it can be archived to cold storage or deleted (subject to your version-history retention policy), which bounds storage growth. The two knobs are the snapshot interval and the retention window, and they trade load latency and storage cost against version-history granularity. The estimation matrix in figure 9 ties the snapshot interval to the document's edit rate — busy documents snapshot more often so their replay tail stays short.

```python
def load_document(doc_id, store, oplog):
    # 1) Fetch the most recent snapshot (state + seq it was taken at).
    snap = store.latest_snapshot(doc_id)         # None for a brand-new doc
    state = snap.state if snap else empty_document()
    from_seq = snap.seq if snap else 0

    # 2) Replay ONLY the operations logged after the snapshot.
    tail = oplog.read_after(doc_id, from_seq)     # bounded by snapshot interval
    for op in tail:
        state = apply(state, op)                  # CRDT integrate / OT apply
    return state

def maybe_snapshot(doc_id, state, seq, store, last_snap_seq, interval=500):
    # Background, off the hot path: compact once enough ops have accrued.
    if seq - last_snap_seq >= interval:
        store.put_snapshot(doc_id, snapshot(state), seq)
        oplog.truncate_before(doc_id, seq)        # respect retention policy
        return seq
    return last_snap_seq
```

The op-log doubles as **version history for free**, which is a feature product managers always ask for and that costs almost nothing once you have chosen this persistence model. Because every operation is retained (within your retention window) with its author and timestamp, "show me the document as of last Tuesday" is just "replay from the nearest prior snapshot up to last Tuesday's sequence number," and "who wrote this paragraph" is a scan of which ops inserted those character identities. This is why event-sourced persistence is such a natural fit for collaborative editors specifically — the audit trail and the time-travel are not bolted-on features, they are the same log you needed anyway for crash recovery and convergence. The one design tension is retention: keeping the full op-log forever gives infinite-granularity history but unbounded storage, so most systems keep fine-grained recent history (every op for the last N days) and coarse-grained older history (just the periodic snapshots beyond that), trading old-version granularity for bounded cost. That trade-off is a product decision dressed as a storage decision, and naming it as such — "how far back do users need character-level history, versus snapshot-level?" — is the senior move in the review.

The senior subtlety is *when* and *where* snapshotting runs. It must be **off the hot path** — never block an op's fan-out to compute a snapshot — so it runs in a background task, on a timer or an op-count trigger, ideally on a replica or a separate worker so it does not steal CPU from live editing. It must also be **idempotent and crash-safe**: if the snapshotter dies mid-write, the half-written snapshot must not be read as authoritative, so you write to a temp key and atomically swap, and you never truncate the log until the new snapshot is durably committed (truncate-after-commit, or you lose data on a crash). For a CRDT, the snapshot also opportunistically runs **tombstone garbage collection** — at a snapshot point where the server knows all replicas have acknowledged up to sequence `S`, tombstones older than `S` can be physically dropped because no future concurrent op can reference them. This is where the CRDT memory bill gets paid down, and tying GC to snapshots is the clean place to do it.

## 9. Estimation: sizing documents, editors, and op-log growth

Now the back-of-the-envelope math, because it sets every other knob. Let us size a Google-Docs-scale service and a single hot document, the two scales that matter. The [estimation deep-dive](/blog/software-development/system-design/back-of-the-envelope-estimation-for-system-design) covers the general method; here are the collaborative-editor-specific numbers. Figure 9 organizes the per-document numbers across three load tiers.

![Capacity matrix showing editors per document, operations per second, op-log growth per hour, snapshot interval, and presence fan-out budget across a quiet document, a busy document, and a flash-crowd document](/imgs/blogs/design-a-collaborative-editor-9.webp)

Start with one operation's size. A text op is small: an insert is roughly a position id, a character or short run, a site id, and a clock — call it **\~100 bytes** on the wire after framing, maybe 30–50 bytes for an OT op without CRDT metadata, 100–200 bytes for a CRDT op with full position metadata. A human types around **5–8 characters per second** at a sprint, but real editing (thinking, pausing, deleting) averats far lower — call a *busy* editor **\~2 ops/sec** sustained. Now the tiers. A **quiet document** (1–3 editors, the overwhelming majority) sees ~2–6 ops/sec total: trivial — its op-log grows ~2 ops/sec × 100 bytes × 3600 ≈ **0.7 MB/hour** at the high end, snapshot hourly, presence is a rounding error. A **busy document** (10–30 active editors, ~50 ops/sec) grows ~50 × 100 × 3600 ≈ **18 MB/hour** — but note most of that compacts away at snapshot time since the *document* is small; the op-log is the firehose, the snapshot is the reservoir. A **flash-crowd document** (100+ editors on a viral public link, ~500 ops/sec) grows ~500 × 100 × 3600 ≈ **180 MB/hour** of raw op-log and demands aggressive snapshotting (every 200 ops) and presence batching.

#### Worked example: a Google-Docs-scale fleet

Size the whole service. Say **50 million** active documents, of which **2%** have at least one editor at any instant — **1 million** concurrent live documents. Of those, suppose the distribution is heavily skewed (it always is): 95% quiet (1–3 editors), 4.9% busy (10–30), 0.1% flash-crowd (100+). That is ~950k quiet, ~49k busy, ~1k flash-crowd documents live at once. **Connection count**: average ~2 editors per quiet doc, ~15 per busy, ~150 per flash-crowd → 950k×2 + 49k×15 + 1k×150 ≈ 1.9M + 735k + 150k ≈ **2.8 million concurrent WebSockets**. At ~1M sockets per beefy connection node (the chat-system number), that is **\~3 connection nodes minimum**, realistically 8–12 for headroom and failure tolerance. **Op throughput**: 950k×4 + 49k×50 + 1k×500 ≈ 3.8M + 2.45M + 0.5M ≈ **6.75 million ops/sec** service-wide. **Op-log write volume**: 6.75M × 100 bytes ≈ **675 MB/sec** of raw appends — which is why the op-log is an append-optimized [LSM-style store](/blog/software-development/system-design/storage-engines-btrees-vs-lsm-trees-for-architects) and why snapshotting + truncation is non-negotiable; without truncation you would be storing ~58 TB/day of mostly-redundant operations. The fleet's cost center is not the convergence CPU; it is the connection memory and the op-log write bandwidth. Sizing those two is where the money goes.

The number that should jump out is **6.75M ops/sec across the fleet but ~500 ops/sec on the single hottest document** — a five-order-of-magnitude spread. That spread is the design's central tension: you must shard by document to spread the 6.75M across thousands of room servers, but you *cannot* shard *within* a hot document (the room is indivisible), so the flash-crowd document is the per-server bottleneck no matter how big your fleet is. Every scaling decision flows from that asymmetry. The next section stress-tests it directly.

## 10. Offline editing and sync on reconnect

A laptop closes, a train enters a tunnel, a phone drops to no signal — and the user keeps typing, because a good editor lets them. Offline editing is where OT and CRDT diverge most sharply, and it is the cleanest illustration of the architectural difference. Offline, the client cannot reach the server, so it cannot get operations linearized. It buffers every local operation in an outbox and applies them locally so the user sees their edits. The question is what happens on **reconnect**, when the client has a stack of local ops composed against a base document that the *rest of the world has been editing the whole time*. The client's base and the server's current state have diverged by potentially thousands of operations.

For **OT**, reconnect is the hard case it tries hardest to avoid: the client's buffered ops must be transformed against *every* operation the server applied while it was offline — which is exactly the n-way TP2 transform that OT struggles with. A long offline session produces a long transform chain, and the risk of a TP2 bug grows with the chain length. Real OT systems bound this by limiting how long a client can stay offline before it must discard local changes or do a full re-sync (refetch the document and re-apply local edits as fresh ops, which can move them to surprising places). For **CRDT**, reconnect is *native and trivial by comparison*: the client's buffered operations are just CRDT updates with position identities that are already globally meaningful. On reconnect, the client ships its buffered updates to the server and pulls the server's updates since its last-known state, applies both in any order, and converges — because that is the entire premise of a CRDT. There is no transform, no chain, no length limit. A client can be offline for a month, come back, and merge cleanly. This is the single most compelling reason CRDTs have won the offline-first market, and it is why products like Figma, Linear, and note-taking apps with offline support lean CRDT.

#### Worked example: a three-day offline edit reconnects

Concretely: a writer drafts on a flight with no wifi for three hours, generating ~2,000 local ops, while back online four collaborators added ~6,000 ops to the same document. On reconnect with a **CRDT**, the client uploads its 2,000 ops (each ~150 bytes ≈ 300 KB, one batched transfer) and downloads a state-vector-diffed bundle of the 6,000 ops it missed (~900 KB), applies both, and converges in well under a second of CPU — the merge is just sorting on position ids. No human conflict resolution, no lost text; the offline insertions land at their stable positions relative to the neighbors they were typed against, even if those neighbors moved. With **OT**, the same reconnect requires transforming all 2,000 buffered ops against all 6,000 server ops — a transform chain whose correctness depends on TP2 holding across 6,000 compositions, which is precisely the regime where OT bugs historically surfaced; most production OT systems would instead force a re-sync that re-applies the offline edits as new ops, risking them landing in less intuitive positions. The CRDT's "offline is just delayed sync" model is qualitatively simpler, and that simplicity *is* the architectural payoff. Note this leans on causal delivery — the [idempotency-and-exactly-once](/blog/software-development/system-design/idempotency-and-exactly-once-by-design) discipline applies, since a client may resend buffered ops if its first reconnect attempt's ack is lost, and re-applying a CRDT op must be a no-op (it is, by construction — applying the same update twice changes nothing).

## 11. Undo and redo in a collaborative context

This is the genuinely hard part, harder than convergence, and it is where I have seen the most senior engineers get the design wrong. In a single-user editor, undo is a stack: pop the last change, invert it, done. In a *collaborative* editor, "undo my last change" is a minefield, because between your change and your undo, *other people changed the document*. The naive global-stack approach — undo whatever the most recent operation was — is unacceptable: you would undo your *collaborator's* edit, which is bewildering and rude. The correct semantics is **selective undo**: each user undoes *their own* last operation, leaving everyone else's intact. And selective undo is hard because the operation you want to invert was composed against a document state that no longer exists — other ops have landed since, shifting positions and possibly deleting the very characters your undo wants to restore.

Concretely: you inserted "cat" at position 10. Then a collaborator inserted a sentence at position 5, shifting your "cat" to position 13. Now you hit undo. A naive inverse "delete three characters at position 10" deletes the wrong characters — it eats part of your collaborator's sentence. The correct undo must be *transformed* (in OT) or *re-targeted by identity* (in CRDT) just like any other operation: in OT you transform the inverse op against all ops that landed after the original; in CRDT you delete *by the position identities* of the characters you inserted, which still point at the right characters wherever they drifted to. The CRDT framing is cleaner here too, because identity-based deletion is naturally robust to intervening edits — you tombstone the exact characters you inserted, regardless of their current index. But even in CRDT, the edge cases bite: what if a collaborator *already deleted* a character you are trying to undo the insertion of? (Answer: the undo is a no-op for that character — it is already gone, and that is fine.) What if your undo and a concurrent edit race? (Answer: they commute, like any two CRDT ops.)

The production pattern most editors converge on is a **per-user undo stack of "undo groups,"** where each group is a set of operations bundled as one logical user action (typing a word, pasting a block), and undo applies the *transformed inverse* of the most recent group *that the user themselves authored*. Yjs ships this as `UndoManager`, scoped to a specific origin (a client id) so it only ever undoes that client's own operations, and it correctly handles the intervening-edits case via the CRDT's identity model. The senior lesson: **do not roll your own collaborative undo unless you must** — it is a swamp of edge cases (redo after a concurrent edit, undo across a reconnect, undo of an op whose target was deleted), and a battle-tested library has already paid that cost. If you are on OT, study how your OT framework scopes undo to the user and transforms the inverse; if you get this wrong, the bug reports ("undo deleted my coworker's paragraph") are severe and erode trust fast. This is the part of the system where I would spend review time disproportionate to its line count, because the blast radius of a bug is the user's own data.

## 12. Stress-testing the design

A design you have not stress-tested is a hypothesis. Push this one past its comfort zone in three directions, the same way you would in a real review, and watch where it bends. The pattern throughout: find the resource that does not scale horizontally and ask what happens when it saturates.

**What breaks at 100 concurrent editors on one document?** The convergence algorithm is *fine* — both OT and CRDT handle 100 concurrent op streams correctly; correctness is not the failure. The **quadratic fan-out** is what breaks. As computed earlier, 100 editors at 2 ops/sec is ~19,800 outbound edit messages/sec from one room, and presence at 30 Hz adds ~297,000 messages/sec — *that* is the real load, and it all originates on one server because the room is indivisible. The CPU and the network egress of a single room server saturate. The mitigations, in order of reach: (1) **batch ops** in a 50ms window — coalesces bursts, cutting message count several-fold with imperceptible added latency; (2) **throttle and batch presence hard** — drop to 10 Hz under load and send one combined "all cursors" frame to the room rather than per-cursor frames, cutting presence messages by the editor count; (3) **cap rendered cursors** client-side so the *clients* are not overwhelmed even if the server sends them; (4) at the extreme, **degrade to a follower model** — past some editor count, only the active typers get full read-write fan-out and the rest become near-real-time *read* followers updated on a slower cadence. Document-level sharding does *not* help here, which is the uncomfortable truth: you cannot split one document across servers without re-introducing the cross-server convergence problem you spent the whole design avoiding.

**What breaks on a long offline edit then reconnect?** Covered in the worked example above, but as a stress case: the danger for **OT** is the transform-chain length (correctness risk grows with offline duration), mitigated by capping offline duration or forcing re-sync; the danger for **CRDT** is the *size* of the reconnect payload and the *tombstone churn* from a large merge, mitigated by state-vector diffing (ship only the ops the peer is missing, not the whole history) and by GC at the next snapshot. Either way, the reconnect must be **idempotent**: if the client's first sync attempt's ack is lost, it resends, and the server must dedup — a re-applied CRDT op is a natural no-op, and an OT system needs explicit op-id dedup. This is the same [exactly-once-effect discipline](/blog/software-development/system-design/idempotency-and-exactly-once-by-design) that every reliable distributed system needs.

**What breaks on a network partition?** If the partition is *client-server* (a client loses the server), it is just the offline case — the client keeps editing locally and syncs on heal. If the partition is *between server replicas* — say you run the op-log replicated across regions and the regions split — you face the [CAP](/blog/software-development/system-design/articulating-tradeoffs-cap-pacelc-and-beyond) choice directly. A CRDT-backed document can stay **available** on both sides of the partition: each side accepts edits independently, and they converge when the partition heals, because that is what CRDTs *do* — this is the AP corner, and it is the CRDT's home turf. An OT-backed document with a single authoritative server *cannot* split its authority, so a partition either makes one side read-only (CP) or risks divergence if both sides keep accepting writes (the failure OT's central-server model exists to prevent). This is the deepest reason the OT-vs-CRDT choice is really a [replication-model](/blog/software-development/system-design/replication-strategies-and-their-failure-modes) choice: OT is leader-based replication (one authority, CP-leaning under partition), CRDT is leaderless/multi-leader replication (no authority, AP, converges on heal). Naming it that way in a review instantly elevates the conversation from "which library" to "which consistency posture does the product need."

## 13. How real systems do it

Theory is cheap; here is how the systems you have used actually solved this, with the concrete lesson each teaches.

**Google Docs — OT, server-authoritative.** Google Docs descends from Google Wave's operational-transformation engine (the Jupiter model). Every keystroke becomes an operation transformed against the server's authoritative sequence; the client keeps a queue of unacknowledged ops and transforms incoming ops against it. The lesson: OT *works* at planetary scale when you commit to a central authority and invest in getting the transform functions right — Google Docs is the existence proof that OT is production-grade. The cost is the one OT always pays: it is online-first (offline support came later and is more limited), and the OT engine is a deep piece of in-house expertise, not a library you import. If you are building an online-first document editor and have the engineering depth, OT is a legitimate, proven choice — but you are signing up to own the hardest correctness surface in the system.

**Figma — a custom CRDT-like multiplayer engine.** Figma's multiplayer (described in their engineering writing) is *not* a general OT or off-the-shelf CRDT; it is a purpose-built system where each document is a tree of objects, and a central server holds the authoritative state while clients send changes that the server orders and rebroadcasts. Crucially, Figma chose **last-write-wins on a per-property basis** for most object properties (because for design objects, "the latest value of this rectangle's color wins" is acceptable and simple) and reserved CRDT-style convergence only for the structures where it matters. The lesson is a senior one: **you do not need full CRDT semantics for every field** — choose the convergence strength per data type, using cheap LWW where loss is acceptable (a property value) and proper convergent merge only where it is not (the document structure, ordered text). Matching mechanism strength to the actual requirement is exactly the trade-off thinking this series preaches, and Figma is the canonical example of it.

**Yjs / Automerge — CRDT libraries, offline-first.** Yjs is the most widely deployed text CRDT in production, powering collaborative features across many editors precisely because it makes the right things easy: it is a library you import, convergence is proven by construction, offline and peer-to-peer are native, and its block-encoding optimization keeps the metadata cost manageable for the common left-to-right typing case. Automerge offers similar guarantees with a different internal representation and a strong focus on local-first software. The lesson: for a *new* collaborative feature, especially one needing offline support, reaching for a battle-tested CRDT library is usually the right call — you inherit correctness instead of building it, and you spend your engineering budget on the product instead of on TP2 bugs. The honest caveat real teams report is memory: a document with a huge edit history needs tombstone GC and snapshotting discipline, and a naive integration can balloon memory — which is exactly the cost the matrix in figure 3 flagged, showing up right where it was predicted to.

The meta-lesson across all three: there is no single right answer, but there *is* a right *process* — name the product constraints (online-only vs offline, central server vs P2P, document size, per-field loss tolerance), map them to the trade-off matrix, and pick deliberately. Google chose OT for an online-first, server-backed product and built deep expertise. Figma chose per-field LWW plus targeted convergence for a structured-object product where most fields tolerate loss. Offline-first products choose CRDTs and inherit convergence from a library. Each is correct *for its constraints*, and none would be correct for the others'.

Now the rejected alternatives, because a senior names what they did *not* choose and *why* — an unstated rejection looks like an oversight.

| Alternative considered | Why it is tempting | Why rejected (and when it is right) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Last-write-wins on the whole document | Trivial to build; just save the latest text | Silently loses concurrent edits — the failure in figure 1. Acceptable only for a single-author doc with rare, low-stakes concurrency. |
| Pessimistic locking (one editor at a time) | Zero convergence problem; no concurrent edits possible | Kills the entire value proposition of real-time collaboration. Fine for a wiki-style "check out to edit" workflow, wrong for Google-Docs-style co-editing. |
| Differential synchronization (diff/patch) | Far simpler than OT or CRDT; tolerant of unreliable transport | Not guaranteed to converge under high concurrency — patches can drop. Right for low-concurrency fields (a shared comment box), wrong for a multi-cursor editor. |
| Operational transformation | Tiny ops, no tombstones, lean memory, clean undo | Requires a central authority; TP2 correctness is hard to own forever; offline is painful. Right for online-first, server-backed, very large documents with in-house OT depth. |
| CRDT (sequence) | No central authority, native offline/P2P, proven convergence | Per-character metadata and tombstones cost memory; needs GC discipline. Right for offline-first, P2P, or when inheriting a library's correctness beats owning OT. |
| Persisting presence in the op-log | One unified write path; "everything is an op" | Generates 10–100× the write volume of edits for worthless data. Always wrong; presence must be ephemeral. |

The stance I would defend: for a **new, server-backed editor that needs offline support**, use a CRDT library (Yjs/Automerge), keep presence on a separate ephemeral plane, persist via an op-log with snapshots, and lean on the library's `UndoManager` for collaborative undo. For an **online-only editor at extreme document scale with an OT-expert team**, OT is a respectable choice that pays off in memory efficiency — but you are accepting ownership of the system's hardest correctness surface. And for **anything low-concurrency** (a form, a comment thread, a config two people occasionally touch), do *not* build a real-time engine at all — differential sync or even careful last-write-wins with conflict detection is the right amount of engineering. The senior failure is over-building: not every "two people might edit this" needs a CRDT. Reach for the convergence engine when concurrency is real and frequent, not when it is theoretically possible.

## 15. Optimization: where the real cost lives

Pull the optimization thread together, because it is woven through but worth stating as a system. The collaborative editor has three cost centers, and a senior optimizes each against its real metric, not against a vibe.

**Cost center 1: the quadratic fan-out (the latency and egress bottleneck).** The metric is outbound messages/sec per room and p99 op-propagation latency. The optimization is **batching**: coalesce ops in a 50ms window into one frame. At 100 editors and 2 ops/sec, naive fan-out is ~19,800 msg/sec; batching a 50ms window means each editor's ops fan out at most 20 times/sec instead of per-op, and combined with per-recipient frame coalescing you cut message count several-fold. Measure it as p99 latency (must stay under ~100ms to feel real-time) and egress bytes/sec per server. The win is concrete: batching turns a melting room server into a comfortable one without the user perceiving any lag, because 50ms is below the threshold of noticing.

**Cost center 2: presence (the surprise write/egress bill).** The metric is presence messages/sec and (critically) presence *persistence* writes, which should be **zero**. The optimization is the whole "presence is ephemeral" design: never persist it, throttle to 20–30 Hz, batch the room's cursors into a single periodic frame under load, and cap rendered remote cursors. The measurable win: presence persistence drops from "10–100× edit volume" to zero, and presence egress under a flash crowd drops from ~297k msg/sec to a bounded per-room rate. This is the optimization that most often saves real money, because the naive version writes a torrent of garbage to durable storage.

**Cost center 3: load and replay (the op-log growth and cold-load latency).** The metric is op-log bytes/sec, cold-document-load p99, and storage cost. The optimization is **snapshots plus truncation**: snapshot every N ops (tighter N for busier docs, per figure 9), replay only the post-snapshot tail on load (bounding cold-load latency to a constant), truncate the pre-snapshot log to cold storage or deletion (bounding storage), and run tombstone GC at snapshot points (bounding CRDT memory). The measurable win: without snapshots, loading an old, heavily-edited document replays millions of ops (seconds of latency, sometimes timeouts); with a 500-op snapshot interval, load replays at most 500 ops (single-digit milliseconds), and op-log storage stops growing unboundedly. At the fleet scale we sized — ~675 MB/sec of raw appends — truncation is the difference between ~58 TB/day of storage and a bounded working set.

The cross-cutting optimization is the **separation of planes** itself (figure 8): because edits, presence, and persistence are separate planes, you optimize each independently without one's requirements distorting another's. That separation is not an accident of the diagram; it is the load-bearing design decision that makes every other optimization possible. Figure 8 shows the layering that enforces it.

## 16. When to reach for this (and when not to)

Be decisive. **Build a full real-time collaborative engine when**: multiple users genuinely edit the *same* document *concurrently and frequently*, sub-100ms collaboration is a product requirement, and lost edits are unacceptable. That is Google Docs, Figma, Notion-style docs, collaborative IDEs, shared whiteboards. For these, the convergence engine (OT or CRDT) and the three-plane architecture are the right amount of engineering, and skimping produces the lost-edit failures that destroy trust.

**Do not build this when** the concurrency is rare or low-stakes. A shared form two people occasionally touch, a comment thread, a config page, a document with a single primary author and an occasional reviewer — these do *not* need a CRDT. Use optimistic concurrency control (a version number; reject a stale write and ask the user to refresh), or differential sync, or last-write-wins with conflict *detection* (warn the user, do not silently merge). The senior anti-pattern is reaching for a CRDT because "users might collaborate someday" — you pay the metadata, the operational complexity, and the undo swamp for a scenario that rarely fires. Match the mechanism to the *actual* concurrency, and when concurrency is rare, the boring solution is the correct one.

And within "build it," **prefer a CRDT library** for new systems unless you have a specific reason for OT (extreme document size, existing OT expertise or protocol, a need for the leanest possible per-character footprint). The default has shifted: the inherited correctness, the native offline support, and the leaderless partition tolerance of a modern CRDT library outweigh OT's memory leanness for most new products. Reach for OT deliberately, as a considered choice for a constraint that demands it — not as a default.

## 17. Key takeaways

- **A collaborative editor is a distributed-systems problem in disguise.** The core requirement is eventual convergence of concurrent edits to shared state with no lost updates — the same property a leaderless replicated store needs after a partition. Everything else is supporting cast.
- **Last-write-wins on the whole document is the wrong default.** It loses concurrent edits because it models a sequence of positions as one opaque blob. The fix is to make different positions independently addressable so non-overlapping edits never conflict.
- **OT and CRDT are two encodings of "impose a deterministic order on concurrent edits."** OT transforms ops at apply time and needs a central authority; CRDT bakes order into per-character identity and converges leaderlessly. The choice is really a leader-based vs leaderless replication choice.
- **The OT-vs-CRDT decision follows three product constraints**: central server (have one?), offline/P2P (need it?), document size (huge?). Need offline or P2P → CRDT. Online-only with enormous docs and OT expertise → OT is defensible. Otherwise default to a CRDT library for inherited correctness.
- **Keep presence on a separate, ephemeral, lossy plane.** Cursors and selections update at 30 Hz and are worthless a second later — never persist them, throttle them, and batch them. Persisting presence is the most common way to make a collaborative editor accidentally expensive.
- **Persist via an op-log with periodic snapshots.** The document is an event-sourced projection; snapshots bound both cold-load replay latency and op-log storage growth, and snapshot points are where CRDT tombstone GC happens.
- **The quadratic fan-out, not the convergence algorithm, melts a hot document.** A room is indivisible, so 100 concurrent editors saturate one server. Batch ops, batch and throttle presence, cap rendered cursors, and at the extreme degrade to a follower model. Document sharding does not help one hot document.
- **Collaborative undo is the hardest part — do not roll your own.** Selective per-user undo must transform or identity-retarget the inverse against intervening edits. Use a battle-tested library's undo manager; the blast radius of a bug here is the user's own data.
