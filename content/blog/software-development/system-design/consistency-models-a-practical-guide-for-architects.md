A junior engineer asks "should this be strongly consistent?" and the honest senior answer is almost always "no, and you should be able to say why in one sentence." The reflex to reach for strong consistency is the single most expensive habit I see in design reviews. It feels safe. It feels correct. And it quietly buys you a cross-datacenter round trip on every write, a system that goes unavailable the moment a network link hiccups, and a p99 that is three times what it needed to be — all to protect a like counter where a momentarily-wrong number is invisible to every human who will ever look at it. The skill that separates a senior here is not knowing more about Paxos. It is the discipline to *pick the weakest consistency model that still meets the product requirement*, and to defend that choice by naming the exact user-visible anomaly the model allows and arguing that the product does not care about it.

This is a guide to making that choice. The `database/` folder on this blog already deep-dives the *mechanisms* — how [consistency models stack up from linearizable to eventual](/blog/software-development/database/consistency-models-from-linearizable-to-eventual) as formal guarantees, how [leader, multi-leader, and leaderless replication](/blog/software-development/database/distributed-replication-leader-multi-leader-leaderless) actually move bytes, how [Spanner's TrueTime](/blog/software-development/database/spanner-truetime-and-external-consistency) buys external consistency with synchronized clocks. This post is the *decision layer* on top of those. Not "what is linearizability," but "for *this* operation, do I need it, what does it cost me if I take it, and what does the user see if I don't?" Figure 1 lays out the whole spectrum we will walk down, from the strong, expensive top to the weak, cheap bottom.

The frame for the entire post is a spectrum, not a binary. Engineers talk about "strong versus eventual" as if there were two choices, and that framing is exactly why so many systems end up paying for strong consistency they do not need. In reality there is a graded ladder — linearizable, sequential, causal, the session guarantees, eventual — and each rung you step *down* drops one specific guarantee in exchange for lower latency, higher availability, and less coordination. The senior move is to start at the *bottom* of the ladder (eventual, cheapest) and climb up only as far as the product genuinely requires, stopping at the first rung that prevents the anomaly your users would actually notice and complain about. Most features stop at session guarantees, the pragmatic middle, and never need to climb higher.

By the end of this post you should be able to take any operation in your system — a like, a transfer, a username check, a feed render — and place it on the spectrum with a one-sentence justification, compute the cheap fix for a "my own update vanished" complaint without going strong, and walk into a design review able to say not just *which* model you chose but *what the user sees during a partition or under replication lag* if you are wrong.

## 1. The spectrum, from strong to weak

The single most useful thing you can internalize is the order of the ladder and what each rung adds over the one below it. Figure 1 is that ladder. Read it top to bottom as a sequence of *promises being subtracted*, because that is the cheapest way to remember it: each step down removes a guarantee and refunds you some latency and availability.

![A vertical spectrum of consistency models from linearizable at the strong top down through sequential, causal, session guarantees, and eventual at the weak bottom, with each step trading a guarantee for lower latency and higher availability](/imgs/blogs/consistency-models-a-practical-guide-for-architects-1.webp)

**Linearizable** (also called atomic or strong) is the top rung and the strongest single-object guarantee. It promises that every operation appears to take effect instantaneously at some point between when you called it and when it returned, and that all clients agree on one global real-time order. The mental shorthand: the system behaves *as if there were a single copy of the data and one operation at a time*, with no way for any observer to tell that replication is even happening. If you write a value and the write returns, every subsequent read by anyone, anywhere, sees that value or a later one — never an older one. This is the only model that gives you the property called *recency*: a read is guaranteed to reflect the most recent completed write in real time.

**Sequential consistency** is one rung down. It still requires that all clients see operations in *the same single order*, and that each client's own operations appear in that order in the order it issued them — but it drops the real-time requirement. The agreed-upon global order does not have to match wall-clock time. So if Alice writes at 10:00:00 and Bob writes at 10:00:01, a sequentially consistent system is allowed to order Bob's write *before* Alice's, as long as everyone agrees on that ordering. In practice sequential consistency is rare as a deliberate target in modern distributed databases — it is mostly a stepping stone in the theory — but it matters because it isolates the one thing linearizability adds: real-time recency. Everything below sequential is where the practical, money-saving choices live.

**Causal consistency** is the most important rung in the middle, and the one most architects underuse. It drops the requirement that everyone agree on a single total order, and keeps only the orderings that *causally matter*: if operation A *happened before* B (A could have influenced B), then everyone sees A before B. Operations that are concurrent — neither caused the other — can be seen in different orders by different observers, and that is fine. The classic example: a reply must never appear before the post it is replying to, because the reply causally depends on having seen the post. But two unrelated posts by two unrelated people can show up in any order without anyone noticing. Causal consistency is the strongest model you can have while *staying available during a network partition*, which is a deep and useful result we will return to.

**Session guarantees** — read-your-writes, monotonic reads, monotonic writes, writes-follow-reads — are not really a separate point on the global spectrum so much as a set of *per-client* promises layered on top of an eventually-consistent store. They guarantee nothing about what *other* clients see; they only guarantee that *your own* view is sensible. Read-your-writes says you always see your own most recent write. Monotonic reads says you never see time go backwards (a value you already read won't be replaced by an older one on a later read). These are the pragmatic middle ground because they fix the overwhelming majority of "my update vanished!" support tickets at almost zero cost, and we will spend a whole section on them.

**Eventual consistency** is the bottom rung. The only promise is *convergence*: if writes stop, all replicas eventually agree on the same value. It says nothing about *when* (could be milliseconds, could be seconds), nothing about what order updates are applied in, and nothing about what you see in the meantime. Reads can be stale, can go backwards, can show your own writes as missing. In exchange you get the cheapest, fastest, most available system possible: every replica answers from local state with no coordination. Eventual consistency done *right* — which means with a defined conflict-resolution strategy and a bounded convergence time — is the correct default for an enormous fraction of features. Eventual consistency done *wrong* — meaning nobody thought about conflicts or convergence and just hoped — is the source of a lot of 3 a.m. pages.

The senior reframing of this whole ladder: **strength is a cost, not a virtue.** Every rung you climb up buys you a guarantee and charges you in latency, availability, and coordination. So the question is never "how much consistency can I get?" It is "how *little* can I get away with, and what breaks if I'm wrong?" That inverts the junior instinct, and it is the inversion this entire post is built to install.

## 2. What each model actually costs

A model is only useful to an architect if you can name its price, so let us put numbers and mechanisms on each rung. Figure 2 collects this as a matrix you can read in a review: rows are the properties you care about (coordination cost, write latency, availability during a partition, whether stale reads are possible), columns are the models. The shape to notice is that the costs all move together — as you climb the ladder, coordination goes up, latency goes up, availability goes down, and stale reads disappear. There is no rung that is cheap *and* strong. That monotonic trade-off is the whole reason the choice matters.

![A matrix with consistency models as columns and coordination cost, write latency, partition availability, and stale-read possibility as rows, showing that stronger models cost more on every axis](/imgs/blogs/consistency-models-a-practical-guide-for-architects-2.webp)

**Linearizable costs coordination.** To guarantee one global real-time order, a write must reach agreement among a majority of replicas before it returns — that is consensus, and consensus is a round trip to a quorum. If your replicas are in one datacenter, that round trip is maybe 1–2ms and you might barely notice. If they span regions for durability — Virginia, Frankfurt, Singapore — that round trip is the speed of light times distance, and a write from Frankfurt that must reach a quorum including Virginia eats ~90ms before it can return. Worse, linearizability is the one model that *forces unavailability during a partition*: by the CAP theorem, a system that stays linearizable must refuse requests on the minority side of a partition, because serving them might violate the single global order. So the price of linearizable is "a quorum round trip on every write, forever, and an outage on the minority during every partition." That is a steep price, and you should only pay it when the alternative is worse — when a wrong answer means lost money, a double-booked seat, or a duplicate username.

**Causal costs metadata, not coordination.** This is the property that makes causal consistency such a sweet spot. To enforce causal order, each write carries a small amount of dependency metadata — a vector of versions saying "this write happened after these other writes I had seen" — and a replica delays applying a write until it has applied that write's dependencies. No consensus, no quorum, no waiting for remote replicas before acknowledging. The write commits locally and replicates asynchronously; the *receiving* replica does the ordering work. So causal consistency can acknowledge writes at local-disk latency (sub-millisecond to a few ms) and *stay available during a partition*, which linearizable cannot. The cost is the metadata overhead (tracking and shipping dependency vectors, which grows with the number of writers you depend on) and the implementation complexity. For the guarantee you get — "nothing ever appears out of causal order" — that is a remarkable bargain, and it is why systems like MongoDB and Azure Cosmos DB offer causal consistency as a named, tunable level.

**Session guarantees cost routing, not data.** The cheapest fix on the whole ladder. Read-your-writes is typically implemented by having the client remember a version token from its last write and refusing to read from a replica that has not caught up to that token (or by routing the client's reads to the same replica that took its write). Monotonic reads is the same trick: remember the highest version you have seen and never read from a replica behind it. The "cost" is some sticky routing logic and a version token carried in the session — a few bytes and a routing rule. No extra round trips on the common path, no loss of availability. You are not changing the consistency of the *store*; you are filtering which replica a given *client* is allowed to read from. That is why session guarantees are the senior's first reach when someone reports an anomaly: they are nearly free and they fix the complaint people actually file.

**Eventual costs nothing on the request path and everything in convergence design.** Reads and writes hit local state with zero coordination, so latency is as low as physics allows and availability is total — a replica answers even if it is the only one reachable. The cost moves *off* the request path and *into* the design of how conflicting writes get reconciled. If two replicas both took a write to the same key during a partition, someone has to decide what the converged value is: last-writer-wins (simple, loses data), a CRDT that merges deterministically (correct, constrained to certain data types), or application-level merge (flexible, complex). Skip that design and "eventual consistency" silently becomes "we lose one of every pair of concurrent writes and nobody knows." So eventual is the cheapest model to *run* and one of the more expensive to *design correctly*, and the bill comes due exactly when you are least prepared — during a partition.

#### Worked example: pricing a global write

Suppose you run a store with replicas in `us-east-1` (Virginia), `eu-central-1` (Frankfurt), and `ap-southeast-1` (Singapore), and a user in Frankfurt issues a write. Round-trip latencies between regions are roughly: Frankfurt↔Virginia ~90ms, Frankfurt↔Singapore ~160ms, Virginia↔Singapore ~230ms.

Under **linearizable** consistency with majority quorum (need 2 of 3 acks), the Frankfurt write commits locally and waits for the *nearest* additional ack. The nearest replica to Frankfurt is Virginia at ~90ms, so the write returns in **~90ms** — the cross-region round trip dominates everything. Every write pays this, on the order of millions of writes a day. Under **causal** or **eventual** consistency, the Frankfurt write commits to the local Frankfurt replica and returns in **~2ms**, replicating to the others asynchronously in the background. The user-visible write latency drops by roughly **45×**. That 45× is not a micro-optimization — it is the difference between a write that feels instant and one that feels like a stall, and you pay it on every single write for the entire life of the product. The architect's job is to know which of your writes can afford 2ms and which genuinely need the 90ms guarantee, and to route them differently rather than charging every write the maximum.

## 3. The anomalies — what the user actually sees

Models are abstract; anomalies are concrete. The way to *feel* the spectrum is to know exactly what a user sees when each guarantee is missing, because that user-visible behavior is what you are really trading. A senior chooses a model by deciding which anomalies the product can tolerate, not by reasoning about formal definitions. Figure 4 (which we will reach in a moment) shows the canonical one — the stale read — as a timeline.

The **stale read** is the most common anomaly. You write a value, the write succeeds, and a subsequent read returns the *old* value because it was routed to a replica that has not yet received the update. This is the bread-and-butter symptom of replication lag, and it is allowed by everything below linearizable. Whether it *matters* depends entirely on the operation: a stale like count is invisible; a stale account balance shown right after a withdrawal generates a furious support ticket.

The **"my own update vanished"** anomaly is the special, painful case of a stale read where *you* are the one who made the write. You change your profile photo, the save succeeds, you reload, and the old photo is back. This is the violation of read-your-writes, and it is the single most-reported consistency complaint in real products because users notice *their own* changes disappearing far more than they notice anyone else's. It feels like a bug to the user even though the system is "working as designed" under eventual consistency. The crucial fact: this specific anomaly is *cheap* to fix (session guarantees) without touching the consistency of the store for anyone else.

**Reads going backwards** (the violation of monotonic reads) is when you read a value, then read again and see an *older* value — because your first read hit a caught-up replica and your second hit a lagging one. Time appears to run backwards. A user refreshing a comment thread sees five comments, refreshes again, sees three. Disorienting, and a classic symptom of round-robin load balancing across replicas with uneven lag.

**Causal violations** are subtler and more embarrassing: a reply appears before the post it answers, a "you have a new message" notification arrives before the message is visible, an unfriend-then-post leaks the post to the person you just unfriended (the famous example from the COPS paper — you remove someone from your friends list, then post "my boss is terrible," and because the unfriend and the post replicate independently, the post arrives before the unfriend on the boss's replica). These are the anomalies causal consistency exists to prevent, and they are exactly the ones that make the news.

**Lost writes / conflicts** are the eventual-consistency endgame: two clients write the same key during a partition, both succeed locally, and on convergence the system must pick one — silently discarding the other under naive last-writer-wins. The user who lost their write never finds out. This is the anomaly that conflict-free design (CRDTs, merge functions) exists to tame.

The discipline this section installs: before you pick a model, write down the anomaly each candidate *allows* and ask "would a user notice and care?" If the answer is no, the cheaper model wins. If the answer is "they would notice but it is cosmetic," session guarantees probably fix the noticeable part. Only when the answer is "a wrong value here costs money or breaks an invariant" do you climb to the top.

## 4. Session guarantees — the pragmatic middle ground

If you take one practical technique from this entire post, take this one. The vast majority of consistency complaints in real products are not "the global state is wrong" — they are "*my own* recent action seems to have been ignored." And that class of complaint is fixable with session guarantees, which are nearly free, without paying for strong consistency or even causal consistency across the whole system. This is the highest-leverage move on the spectrum: a tiny amount of routing logic that eliminates the most-reported anomaly.

![A before and after comparison where reading from a lagging replica shows an old value, and tagging the write with a version then requiring reads to meet that version makes the user see their own write](/imgs/blogs/consistency-models-a-practical-guide-for-architects-3.webp)

Figure 3 shows the fix. The "before" is the default eventual setup: the client writes to the leader, then its next read is load-balanced to *some* replica, which happens to be 300ms behind, so the user sees the old value and files a "my update vanished" ticket. The "after" is read-your-writes: the write returns a version token (say, the log sequence number or a commit timestamp, `v42`), the client stashes it in its session, and every subsequent read carries "I need a replica caught up to at least `v42`." The read is routed only to replicas that satisfy that constraint, so the user *always* sees their own write. Nobody else's consistency changed. The store is still eventually consistent for everyone else. You spent a version token and a routing rule.

There are four session guarantees and they fix four distinct complaints:

- **Read-your-writes (read-your-own-writes):** you always see your own most recent write. Fixes "my profile edit vanished."
- **Monotonic reads:** successive reads never go backwards in time. Fixes "the comment count jumped from 5 to 3."
- **Monotonic writes:** your own writes are applied in the order you issued them. Fixes "I set status to A then B, but the system ended on A."
- **Writes-follow-reads (session causality):** if you read a value and then write, your write is ordered after the value you read. Fixes "I replied to a comment but my reply shows before it."

Here is a compact read-your-writes implementation against a primary/replica store. The client tracks the highest write version it has seen and refuses replicas that are behind:

```python
import time

class SessionStore:
    """Read-your-writes + monotonic reads via a per-session version watermark."""

    def __init__(self, primary, replicas):
        self.primary = primary            # accepts writes, returns a version
        self.replicas = replicas          # async followers, expose .lag_version()
        self.watermark = 0                # highest version this session has seen

    def write(self, key, value):
        version = self.primary.put(key, value)   # monotonically increasing LSN
        # Our own write moves the watermark forward: future reads must see >= it.
        self.watermark = max(self.watermark, version)
        return version

    def read(self, key):
        # Pick any replica caught up to our watermark; fall back to primary.
        for r in self.replicas:
            if r.lag_version() >= self.watermark:
                value, version = r.get(key)
                # Monotonic reads: never let the watermark go backwards.
                self.watermark = max(self.watermark, version)
                return value
        # No replica is caught up yet — read from the primary (always current).
        value, version = self.primary.get(key)
        self.watermark = max(self.watermark, version)
        return value
```

Notice what this costs: a single integer per session and a `lag_version()` check per replica. No quorum, no consensus, no cross-region round trip on the common path. The only degradation is that immediately after a write, if no replica has caught up, the read falls back to the primary — slightly more load on the primary for a brief window. That is a trivial price for eliminating your most common consistency ticket.

The two less-famous session guarantees are worth naming because they fix anomalies that surface in subtler ways. **Monotonic writes** guarantees that *your own* writes are applied in the order you issued them — without it, a user who rapidly sets their status to "away" then "online" can end up "away" if the two writes raced to different replicas and converged in the wrong order. The fix is to make a session's writes go through a path that preserves their order (route a session's writes to the same primary, or carry a per-session sequence number the store honors). **Writes-follow-reads** (session causality) guarantees that if you read a value and then write based on it, your write is ordered *after* the value you read — this is what keeps a reply ordered after the comment it answers *within your own session*, the single-user slice of the cross-user guarantee that full causal consistency provides. Both are implemented with the same cheap machinery as read-your-writes: a per-session watermark and a routing or sequencing rule, not coordination. The practical upshot is that the four session guarantees together give a *single user* a completely coherent view of the world — their writes stick, their reads don't go backwards, their writes apply in order, and their write-after-read causality holds — for the price of a session token, while every other user's reads stay fast and local. That is why session guarantees are the highest return-on-effort technique on the entire ladder.

The deeper point for design reviews: when someone proposes "let's make the user store strongly consistent because people complain their edits don't show up," the senior counter is "the complaint is read-your-writes, not linearizability — we can fix it with a session token and keep our fast local reads for everyone else." That reframe saves a quorum round trip on millions of reads. Session guarantees are how you say "yes, we'll fix the complaint" and "no, we won't pay for strong consistency" *in the same sentence*.

#### Worked example: fixing a replication-lag complaint

A product team reports that ~2% of users who update their display name see the old name on the very next page load, and they want to "switch the user table to strong consistency." Let us cost both fixes.

The system has 1 primary and 3 read replicas; replication lag is p50 ~40ms, p99 ~300ms. Read traffic is **50,000 QPS**, of which writes are ~**1,000 QPS** (display-name and profile edits are rare). The "next page load" after a write happens within ~200ms for most users, which is inside the p99 lag window — that is why ~2% see staleness.

**Option A, strong consistency:** route *all* 50,000 read QPS to the primary (or quorum reads). This eliminates staleness but moves 50,000 QPS of reads onto a path that was sized for 1,000 writes/s, blows up primary load by ~50×, and adds a quorum round trip (say +5ms in-region, +90ms cross-region) to *every* read — including the 98% of reads that had no preceding write and never had a problem. You have made 50,000 QPS slower to fix a 2% anomaly.

**Option B, read-your-writes:** the ~1,000 writes/s return a version token; those users' *next* reads (a tiny fraction of the 50,000 QPS — only users who *just* wrote) carry the watermark and route to a caught-up replica or the primary. That is at most ~1,000 reads/s touching the primary in the post-write window, a **0.04%** of total reads going to the primary instead of **100%**. Latency for the other 49,000 QPS is unchanged. The 2% anomaly goes to zero.

Option B fixes the exact complaint at roughly **1/1000th** the cost of Option A. This is the calculation that should happen *every time* someone proposes strong consistency to fix a staleness complaint, and it almost always points at session guarantees instead.

## 5. A stale read under lag — the anomaly in slow motion

To choose a model under pressure you need to *see* the anomaly happen, frame by frame, so you can reason about its window. Figure 4 is the stale read as a timeline: the precise sequence by which a write succeeds and a read still returns the old value.

![A timeline showing a leader write of balance one hundred at time zero acknowledged at five milliseconds, a read at twenty milliseconds routed to a follower that still returns balance zero, and the follower converging at three hundred milliseconds](/imgs/blogs/consistency-models-a-practical-guide-for-architects-4.webp)

Walk the timeline. At T+0 the leader applies the write `balance = 100`. At T+5ms the leader acknowledges the client — from the *writer's* perspective, the write is done and durable. At T+20ms a *different* client (or the same client on a different connection) issues a read that the load balancer routes to a follower replica. That follower has not yet received the replicated write, so it returns `balance = 0` — the old value. The reader sees stale data. At T+300ms (p99 lag) the follower finally applies the replicated write and converges; from then on it returns `100`. The **anomaly window is the gap between the leader's ack and the follower catching up** — here, ~295ms — and any read routed to that follower inside the window sees the stale value.

This timeline is the mechanical root of every staleness anomaly, and seeing it tells you exactly which knobs you have. You can **shrink the window** (synchronous or semi-synchronous replication, so the leader does not ack until a follower has the write — but now you have paid latency on the write, climbing toward linearizable). You can **route around the window** (read-your-writes: don't let *that* reader hit *that* follower until it has caught up — session guarantees, cheap). You can **tolerate the window** (accept the stale read because the value is cosmetic — eventual, free). Or you can **close the window entirely** (linearizable reads via quorum, paying the round trip on every read). Four knobs, four costs. The model you pick *is* the knob you pick. The mechanics of how leaders and followers replicate, and how sync versus async changes the window, live in the [replication mechanism deep-dive](/blog/software-development/database/distributed-replication-leader-multi-leader-leaderless) and the [sync vs async replication post](/blog/software-development/database/database-replication-sync-async-logical-physical); your job is to choose the knob per read path.

A subtle, senior-level point hides in this timeline: the anomaly window is a property of your *replication topology and lag*, not of the consistency model in the abstract. A "strongly consistent" system with a buggy clock or a slow quorum can have a longer practical anomaly window than a well-tuned eventually-consistent one with sub-10ms lag. Consistency models are guarantees about *what is permitted*, not promises about *how bad it gets in practice*. When you reason about real risk, you reason about the *measured lag distribution*, not just the model name. "We're eventually consistent but our p99 lag is 12ms and reads are read-your-writes" is a far stronger real-world guarantee than "we're strongly consistent" with no numbers behind it.

## 6. Bounded staleness — the dial between strong and eventual

There is a rung the textbook spectrum often omits but that production systems lean on constantly: **bounded staleness**. It sits between strong and eventual and turns "stale reads are possible" into "stale reads are possible *but never by more than X*." Instead of a binary "fresh or who-knows," you put a *bound* on how far behind a read can be — measured in time (no more than 5 seconds stale) or in versions (no more than 100 writes behind). Azure Cosmos DB exposes exactly this as a named consistency level (`Bounded Staleness`, configured with a lag bound in time or operations), and it is one of the most practically useful settings on the whole ladder because it lets you *quantify* the risk you are accepting instead of leaving it open-ended.

Why does bounding matter so much to an architect? Because an unbounded stale read is un-reasonable-about — you cannot put it in an SLO, you cannot reassure a stakeholder, you cannot test against it. A *bounded* stale read is a number you can defend: "this dashboard is at most 10 seconds behind reality, and the business has agreed 10 seconds is fine for a metrics view." That sentence is fundamentally different from "this dashboard might be arbitrarily stale," even though both are weaker than strong consistency. The bound converts a vague risk into a contract. And the bound is *tunable*: tighten it (more synchronous replication, more coordination, higher latency) when the product needs fresher data; loosen it (more async, less coordination, lower latency) when it can tolerate more lag. You are dialing the consistency-latency trade-off continuously rather than picking a discrete rung.

Operationally, bounded staleness is enforced by *measuring lag and refusing reads that would exceed the bound*. A replica that has fallen more than the bound behind the leader stops serving reads (it is "too stale to trust") until it catches up, or the read is redirected to a fresher replica or the leader. This is the same machinery as read-your-writes — a version/time watermark and a routing decision — generalized from "caught up to *my* write" to "caught up to within *X* of the leader." The cost is that during a lag spike, replicas that blow the bound drop out of the read pool, shifting load onto fresher replicas and the leader; you trade some availability headroom for a freshness guarantee. The senior use: set the bound to the largest value the product genuinely tolerates (not the smallest you can imagine), because every notch tighter costs you availability and latency during lag spikes.

Bounded staleness is also the honest way to describe most "eventually consistent" systems that work well in practice. A team that says "we're eventually consistent" but runs p99 replication lag at 12ms and alarms at 1s is, in operational reality, running *bounded staleness with a ~1s bound* — they just haven't named it. Naming it is the upgrade: it turns an implicit hope into an explicit, monitored, defendable bound. When you write a design doc, prefer "bounded staleness, 1-second SLO, alarmed" over "eventual consistency" wherever you actually have a measured lag bound, because the former is a commitment and the latter is a shrug.

## 7. Does this operation need strong consistency? A decision walk

Now the core architect's skill: placing an operation on the spectrum. The good news is that the decision is short and mostly routes *downward*. Figure 5 is the decision tree I actually use in reviews. It has two questions and three outcomes, and the design is deliberate: it makes you *justify* climbing up rather than assuming you must.

![A decision tree starting from a new operation, branching on whether a wrong value costs money toward linearizable, otherwise branching on whether the user must see their own write toward session guarantees, otherwise reaching eventual consistency](/imgs/blogs/consistency-models-a-practical-guide-for-architects-5.webp)

**Question 1: would a wrong or stale value here cost real money, double-spend a resource, or break a hard invariant like uniqueness?** If yes — a payment, an inventory decrement that must not oversell, a username that must be globally unique, a seat that must not be double-booked — you need **linearizable** consistency for *that operation*, and you pay the quorum round trip. There is no cheaper correct option, because these operations have an invariant that a stale or conflicting read can violate in a way that causes irreversible harm. Note "for that operation" — you are not making the whole system strong, just the path that touches the invariant.

**If no, Question 2: does the user need to see their own writes reflected immediately?** If yes — a profile edit, a posted comment, a setting change, anything where "I just did that and it should show up" is a reasonable user expectation — you need **session guarantees** (read-your-writes plus monotonic reads). This is the middle of the tree and the destination for a huge fraction of operations. It costs routing, not coordination.

**If no to both — eventual consistency is enough.** A like count, a view counter, a "trending" list, a recommendation feed, an analytics aggregate, a presence indicator. The value is cosmetic or statistical; a brief wrong number or a slightly stale list harms nobody. Take the cheapest, fastest, most available model and move on.

The reason this tree is shaped to push downward is that the failure mode in real teams is *over*-consistency, not under-consistency. Left to instinct, engineers route everything to the top because it feels safe, and then the system is slow and fragile and nobody can say which operations actually needed it. The tree forces the question "what specifically breaks if this is stale?" and most of the time the honest answer is "nothing a user would notice," which routes you down. When you *do* land on linearizable, you land there with a *reason* you can defend — "this is the inventory decrement, overselling is a refund and an angry customer" — rather than a vague unease.

One refinement for the staff-level reader: causal consistency is the missing fourth outcome that the two-question tree compresses away. Reach for causal (above session, below linearizable) when the product has *cross-user* ordering requirements that session guarantees don't cover — comment threads that must stay coherent across users, messaging where notification-before-message would be a bug, collaborative apps where one user's action must be seen in order by others. Causal is "session guarantees, but the ordering holds across users, not just within one session." It is the right answer more often than people reach for it, precisely because it is the strongest model that stays available during a partition.

## 8. Causal consistency and why it is often "enough"

Causal consistency deserves its own section because it is the most underused rung on the ladder and the one that most often turns out to be the *right* answer for interactive, multi-user products. The reason it is underused is that engineers reach past it: they assume that if session guarantees aren't enough, they need to jump all the way to linearizable. Causal is the rung in between, and it is frequently exactly enough — with the enormous bonus that it is the *strongest* model compatible with staying available during a partition.

![A dependency graph where a created post produces two replies that read it, a third reply reads both the post and the first reply, and replicas display all of them in causal order](/imgs/blogs/consistency-models-a-practical-guide-for-architects-6.webp)

Figure 6 shows what causal consistency tracks. A post `P` is created. Reply `A` and reply `B` both *read* `P` before they were written, so each causally depends on `P`. Reply `C` read both `P` and reply `A`, so `C` depends on both. The graph of these "read-before-write" dependencies is the *happens-before* relation, and causal consistency's whole promise is that every replica applies writes in an order consistent with this graph: no replica ever shows `A` before `P`, or `C` before `A`. But `A` and `B` are *concurrent* — neither read the other — so a replica is free to show them in either order, and different users might see different orderings of `A` and `B`. That freedom is exactly what lets causal consistency avoid the coordination that linearizability requires: it only constrains the orderings that *causally matter*, and leaves concurrent operations unordered.

Why is this so often enough? Because the anomalies users actually notice and complain about are almost all *causality* violations, not *total-order* violations. Nobody is harmed if two strangers' comments appear in a different order on two phones. People *are* confused and annoyed when a reply shows before the post, a notification arrives before the thing it notifies about, or an edit is visible but the thing it edited isn't. Those are all causal violations, and causal consistency prevents every one of them — while still letting every write commit locally and the system stay available through a partition. You get the anomaly-prevention that matters and you keep the availability and latency that matter, and you give up only the orderings nobody can perceive.

The implementation cost, as noted, is dependency metadata. Each write carries a compact summary of what it causally depends on — historically a vector clock, in modern systems often a more compact dependency representation — and replicas buffer a write until its dependencies have arrived. Real systems expose this: MongoDB's causal consistency uses a per-session `afterClusterTime` token so reads wait until the replica has applied everything up to that logical time; the COPS and Eiger research systems demonstrated causal consistency at scale with explicit dependency tracking. The metadata grows with the number of distinct things you depend on, which is why production systems bound it (track dependencies coarsely, garbage-collect old ones, or use logical-timestamp summaries instead of full vectors).

The senior heuristic: **for any interactive multi-user feature, default to causal and only climb to linearizable for the specific operations with a money/uniqueness/inventory invariant.** Causal gives you coherent, available, low-latency behavior for feeds, threads, messaging, and collaboration, and you reserve the expensive top rung for the handful of operations that genuinely cannot tolerate a stale or reordered read.

## 9. Eventual consistency done right — convergence and conflict resolution

Eventual consistency gets a bad reputation, and the reputation is earned — by everyone who deployed it without designing for convergence. Done right, eventual consistency is the highest-performing, most-available model and the correct default for a large class of features. The difference between "done right" and "done wrong" is entirely whether you answered two questions in advance: *how do conflicting writes get reconciled?* and *how long until replicas converge?* Skip those and "eventual" becomes a euphemism for "we lose data and don't know it."

The conflict problem is the crux. Under eventual consistency, two replicas can each accept a write to the same key during a partition. When the partition heals, they have two different values and must converge to one. Your reconciliation strategy decides what happens, and there are three honest options:

**Last-writer-wins (LWW):** attach a timestamp to each write and keep the one with the higher timestamp. Dead simple, and it *silently discards* the other write. If two users edited the same document during a partition, one user's edits vanish with no error. LWW is acceptable only when losing a concurrent write is genuinely fine (a "last seen" timestamp, a cache entry) and dangerous everywhere else. It also depends on clock synchronization — skewed clocks make LWW lose the "wrong" write.

**Conflict-free replicated data types (CRDTs):** data structures designed so that concurrent updates *merge deterministically* with no lost writes, regardless of order. A grow-only counter (G-Counter) sums per-replica increments, so two concurrent increments both count — perfect for a like counter that must not lose likes during a partition. An OR-Set handles add/remove with add-wins semantics. CRDTs are correct and require no coordination, but they constrain you to data types that *have* a merge function; you cannot CRDT an arbitrary invariant. Where they fit (counters, sets, registers, collaborative text), they are the gold standard for eventual consistency.

**Application-level merge:** keep both conflicting versions (siblings) and hand them to application code to reconcile — the approach Dynamo and Riak made famous with vector clocks and sibling values. Maximally flexible (you can merge a shopping cart by unioning its items, which is why "deleted items come back in your Amazon cart" is the textbook example of a deliberate merge choice), but it pushes complexity into the application and requires every read path to handle siblings.

```python
# A grow-only counter CRDT: per-replica counts that merge by taking the max
# of each replica's contribution, so concurrent increments are never lost.
class GCounter:
    def __init__(self, replica_id):
        self.replica_id = replica_id
        self.counts = {}                      # replica_id -> count

    def increment(self, n=1):
        self.counts[self.replica_id] = self.counts.get(self.replica_id, 0) + n

    def value(self):
        return sum(self.counts.values())      # the converged total

    def merge(self, other):
        # Convergent: take the max per replica. Commutative, associative,
        # idempotent -> any merge order yields the same result. No lost writes.
        for rid, c in other.counts.items():
            self.counts[rid] = max(self.counts.get(rid, 0), c)
```

The second question — *how long until convergence?* — is where eventual consistency becomes operable. "Eventually" with no bound is unmanageable; "p99 convergence under 100ms, alarmed at 1s" is a system you can run. You measure replication lag continuously, you alert when it exceeds your SLO, and you understand that your real anomaly window is that lag distribution. Anti-entropy mechanisms — read repair, hinted handoff, Merkle-tree-based background reconciliation — are how leaderless stores bound convergence and heal divergence; their mechanics live in the [quorums, anti-entropy, and read-repair deep-dive](/blog/software-development/database/quorums-anti-entropy-and-read-repair). The architect's contribution is to *set the convergence SLO*, choose the reconciliation strategy that matches the data, and make both visible on a dashboard. Eventual consistency with a named conflict strategy and a measured convergence bound is a precise, defensible engineering choice. Eventual consistency with neither is a liability waiting for a partition.

## 10. Consistency is not isolation — the distinction that confuses everyone

A staff-level clarification that resolves a surprising amount of design-review confusion: **consistency models and isolation levels are different axes, and people constantly conflate them.** Consistency (the spectrum in this post — linearizable, causal, eventual) is about the ordering and recency of operations on a *single object* across *replicas*. Isolation (the spectrum of serializable, snapshot, read-committed, read-uncommitted) is about how *concurrent transactions over multiple objects* interleave on a *single logical copy* of the data. They answer different questions, and a system has a position on *both* axes simultaneously.

The clearest way to see the difference: linearizability is about a *single object* and *real-time order*; serializability is about *multiple objects* in a *transaction* and *some* serial order (not necessarily real-time). A system can be serializable but not linearizable (transactions appear to run one at a time, but not necessarily in real-time order — you might commit a transaction and a later transaction doesn't see it). It can be linearizable but offer no multi-object transactions at all (every single-key operation is real-time ordered, but you cannot atomically touch two keys). The combination of both — serializable *and* linearizable — is called **strict serializability** (or external consistency), and it is the strongest guarantee a database can offer; Spanner is the famous example, and it pays for it with TrueTime's synchronized clocks, as the [Spanner deep-dive](/blog/software-development/database/spanner-truetime-and-external-consistency) explains.

Why does this matter to an architect? Because the anomalies you prevent live on *both* axes, and you must reason about both. Choosing eventual consistency does not free you from thinking about transaction isolation; if you do have transactions, you still pick an isolation level, and the [isolation-levels deep-dive](/blog/software-development/database/isolation-levels-and-the-anomalies-they-prevent) walks through which anomalies (dirty reads, non-repeatable reads, phantoms, write skew) each level allows. A common real-world configuration is "snapshot isolation for transactions, eventual consistency for replication" — strong-ish within a transaction on the primary, relaxed across replicas — and you need both halves of that sentence to describe the system honestly. When a teammate says "we're strongly consistent," the senior question is "do you mean linearizable single-object reads, or serializable transactions, or both?" — because those are three different commitments with three different costs.

The practical takeaway: keep the two axes separate in your head and in your design docs. Specify your consistency model (how replicas order single-object operations) *and* your isolation level (how concurrent transactions interleave) as two distinct decisions. Conflating them leads to designs that over-pay on one axis while ignoring an anomaly on the other.

## 11. The optimization lens — relax the hot path, reserve strong for the few

Here is where the whole post becomes an *optimization* discipline rather than a taxonomy. The senior principle restated as a performance strategy: **relax consistency on the hot read path, and reserve strong consistency for the few operations that genuinely need it.** Your hot path is where 95%+ of your traffic lives — feeds, timelines, product pages, search results, dashboards — and almost none of it has a money/uniqueness/inventory invariant. Charging that traffic for strong consistency is paying the maximum price on the highest volume, which is exactly backwards.

![A before and after where linearizable everywhere costs a quorum round trip and a ninety millisecond p99 and goes unavailable in a partition, while the weakest-that-works design serves local session reads at eight millisecond p99 and keeps only the money path strong](/imgs/blogs/consistency-models-a-practical-guide-for-architects-8.webp)

Figure 8 shows the optimization as a before/after. The "before" is the naive uniform design: linearizable everywhere, so every read pays a quorum round trip (~90ms cross-region p99) and the system goes unavailable on the minority side of every partition. The "after" is the senior design: the hot read path is relaxed to local session reads (read-your-writes via version token, served from the nearest replica) at ~8ms p99, while only the *write of record* — the ledger entry, the inventory decrement — keeps its linearizable quorum. The hot path p99 drops by roughly **11×**, the system stays available for reads during a partition, and the one operation that needed strength still has it. You did not weaken the thing that mattered; you stopped charging everything else for protection it never needed.

Figure 9 generalizes this into a layered view of where each consistency level lives in a request's path.

![A layered stack where the CDN edge serves eventual reads with a thirty second TTL, a read cache serves session-token reads, a service replica serves causal reads, and only the ledger write at the bottom is linearizable](/imgs/blogs/consistency-models-a-practical-guide-for-architects-9.webp)

Figure 9 is the architecture you are aiming for. A request relaxes consistency *layer by layer* as it travels inward, and only reaches the strong layer for the one operation that needs it. The **CDN edge** serves the cheapest, most-eventual reads (a 30-second TTL on a product page is fine — nobody notices a 30-second-stale price on a catalog page, and you offload the majority of read traffic to the edge for near-zero cost and near-zero latency). The **read cache** serves session-token reads (read-your-writes for logged-in users, so they see their own actions). The **service replica** serves causal reads for the interactive multi-user surfaces (threads, feeds). And only at the very bottom, the **ledger write**, do you pay for linearizable consistency. The latency budget is spent overwhelmingly on the cheap outer layers; the expensive inner layer is touched rarely. This is the mental model behind every well-optimized large system: *consistency is a resource you spend, and you spend it only where the product cannot survive without it.*

How to measure the win: track read **p50/p99 latency** per path, the **fraction of reads served from cache/edge** (your offload ratio), **availability** during induced partitions (chaos testing), and the **cost** of the quorum path in cross-region round trips. A successful relaxation shows up as p99 falling by a multiple, offload ratio climbing toward 90%+, and read availability staying at 100% through a partition — while the strong-path operations show *no* change in correctness. If you relax a path and a correctness metric moves, you relaxed the wrong path; back it out. The optimization is not "make everything eventual" — it is "make everything as weak as it can be *without moving a correctness metric*, and no weaker."

#### Worked example: relaxing a product-page read path

An e-commerce product page does ~**80,000 read QPS** at peak. Originally every read went to the primary database with a strongly consistent read (the team's default), giving a p99 of ~**70ms** and putting the primary at ~85% CPU — one bad query from a meltdown. Let us relax it.

The product page shows: price, description, stock-availability badge, and a "you viewed this" personalization strip. Decompose by invariant. **Price and description** change rarely and a 30-second-stale value harms nobody — push them to the **CDN edge** with a 30s TTL. That offloads the bulk of the 80,000 QPS to the edge at single-digit-millisecond latency and near-zero database cost. The **stock badge** ("In stock" / "Only 3 left") can be eventually consistent with a short cache — being briefly wrong shows "in stock" when there are 2 left, which is fine because the *actual* oversell protection lives at checkout, not on the page. Serve it from a **read replica** with read-your-writes for the seller's own inventory edits. The **personalization strip** is per-user and cosmetic — session-consistent read from cache.

Result: the **linearizable** read count on the product page drops from 80,000 QPS to **zero** — there is no invariant on a product page that requires it. The actual oversell-prevention linearizable operation moves to *checkout*, which runs at maybe **500 QPS** (the conversion-rate fraction of page views). The primary's read load collapses, p99 on the page drops to ~**6ms** (edge-served), and the one operation that needs strong consistency — decrementing inventory at purchase — pays the quorum round trip on 0.6% of the original traffic instead of 100%. You moved the strong consistency from where the *traffic* was to where the *invariant* was, and that single relocation is the entire optimization.

## 12. The trade-off matrix as a design artifact

Everything so far compresses into one table you can put in a design doc and defend in a review. Figure 7 maps the models to the real systems that implement them, so "we'll use causal consistency" becomes a concrete, configurable choice rather than an abstraction.

![A matrix mapping each consistency model to where it shows up in real systems and its typical configuration, from linearizable in Spanner and etcd down to eventual in Cassandra with the ONE consistency level](/imgs/blogs/consistency-models-a-practical-guide-for-architects-7.webp)

Figure 7 grounds the spectrum in systems you actually deploy. **Linearizable** is what you get from consensus-backed stores — Spanner, etcd, ZooKeeper, CockroachDB's default — paying the quorum round trip. **Causal** is a named, tunable level in MongoDB (via `afterClusterTime` causal-consistency sessions) and Azure Cosmos DB (which exposes causal as one of five explicit consistency levels you choose per account). **Session** guarantees are the default-ish middle in DynamoDB and Cosmos DB, configured via a session token the SDK carries for you. **Eventual** is Cassandra or DynamoDB at the weakest read level (`ONE` / eventually-consistent reads), async replicas answering from local state. The crucial fact this table encodes: **the model is a knob, not a product.** The same Cassandra cluster gives you eventual reads at `ONE` and effectively-linearizable reads at `QUORUM` for a key; the same DynamoDB table offers eventually-consistent and strongly-consistent reads per request. You choose per operation, which is the whole discipline.

Here is the decision matrix as a markdown table you can paste into a design doc. Each row is a model; the columns are what you GAIN, what you PAY, and WHEN it wins:

| Model | What you GAIN | What you PAY | WHEN it wins |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Linearizable** | Real-time recency; one global order; no anomalies on the object | Quorum round trip per op (~90ms cross-region); unavailable on minority during partition | Money, inventory, uniqueness, locks — any hard invariant where a stale/conflicting value is irreversible |
| **Causal** | Coherent cross-user ordering; available during partition; local-latency writes | Dependency metadata overhead; implementation complexity; concurrent ops unordered | Interactive multi-user features — threads, feeds, messaging, collaboration |
| **Session** | "See your own actions"; no backwards reads; nearly free | Sticky/version-aware routing; brief extra primary load post-write | The default for user-facing writes — profile edits, posts, settings, anything "I just did that" |
| **Eventual** | Lowest latency; total availability; cheapest to run | Stale reads; needs an explicit conflict + convergence strategy | Cosmetic/statistical data — counters, feeds, presence, analytics, trending lists |

The senior way to use this table: walk each *operation* (not each system) across it, place it on the row whose "WHEN it wins" matches, and write the one-sentence justification in the design doc. "Display-name update → Session, because users expect to see their own edit but a stranger seeing the old name for 200ms is invisible." "Inventory decrement at checkout → Linearizable, because overselling is a refund and an angry customer." "Like counter → Eventual with a G-Counter CRDT, because a momentarily-wrong count harms nobody and we must not lose likes during a partition." Four operations, four rows, four defensible sentences. That document *is* thinking like a senior, and it is what survives a design review.

## 13. Stress-testing the choice — what breaks at 10× and during a partition

A model choice is not finished until you have stress-tested it, because the whole point of consistency models is *behavior under adversity*. The senior move is to deliberately walk through the partition, the lag spike, and the hot key, and state what the user sees in each. Let us stress-test a system that has applied this post's principles: eventual+CRDT for counters, session for user writes, causal for feeds, linearizable for the money path.

**During a network partition** (a region split off from the others): the eventual paths (counters, feeds presence) keep serving from the local region with zero disruption — users in the cut-off region see slightly stale counts and a feed that doesn't include the other region's brand-new posts, which is invisible to them. The session paths keep working *within* a region (a user's own writes go to their region's primary and they read them back). The causal feed stays available and coherent within the region's causal history. The **linearizable money path goes unavailable on the minority side** — and *that is correct*: a payment that cannot reach a quorum *must* fail rather than risk a double-spend. The user trying to pay sees an error and retries; the user scrolling their feed sees nothing wrong. You have localized the unavailability to exactly the operation where unavailability is the safe choice, and kept everything else serving. That is the payoff of per-operation consistency.

**At 10× traffic** (a viral moment): the hot read path is edge/cache-served and scales horizontally — 10× reads is 10× cache nodes, no coordination, fine. The session and causal paths scale with read replicas. The pressure point is the **linearizable write path**, because it is the one path with coordination, and coordination does not shard for free. If the 10× hits the money path (a flash sale), you discover that a single linearizable counter or row is a serialization bottleneck — every write to it must be ordered, so they queue. The fix is the standard one (covered in the [partitioning post](/blog/software-development/system-design/partitioning-and-sharding-without-downtime)): shard the hot invariant so concurrent writes hit different shards, or restructure so the strong operation is per-item (per-SKU inventory) rather than global. The stress test *finds* this bottleneck in the design phase instead of in the incident.

**At a hot key** (one celebrity post getting all the likes): the eventual+CRDT counter shines — each replica accumulates likes locally and merges, so there is no single hot row and no contention; the like count is *designed* to absorb a hot key. Contrast a naive linearizable counter on the same key: every like is a serialized quorum write to one row, and a hot key melts it. The stress test confirms that the model choice (eventual CRDT for the counter) was right *because* it survives the hot key that the strong alternative would not. This is the deepest argument for picking the weakest model that works: the weak model is often not just cheaper but *more resilient* under exactly the load that breaks the strong one.

The discipline: for every consistency choice, write the three sentences — "during a partition the user sees X," "at 10× the bottleneck is Y," "at a hot key the behavior is Z." If you cannot write those sentences, you have not finished the design. If the sentences are acceptable, you have a defensible choice. If one of them is unacceptable, you have found a problem while it is still cheap to fix.

## 14. Case studies — consistency choices paid in production

Real systems make these trade-offs explicitly, and their public engineering writing is the best teacher. Here are three patterns, each illustrating one rung of the spectrum chosen for a concrete reason.

**A like-counter that needed only eventual.** Large social platforms universally treat engagement counters — likes, views, reactions — as eventually consistent, and many use CRDT-style or sharded-counter approaches so the count never blocks and never loses increments during a partition. The reasoning is exactly this post's: a like count is read enormously, written enormously, and *wrong-by-a-few is invisible to humans*. Charging it linearizable consistency would put a serialized quorum write on the single hottest key in the system — the celebrity post — and melt it. So the architecture is eventual: each replica increments locally, counts merge, and the displayed number converges within seconds. The lesson: the *highest-volume, hottest-key* operations are precisely the ones that most need to be weak, because the strong alternative cannot survive their load. Weak consistency here is not a compromise; it is the only thing that scales.

**A payments path that needed linearizable.** Payment systems like Stripe's are built on the opposite end of the spectrum for the operations that move money: a charge must be linearizable and idempotent, because double-charging a customer or losing a payment is an unrecoverable, trust-destroying error. The architectural discipline is to make the *ledger* — the record of money moving — strongly consistent and transactionally correct, while keeping everything *around* it (dashboards, analytics, notifications, the merchant's view of recent activity) eventually consistent and fast. They do not make the whole system strong; they make the *one operation with the money invariant* strong and relax the rest. Idempotency keys make the linearizable charge safe to retry, which is the companion technique to strong consistency on the write of record (covered in the [idempotency-by-design sibling post](/blog/software-development/system-design/idempotency-and-exactly-once-by-design)). The lesson: reserve linearizability for the *ledger*, not the whole product, and pair it with idempotency.

**A session-guarantee fix for a "my edit vanished" complaint.** The most common production consistency fix is not a consistency-model change at all — it is adding read-your-writes routing to an existing eventually-consistent store. Teams running read-replica architectures (a primary plus async replicas) repeatedly discover that users complain their just-saved changes don't appear, because the post-write read load-balanced to a lagging replica. The fix is the cheap one from Section 4: carry a write version in the session and route post-write reads to a replica caught up to that version (or briefly to the primary). This is how mature systems make eventual consistency *feel* strong to each individual user without paying for global strong consistency — the user's own timeline is coherent, everyone else's reads stay fast and local. The lesson: the most-reported anomaly has the cheapest fix, and reaching for strong consistency to solve it is the expensive mistake a senior prevents.

The thread through all three: **none of these systems is uniformly consistent or uniformly eventual.** Each is a deliberate *mixture*, with the strong, expensive consistency placed surgically on the few operations that need it and weak, cheap consistency everywhere else. That mixture — not a single global choice — is what production-grade consistency design looks like.

## 15. When to reach for each model (and when not to)

A decisive recommendation section, because "it depends" is only useful if you name what it depends on.

**Default to eventual** for any data that is cosmetic, statistical, or aggregate — counters, feeds, trending lists, presence, recommendations, analytics, search results, catalog pages behind a short TTL. Pick a conflict strategy (CRDT for counters/sets, LWW only where losing a write is fine, app-merge for carts) and set a convergence SLO. *Do not* default to eventual for anything with a hard invariant, and *do not* deploy eventual without a named conflict-resolution strategy — that is the failure mode that loses data silently.

**Reach for session guarantees** the moment a user-facing write needs to be visible to its author — profile edits, posted comments, settings, drafts, "my orders" lists. This is the right answer for most logged-in write paths, and it is nearly free. *Do not* reach past it to strong consistency to fix a "my update vanished" complaint; that is the expensive over-correction. The complaint is read-your-writes, and read-your-writes is the fix.

**Reach for causal consistency** for interactive multi-user surfaces where cross-user ordering matters — comment threads, group messaging, collaborative editing, activity feeds with reply structure. It is the strongest model that stays available during a partition, so it is the right ceiling for "this must be coherent across users but must never go down." *Do not* skip it and jump to linearizable for these — you will pay availability and latency you did not need to.

**Reach for linearizable** only for operations with a money/uniqueness/inventory/lock invariant where a stale or conflicting value causes irreversible harm — payments, inventory decrements that must not oversell, unique-username allocation, distributed locks and leader election, the financial ledger. Pay the quorum round trip, accept the partition-time unavailability (it is the *safe* failure), and pair it with idempotency for retry safety. *Do not* make the whole system linearizable; surgically place it on the ledger and relax everything around it.

The meta-rule: **start at the bottom of the ladder and climb only as far as the named anomaly forces you.** If you cannot name the user-visible anomaly that the weaker model allows and argue that it is unacceptable, you have not justified climbing. The default direction is down, toward cheap and available, and every step up needs a defended reason.

## Key takeaways

- **Pick the weakest consistency model that still meets the product requirement.** Strength is a cost — coordination, latency, partition-time unavailability — not a virtue. Start at eventual and climb only as far as a named, unacceptable user-visible anomaly forces you. Over-consistency, not under-consistency, is the common failure in real teams.
- **Classify the operation, not the database.** The same store wants opposite models for a like counter (eventual) and a payment (linearizable). Walk each operation across the decision tree and write a one-sentence justification; that document is thinking like a senior.
- **Session guarantees are the highest-leverage move on the spectrum.** Read-your-writes plus monotonic reads fix the most-reported anomaly ("my own update vanished") for the cost of a version token and a routing rule — roughly 1/1000th the cost of switching the path to strong consistency. Reach for them before anything stronger.
- **Causal consistency is the underused middle and is often "enough."** It prevents exactly the anomalies users notice (reply-before-post, notification-before-message) while staying available during a partition and committing writes at local latency. It is the right ceiling for interactive multi-user features; reach for it before linearizable.
- **Eventual consistency is only "done right" with a named conflict strategy and a measured convergence SLO.** CRDTs for counters/sets (no lost writes), LWW only where losing a concurrent write is fine, app-merge for flexible cases. Without those, "eventual" means "we silently lose data during partitions."
- **Consistency is not isolation.** Consistency orders single-object operations across replicas; isolation orders multi-object transactions on one copy. A system has a position on both axes; strict serializability is the rare top of both. Specify them as two separate decisions.
- **Relax the hot read path, reserve strong for the few operations that need it.** 95% of traffic has no hard invariant; charging it strong consistency pays the maximum price on the highest volume. Move strong consistency from where the *traffic* is to where the *invariant* is — that relocation is the core optimization.
- **The weakest model is often the most resilient, not just the cheapest.** The hottest-key, highest-volume operations (the celebrity like count) are exactly the ones a strong model cannot survive; a CRDT counter absorbs the hot key that would melt a linearizable row. Weak consistency here is the only thing that scales.
- **Stress-test every consistency choice with three sentences:** what the user sees during a partition, where the bottleneck is at 10×, and how it behaves at a hot key. If you can't write those, the design isn't finished; if one is unacceptable, you found a problem while it's still cheap.
