A broker is a machine, and machines die in boringly predictable ways. The kernel panics. The disk fills. A network cable to the top-of-rack switch flaps and one host vanishes from half the cluster. A garbage-collection pause runs forty seconds and the controller decides the broker is dead while the broker itself thinks it is perfectly healthy. Every one of these is routine, and every one of them lands you in a different failure regime with a different correct response. The tragedy of broker failures is that the *wrong* response — the one that feels like restoring service — is frequently the one that silently destroys data. The day you flip `unclean.leader.election.enable=true` to get a partition back online is the day you may have thrown away fifty thousand acknowledged records, and the dashboards will go green and nobody will know until a customer notices a gap three weeks later.

This post is a field guide to the four ways a broker dies and how to survive each one. First, the **plain outage**: a single broker goes down and its partition leaderships migrate to in-sync followers. A controlled shutdown migrates them gracefully with a blip; a hard crash forces an election and a few seconds of unavailability. Second, **split-brain**: a network partition cuts the cluster so two halves could each believe they are in charge — and why a majority quorum, whether KRaft or ZooKeeper, makes that impossible, plus the one cluster topology that defeats the quorum and re-opens the door. Third, **unclean leader election**: the moment every in-sync replica for a partition is down and you must choose, with no good options, between availability and durability. Fourth, **resource exhaustion** — the classic "the day the disk filled up," where retention or quotas are misconfigured, the disk climbs to 100%, the broker stops accepting writes, and replication faithfully amplifies the problem across the cluster.

![A two-column figure contrasting a naive cluster where a network partition produces two leaders that diverge against a quorum cluster where the minority side stops and only the majority makes progress](/imgs/blogs/broker-outages-split-brain-unclean-leader-election-1.webp)

By the end you will be able to look at an alert that says "broker 3 is down" and know, within seconds, which of these four regimes you are in, what is happening to your data right now, and what the correct move is — including the moves that look like inaction and are correct anyway. This is the operational complement to three posts you should read alongside it: [Kafka replication, the ISR, acks, and durability](/blog/software-development/message-queue/kafka-replication-isr-acks-durability) gives you the replication machinery these failures act on; [Durability and disaster recovery](/blog/software-development/message-queue/durability-and-disaster-recovery-for-message-queues) covers backups and cross-region recovery for the cases where a single cluster cannot save you; and [Cluster operations](/blog/software-development/message-queue/cluster-operations-scaling-upgrades-partition-reassignment) covers the planned-change side — reassignments, upgrades, and the controlled shutdowns that turn a scary failure into a non-event. Figure 1 frames the whole post: the difference between a cluster that survives a partition and one that diverges into two timelines is one design decision, the quorum, and we will spend a full section on why it works and when it does not.

## 1. The ways a broker dies

Before we treat each failure, it helps to name the regimes precisely, because the single most expensive operational mistake is treating one regime as if it were another. An operator who treats a split-brain as a plain outage and "helps" by restarting the isolated minority can corrupt metadata. An operator who treats an all-replicas-down partition as a plain outage and waits patiently can leave a critical partition offline for an hour when a five-minute manual election would have been correct. The regimes look similar from the outside — a partition is unavailable, a broker is unreachable — but the underlying state and the correct response are different, and the cost of confusing them is measured in either data loss or downtime.

A broker can fail in essentially four ways, and they form a clean taxonomy along the axis of *what resource or guarantee broke*. The first is a **process or host failure** — the broker dies, fast or slow, but the rest of the cluster can still see each other and reach the controller. This is the easy case: replication did its job, an in-sync replica is sitting right there, and recovery is a leader election that takes seconds. The second is a **network failure** that does not kill any broker but cuts the cluster into pieces that cannot all see each other. Now the danger is not "is there a surviving replica" but "could two pieces each elect a leader and diverge" — split-brain. The third is a **consensus or metadata failure**, which is really split-brain's nastier cousin: the cluster cannot form a majority quorum to agree on who the controller is, so it cannot make *any* metadata progress, and partitions freeze even though their data brokers are alive. The fourth is **resource exhaustion** — disk, memory, file handles, or network buffers run out, and the broker degrades or dies from the inside while looking, from the network's point of view, perfectly reachable right up until it falls over.

![A two-column before-and-after figure showing leadership migration where a leader broker goes down and an in-sync follower is promoted to leader with no data loss](/imgs/blogs/broker-outages-split-brain-unclean-leader-election-2.webp)

These four regimes are not equally dangerous, and the danger is *inversely* correlated with how dramatic they look. A hard crash is loud — pages fire, a host disappears, dashboards turn red — and it is the *least* dangerous, because replication handles it. The disk slowly filling is quiet — a gauge creeps up over days — and it is among the *most* dangerous, because by the time it is loud the broker is already refusing writes and replication has spread the pain. The whole discipline of operating a broker fleet is learning to fear the quiet failures more than the loud ones, and to build the alerting that makes the quiet ones loud while there is still time to act. Throughout this post I will assume a Kafka-shaped system with per-partition leader-follower replication and a quorum-based metadata layer, because that is the dominant architecture and the one whose failure modes are best documented; the principles transfer directly to Pulsar (with BookKeeper as the replicated store) and to RabbitMQ quorum queues (which use Raft per queue), and I will note the differences where they matter.

### Why the taxonomy matters operationally

The reason to internalize the taxonomy is that your first action in an incident is determined by *which box you are in*, and you have to decide that box from incomplete information under time pressure. The diagnostic questions are short. Can the surviving brokers reach each other and the controller? If yes, you are in regime one (outage) and the fix is automatic or a manual clean election. If no — if brokers are alive but cannot all see each other — you are in regime two or three (split-brain or quorum loss), and the correct action is almost always to *not touch the minority* and let the majority carry the cluster while you fix the network. Is the unreachable broker actually down, or is it up but its disk is full or it is in a death-spiral GC pause? That is regime four, and the fix is to relieve the resource, not to restart blindly. We will build the full diagnostic flow in the recovery playbook at the end; for now, hold onto the idea that naming the regime correctly is ninety percent of responding correctly.

## 2. Broker outage: leadership migration

Start with the easy and common case, because understanding it cleanly makes everything else easier: a single broker goes down in a healthy cluster. The other brokers are fine, they can all see each other, the controller is up. What happens to the partitions that broker was leading?

Recall from [Kafka replication, the ISR, acks, and durability](/blog/software-development/message-queue/kafka-replication-isr-acks-durability) that leadership is a *per-partition* role. A broker that dies was the leader of some partitions and a follower of others. For the partitions it merely followed, almost nothing happens — those partitions keep serving from their existing leaders, now with one fewer replica until the dead broker returns. The interesting work is for the partitions the dead broker *led*. Each of those needs a new leader, and the new leader must come from the partition's in-sync replica set, because only an ISR member is guaranteed to have every acknowledged record. The controller detects the broker's death, and for each affected partition it picks a new leader from the surviving ISR members and broadcasts the new leadership to the cluster. Producers and consumers, which were talking to the old leader, get a `NOT_LEADER_FOR_PARTITION` error, refresh their metadata, discover the new leader, and reconnect. Figure 2 shows this migration: the old leader is gone, an in-sync follower is promoted, and because that follower was in the ISR it holds every committed record, so no acknowledged data is lost.

The promotion is cheap because the new leader already has the data. This is the entire payoff of replication and the ISR: the follower has been fetching from the leader continuously, it is byte-for-byte caught up to the high watermark, so "becoming leader" is just a role change, not a data copy. The follower starts accepting produce requests, the other surviving followers redirect their fetcher threads to the new leader, and the partition is live again. The whole thing, for a single partition, takes milliseconds once the controller acts. The latency you actually observe is dominated not by any one partition's election but by *detection* and by the *number* of partitions that have to migrate, which we will quantify in the next section.

You can watch this migration happen in real time. The single most important number during an outage is the count of under-replicated and offline partitions, and a controlled one-liner tells you whether the cluster is healing or stuck:

```bash
# Partitions that have fewer in-sync replicas than their replication factor.
# Climbs the instant a broker dies, then drains back to 0 as the cluster heals.
kafka-topics.sh --bootstrap-server broker1:9092 \
  --describe --under-replicated-partitions

# The scary one: partitions with NO leader at all (offline). On a healthy
# outage this stays at 0 because an ISR member is always promoted. A non-zero
# count here means you are at the unclean-election fork, not a plain outage.
kafka-topics.sh --bootstrap-server broker1:9092 \
  --describe --unavailable-partitions
```

The mental model for an outage is: under-replicated partitions spike and then drain (good, the cluster is re-replicating); offline partitions stay at zero (good, every partition kept a leader). If offline partitions go non-zero, you have left the plain-outage regime and entered the all-ISR-down regime from section 6, and the response changes completely. Wiring these two counts into your alerting — `UnderReplicatedPartitions` and `OfflinePartitionsCount` are both exposed as broker JMX metrics — is the difference between an outage you watch heal and one you discover too late.

### How leadership is detected and reassigned

The controller learns a broker is dead through the cluster's liveness mechanism. Under ZooKeeper, each broker holds an ephemeral znode and a session; when the broker's session times out (governed by `zookeeper.session.timeout.ms`, commonly 6,000 to 18,000 milliseconds), ZooKeeper deletes the ephemeral node and the controller, watching that path, is notified the broker is gone. Under KRaft, the modern replacement for ZooKeeper, brokers send periodic heartbeats to the active controller, and a broker that misses heartbeats past `broker.session.timeout.ms` (default 9,000 milliseconds) is fenced — marked ineligible to lead. Either way there is a *detection delay*: the broker died, but the cluster does not know yet, and during that window the partitions it led are unavailable for writes because clients are still trying to reach a dead leader.

Once detection fires, the controller iterates the dead broker's led partitions and runs the preferred-leader logic. For each partition it consults the current ISR and chooses a new leader — by default the next replica in the partition's assigned replica list that is also in the ISR. It then bumps the partition's **leader epoch**, a monotonic counter that fences the old leader: any late-arriving request stamped with the stale epoch is rejected, which is the mechanism that prevents a "zombie" old leader from accepting writes after it has been superseded. The leader epoch is the quiet hero of clean failover; it is what guarantees that even if the old leader's process is somehow still running and reachable, it cannot corrupt the log, because every follower and the controller agree to only honor the current epoch. We will see in the unclean-election section what happens when this clean machinery has no ISR member to promote and the guarantee has to be broken on purpose.

## 3. Controlled shutdown vs hard crash

The same broker going down produces two very different experiences depending on *how* it goes down, and the gap between them is one of the highest-leverage operational facts in this whole post. A **controlled shutdown** — the broker is told to stop and gets to run its shutdown sequence — is nearly a non-event. A **hard crash** — the broker vanishes with no warning — costs you a detection delay plus an election storm. Knowing the difference, and arranging for as many of your broker stops as possible to be controlled, is the single cheapest reliability win available to a Kafka operator.

When a broker shuts down cleanly (`kafka-server-stop`, a `SIGTERM` to the process, a graceful node drain), and `controlled.shutdown.enable=true` (the default), the broker does something polite before it dies: it tells the controller "I am going away, please move my leaderships *now*." The controller proactively migrates every partition the broker leads to in-sync followers *before* the broker actually stops. From the clients' perspective there is a brief metadata refresh as leadership moves, but there is no detection delay — the cluster knew the broker was leaving because the broker announced it — and there is no scramble, because the migration is orchestrated rather than triggered by a timeout. A well-run rolling restart of an entire cluster, broker by broker with controlled shutdown, produces only small, brief latency blips per broker and zero unavailability if your topics have RF≥2 and `min.insync.replicas` leaves headroom.

A hard crash is the opposite. The broker is simply *gone* — kernel panic, power loss, an OOM-kill, a `SIGKILL`, an instance terminated out from under you. There is no goodbye. The cluster only finds out when the session or heartbeat times out, and that detection delay is pure unavailability for every partition the dead broker led: clients keep trying to reach a leader that is not answering, getting timeouts, until the controller declares the broker dead and elects new leaders. Then comes the election work itself, which for a broker that led hundreds or thousands of partitions is not instantaneous. The controller has to process all those leadership changes and propagate the new metadata to the cluster, and clients have to refresh and reconnect. Figure 3, the failure-mode matrix, lays the four regimes side by side on cause, impact, and prevention so you can see where controlled-vs-crash sits relative to the others.

![A matrix figure mapping four broker failure modes against their cause, impact, and prevention with color-coded severity cells](/imgs/blogs/broker-outages-split-brain-unclean-leader-election-3.webp)

#### Worked example: detection delay and election cost on a hard crash

Take a cluster of six brokers, RF=3, with 6,000 partitions total spread evenly, so each broker leads about 1,000 partitions (6,000 partitions, each with one leader, 6,000 leaderships over 6 brokers). Broker 3 is hard-killed. Under KRaft with `broker.session.timeout.ms=9000`, the controller will not declare broker 3 dead until roughly nine seconds after the last heartbeat — call the detection delay nine seconds in the worst case, four to five on average depending on where in the heartbeat interval the crash landed. During that window, every one of broker 3's ~1,000 led partitions is unavailable for writes: producers targeting them retry and stall. Once detection fires, the controller must elect new leaders for ~1,000 partitions. Modern KRaft controllers process leadership changes far faster than the old ZooKeeper controller — thousands of partition leadership changes per second rather than the tens-of-seconds stalls the ZooKeeper-era controller suffered on large clusters — so the election itself completes in well under a second for 1,000 partitions. The dominant cost is therefore the *detection delay*, not the election: roughly 5 to 9 seconds of write unavailability for the affected third of the cluster's partitions, after which everything is back, with no data loss because every new leader came from the ISR.

Now contrast a controlled shutdown of the same broker. Controlled shutdown migrates those ~1,000 leaderships proactively, with no detection delay because the broker announced its departure, so the affected partitions experience only a sub-second metadata refresh as leadership hands off, and producers with retries configured do not even surface an error to the application. The difference between the two scenarios — 5 to 9 seconds of partial write unavailability versus effectively none — is entirely a function of whether the broker got to say goodbye. This is why your deploy automation, your autoscaler's scale-in, your node-drain hooks, and your chaos-testing exception list should all route through controlled shutdown, and why an `OutOfMemoryError` that `SIGKILL`s the broker is so much worse than the same broker being told to stop: the OOM skips the goodbye.

The producer side of this matters too, because whether a 5-to-9-second hard-crash blip becomes an application error depends entirely on the producer's retry and timeout configuration. A producer that gives up after one attempt surfaces the outage to your application; a producer configured to retry through a leader change rides it out invisibly:

```properties
# Producer config that survives a leader change without surfacing an error.
acks=all
enable.idempotence=true
retries=2147483647
delivery.timeout.ms=120000     # total time to keep retrying a send
request.timeout.ms=30000       # per-attempt timeout, > a detection delay
retry.backoff.ms=100           # back off between metadata-refresh retries
max.in.flight.requests.per.connection=5   # safe with idempotence on
```

With `delivery.timeout.ms` set to two minutes and effectively unlimited `retries`, a `send()` that hits a `NOT_LEADER_FOR_PARTITION` during a 9-second detection delay simply retries, refreshes metadata, finds the new leader, and succeeds — the application never sees an error, only a slightly elevated send latency for the affected window. This is why the same hard crash can be a non-event for one team and a flood of failed requests for another: the cluster behaved identically, but one producer was configured to wait out the detection delay and the other was not. Tuning the producer to ride out a detection delay is the client-side complement to controlled shutdown on the broker side, and you want both.

### Making more of your stops controlled

The practical program here is to maximize the fraction of broker stops that are controlled. Set generous `controlled.shutdown.max.retries` and `controlled.shutdown.retry.backoff.ms` so a broker that hits transient resistance while migrating leadership keeps trying rather than giving up and dying hard. Make sure your process manager sends `SIGTERM` and waits — a common misconfiguration is a systemd unit or container runtime with too short a `TimeoutStopSec`/grace period, which sends `SIGTERM`, waits ten seconds, then `SIGKILL`s a broker that was halfway through a controlled shutdown, converting a clean stop into a hard crash at the worst possible moment. Give the broker a stop timeout comfortably larger than a controlled shutdown takes on your cluster (often 2 to 5 minutes for a heavily loaded broker). And test it: a chaos drill that does a controlled shutdown and measures client impact should show near-zero unavailability, and if it does not, your `min.insync.replicas` headroom or your retry config is wrong, and you want to find that out in a drill, not an incident.

## 4. Split-brain and why quorum prevents it

Now the genuinely dangerous regime, the one that has destroyed data in real systems that did not guard against it: split-brain. A network partition — not a broker dying, but the *network* between brokers failing — cuts the cluster into two or more groups that can each talk internally but cannot talk across the divide. The catastrophic outcome, if the system is naive, is that each side concludes the other side is dead, each side elects its own leaders and controller, and now you have two clusters writing to the same partitions independently. When the network heals, you have two divergent logs for the same partition and no principled way to merge them — some writes on one side, some on the other, conflicting offsets, irreconcilable. That is split-brain, and it is the failure that the entire quorum mechanism exists to make impossible.

The cure is a **majority quorum** for the metadata layer. The cluster's authoritative state — who is the controller, which broker leads which partition, what the ISR is — is owned not by individual brokers but by a quorum-replicated metadata store: ZooKeeper in the classic architecture, KRaft (a Raft quorum of controller nodes) in the modern one. The rule is simple and absolute: *you cannot make a metadata change, including electing a controller or a partition leader, unless you can reach a strict majority of the quorum.* A quorum of five nodes requires three to agree. A quorum of three requires two. When a network partition splits the cluster, at most one side can hold a majority — because a majority is more than half, and two disjoint groups cannot both be more than half of the same whole. The majority side keeps the controller, keeps electing leaders, keeps the cluster making progress. The minority side, no matter how many brokers it contains, *cannot reach a majority of the quorum*, so it cannot elect a controller, cannot change any leadership, cannot make progress. It freezes. And a frozen minority cannot diverge — it is not accepting authoritative writes against the metadata at all.

![A two-column figure contrasting a naive cluster where a network partition produces two competing leaders that both accept writes against a quorum cluster where the minority cannot reach a majority and stops](/imgs/blogs/broker-outages-split-brain-unclean-leader-election-1.webp)

Figure 1, which we met in the introduction, draws exactly this contrast. On the naive side, the partition splits the cluster and both halves elect a leader; both accept writes; the logs diverge; when the network heals there is no safe merge. On the quorum side, the same partition splits the cluster, but only the majority can form a quorum and elect leaders; the minority is blocked from making any authoritative change, so when the network heals the minority simply rejoins the one true timeline by catching up from the majority's log. The minority side experiencing a self-imposed outage is not a bug — it is the system *correctly choosing consistency over availability* for the isolated minority, exactly the tradeoff that [CAP theorem and PACELC](/blog/software-development/database/cap-theorem-and-pacelc) formalizes. A consistent system *must* make the minority unavailable; the alternative is divergence, which is worse than downtime because downtime is recoverable and divergence is not.

### What the data brokers do during a partition

![A branching figure showing a network partition splitting a five-node cluster into a majority side that elects a controller and keeps writing and a minority side blocked with no quorum](/imgs/blogs/broker-outages-split-brain-unclean-leader-election-8.webp)

Figure 8 traces the partition at the node level: the split sends three nodes to the majority side and two to the minority, the majority forms its quorum, elects a controller, and keeps writing, while the minority — alive but two-of-five — is blocked because it cannot reach a majority. There is a subtlety worth being precise about, because it is where people get confused: the quorum protects the *metadata*, and the data partitions inherit that protection through the leader epoch. Suppose broker 4 is on the minority side and was leading partition P. Broker 4 still has the data and is still *running*. Can it keep accepting writes to P? Only from clients that can still reach it — and here the leader epoch and the ISR save you. The majority-side controller, unable to reach broker 4, will fence it: it elects a new leader for P on the majority side from the ISR members it *can* reach, and bumps the leader epoch. Producers that can reach the majority now write to the new leader at the new epoch. Broker 4, on the minority side, is stuck at the old epoch; even if a stranded producer is still talking to it, broker 4 cannot advance the partition's committed offset because it cannot get acknowledgements from a quorum of in-sync replicas (its followers are on the other side of the partition), so with `acks=all` and a sane `min.insync.replicas`, those writes are never acknowledged — they sit unreplicated and are discarded when broker 4 rejoins and truncates to the majority's log. This is the deep reason `min.insync.replicas` is a *correctness* setting and not just a durability dial: it is what stops a stranded leader from accepting writes it cannot safely commit.

## 5. The even-cluster trap

The quorum is only as good as your ability to *form a majority*, and there is exactly one cluster topology that quietly defeats it: an even number of quorum members split evenly. This is the even-cluster trap, and it is responsible for a surprising share of "we had a quorum, why did everything still freeze" incidents. The rule that a majority is more than half has a sharp edge: if you have a quorum of *four* nodes and a network partition splits them two-and-two, *neither* side has a majority — three is the majority of four, and neither side has three. Both sides freeze. The entire cluster loses its metadata layer and grinds to a halt, even though every node is alive and four out of four are running. You traded the *divergence* of split-brain for the *total unavailability* of a quorum that cannot form on either side, which is better — no data loss — but it is a self-inflicted full outage that an odd-sized quorum would have avoided.

The fix is to **always run an odd number of quorum members** — three or five, essentially never two, four, or six. With a quorum of three, any partition leaves a majority on one side (3 splits into 2-and-1, and 2 is a majority) so the 2-side carries on. With five, a partition can be 3-and-2 or 4-and-1, and the larger side always has a majority. Odd sizing guarantees that *some* side can form a majority for any single partition, which is the property you want. Five is the sweet spot for production: it tolerates the loss of two quorum members and still functions (3 of 5 is a majority), whereas three tolerates only one. Going beyond five rarely pays — seven tolerates three failures but every metadata write must now reach four nodes, raising latency, and the marginal availability gain over five is tiny. The canonical advice is three for small or non-critical clusters, five for anything you care about, and never an even number.

![A failure-taxonomy tree rooting broker failures into network, consensus, and resource branches with leaf examples for each](/imgs/blogs/broker-outages-split-brain-unclean-leader-election-6.webp)

The even-cluster trap has a second, sneakier form that catches people who *did* size their quorum correctly: the quorum members co-located in too few failure domains. A five-node KRaft quorum spread across only two availability zones — say three nodes in AZ-A and two in AZ-B — looks healthy and is odd-sized, but if AZ-A goes dark you lose three of five at once and the surviving two cannot form a majority. The quorum's failure tolerance is bounded by the *worst-case correlated failure*, not the node count, so the placement rule is: spread the quorum across at least three failure domains so that no single domain holds a majority. Three AZs with a 2-2-1 split of a five-node quorum survives the loss of any one AZ (the worst case removes two nodes, leaving three, a majority). Figure 6 organizes all of this — the network, consensus, and resource families of failure — into a taxonomy so you can see the even-cluster trap as one specific branch of the consensus family, sitting alongside the lost-quorum and stale-controller failures it is related to.

#### Worked example: why a four-node quorum is strictly worse than three

Compare a three-node and a four-node controller quorum on the metric that matters, failure tolerance. Three nodes: majority is 2, so you tolerate 1 failure (one node down leaves 2, still a majority) and you cannot tolerate 2 (two down leaves 1, not a majority). Four nodes: majority is 3, so you *still* tolerate only 1 failure (one down leaves 3, a majority) and you cannot tolerate 2 (two down leaves 2, not a majority — and a 2-2 network split freezes both halves). So a four-node quorum tolerates exactly the same number of failures as a three-node quorum — one — while costing you an extra machine, an extra metadata write target on every change (slightly higher latency), and a brand-new failure mode in the even split. There is no configuration in which four beats three; four is three plus cost plus a trap. This is why every distributed-consensus system from ZooKeeper to etcd to KRaft tells you to use odd quorum sizes, and why "we added a fourth controller for extra safety" is one of the most common well-intentioned mistakes in the field.

## 6. Unclean leader election: availability vs durability

Now the choice with no good answer, the one that separates engineers who understand their durability guarantees from those who merely hope. Everything so far has assumed that when a leader dies, there is an in-sync replica ready to take over. But what if there is not? What if *every* in-sync replica for a partition is down — the leader and all its ISR followers, all of them, simultaneously? This is rare, because it requires multiple correlated failures (an entire rack or AZ, a bad deploy that crashes several brokers, a shared-fate storage outage), but rare is not never, and when it happens you are forced into the worst trade in distributed systems: availability versus durability, with no way to have both.

Here is the exact situation. Partition P has RF=3, replicas on brokers 1, 2, 3, with brokers 1 and 2 in the ISR and broker 3 having fallen out of the ISR an hour ago because it was lagging — say it is 50,000 records behind. A rack outage takes down brokers 1 and 2. Now the *only* surviving replica of P is broker 3, which is *out of sync*: it is missing the last 50,000 records that were acknowledged to producers and committed by the dead ISR. You have two choices and only two. Keep the partition **offline** and wait for broker 1 or 2 to come back, which preserves every acknowledged record but leaves the partition unavailable for as long as it takes — minutes, hours, or, if the dead brokers' disks are gone, forever. Or perform an **unclean leader election**: promote broker 3, the out-of-sync replica, to leader, which restores availability *immediately* but silently throws away the 50,000 records broker 3 never replicated. There is no third option. The data either survives and you wait, or you proceed and the data is gone.

![A two-column figure contrasting clean failover where an in-sync replica is promoted with no loss against unclean election where an out-of-sync replica is promoted and 50000 records are lost](/imgs/blogs/broker-outages-split-brain-unclean-leader-election-5.webp)

The knob that decides this is `unclean.leader.election.enable`, and Kafka's default has, correctly, been `false` since version 0.11 — meaning the partition stays offline and your data is preserved unless you explicitly opt into loss. Figure 5 contrasts the two paths: clean failover promotes an ISR member and loses nothing; unclean election promotes the out-of-sync survivor and loses the un-replicated tail. The danger of `true` is precisely that it makes the system *look* healthier — partitions recover automatically from even total-ISR-loss events — while it is quietly the setting that trades your durability guarantee away. A cluster running `unclean.leader.election.enable=true` does not have a "no acknowledged data loss" guarantee, full stop, no matter what its replication factor is, because the one scenario the guarantee is supposed to cover is exactly the one this knob abandons.

#### Worked example: quantifying the unclean-election trade at RF=3

Make the trade concrete. Partition P, RF=3, throughput 10,000 records/second, average record 1 KB, so 10 MB/s into this partition. Broker 3 fell out of the ISR and is 50,000 records behind — at 10,000 records/second that is a 5-second replication gap, which is plausible during a load spike or a GC pause that pushed broker 3 past `replica.lag.time.max.ms`. Then brokers 1 and 2 (the ISR) die in a rack outage. The choice:

- **Wait (`unclean...enable=false`).** The partition is offline. If broker 1 or 2 comes back in, say, 8 minutes (a reboot, a replaced instance attaching the same EBS volume), you lose *zero* acknowledged records — broker 1 or 2 holds the complete log, becomes leader, broker 3 truncates its divergent tail and re-replicates. Cost: 8 minutes of unavailability for partition P, during which producers either block, buffer, or shed load depending on their config. For an order-processing partition, 8 minutes of buffering is annoying but recoverable.
- **Proceed (`unclean...enable=true`).** Broker 3 becomes leader immediately — sub-second recovery. But broker 3 is missing 50,000 records (50 MB of data) that were acknowledged to producers. Those records are *gone*: when brokers 1 and 2 eventually return, they will *truncate their own logs down to broker 3's shorter log* to match the new leader, deleting the 50,000 records they correctly held, because the new leader's log is now authoritative. So the unclean election does not just expose missing data — it actively destroys good copies that survived, to enforce consistency with the lossy new leader. You traded 50,000 acknowledged records (potentially 50,000 orders, payments, or events) for 8 minutes of availability.

Which is right depends entirely on the partition's *purpose*, and that is the real lesson: this is a per-topic decision, not a cluster-wide one. For a topic of financial transactions, order events, or anything where a silent gap is a correctness violation, you want `false` — wait, page a human, accept the downtime, never lose a committed record. For a topic of high-volume telemetry, metrics, or click-stream where a 5-second gap during a once-a-year double-rack outage is genuinely fine and continuous availability matters more, `true` can be the right call. Kafka lets you set `unclean.leader.election.enable` per topic precisely so you can make this choice with the grain of the data, and the discipline is to make it *deliberately*, topic by topic, rather than flipping it cluster-wide in a panic during an incident — which is exactly when people flip it and exactly when they regret it.

The decision is clearer when you lay the two paths side by side against the dimensions that actually matter — recovery time, data loss, and the kind of topic each path suits:

| Dimension | `enable=false` (wait) | `enable=true` (proceed) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Recovery time | Until an ISR member returns (minutes to hours) | Sub-second, immediate |
| Acknowledged data loss | Zero | The un-replicated tail (e.g. 50,000 records) |
| Surviving ISR copies | Preserved, become the new truth | Truncated down to the lossy survivor |
| Right for | Payments, orders, anything where a gap is a bug | High-volume telemetry, metrics, clickstream |
| Failure visibility | Loud — partition stays offline, pages a human | Quiet — partition recovers, dashboards go green |
| Default since 0.11 | Yes (correct default) | No (opt-in only) |

The most dangerous row is the last behavioral one: `enable=true` is *quiet*, which is exactly why it is dangerous — it makes the worst data-loss event in Kafka look like a successful auto-recovery. You set this per topic, and you can change it without a restart:

```bash
# Durability-critical topic: never trade away committed data.
kafka-configs.sh --bootstrap-server broker1:9092 --alter \
  --entity-type topics --entity-name payments \
  --add-config unclean.leader.election.enable=false

# Loss-tolerant telemetry topic: availability outranks a rare short gap.
kafka-configs.sh --bootstrap-server broker1:9092 --alter \
  --entity-type topics --entity-name device-telemetry \
  --add-config unclean.leader.election.enable=true
```

### Recovering from an unclean election

If an unclean election fires (or you trigger one manually via `kafka-leader-election.sh --election-type UNCLEAN` during an incident), treat the data loss as real and reconcile it. The recovery is not "the partition is back, we're done" — it is "the partition is back, and we now have a known gap to account for." If you have a replicated copy of the stream elsewhere (a [Durability and disaster recovery](/blog/software-development/message-queue/durability-and-disaster-recovery-for-message-queues) target — a tiered-storage archive, a MirrorMaker replica in another region, or a downstream sink that captured the records before the loss), you can identify which offsets are missing and replay them from the surviving copy. If you have the transactional outbox or the upstream source of truth, you can re-derive the lost events. The worst position is having *no* second copy, in which case the records are simply gone and your only honest move is to quantify the gap (offsets, time window, record count) and communicate it. This is why the durability and DR work is the safety net under the unclean-election knob: with a second copy, an unclean election is a recoverable inconvenience; without one, it is permanent loss.

## 7. The day the disk filled up

Every Kafka operator has a story about the day the disk filled up, and they are all the same story, which is what makes it worth telling precisely. Kafka is a log; logs grow; the only thing standing between a topic and an infinite disk is retention, and retention is a setting that someone, someday, will change without doing the arithmetic. The failure is almost always self-inflicted and almost always preventable, and it is among the most damaging because of how it interacts with replication: a full disk does not just take down one broker, it cascades.

Here is the anatomy. Someone bumps a topic's retention — `retention.ms` from 7 days to 30 days, or `retention.bytes` removed entirely "to be safe" — to satisfy a request to keep more history, and nobody multiplies the daily ingest rate by the new retention to check whether the disk can hold it. The log starts retaining more, the disk usage climbs steadily over days, and because it climbs slowly nobody notices until it crosses a threshold. Then it hits the wall. When a Kafka log directory's disk fills, the broker cannot append to the log, and Kafka's response is deliberately drastic: the broker marks that log directory as failed (in older single-dir setups, it shuts the whole broker down) to avoid a corrupt half-write. Now a broker is down, and that broker was leading partitions, so those leaderships migrate to *other* brokers — which are storing replicas of the same fast-growing topics on *their* disks, so the load and the growth shift to brokers that are themselves close to full. The full disk on broker A accelerates the fill on brokers B and C. That is the cascade, and it is how a single misconfigured retention setting takes down a whole cluster within hours.

![A timeline figure tracing the day the disk filled from a retention bump through steady disk climb to broker stop and cluster cascade](/imgs/blogs/broker-outages-split-brain-unclean-leader-election-4.webp)

Figure 4 is the timeline of exactly this incident: retention bumped, disk climbs steadily, alert threshold crossed, broker stops on the full disk, leadership migrates and the cascade begins, and finally recovery once an operator intervenes. The shape of the curve is the lesson — it is a *slope*, not a spike, which means there is almost always a window of hours to days between "retention changed" and "broker down" in which an alert on disk *slope* (not just disk level) would have caught it. The reason these incidents still happen despite being slow is that most teams alert on a static threshold (page at 85% full) and not on the *rate of change*, so a disk that will hit 100% in six hours looks fine at 60% right up until it is suddenly at 90% and the runway is gone.

#### Worked example: the disk-fill timeline with numbers

A broker has a 2 TB log disk, currently 85% full — 1,700 GB used, 300 GB free. A topic's retention was just bumped from 7 to 30 days, and that topic ingests 120 GB/day *net* of what retention was already removing — that is, with the old 7-day retention the topic was in steady state, deleting old segments as fast as new ones arrived, but the longer retention means the deletes now lag the writes by 120 GB/day of additional retained data until the log reaches its new 30-day steady state (which it never will, because the disk fills first). At 120 GB/day of net growth against 300 GB of free space, the disk hits 100% in 300 ÷ 120 = 2.5 days. That is your runway: two and a half days from the retention change to the broker falling over.

Where do you intervene, and how much warning would each strategy have given you? A static "page at 95% full" alert fires at 1,900 GB used — that is 200 GB into the 300 GB of headroom, so (1,900 − 1,700) ÷ 120 = 1.67 days after the change, leaving you about 0.83 days (20 hours) of runway. Tight but survivable if someone responds. A static "page at 85%" alert *already fired* (we started at 85%) — so a level alert at a sane threshold should have caught this immediately, which tells you the real failure is that nobody acted on it or it was tuned too high. A *slope* alert — "disk will be full in under 72 hours at the current fill rate" — fires the moment the retention change takes effect and the slope jumps, giving you the full 2.5 days of runway and, crucially, pointing at the *cause* (the rate changed) rather than just the symptom (the level is high). The intervention itself is fast and reversible: revert the retention change (the broker will delete the now-over-retention segments and the disk will fall), or, if you genuinely need the 30-day history, add disk or move replicas off this broker via reassignment *before* the wall — but you can only do any of that if the alert gave you runway, which the slope alert does and the static level alert barely does.

The protective config is a small set of settings applied with intent. A `retention.bytes` ceiling per partition, sized so the partitions on a broker cannot collectively exceed the disk, is the backstop that time-based retention cannot give you:

```bash
# Cap each partition at 50 GB regardless of time, as a hard disk backstop.
# With N partitions on a broker, N * 50 GB must fit on the disk with headroom.
kafka-configs.sh --bootstrap-server broker1:9092 --alter \
  --entity-type topics --entity-name events \
  --add-config retention.bytes=53687091200,retention.ms=2592000000
```

```yaml
# Slope-based disk alert (Prometheus): page when the disk will fill in < 72h
# at the current fill rate. Fires on a retention change days before the wall,
# and points at the CAUSE (rate jumped), not just the symptom (level is high).
- alert: KafkaDiskWillFillSoon
  expr: |
    predict_linear(node_filesystem_avail_bytes{mountpoint="/var/lib/kafka"}[6h], 72*3600) < 0
  for: 15m
  labels: { severity: page }
  annotations:
    summary: "Kafka log disk projected to fill within 72h at current rate"
```

The `predict_linear` alert is the single highest-leverage piece of disk monitoring you can deploy, because it converts the *slope* into a *runway* and pages while there is still time to act, rather than pinging a static level that gives you hours when the slope gave you days.

### Why retention is a footgun and how to defang it

The defense is to make retention changes go through the arithmetic, every time. Treat `retention.ms` and `retention.bytes` like a capacity decision, because that is what they are: new retention times daily ingest must fit on the disk with headroom, and if it does not, the change must come with more disk or fewer replicas first. Prefer `retention.bytes` as a hard backstop even when you primarily manage by time — a `retention.bytes` ceiling per partition that, multiplied by the partitions on a broker, cannot exceed the disk gives you a guarantee that time-based retention alone cannot, because `retention.bytes` caps the log regardless of how the ingest rate changes. Set `log.retention.check.interval.ms` low enough that the cleaner actually enforces retention promptly (the default 5 minutes is usually fine). And reserve headroom: a disk run past about 80% on a log-structured store is asking for trouble, because compaction, segment rolls, and index files all need scratch space, so size for a steady-state ceiling around 70 to 75% and treat the rest as the buffer that gives your alerts time to work.

## 8. Memory and file-handle exhaustion

Disk is the famous resource failure, but it is not the only one, and the others are sneakier because they do not have a clean "the disk is full" gauge that everyone already watches. The two that bite hardest are memory pressure and file-handle exhaustion, and both can take a broker down while it still looks reachable and its disk looks fine.

![A layered stack figure of broker resource limits to watch from disk through memory and file handles to network buffers](/imgs/blogs/broker-outages-split-brain-unclean-leader-election-7.webp)

Memory pressure on a Kafka broker is rarely about the JVM heap directly, because Kafka deliberately uses a *small* heap (commonly 5 to 8 GB even on large brokers) and relies on the operating system **page cache** — off-heap, OS-managed memory — to make reads and writes fast. That design is a strength, but it creates two memory failure modes. The first is heap exhaustion leading to an `OutOfMemoryError`: if the heap is too small for the connection count, the partition count, or a request-handling burst, the broker throws an OOM, and a JVM OOM frequently corrupts the process state badly enough that the only safe response is to die — and it dies *hard*, skipping the controlled shutdown, converting a memory problem into the worst kind of crash (section 3). The second is page-cache thrash: if other processes or an over-large heap squeeze the page cache, Kafka's reads start missing cache and hitting disk, latency spikes, fetchers fall behind, followers drop out of the ISR, and you slide toward the all-ISR-down scenario from a *performance* problem rather than a crash. The fix for the first is sizing the heap for the workload (and setting `-XX:+ExitOnOutOfMemoryError` or `-XX:+CrashOnOutOfMemoryError` so an OOM produces a clean, fast, *diagnosable* exit rather than a half-dead broker limping along); the fix for the second is leaving the page cache alone — do not co-locate memory-hungry processes on broker hosts and do not over-size the heap at the page cache's expense.

File-handle exhaustion is the quiet killer that surprises people because nothing in the obvious dashboards points at it. A Kafka broker holds an open file descriptor for *every* log segment file and index file across *every* partition it hosts, plus one for every network connection. A broker hosting thousands of partitions, each with multiple segments and indexes, plus tens of thousands of client connections, can legitimately need *hundreds of thousands* of open file descriptors. The default per-process `ulimit` on many systems is 1,024 — comically, catastrophically too low for Kafka. When the broker hits the limit, it cannot open new segment files (so it cannot roll a log segment, which it must do constantly) and cannot accept new connections, and it fails with `Too many open files` errors that cascade into partition failures and, often, a broker shutdown. Figure 7 stacks these resource limits — disk, heap, page cache, file handles, network buffers — as the layers you must watch, because a broker is only as healthy as its *most* exhausted resource, and the one you are not watching is the one that takes you down.

The file-descriptor limit in particular is set in two places that must agree, and a mismatch is a classic trap: you raise the shell `ulimit` but the service runs under systemd, which ignores the shell and enforces its own much lower default. Both must be raised:

```ini
# systemd: /etc/systemd/system/kafka.service.d/limits.conf
# Without this, systemd caps the broker far below what the shell allows.
[Service]
LimitNOFILE=200000

# JVM flags so an OutOfMemoryError produces a clean, fast, diagnosable exit
# (a controlled crash you can restart) rather than a half-dead broker limping.
Environment="KAFKA_OPTS=-XX:+ExitOnOutOfMemoryError -XX:+HeapDumpOnOutOfMemoryError"
```

After applying it, verify the *running* process actually got the limit — checking the shell value proves nothing about what the broker process inherited:

```bash
# Confirm the broker PROCESS (not your shell) has the raised file-handle limit.
cat /proc/$(pgrep -f kafka.Kafka)/limits | grep "open files"

# Watch how many descriptors the broker is actually holding right now.
ls /proc/$(pgrep -f kafka.Kafka)/fd | wc -l
```

If the first command shows the old 1,024 even after you edited `limits.conf`, you forgot to `systemctl daemon-reload` and restart, or the broker runs under a manager that reads its limits from somewhere else — and discovering that during a quiet drill is enormously cheaper than discovering it when the broker is throwing `Too many open files` in production.

### Sizing the limits before they bite

The prevention is unglamorous and entirely effective: set the limits generously and monitor headroom on each. Raise the file-descriptor `ulimit` for the Kafka process to at least 100,000, and on large brokers to several hundred thousand — this is a one-line config (`LimitNOFILE` in the systemd unit, or `nofile` in `limits.conf`) and skipping it is one of the most common production Kafka mistakes. Monitor open file descriptors as a fraction of the limit and alert at, say, 70%, because the count grows with partitions and connections and you want warning before you hit the wall. Size the heap deliberately and watch GC pause time, because long GC pauses are both a latency problem and an ISR-eviction problem (a broker paused in GC for 30 seconds stops fetching and its followers think *it* is dead, or it gets fenced by the controller). And watch the page-cache hit rate indirectly through fetch latency and disk read throughput — rising disk reads on a workload that should be served from cache is the early sign of page-cache pressure. Every one of these is a *gauge with a known ceiling*, which means every one is alertable on headroom, which means every one is preventable with the discipline to watch the boring numbers.

## 9. Recovery and prevention playbook

Pulling the four regimes together, here is the operational playbook — first the recovery flow you run *during* an incident, then the prevention program you run *before* one. The recovery flow is structured around the diagnostic question from section 1: which regime am I in? Because the correct first action diverges sharply by regime, and the most expensive incidents are the ones where the responder acted as if they were in a different regime than they were.

The classification itself can be made nearly mechanical, and turning it into a runbook your on-call follows under stress is worth more than any single configuration change. The diagnostic sequence is short: check whether partitions are offline (`--unavailable-partitions`), check whether the controller quorum is healthy, and check the failing broker's resource gauges:

```bash
# 1. Are any partitions leaderless? Non-zero => all-ISR-down, NOT a plain outage.
kafka-topics.sh --bootstrap-server broker1:9092 --describe --unavailable-partitions

# 2. Is the controller quorum healthy and is there a majority? (KRaft)
kafka-metadata-quorum.sh --bootstrap-server broker1:9092 describe --status

# 3. On the suspect host, which resource is exhausted? (disk / fds / memory)
df -h /var/lib/kafka          # disk: is it at 100%?
ls /proc/$(pgrep -f kafka.Kafka)/fd | wc -l   # file handles near the ulimit?
```

Step 1 distinguishes a plain outage (offline count is zero, cluster self-heals) from the unclean-election fork (offline count is non-zero). Step 2 distinguishes a network or quorum problem (the quorum cannot reach a majority) from everything else. Step 3 distinguishes resource exhaustion (a gauge is pegged) from a genuine crash. Three commands, four regimes, and the correct first action falls right out of which one tripped. Wrapping these three checks in a single runbook script that your on-call runs the moment a broker-down page fires removes the slowest and most error-prone step in the whole response — the human deciding, under stress at 3 a.m., which kind of failure they are looking at.

When an alert says a broker is unreachable, your first job is to classify, not to act. Ask: can the surviving brokers reach each other and the controller quorum? If yes, you are in a **plain outage** — the cluster is already self-healing via leadership migration, your job is to confirm new leaders were elected from the ISR (so no data loss), watch the under-replicated-partition count climb and then recover as the cluster re-replicates, and either bring the dead broker back or replace it. Do *not* flip any durability knobs. If the surviving brokers *cannot* all reach each other — brokers are alive but the network is split — you are in **split-brain or quorum loss**, and the correct action is the counterintuitive one: leave the minority side completely alone, let the majority carry the cluster, fix the *network*, and let the minority rejoin and catch up automatically. The dangerous move here is "helping" the isolated minority by restarting it or, worse, forcing it to elect a controller, which is how you manufacture the divergence the quorum was preventing. If the quorum itself cannot form a majority (the even-cluster trap, or too many quorum members down), the cluster is frozen but *safe* — restore enough quorum members to reach a majority and it unfreezes with no data loss; never try to force progress past a broken quorum.

![A two-column figure contrasting an unmonitored cluster where the disk silently fills to an outage against a monitored cluster with quotas and slope alerts that prevents it](/imgs/blogs/broker-outages-split-brain-unclean-leader-election-9.webp)

If the partition is offline because *all* its in-sync replicas are down, you are at the **unclean-election fork** from section 6, and the decision is governed by the topic's `unclean.leader.election.enable` and, above that, by the topic's purpose. For durability-critical topics, wait for an ISR member to return (page for the dead brokers' recovery; consider whether their disks are recoverable); only perform a manual unclean election if you have decided, deliberately and ideally in advance, that this topic's availability outranks its tail of un-replicated records — and if you do, treat the loss as real and reconcile against a second copy. If the broker is unreachable because a **resource is exhausted**, identify which one (disk, heap, file handles) before restarting, because a blind restart of a disk-full broker just refills the disk and a blind restart of a file-handle-exhausted broker just re-exhausts the handles — relieve the resource (revert retention, raise the ulimit, add disk, move replicas off) and *then* recover. Figure 9 contrasts the two operating postures that determine whether you ever reach these incidents: an unmonitored cluster where the disk silently fills to an outage, versus one with quotas, slope alerts, and headroom that prevents the failure from ever becoming an incident.

The prevention program is the cheaper half and the half that determines how often you run the recovery flow at all. Run an odd-sized controller quorum (three or five) spread across at least three failure domains so no single domain holds a majority. Use RF≥3 with `min.insync.replicas=2` and `acks=all` on durability-critical topics so a single failure never forces a hard choice. Default `unclean.leader.election.enable=false` and override to `true` only per-topic, deliberately, for genuinely loss-tolerant data. Route every planned broker stop through controlled shutdown, and make sure your process manager's stop timeout is generous enough not to `SIGKILL` a broker mid-shutdown. Cap retention with `retention.bytes` backstops and alert on disk *slope*, not just level, so you get days of runway instead of hours. Raise `ulimit -n` to at least 100,000 and alert on file-descriptor headroom. Size the heap for the workload, set `-XX:+ExitOnOutOfMemoryError`, and leave the page cache room to breathe. And rehearse: a chaos drill that hard-kills a broker, splits the network, and fills a disk on a staging cluster turns all of this from theory into muscle memory, so that when it happens in production at 3 a.m. you classify the regime in seconds and reach for the correct action without thinking. None of this is exotic; it is the boring discipline of watching gauges with known ceilings and never trading away a guarantee in a panic.

## Case studies and war stories

**The retention bump that took out a cluster.** A team needed to keep 30 days of a high-volume event topic for a compliance audit and changed `retention.ms` from 7 days to 30 days across the board on a Friday afternoon. The topic ingested roughly 100 GB/day per broker net of the old retention, and the brokers' log disks were already at 70%. Nobody multiplied 100 GB/day by the extra 23 days of retention against the available headroom. Over the weekend the disks climbed; by Sunday night the first broker hit 100%, marked its log dir failed, and shut down. Its leaderships migrated to brokers that were *also* near full with the same growing topic, and within four hours three brokers were down in a cascade. The on-call engineer, paged into a half-dead cluster, correctly diagnosed it as resource exhaustion (not a "broker crash" to be restarted), reverted the retention change, and watched the cleaner delete the over-retention segments and the disks fall back under 75% over the next hour, after which the brokers came back cleanly. The lesson the team took away was the slope alert: they added a "disk full in under 72 hours at current rate" alert and a code-review rule that any retention change must include the capacity arithmetic in the change description.

**The four-node quorum that froze.** A team running a three-node ZooKeeper ensemble decided to "add redundancy" by adding a fourth node, reasoning that more nodes is more reliable. Months later a network event split their data center such that two ZooKeeper nodes were on each side. Neither side had the three-node majority of a four-node ensemble, so *both* sides refused to make progress, the Kafka controller could not be elected, no leadership changes could happen, and the entire cluster's metadata layer froze even though all four ZooKeeper nodes and all the Kafka brokers were alive. Production writes stalled cluster-wide for the duration of the network event. The post-mortem's single recommendation was to return to three nodes (or go to five if they wanted to tolerate two failures), and the broader lesson — that even-sized quorums tolerate the same failures as the next-smaller odd size while adding a split-brain freeze mode — became a standing review item for every consensus system the team ran.

**The unclean election nobody decided to make.** A cluster ran with the *old* Kafka default of `unclean.leader.election.enable=true` (the default before 0.11) on a topic carrying payment events, and nobody had ever revisited it. A bad deploy crashed two of the three brokers holding a critical partition's ISR within seconds of each other, leaving only an out-of-sync replica that had fallen behind during the deploy. With the unclean default, Kafka automatically promoted the out-of-sync replica, restored availability in under a second, and silently dropped the tail of payment events the survivor had not replicated. The dashboards went green. Three weeks later, finance reconciliation found a gap of several thousand payment records, and the team traced it back to that election. Because they had a transactional-outbox source of truth in their primary database, they were able to re-derive and re-emit the missing events — but only because that second copy existed. They set `unclean.leader.election.enable=false` cluster-wide, overrode it to `true` only on a handful of explicitly loss-tolerant telemetry topics, and added an alert on any unclean election firing so a human would always know it happened.

**The file-handle ceiling on a growing cluster.** A cluster that had run fine for a year started throwing `Too many open files` on its busiest broker as the team scaled partition counts and connection counts to handle growth. The broker would fail to roll log segments and refuse new connections, then shut down; restarting it bought a few hours before it hit the wall again. The default `ulimit -n` of 1,024 had been fine when the broker hosted a few hundred partitions and was now wildly insufficient at a few thousand partitions plus tens of thousands of connections. The fix was a one-line change raising `LimitNOFILE` to 200,000 in the systemd unit, plus a new alert on file-descriptor usage as a fraction of the limit. The lesson was that resource limits that were generous at launch become ceilings as the cluster grows, and every gauge with a fixed limit needs a headroom alert *before* it becomes a wall.

## When to reach for each response (and when not to)

Treat a **plain outage** as the cluster's problem to self-heal, not yours to fix: confirm ISR-based leader election happened, watch under-replicated partitions recover, replace the dead broker, and resist the urge to touch durability knobs. The trap is over-reacting — flipping unclean election or restarting good brokers — when the cluster was already recovering on its own.

Treat a **split-brain or quorum loss** as a network problem, and keep your hands off the metadata. Let the majority carry the load, fix the network, let the minority rejoin. The trap is "helping" the isolated minority, which is the one action that can manufacture divergence the quorum was preventing. Never force a controller election or restart the minority to "speed things up."

Reach for an **unclean leader election** only when you have *deliberately* decided that a specific topic's availability outranks its durability, ideally in advance via per-topic config, and never as a panic move during an incident on a durability-critical topic. The trap is flipping it cluster-wide to make red partitions go green; that is the move that silently destroys committed data. For anything where a gap is a correctness violation, wait — downtime is recoverable, the dropped tail is not.

Treat **resource exhaustion** as a sizing and monitoring discipline, not an incident response. The right time to handle a full disk is days before it fills, via slope alerts and `retention.bytes` backstops; the right time to handle file-handle exhaustion is at provisioning, via a generous `ulimit`. The trap is the blind restart, which just re-exhausts the resource. If you find yourself restarting a broker repeatedly and it keeps dying the same way, stop — you are in a resource regime and the restart is not the fix.

Across all four, the meta-rule is: classify before you act, and fear the quiet failures more than the loud ones. The loud failures — hard crashes — are handled by replication you already have. The quiet ones — the slowly filling disk, the even-sized quorum, the unclean default nobody revisited, the `ulimit` that became a ceiling — are the ones that destroy data, and they are all preventable with boring discipline applied before the incident.

## Key takeaways

- A broker dies in four distinct regimes — plain outage, split-brain, unclean-election, and resource exhaustion — and your first job in any incident is to **classify the regime**, because the correct first action diverges sharply and confusing them costs either data or downtime.
- A **plain outage self-heals**: leadership migrates from the dead broker to in-sync followers with no data loss, because an ISR member already holds every acknowledged record. Your job is to confirm it, not to intervene with durability knobs.
- **Controlled shutdown is nearly free; a hard crash costs a detection delay plus an election.** Route every planned stop through controlled shutdown and make sure your process manager's stop timeout is generous enough not to `SIGKILL` a broker mid-shutdown.
- **Quorum prevents split-brain** by making it impossible for two sides of a network partition to both reach a majority; the minority freezes rather than diverges, correctly choosing consistency over availability for the isolated side.
- **Always run an odd-sized quorum (three or five)** across at least three failure domains. A four-node quorum tolerates the same single failure as three while adding the even-split freeze mode — four is strictly worse than three.
- **Unclean leader election is a per-topic availability-versus-durability choice with no good answer when all ISR replicas are down.** Default `unclean.leader.election.enable=false`, override to `true` only deliberately for loss-tolerant topics, and never flip it cluster-wide in a panic.
- **The disk fills on a slope, not a spike** — alert on the *rate* of disk fill, not just the level, cap with `retention.bytes` backstops, run retention changes through the capacity arithmetic, and keep a steady-state ceiling near 70 to 75%.
- **File handles, heap, and page cache are gauges with known ceilings** — raise `ulimit -n` to at least 100,000, size the heap for the workload with `-XX:+ExitOnOutOfMemoryError`, leave the page cache room, and alert on headroom for each.
- **The quiet failures are the dangerous ones.** Hard crashes are loud and handled by replication; the slowly filling disk, the even quorum, and the unclean default nobody revisited are quiet and destroy data — make them loud with alerting before they become incidents.
