A broker is going to die in the middle of a write. Not *if* — when. A kernel will panic, an EBS volume will detach, a noisy neighbor will steal your CPU for forty seconds, a deploy will roll a node while a producer is mid-flight. The entire reason Kafka exists as a *replicated* log rather than a single fast append-only file is that this event is routine, and your job is to make sure that when it happens, not one acknowledged record disappears. The terrifying part is that Kafka will happily let you build a cluster that *looks* bulletproof — three brokers, replication factor three, green dashboards — and still throws away data the instant a leader fails, because two config defaults are wrong for durability and nobody told you. This post is about closing that gap, knob by knob, until you can stand in front of your VP during an incident and say, with a straight face, "we did not lose a single committed message," and be right.

By the end you will be able to do something most engineers who *use* Kafka cannot: trace a single produced record from the moment your producer calls `send()` all the way through the leader's append, the followers' fetch, the high watermark advancing, and the acknowledgement coming back — and at every step tell me exactly what survives a crash and what does not. You will know why `acks=1` is a loaded gun, why `min.insync.replicas` and not `acks` is the real durability knob, what the in-sync replica set actually is and how a follower falls out of it, and what unclean leader election is really trading away when it keeps you available. You will leave with one end-to-end configuration that loses zero acknowledged data under a single broker failure, and the precise reasoning for every line of it.

This is part three of the Kafka deep dive. Part one, [Kafka as a distributed log](/blog/software-development/database/kafka-as-a-distributed-log), introduced replication with a single picture and one sentence of hand-waving — "followers copy the leader, and that gives you fault tolerance." That sentence is true and it is also where almost every production data-loss incident hides. We are going to take that picture apart and rebuild it with every mechanism intact: the leader-follower roles, the ISR, the high watermark, leader epochs, the fetch protocol, and the four knobs that decide your durability. We will lean on [distributed replication](/blog/software-development/database/distributed-replication-leader-multi-leader-leaderless) for the general theory of single-leader systems, on [delivery semantics](/blog/software-development/message-queue/delivery-semantics-at-most-at-least-exactly-once) for what "no loss" composes into end to end, and on [CAP theorem and PACELC](/blog/software-development/database/cap-theorem-and-pacelc) for why the unclean-election knob is a consistency-versus-availability dial wearing a boolean's clothing.

## 1. Leader-follower replication per partition

Replication in Kafka happens at the granularity of the *partition*, not the topic and not the broker. This is the first thing to get right, because it changes how you reason about everything that follows. A topic with twelve partitions and replication factor three is not "three copies of a topic." It is twelve independent replication groups, each with its own leader, its own followers, and its own high watermark, scattered across the brokers so that the leadership load spreads out. Broker 1 might lead partitions 0, 3, and 6 while following partitions 1, 2, 4, and 5. There is no such thing as a "leader broker" — leadership is a per-partition role, and a single broker is simultaneously leader for some partitions and follower for others. When we say "the leader," we always mean the leader *of one specific partition*.

For each partition, exactly one replica is the leader. Every produce request and every consumer fetch for that partition goes to the leader and only the leader. The followers do not serve client traffic at all — they exist for one purpose, which is to stay byte-for-byte identical to the leader's log so that if the leader dies, one of them can take over without losing data. (Kafka 2.4+ added the ability for consumers to fetch from the *closest* follower replica to save cross-AZ bandwidth, but that is a read-locality optimization layered on top; the write path and the durability story still run entirely through the leader, and we will set follower-fetching aside for this post.) Figure 1 shows the shape: a producer on the left writing to the leader, two followers replicating, a consumer on the right reading from the leader. Notice the consumer reads only up to the high watermark, a constraint we will spend a whole section on.

The replication itself is *pull-based*, and this surprises people coming from databases where the primary pushes the write-ahead log to replicas. In Kafka, the followers fetch. Each follower runs a fetcher thread that issues a `Fetch` request to the leader exactly the way a consumer would — "give me everything from offset 95 onward" — receives a batch of records, appends them to its own copy of the log, and immediately fetches again from its new position. The leader does not track followers by pushing to them; it learns how far each follower has gotten by the offset each follower asks for next. A follower requesting offset 100 has, by definition, durably appended everything through offset 99. This single fact — that the fetch offset *is* the acknowledgement — is the quiet hinge that the entire ISR and high-watermark machinery turns on, and we will return to it in detail in section 8.

### Why per-partition leadership matters for durability

Per-partition leadership means failure is granular. When a broker dies, it was the leader of some partitions and a follower of others. For the partitions it led, Kafka must elect new leaders from the surviving replicas of *those specific partitions* — a flurry of small, independent leader elections, not one big cluster failover. For the partitions it merely followed, nothing dramatic happens; those partitions keep serving from their existing leaders, just with one fewer replica until the dead broker returns or a reassignment heals them.

This granularity is also why replication factor is a per-topic setting and why it is the foundation of everything else here. Replication factor (RF) is how many copies of each partition exist, leader included. RF=1 means no replication at all — one copy, and if its broker dies the partition is offline and any data not yet read by consumers is gone until the broker comes back (if it ever does). RF=3 means three copies: one leader, two followers, tolerating the loss of up to two brokers without the partition going offline, and — *with the right knobs* — without losing acknowledged data when one fails. RF=3 is the near-universal production default for data you care about, and every configuration we discuss assumes it unless stated otherwise. The reason is simple arithmetic we will make precise in section 5: with RF=3 you can require two copies of every write and still tolerate one failure, and "two copies, tolerate one failure" is the smallest configuration that loses nothing.

Where do the three replicas physically live? On three different brokers, always — Kafka will never place two replicas of the same partition on the same broker, because that would defeat the entire point of replication (one broker death would kill two copies at once). On a rack-aware or zone-aware cluster, Kafka goes further and spreads the replicas across failure domains using `broker.rack`: it tries to place the leader and its followers in different racks or availability zones so that a whole-rack power event or a single-AZ outage cannot take out more than one replica of any partition. This rack-aware placement is what turns RF=3 from "survives one broker dying" into "survives one *availability zone* dying," which is the failure mode that actually keeps SREs up at night. The cost, as the cross-AZ case study later will show, is that replication traffic now crosses zone boundaries and `acks=all` pays a cross-zone round trip — a real latency tax you budget for deliberately, not a bug.

It is also worth being precise about what RF does *not* buy you. Replication protects against broker and disk and zone *failure*; it does nothing about *bugs in your data*. If a producer writes a poisoned record, all three replicas faithfully store the poison. If retention deletes a segment, it deletes it on all three. Replication is a fault-tolerance mechanism, not a backup — three identical copies of corrupted or expired data are still corrupted or expired. Durability in the sense this post means it is specifically "an acknowledged record survives the loss of brokers," and that is a narrower and more precise guarantee than the colloquial "my data is safe."

## 2. The in-sync replica set (ISR) and lag

Here is the central abstraction of Kafka replication, the one that makes durable replication possible without making it unbearably slow: the **in-sync replica set**, universally abbreviated ISR. The ISR is the subset of a partition's replicas that are currently *caught up* with the leader. The leader is always in its own ISR. A follower is in the ISR if it has fetched up to the leader's log recently enough — specifically, within the window defined by `replica.lag.time.max.ms`, which defaults to 30000 milliseconds, thirty seconds.

Why a *set* that membership comes and goes from, rather than just "all the followers"? Because followers fall behind, and Kafka needs a principled answer to the question "how caught up is caught up enough to count?" A follower can lag for a hundred reasons: a long garbage-collection pause, a slow disk, a saturated network link, a noisy neighbor stealing CPU, the broker simply being overwhelmed by traffic on its other partitions. If Kafka demanded that *every* replica acknowledge every write before considering it durable, then a single slow follower would stall every producer in the cluster — the slowest replica would set the pace for everyone, and your p99 produce latency would be hostage to your unluckiest broker. The ISR solves this by drawing a moving line: replicas on the fast side of the line are "in sync" and count toward durability; replicas that drift to the slow side are ejected from the ISR, the partition keeps making progress with the remaining members, and the ejected replica rejoins automatically once it catches back up.

The lag metric is *time-based*, not message-based, and this is a deliberate and important design choice. Older Kafka versions also had `replica.lag.max.messages`, a threshold on how many messages a follower could be behind. That knob was a disaster in practice: pick it too low and a brief traffic spike — a perfectly healthy follower that is momentarily 5,000 messages behind during a burst — gets kicked out of the ISR for no good reason, churning the set and hurting durability exactly when load is highest. Pick it too high and a genuinely broken follower lingers in the ISR for far too long. The number of messages is the wrong unit because it conflates "this follower is unhealthy" with "traffic is bursty right now." Time is the right unit: a follower is unhealthy if it has not managed to *request the leader's latest data within thirty seconds*, regardless of how many messages that represents. A healthy follower under a traffic spike fetches constantly and stays in the ISR even while temporarily many messages behind, because it keeps asking for the newest offset; an unhealthy follower stops asking and gets ejected. Since version 0.9, time-based lag is the only mechanism.

### How a follower drops out and rejoins

A follower is dropped from the ISR when the leader notices it has not caught up to the leader's log end offset within `replica.lag.time.max.ms`. Concretely, the leader tracks, for each follower, the last time that follower's fetch request reached (or requested past) the leader's current log end offset. If that timestamp falls more than 30 seconds into the past, the leader removes the follower from the ISR, records the new ISR membership in the cluster metadata (in ZooKeeper on older clusters, in the `__cluster_metadata` log on KRaft clusters), and continues serving with the smaller ISR.

Rejoining is symmetric and automatic. The ejected follower keeps fetching — being out of the ISR does not stop it from replicating, it just stops it from *counting*. Once its fetches have pulled it all the way up to the leader's current log end offset, the leader adds it back to the ISR and updates the metadata again. There is no manual intervention, no restart required. A GC pause that ejects a follower for fifteen seconds and then resolves will see that follower rejoin the ISR within a few seconds of the pause ending, as soon as it has fetched the backlog it missed.

![A timeline showing the in-sync replica set starting at three members, a follower dropping out after a long garbage-collection pause exceeds the lag threshold, the set shrinking to two, and the follower rejoining once it catches up](/imgs/blogs/kafka-replication-isr-acks-durability-9.webp)

Figure 9 traces this full cycle, and it is worth internalizing because the shrink half is where durability quietly degrades. At t0 the ISR is the full set of three. At t1 broker 3 hits a forty-second GC pause and stops fetching. At t2 its lag crosses the thirty-second threshold and the leader ejects it, so by t3 the ISR is just brokers 1 and 2. The cluster is still up and — if `min.insync.replicas` is 2 — producers are still being acknowledged, but the partition is now one failure away from trouble instead of two. At t4 broker 3's GC pause ends and it resumes fetching, racing to catch up; at t5 it reaches the leader's log end offset and rejoins, restoring the full ISR. The whole episode might be invisible on your throughput graph and yet it is the most important thing that happened to your durability all day, which is why the metric `kafka.server:type=ReplicaManager,name=IsrShrinksPerSec` belongs on your alerting dashboard, not buried three clicks deep.

### Watching the ISR in production

Because the ISR is invisible in normal operation — it does its work silently and your producers never see it unless it shrinks below the floor — the only way to know your durability margin is to watch the right metrics, and there are exactly two that matter most. The first is `UnderReplicatedPartitions` (`kafka.server:type=ReplicaManager,name=UnderReplicatedPartitions`), a per-broker gauge counting partitions whose ISR is smaller than their replication factor. In a healthy cluster this is *zero*, always, on every broker. Any sustained nonzero value means some partition is running with fewer in-sync copies than it should — degraded durability margin — and a value that stays nonzero for minutes is a partition whose follower is genuinely unable to keep up, not just blipping. The second is `IsrShrinksPerSec` and its partner `IsrExpandsPerSec`: the *rate* of ISR membership changes. A low background rate is normal (occasional GC pauses happen), but a spike or a sustained high churn rate is the flapping-follower signal from the war story — a dying disk, a saturated NIC, a broker drowning in its own traffic.

There is a third number worth a dashboard panel: replica fetch lag, the gap between a follower's log end offset and the leader's, exposed per-partition. Watching it tells you *how close* a follower is to dropping out before it actually does, giving you lead time. The operational rule that ties these together: alert on `UnderReplicatedPartitions > 0` for more than a minute (paging severity if it touches a topic where you cannot afford reduced margin), and alert on sustained `IsrShrinksPerSec` even when writes are succeeding, because a partition can be perfectly *available* while running with zero durability margin, and the ISR metrics are the only place that condition shows up before it becomes an outage. Availability dashboards lie to you here; the ISR metrics tell the truth.

## 3. The high watermark and what consumers can see

Now we connect replication to what consumers are allowed to read, and it is here that Kafka makes its strongest durability promise. Define two offsets per replica. The **log end offset** (LEO) is the offset of the next record to be appended — one past the last record that replica physically holds. The **high watermark** (HW) is the highest offset that has been replicated to *all* members of the ISR. The high watermark is, by definition, less than or equal to every ISR member's LEO, and the leader computes it as the minimum LEO across the current ISR.

The rule that makes this matter: **consumers can only read up to the high watermark.** Records that the leader has appended but that have not yet been replicated to the full ISR — records sitting between the HW and the leader's LEO — are invisible to consumers. They are physically on the leader's disk, they exist, but no consumer will ever be handed them until the high watermark advances past them. This is not a performance optimization; it is the load-bearing wall of Kafka's durability model. The reason is that an under-replicated record might not survive a leader failure. If the leader appended a record, acknowledged nothing about it yet, and then died before any follower copied it, that record vanishes — and you must never let a consumer have acted on a record that can later vanish. By capping consumer reads at the high watermark, Kafka guarantees that consumers only ever see records that already exist on every in-sync replica, which means those records will survive any single failure. A consumer reading from Kafka is reading committed history, never speculative history.

This is the same idea as a committed read in a database, and it has the same consequence: there is a window of records that are *written but not yet visible*. On a busy partition the high watermark trails the leader's log end offset by however many records are currently in flight to the followers — typically a small number representing a few milliseconds of replication, but it can grow when followers lag. Consumers are always reading slightly behind the absolute newest record, by exactly the replication delay, and that lag is the price of the guarantee that what they read cannot be un-written.

There is one further read boundary worth naming, because it confuses people who turn on transactions. When you use the transactional producer (the machinery behind exactly-once stream processing), consumers configured with `isolation.level=read_committed` are capped not at the high watermark but at the **last stable offset** (LSO) — the offset below which there are no still-open transactions. The LSO sits at or below the high watermark: a record can be fully replicated to the ISR (under the HW) and yet still belong to a transaction that has not committed, in which case a read-committed consumer must not see it yet. The high watermark answers "is this record durable?"; the last stable offset answers "is this record's transaction decided?". For non-transactional workloads the two coincide and you can think purely in terms of the high watermark; the moment you introduce transactions, the LSO becomes the real read ceiling, and it is one more reason consumers always read strictly behind the leader's newest append. We leave the transaction machinery itself to [delivery semantics](/blog/software-development/message-queue/delivery-semantics-at-most-at-least-exactly-once); the point here is just that the high watermark is the *durability* boundary, distinct from the *transactional visibility* boundary layered above it.

#### Worked example: the high watermark advancing

Let us make the high watermark concrete with numbers. Take a partition with RF=3 and ISR = {leader, follower A, follower B}. Suppose the state is: leader LEO = 100, follower A LEO = 95, follower B LEO = 92. The high watermark is the minimum LEO across the ISR, which is min(100, 95, 92) = 92. So consumers can read records 0 through 91 (offset 92 is the next-to-be-committed boundary). Records 92 through 99 are on the leader's disk but invisible — they have not yet reached follower B, the slowest ISR member.

Now both followers issue fetches. Follower A fetches from offset 95, receives records 95 through 99, appends them, and its LEO becomes 100. Follower B fetches from offset 92, receives records 92 through 99, appends them, and its LEO also becomes 100. On the *next* fetch round, the leader sees that both followers have now requested offset 100 (proving they hold everything through 99), recomputes the high watermark as min(100, 100, 100) = 100, and the HW jumps from 92 to 100. Only now do consumers become able to read records 92 through 99.

![A timeline showing the high watermark held back at offset 90 while the leader log end offset sits at 100, then the followers fetching up to 95, the minimum in-sync replica offset reaching 95, and the high watermark advancing to 95 so consumers can read those records](/imgs/blogs/kafka-replication-isr-acks-durability-4.webp)

Figure 4 walks a slightly different instance of the same dance and it repays close reading. The crucial subtlety, which trips up even experienced engineers, is that the high watermark advances *one fetch round late*. When follower B fetches and appends records up to offset 99, the leader does not yet know that — it learns about follower B's new position only on follower B's *next* fetch request, which carries the offset 100. So there is always a one-round-trip delay built into the high watermark: a record is durable on all replicas at moment T, but the leader does not advance the HW to reflect that until moment T plus one fetch round, and only then can the leader's response also carry the new HW value back down to the followers. This piggybacking — the follower learns the leader's HW as a field in the fetch response — is why the HW propagation lags the actual replication by a full round trip, and it is the seed of a famous log-divergence bug we will resolve with leader epochs in section 6.

## 4. Producer acks: 0, 1, and all

The producer's `acks` setting is the knob most engineers reach for first, and it is the most misunderstood, because people treat it as a durability dial when it is really only *half* of one. `acks` controls how many acknowledgements the producer waits for before it considers a `send()` successful and lets the calling code believe the record is safe. There are exactly three settings, and the gap between them is the gap between "I lied to my application" and "this record will survive a crash."

**`acks=0` — fire and forget.** The producer writes the record to its socket and immediately considers it sent. It does not wait for any response from the broker. It does not even wait to learn whether the broker received the bytes, whether the leader was up, or whether the partition existed. This is the lowest possible latency and the highest possible throughput, and it offers *no durability guarantee whatsoever*. If the leader is down, if the network drops the packet, if the broker's receive buffer overflows — the record is gone and your producer never knows. There is no retry, because retries are triggered by error responses and `acks=0` never reads a response. Use this only for data where loss is genuinely acceptable: high-volume metrics, sampled telemetry, clickstream where a dropped event among millions changes nothing. Never for anything a human or a ledger will miss.

**`acks=1` — the leader acknowledges.** The producer waits until the *leader* has written the record to its own log (its page cache, to be precise — more on fsync in a moment) and sent back an acknowledgement. The producer now knows the leader received and stored the record. This feels safe, and it is the default in many client libraries' mental models, and it is a trap. The record is on exactly *one* broker — the leader — and no follower has necessarily copied it yet. If the leader dies after acknowledging but before any follower replicates that record, the record is permanently lost, and your producer received a *success* response for data that no longer exists. This is the silent-loss scenario, and it is the single most common way well-meaning teams lose data on a cluster that looks fully replicated. We will count the lost records precisely in a worked example below.

**`acks=all` (also written `acks=-1`) — the full ISR acknowledges.** The producer waits until the record has been replicated to *all members of the current ISR* before the leader sends back an acknowledgement. This is the only setting where the producer's success response means the record exists on more than one broker. With `acks=all`, an acknowledged record is, by construction, at or below the high watermark on every in-sync replica — which is exactly the condition for surviving any single failure. But — and this is the twist that makes section 5 necessary — `acks=all` alone is *not* enough, because "all of the ISR" can be just the leader if every follower has dropped out. `acks=all` is necessary for no-loss durability but not sufficient; the sufficiency comes from pairing it with `min.insync.replicas`.

A frequent misreading is to assume `acks=all` waits for all *replicas* (all RF copies). It does not — it waits for all *in-sync* replicas, which is a shrinking set under failure. This distinction is the whole reason `min.insync.replicas` has to exist as a separate knob: `acks=all` defines *how many of the current ISR* must ack (all of them), while `min.insync.replicas` defines *how large the ISR must be* for that to mean anything. Read them as a pair: "wait for the whole ISR, and refuse to proceed unless the ISR has at least N members." Without the second clause, "the whole ISR" silently degrades to "the leader" exactly when you most need the guarantee. It is also worth knowing what `acks=all` does *not* control: it says nothing about fsync. An `acks=all` acknowledgement means the record is in the *page cache* of every ISR member, replicated across brokers, but not necessarily flushed to physical disk on any of them — Kafka deliberately relies on cross-broker replication rather than per-broker fsync for durability, because two independent page caches surviving is both faster and statistically safer than one disk's fsync. We return to that fsync nuance in the stack figure of section 5.

![A before-and-after comparison contrasting acks=1 where the leader acknowledges before any follower has a copy and a leader crash silently loses data, versus acks=all with min.insync.replicas set to two where the record is acknowledged only after two brokers hold it and a leader crash loses nothing](/imgs/blogs/kafka-replication-isr-acks-durability-2.webp)

Figure 2 puts the two regimes side by side. On the left, `acks=1`: the leader appends, acks immediately, the follower has nothing yet, and a leader crash at that instant is silent data loss with a success already returned to the application. On the right, `acks=all` paired with `min.insync.replicas=2`: the leader waits until at least one follower has also appended the record before acknowledging, so two brokers hold every acknowledged record, and a leader crash promotes the follower with zero loss. The whole rest of this post is, in a sense, an elaboration of why the right column is the only one you can trust.

#### Worked example: acks=1 silent loss, counting the records

Let us count exactly how much data `acks=1` can lose. Configuration: RF=3, `acks=1`, a producer sending steadily at 50,000 records per second to one partition. Replication lag — the typical gap between a record being appended on the leader and being copied to the followers — is, say, 10 milliseconds under normal load.

At 50,000 records per second, 10 milliseconds of records is 50,000 × 0.010 = 500 records. So at any given instant, there are roughly 500 records that the leader has appended and acknowledged to producers (because `acks=1` acks on leader append) but that have not yet reached the followers. These 500 records exist on exactly one broker.

Now the leader's broker suffers a kernel panic and dies. A follower is promoted to leader. That follower's log ends 500 records short of where the dead leader's log ended, because it had not yet fetched those last 500 records. Those 500 records are gone. Every producer that sent them received a *success* acknowledgement. The application believes 500 transactions committed; Kafka has no record of them; no error was ever raised. That is the silent loss, and the number scales with both your throughput and your replication lag: double the rate or double the lag and you double the records at risk. Under a network hiccup that pushes replication lag to 200 milliseconds, the same partition has 50,000 × 0.200 = 10,000 records at risk at the moment of a leader crash. With `acks=all` and `min.insync.replicas=2`, the count is exactly zero, because no record is ever acknowledged to the producer until a second broker holds it.

## 5. min.insync.replicas: the real durability knob

Here is the knob nobody tells you about and the single most important configuration in this entire post: **`min.insync.replicas`**. This is a *broker/topic* setting (not a producer setting), and it specifies the minimum number of replicas that must be in the ISR for a write with `acks=all` to be accepted. If the ISR shrinks below this number, the leader *rejects* produce requests with a `NotEnoughReplicas` (or `NotEnoughReplicasAfterAppend`) error rather than accepting a write it cannot durably replicate. It is the floor under your durability — the line below which Kafka refuses to lie to you.

Walk the logic of why `acks=all` needs it. `acks=all` means "wait for all of the *current* ISR to acknowledge." But the ISR shrinks under failure. If two of your three replicas have fallen out of the ISR — say a GC pause ejected one and a network partition ejected another — then the ISR is just {leader}, a set of one. Now `acks=all` means "wait for the leader to acknowledge," because the leader is the entire ISR. You have silently degraded all the way back to `acks=1` semantics *without changing any config and without any warning*. The next leader crash loses data, exactly as in the section-4 worked example, even though you carefully set `acks=all`. `acks=all` by itself promises "all of the ISR," and "all of the ISR" can be one.

`min.insync.replicas` closes this hole. Set it to 2, and the rule becomes: a write with `acks=all` is accepted only if at least 2 replicas are in the ISR. If the ISR shrinks to 1 (just the leader), the leader stops accepting writes and returns `NotEnoughReplicas`. Producers get an error — a loud, retriable error — instead of a silent acknowledgement for data that lives on a single broker. The partition becomes *unavailable for writes* rather than *available but lossy*. This is the durability-availability tradeoff stated in one number, and it is, at heart, the CAP theorem made operational — under partition, do you stay available and risk inconsistency, or do you reject writes to preserve consistency? Kafka with `min.insync.replicas=2` chooses consistency; we explore the full theory in [CAP theorem and PACELC](/blog/software-development/database/cap-theorem-and-pacelc).

The magic configuration, the one you should burn into memory, is **RF=3, `acks=all`, `min.insync.replicas=2`**. Let us see why these three numbers specifically. RF=3 gives you three copies. `min.insync.replicas=2` requires that every acknowledged write lives on at least 2 of them. `acks=all` makes the producer actually wait for that. Now lose one broker: the ISR drops from 3 to 2, which still meets the minimum of 2, so writes continue uninterrupted — you stayed available *and* every acknowledged record is on 2 brokers, so the failure cost you nothing. The cluster tolerates one failure with zero loss and zero downtime. That is the whole game, and it falls out of the arithmetic RF − min.insync.replicas = 3 − 2 = 1, the number of simultaneous failures you can absorb while still accepting writes.

![A layered stack showing the four durability layers from top to bottom: producer acks, the in-sync replica set, min.insync.replicas, and the fsync flush to disk, each adding one more guarantee that a record survives](/imgs/blogs/kafka-replication-isr-acks-durability-3.webp)

Figure 3 stacks the layers that together produce durability, and the point of drawing them as a stack is that durability is the *product* of all of them, not any one alone. The producer acks layer decides whether the producer waits at all. The ISR layer decides which replicas count as durable. The `min.insync.replicas` layer decides how many of those must exist before a write is accepted. And the bottom layer, fsync, decides whether "written" means "in the OS page cache" or "physically on the disk platter." We have been glossing fsync, so let us be precise: by default Kafka does *not* fsync on every write. It writes to the page cache and lets the OS flush to disk on its own schedule, relying on replication rather than fsync for durability — the reasoning being that two brokers' page caches surviving is more reliable and far faster than one broker's fsync. This is the right call almost always, but it means that a *simultaneous* power loss across multiple brokers (a whole-rack power event) could lose page-cache data that was acknowledged but not yet flushed. For the vast majority of deployments, replication across failure domains is the durability story and fsync is left to the OS; only the most paranoid, regulation-bound workloads tune `flush.messages` or `flush.ms` to force earlier fsyncs, paying a steep throughput price.

#### Worked example: ISR shrinking under RF=3, min.insync.replicas=2

Let us trace what producers and durability experience as replicas drop out, one at a time, under the magic config: RF=3, `acks=all`, `min.insync.replicas=2`. Start with ISR = {1, 2, 3}, broker 1 the leader.

**State 0 — all healthy.** ISR size 3. A write with `acks=all` waits for all three to append, then acks. Every acknowledged record is on 3 brokers. Producers see normal latency. Durability: tolerates 2 failures with no loss.

**State 1 — one replica drops.** Broker 3 hits a long GC pause, lags past 30 seconds, and is ejected. ISR = {1, 2}, size 2. This still meets `min.insync.replicas=2`, so writes are *still accepted* — but now `acks=all` waits for only brokers 1 and 2. Every acknowledged record is on 2 brokers. Producers see *slightly lower* latency, ironically, because they now wait for one fewer replica. Durability: still tolerates 1 failure with no loss, but the safety margin has shrunk. You are one drop away from rejection.

**State 2 — a second replica drops.** Now broker 2 also lags out (a network blip, say). ISR = {1}, size 1. This is *below* `min.insync.replicas=2`. The leader now *rejects* every `acks=all` produce request with `NotEnoughReplicas`. Producers get a retriable error and back off, buffering records in memory (up to `buffer.memory`, after which `send()` blocks or throws). The partition is unavailable for writes. **But it has lost nothing.** Every record acknowledged so far is still on at least 2 brokers (it was acknowledged in State 0 or State 1, when 2+ replicas were required). The cluster chose to stop accepting writes rather than accept writes it could not durably replicate. When broker 2 or broker 3 catches back up and rejoins the ISR — restoring size to 2 — writes resume automatically. No data was lost; availability was sacrificed for the duration of the double-degradation.

Contrast this with `min.insync.replicas=1` (or leaving it at the *default*, which is 1): in State 2 the ISR of 1 would still be accepted, writes would continue landing on the lone leader, and a crash of broker 1 at that point would lose every record acknowledged while the ISR was 1. The default of `min.insync.replicas=1` is precisely why a "fully replicated RF=3 cluster" can still lose data. The knob's default is wrong for durability, and changing it is the single highest-leverage durability action you can take.

## 6. Leader epochs and avoiding log divergence

We now have to confront a subtle failure that the high-watermark mechanism, as described so far, does not by itself prevent: **log divergence on failover**. This is the bug that leader epochs exist to fix, and understanding it is what separates someone who has read the docs from someone who understands the protocol.

Recall from section 3 that the high watermark propagates to followers *one fetch round late* — a follower learns the leader's current HW as a field in the fetch *response*, so its knowledge of the HW always trails the leader's by a round trip. Now consider a leader change at exactly the wrong moment. The old leader has HW=100 and LEO=110 (records 100–109 are appended on the leader but not yet committed). A follower has replicated up to LEO=105 but, because of the round-trip delay, still believes the HW is 90. The old leader crashes. The follower is promoted to leader.

Without leader epochs, here is the disaster. The new leader (former follower) has records up to offset 104 in its log. The old leader, when it restarts and becomes a follower, has records up to offset 109 in *its* log. These logs *disagree* about what records exist at offsets 105–109 — the old leader has records there that the new leader never received, and worse, the new leader may have *already accepted new writes* at offsets 105 onward from producers, writes that are entirely different records than what the old leader had at those same offsets. Now you have two logs that both claim to be authoritative and that contain *different records at the same offsets*. When the old leader rejoins as a follower, the naive recovery — "truncate my log down to my last known HW (90) and re-fetch from the new leader" — works, but a subtler version of this scenario, where followers used the HW alone to decide truncation, could cause a follower to keep records that the new leader had overwritten, silently corrupting the log with records that no longer belong at those offsets. The pre-epoch protocol (Kafka before 0.11) had exactly this class of bug: under specific crash-and-recover orderings, logs could diverge and the HW-based truncation could either lose committed data or resurrect uncommitted data.

The fix, introduced in KIP-101, is the **leader epoch**. An epoch is a monotonically increasing integer — a generation number — that increments every time a partition gets a new leader. The leader epoch 0 is the first leader, epoch 1 the second, and so on. Every record batch written to the log is tagged with the leader epoch under which it was written, and each broker maintains a *leader epoch file* mapping each epoch to the first offset written in that epoch. This turns "what offset should I truncate to?" from a guess based on the stale high watermark into a precise question answered by the epoch history.

When a follower rejoins after a leader change, it does not blindly truncate to its high watermark. Instead it sends an `OffsetsForLeaderEpoch` request to the new leader, asking "for the last epoch I have in my log, what was the largest offset?" The new leader answers from its epoch history. If the follower has records in an epoch beyond what the new leader recognizes — records the new leader never had — the follower truncates exactly those records, no more and no less. If the follower's last epoch matches the leader's and the offset is within range, the follower keeps everything and resumes fetching. The epoch history makes the truncation point *exact* instead of a high-watermark approximation, and that exactness is what eliminates divergence. The old leader that restarts with records 105–109 written under epoch 5 will discover that the new leader's epoch 5 ended earlier (or that the new leader is on epoch 6 with different records), and will truncate its phantom records cleanly before re-fetching the authoritative versions. Two logs can no longer disagree about what lives at a given offset, because the epoch tag plus the offset uniquely identifies a record's lineage. Every Kafka cluster you run today does this; it is invisible until you go looking, but it is the reason failover is safe.

## 7. Failover: clean promotion vs unclean leader election

When a leader dies, the controller (the broker elected to manage cluster metadata, or the KRaft quorum on modern clusters) must pick a new leader for each affected partition. There are two fundamentally different ways this can go, and the difference between them is the difference between losing zero data and losing arbitrarily much.

**Clean leader election.** The controller promotes a replica that is *in the ISR*. By definition, an ISR member has replicated up to the high watermark — every committed record. Promoting it loses nothing: the new leader has all committed data, it bumps the leader epoch, it starts accepting writes, and consumers continue from where they were. This is the normal, healthy failover, and with the magic config it is what always happens on a single broker failure, because the ISR still has at least one other member to promote. Clean election is the entire payoff of replication: a broker dies and the only thing your application notices is a brief blip in produce latency while the new leader is elected (typically a few seconds, governed by failure detection and metadata propagation).

**Unclean leader election.** This happens when *no ISR member is available* — every in-sync replica is also down or unreachable. Now the controller faces a brutal choice. It can either leave the partition *offline* until an ISR member comes back (preserving durability, sacrificing availability), or it can promote a replica that is *not* in the ISR — a follower that had fallen behind and was ejected — to leader (restoring availability, sacrificing durability). Promoting an out-of-sync replica means promoting a log that is *missing committed records*. The moment that stale replica becomes leader, every record that was committed on the dead leaders but had not reached this lagging replica is *gone*, and worse, when the former leaders come back they will truncate their logs down to match the new (shorter) leader, deliberately *discarding* committed records to converge. You stayed up, and you threw away acknowledged data to do it.

![A before-and-after comparison contrasting clean failover where an in-sync follower with the full high watermark is promoted and no committed record is lost, versus unclean election where the only surviving replica is out of sync and promoting it truncates newer offsets causing data loss](/imgs/blogs/kafka-replication-isr-acks-durability-6.webp)

Figure 6 contrasts the two outcomes, and the visual makes the stakes plain: clean election promotes a replica that already holds every committed record, while unclean election promotes a replica that is missing them and then forces everyone else to match it, throwing committed data away. The controlling knob is **`unclean.leader.election.enable`**, a per-cluster (and per-topic-overridable) boolean. When it is `false` — the modern default — Kafka refuses unclean elections: if no ISR member is available, the partition goes offline and waits, preserving every committed record at the cost of availability for that partition. When it is `true`, Kafka will promote an out-of-sync replica to stay available, accepting the data loss.

This is a pure consistency-versus-availability dial, and which way you set it depends entirely on the data. For a financial ledger, an order pipeline, anything where a lost record is a real-world correctness violation, you set it `false` and accept that a sufficiently bad multi-broker failure can take a partition offline — being down is recoverable, losing committed money is not. For a metrics firehose or a recommendation-event stream where availability matters more than perfect completeness, `true` can be the right call: better to keep ingesting and lose a sliver of history than to halt the pipeline. There is no universally correct answer, which is exactly why it is a knob and not a hardcoded behavior, and it maps directly onto the theory in [distributed replication](/blog/software-development/database/distributed-replication-leader-multi-leader-leaderless) and [CAP theorem and PACELC](/blog/software-development/database/cap-theorem-and-pacelc). The forward-looking lesson, which we develop in a later post on broker outages and split-brain, is that `unclean.leader.election.enable=true` is the knob most likely to surprise you during a real incident, because it does its damage silently and only the next reader notices the missing records.

### Who runs the election, and how long it takes

The mechanics of *who* performs the election matter for how a failover feels in production. On a ZooKeeper-based cluster the *controller* — one broker elected to hold cluster metadata authority — watches for broker failures (via ZooKeeper session expiry, which takes as long as the session timeout, often six to eighteen seconds) and then, for every partition the dead broker led, picks a new leader from that partition's ISR and propagates the new leadership to all brokers. On a KRaft cluster the same job is done by the controller quorum reading from the metadata log, and failure detection is faster and more deterministic because it does not depend on ZooKeeper session semantics. Either way, the election itself is cheap — picking the head of the ISR list is nearly instant — and the wall-clock time a producer experiences as a blip is dominated by *failure detection* (noticing the broker is gone) plus *metadata propagation* (telling every client who the new leader is), typically a few seconds end to end on a healthy cluster. Clients handle this transparently: a produce or fetch to the old leader returns `NotLeaderForPartition`, the client refreshes metadata, finds the new leader, and retries. Your application sees a brief latency bump, not an error, provided your producer retries (which the no-loss config below ensures).

The deeper point is that clean election's speed is itself a durability feature: the faster a healthy ISR member is promoted, the smaller the window in which the partition is leaderless and producers are buffering. A cluster with aggressive-but-safe failure detection and a fat ISR fails over in seconds with zero loss; a cluster with a thin ISR (everything flapping near the edge) risks the ISR emptying entirely during the failover window and forcing the offline-or-unclean choice you never want to face.

## 8. The follower fetch protocol and HW advance

Let us now zoom all the way in on the mechanism that powers everything above: the **follower fetch protocol**, the actual request-response cycle by which a follower replicates and by which the high watermark advances. We touched it in section 1; here is the full machinery, because the precise ordering is where the high-watermark subtlety and the durability guarantee both live.

A follower's fetcher thread runs a tight loop. It issues a `Fetch` request to the leader carrying its current fetch offset — the offset of the next record it wants, which equals its own log end offset. The leader receives the request and does several things in one shot. First, the fetch offset *tells the leader how far this follower has gotten*: a request for offset 100 proves the follower durably holds everything through 99, so the leader updates its record of that follower's LEO to 100. Second, the leader reads records from the requested offset onward out of its log (served from page cache, zero-copied to the socket) and packs them into the response. Third — and this is the piece that makes consumers safe — the leader includes its *current high watermark* in the response, so the follower learns the committed boundary and can advance its own local HW to match (capped at what it has actually replicated). The follower appends the returned records, advances its LEO, updates its HW from the response, and immediately issues the next fetch from its new position.

![A linear pipeline of the follower fetch cycle: the follower requests a fetch offset, the leader reads its log from that offset, replies with records plus the leader high watermark, the follower appends and advances its log end offset, and the leader recomputes the high watermark as the minimum log end offset across the in-sync replica set](/imgs/blogs/kafka-replication-isr-acks-durability-7.webp)

Figure 7 lays out the cycle as a pipeline, and the ordering it encodes is the whole point. The leader recomputes the high watermark as the minimum LEO across the current ISR *after* learning each follower's new position from its fetch request. Because the leader learns a follower's new LEO only when that follower's *next* fetch arrives, the high watermark always advances one round trip behind the actual replication — exactly the delay we flagged in section 3 and resolved with leader epochs in section 6. Walk one concrete round: leader LEO=100, follower LEO=95, HW=95. Follower fetches from 95, leader updates nothing about the follower yet (it just learned the follower wants 95, which it already knew), reads records 95–99, replies with them plus HW=95. Follower appends, its LEO becomes 100. Follower fetches *again*, this time from 100. *Now* the leader learns the follower's LEO is 100, recomputes HW = min(100, 100) = 100, and the next response carries HW=100 back down. The commit is one fetch round behind the replication, always, and that delay is structural, not a bug.

### Why pull-based replication is the right design

It is worth pausing on why Kafka makes followers *pull* rather than having the leader *push*, because it is a design choice with real consequences. Pull-based replication means the leader's job is uniform: it serves `Fetch` requests, and it does not care whether the requester is a follower or a consumer — the same code path, the same zero-copy read from page cache, serves both. The leader does not maintain per-follower push state, does not retry pushes, does not get blocked waiting for a slow follower to accept a push. A slow or dead follower simply stops fetching; the leader notices via the lag timer and ejects it from the ISR. Backpressure is natural — a follower fetches as fast as it can keep up, no faster, and never gets overwhelmed by a leader pushing faster than it can append. The leader's view of each follower's progress is always exactly the last fetch offset it received, a single integer per follower, trivially cheap to track. This is the same pull-based, offset-driven design that makes Kafka *consumers* scale, applied to replication, and it is a large part of why a single Kafka broker can sustain replication to many followers across many partitions without the per-follower bookkeeping melting down.

### Why a follower never exposes records past its own high watermark

There is a final, subtle invariant in the fetch protocol that closes a real correctness gap, and it is the reason the round-trip HW delay from section 3 does not cause trouble even mid-failover. A follower, even though it has physically appended records up to its own log end offset, only advances its *local* high watermark to the value it learned from the leader's last fetch response — and that learned value lags by a round trip. This means a follower always knows a high watermark that is *no higher* than the true committed boundary. So if that follower is suddenly promoted to leader, it will not immediately treat its most-recently-fetched (but not-yet-confirmed-committed) records as readable by consumers; it inherits a conservative high watermark and only advances it once its own followers (the surviving replicas) confirm. Combined with leader epochs from section 6, this is what makes promotion safe: the new leader never exposes a record as committed that the old ISR had not actually committed, and the epoch mechanism cleanly truncates any phantom records the old leader had written past the real commit point. The conservative-follower-HW rule and the leader-epoch truncation are two halves of the same guarantee — that the committed prefix of the log is identical across every replica that could become leader, so failover can never resurrect uncommitted data nor lose committed data. It is a small rule with an enormous payoff: it is the difference between "consumers might briefly see a record that later disappears" and "consumers never see a record that can disappear," and the latter is the entire promise of the high watermark.

This is also why you should never reason about Kafka durability in terms of "the record is on disk." A record on the leader's disk but above the high watermark is not yet committed and can vanish on failover; a record at or below the high watermark is committed and on at least every ISR member's disk. Disk presence is necessary but not the boundary that matters — the high watermark is the boundary, and it is a *replication* property, not a *storage* property. The storage mechanics of how those records land on disk, segment by segment, are the subject of [Kafka as a distributed log](/blog/software-development/database/kafka-as-a-distributed-log); here the lesson is that committed-ness lives one level above the disk, in the replication protocol's agreement about what the whole ISR holds.

## 9. A no-data-loss configuration, end to end

Time to assemble everything into one configuration you can actually deploy, with every line justified by the mechanisms above. The goal: lose zero acknowledged records under a single broker failure, with the cluster staying available throughout. Here is the broker/topic side first.

```properties
# Broker / topic configuration for a no-data-loss topic
# Replication factor: 3 copies of every partition
replication.factor=3
# The real durability floor: every acks=all write needs 2 in-sync replicas
min.insync.replicas=2
# Refuse to promote an out-of-sync replica; prefer offline over data loss
unclean.leader.election.enable=false
# Time a follower may lag before it falls out of the ISR (default 30s)
replica.lag.time.max.ms=30000
```

And the producer side, which has to actually wait for that durability and recover correctly from the transient errors the broker will throw when the ISR is thin:

```java
Properties props = new Properties();
props.put("bootstrap.servers", "kafka-1:9092,kafka-2:9092,kafka-3:9092");
props.put("key.serializer", "org.apache.kafka.common.serialization.StringSerializer");
props.put("value.serializer", "org.apache.kafka.common.serialization.StringSerializer");

// Wait for the FULL in-sync replica set, not just the leader
props.put("acks", "all");

// Turn on the idempotent producer: dedups retried batches, preserves order.
// Implies acks=all, retries>0, max.in.flight<=5 automatically on modern clients.
props.put("enable.idempotence", "true");

// Retry transient errors (NotEnoughReplicas, leader election) instead of giving up
props.put("retries", Integer.MAX_VALUE);
props.put("delivery.timeout.ms", 120000);   // total time budget per record

// Cap in-flight batches so idempotence can preserve ordering on retry
props.put("max.in.flight.requests.per.connection", 5);

KafkaProducer<String, String> producer = new KafkaProducer<>(props);
```

Walk the reasoning line by line. `acks=all` makes the producer wait until the full ISR holds the record. `min.insync.replicas=2` on an RF=3 topic guarantees that "the full ISR" is never fewer than 2 brokers — and if it would be, the broker rejects the write rather than accept it onto a single broker. `unclean.leader.election.enable=false` guarantees that failover only ever promotes a replica that already holds every committed record. Together those three mean: every acknowledged record is on at least 2 brokers, and leadership only moves to a replica that has it — so no single failure can lose an acknowledged record.

The producer settings handle the *availability* side of the bargain. When the ISR shrinks to 1 and the broker starts returning `NotEnoughReplicas`, you do *not* want the producer to give up — you want it to retry, buffering in memory, until the ISR recovers and writes are accepted again. `retries=Integer.MAX_VALUE` plus a generous `delivery.timeout.ms` gives the producer a long time budget to wait out a transient under-replication. And `enable.idempotence=true` is the crucial pairing: without it, retrying a write that *actually succeeded* but whose acknowledgement was lost would *duplicate* the record — you would trade loss for duplication. The idempotent producer attaches a producer ID and a per-partition sequence number to every batch so the leader can recognize and discard a retried duplicate, giving you no-loss *and* no-duplicate on the producer side. This is where this post hands off to [delivery semantics](/blog/software-development/message-queue/delivery-semantics-at-most-at-least-exactly-once): the configuration here gives you the durable, no-loss producer half of an end-to-end exactly-once (effectively-once) story; the consumer half is offset management and idempotent processing.

![A matrix comparing acks settings 0, 1, and all across latency, durability, and throughput, showing that acks=all is the only setting with no data loss on replication factor three at a modest latency cost](/imgs/blogs/kafka-replication-isr-acks-durability-5.webp)

Figure 5 summarizes the acks tradeoff as a decision matrix, and it is the cheat sheet to keep next to your producer config. The honest cost of the no-loss configuration is latency: `acks=all` means every produce request waits for a round trip to at least one follower, which on a cross-AZ cluster might add a few milliseconds per batch versus `acks=1`. For almost every workload that touches money, orders, or anything a human will miss, those few milliseconds are the cheapest insurance you will ever buy. Throughput, notably, is barely affected — because Kafka batches aggressively and pipelines requests, `acks=all` throughput is typically within ten to twenty percent of `acks=1`, since the latency hit is amortized across large batches rather than paid per record.

![A tree taxonomy of the durability knobs, splitting into producer-side settings of acks 0 or 1 versus acks=all, and broker-side settings of min.insync.replicas equal to two and unclean leader election disabled](/imgs/blogs/kafka-replication-isr-acks-durability-8.webp)

Figure 8 organizes the knobs into a taxonomy, and the reason to see them as a tree is that they live on different actors and you must set *all the branches correctly* — getting the producer side right while leaving the broker side at defaults, or vice versa, leaves a hole. The producer owns `acks`. The broker (or topic) owns `min.insync.replicas` and `unclean.leader.election.enable`. A team that sets `acks=all` in their producer but never touches the broker default of `min.insync.replicas=1` has done half the work and built a cluster that *looks* durable and silently degrades to `acks=1` semantics under double-degradation. A team that sets `min.insync.replicas=2` on the broker but leaves producers at `acks=1` has done the *other* half and gets no benefit, because `acks=1` never waits for the second replica in the first place — `min.insync.replicas` only constrains `acks=all` writes. You need both branches. That cross-actor split is the single most common reason "we configured for durability" turns out to be false during the post-incident review.

## Case studies and war stories

### The default that ate the data

The most common Kafka data-loss story in the wild needs no exotic failure — just defaults. A team stands up a three-broker cluster, RF=3, feels safe, and runs producers at `acks=1` (or at `acks=all` but leaves `min.insync.replicas` at its default of 1, which is the same trap by a different door). Everything is fine for months. Then a broker — the leader of a hot partition — is terminated by an autoscaler or a spot-instance reclaim during peak traffic. In the window between the leader appending records and the followers fetching them, a few thousand records existed only on the dying broker. They are gone. Producers got success responses. The downstream system is missing orders, and nobody can explain it because every metric is green and the cluster is "fully replicated." The lesson, learned over and over across the industry: **replication factor three does not mean durable; `acks=all` plus `min.insync.replicas=2` means durable.** RF is necessary and not sufficient. The fix is two config lines and a producer redeploy, and the only hard part is discovering you needed it.

### LinkedIn, unclean elections, and the availability they chose

Kafka was born at LinkedIn for activity-stream and metrics data, and in those early high-volume, loss-tolerant pipelines, `unclean.leader.election.enable` defaulted to `true` for years — availability was prioritized because a dropped activity event among billions did not matter, and keeping the firehose flowing did. As Kafka spread into use cases where data *did* matter — database change capture, financial events, the source of truth for downstream systems — that default became a liability, because the same knob that harmlessly dropped a metrics event would now silently drop a committed financial record. The default was eventually flipped to `false` (Kafka 0.11 / the durability-focused releases), making durability the out-of-the-box behavior and forcing teams who genuinely want availability-over-consistency to opt into it explicitly. The lesson: a sensible default for one workload is a data-loss bug for another, and the right move when the user base diverges is to default to the *safe* behavior and make the dangerous one opt-in. If you run a loss-tolerant stream and have deliberately set `unclean.leader.election.enable=true`, that is a legitimate choice — just make sure it is a *choice* and not a forgotten default. See the consistency-versus-availability framing in [CAP theorem and PACELC](/blog/software-development/database/cap-theorem-and-pacelc).

### The flapping follower that hid a dying disk

A subtler incident: a partition's ISR kept oscillating between 3 and 2 every few minutes — a follower repeatedly dropping out and rejoining. Writes never failed (`min.insync.replicas=2` was satisfied by the stable two), so no alert fired on availability. But the `IsrShrinksPerSec` metric was elevated, and an attentive operator dug in. The flapping follower was on a broker with a *failing disk* — fsyncs occasionally stalled for tens of seconds as the disk retried bad sectors, pushing the follower past the lag threshold, then it would recover and rejoin. The cluster was durably fine the whole time (always 2 in-sync), but it was running with *zero margin* on that partition: any blip on either of the other two brokers during a drop-out window would have taken the ISR to 1 and started rejecting writes (or, if `min.insync.replicas` had been 1, losing data). The lesson: **ISR shrink/expand churn is a leading indicator**, not noise. Alert on `IsrShrinksPerSec` and `UnderReplicatedPartitions`, because they surface dying hardware and saturated networks *before* they turn into an outage. The cluster told them the disk was dying; they just had to be listening to the ISR.

### The cross-AZ tax nobody budgeted for

A team moved their Kafka cluster from single-AZ to multi-AZ for fault tolerance — correct and necessary. But they kept `acks=all` (good) and suddenly saw producer p99 latency triple. The cause: with replicas now spread across availability zones, `acks=all` meant every produce request waited for a cross-AZ round trip (1–2 ms each way) to a follower in another zone, where before it had been an intra-AZ hop (under 0.5 ms). Nothing was broken — this was the *correct, durable* behavior — but the latency budget had not accounted for it. The fixes were tuning, not abandoning durability: larger producer batches (`linger.ms` up, `batch.size` up) to amortize the round trip across more records, and accepting that the multi-AZ durability they wanted has an inherent latency floor set by the speed of light between zones. The lesson: **`acks=all` durability has a geography-dependent latency cost**, and the answer is to budget for it and batch harder, not to weaken acks. Cross-link the storage and batching mechanics in [Kafka as a distributed log](/blog/software-development/database/kafka-as-a-distributed-log).

## When to reach for each configuration (and when not to)

Durability is not free, and not every stream needs the maximum, so here is the decisive guidance.

**Use RF=3, `acks=all`, `min.insync.replicas=2`, `unclean.leader.election.enable=false`** — the full no-loss config — for any data where a lost record is a real-world correctness problem: financial transactions, orders, payments, inventory, database change-data-capture, any event that is a source of truth for downstream systems, any audit or compliance log. This is your default for anything that matters. The cost is a few milliseconds of produce latency and the possibility that a *double* broker failure takes a partition offline (rather than losing data) until a replica recovers. Both are acceptable for data you cannot lose.

**Use `acks=1`** — leader-only — when you have a high-volume stream where occasional loss during a leader failure is genuinely acceptable and the latency/throughput savings matter: real-time metrics, application logs at scale, sampled telemetry, recommendation-impression events. Be honest with yourself about what "acceptable loss" means: it means *that record is gone forever and your application got a success response*. If that sentence makes you uncomfortable for a given stream, that stream needs `acks=all`.

**Use `acks=0`** — fire and forget — almost never, and only for the highest-volume, most loss-tolerant data where even waiting for the leader's ack is too expensive: dense sensor telemetry, high-frequency metrics where you sample anyway, data where the *aggregate* matters and individual records are noise. The moment you find yourself wishing you knew whether a record landed, you have outgrown `acks=0`.

**Set `unclean.leader.election.enable=true`** only deliberately, only for loss-tolerant streams, and only when you have decided that availability beats completeness for that specific topic. Override it per-topic rather than cluster-wide if you have a mix of workloads — Kafka lets you set it at the topic level precisely so a financial topic and a metrics topic on the same cluster can choose differently. And document *why*, because the next operator will not understand the choice during an incident and may "fix" it the wrong way under pressure.

**Do not over-replicate.** RF=5 with `min.insync.replicas=3` exists, and it tolerates two simultaneous failures with no loss, but it costs you 5x storage and waits for 3 brokers per write. Reserve it for the rare data that genuinely justifies tolerating a double failure with zero downtime — most workloads are well served by RF=3, which already survives any single failure with no loss and no downtime. More replicas is more cost and more latency for a tail-risk most teams never hit.

## Key takeaways

- **Replication is per-partition, leader-and-followers.** One leader per partition serves all reads and writes; followers pull-fetch to stay caught up. There is no "leader broker" — every broker leads some partitions and follows others.
- **The ISR is the set of caught-up replicas**, defined by time (`replica.lag.time.max.ms`, default 30s), not message count. Followers drop out when they lag and rejoin automatically when they catch up. Alert on `IsrShrinksPerSec` and `UnderReplicatedPartitions`.
- **Consumers can only read up to the high watermark** — the highest offset replicated to all ISR members — so they never see a record that could vanish in a failure. The HW always trails the leader's log end offset by one fetch round.
- **`acks` is only half the durability knob.** `acks=0` can lose silently, `acks=1` loses if the leader dies before replication, `acks=all` waits for the full ISR — but "all of the ISR" can be just the leader.
- **`min.insync.replicas` is the real durability knob.** Its default of 1 is why a fully-replicated RF=3 cluster can still lose data. RF=3 + `acks=all` + `min.insync.replicas=2` is the only no-data-loss config, and the arithmetic is RF − min.insync = failures tolerated while writing.
- **When the ISR shrinks below `min.insync.replicas`, producers get `NotEnoughReplicas`** — a loud, retriable error — instead of a silent acknowledgement onto a single broker. Availability is sacrificed; durability is preserved.
- **Leader epochs prevent log divergence on failover** by tagging records with a generation number and using `OffsetsForLeaderEpoch` for exact truncation, replacing the stale-high-watermark guess that could corrupt logs in old Kafka.
- **`unclean.leader.election.enable` is a consistency-vs-availability dial.** `false` (the modern default) keeps a partition offline rather than promote an out-of-sync replica and lose committed records; `true` stays available and accepts data loss. Choose per topic.
- **The no-loss producer also needs idempotence.** Pair `acks=all` with `enable.idempotence=true` and generous retries so that recovering from transient under-replication does not turn no-loss into duplication.
