It is 3:14 in the morning and your pager is screaming. One partition of your most important Kafka topic has a consumer lag of forty thousand messages and climbing, while the other forty-seven partitions on the same topic are perfectly healthy, lag near zero, throughput nominal. Nothing has deployed. No broker is down. CPU on the consumers is idle. And yet that one partition has not advanced its offset in eleven minutes. When you finally pull the consumer logs you find the same stack trace, over and over, hundreds of times a second: a `JsonParseException` on the exact same offset, the consumer trying to deserialize the message at the head of the partition, failing, restarting the poll loop, fetching the *same* message again, and failing again in an infinite tight loop. One message — a single sixteen-kilobyte record that some upstream service emitted with a truncated payload — has brought a quarter of your pipeline to a dead stop, and it will stay stopped forever until a human intervenes. That message is a *poison message*, and the failure mode it caused is *head-of-line blocking*, and together they are responsible for more 3 a.m. pages than almost anything else in event-driven systems.

The cruel cousin of the poison message is the *retry storm*. Here nothing about any individual message is wrong. Instead, one downstream dependency — a database, a payment gateway, a recommendation service — gets slow. Maybe its p99 latency triples after a bad index migration. Every consumer that calls it starts timing out. And every consumer, being a well-engineered piece of software written by people who read the reliability chapter, *retries*. The retries land on the already-struggling dependency on top of the fresh traffic, doubling or tripling the offered load at the worst possible moment. The dependency, which might have recovered on its own in thirty seconds if left alone, now cannot recover at all, because every time it claws back a sliver of capacity a wall of retries consumes it. This is a *positive feedback loop*: the response to the failure makes the failure worse. The system is now stuck in a stable bad state that it cannot exit without external intervention, and the thing keeping it there is your own retry logic — the safety mechanism became the murder weapon.

This post is about those two failure families — the bad message and the bad feedback loop — and, far more importantly, about how to contain them. By the end you will be able to spot a poison message from its signature (a retry count that climbs while progress stays flat), explain to a skeptical colleague exactly why one bad record halts an ordered partition while a competing queue shrugs it off, and detect the condition automatically instead of at 3 a.m. You will understand retry storms as the feedback loops they are, and you will have five concrete containment tools in your hands: circuit breakers that fail fast so a dependency can breathe, rate limiting and load shedding that cap the damage, backpressure that pushes the slowdown back up the pipe instead of amplifying it, dead-letter quarantine that gets poison out of the hot path, and bulkheading that walls off blast radius so one tenant or one partition cannot sink the rest. The figure below is the whole drama in one frame: the same poison message, on the left blocking a partition and freezing fifty thousand messages behind it, and on the right quarantined to a dead-letter queue so the flow continues.

Then we dissect head-of-line blocking on an ordered partition — the structural reason one bad record can stop a whole partition cold. We cover detection: how to tell a poison message apart from a flaky-but-recoverable one. ## 1. The poison message and why it is dangerous

A **poison message** is a message that always fails. Not *might* fail, not *fails under load* — fails deterministically, every single time, no matter how many times you redeliver it, because the cause of the failure is in the message itself or in its relationship to the world, not in any transient condition. Hand a poison message to your consumer a thousand times and it fails a thousand times in exactly the same way. That determinism is what makes it categorically different from an ordinary error, and it is precisely why naive retry logic — the logic that saves you from transient errors — turns lethal in its presence.

It helps to name the common species, because the containment differs subtly between them. The first is the **unparseable message**: the bytes do not deserialize. A truncated payload, an invalid UTF-8 sequence, a message published with the wrong serializer so your Avro consumer is handed JSON, a corrupt protobuf. Deserialization throws before your business logic even runs. The second is the **schema-incompatible message**: it parses, but it carries a field your code does not understand, or omits a field your code requires, or has a type where your code expects another. A producer rolled out a schema change before the consumers were ready, and now every message in the new format is poison to the old consumers. The third — the sneakiest — is the **referential poison**: the message parses fine, has a valid schema, and the bug is semantic. It references an entity that no longer exists. An `order.shipped` event for an order ID that was hard-deleted from the database; a `user.updated` event for a tenant whose account was purged. Your handler does a lookup, gets nothing back, and throws a `NotFoundException` — forever, because that order is never coming back.

The reason all three are dangerous is the collision between determinism and retry. Your retry logic exists to handle *transient* failures: a network blip, a momentary database deadlock, a brief downstream timeout. The whole premise of retry is "the next attempt might succeed because the condition that caused the failure might have cleared." For a poison message that premise is simply false. The condition that caused the failure is *the message*, and the message does not change between attempts. So every retry is guaranteed to fail, and if your retry policy is "retry forever" or even "retry many times with backoff," you have built a machine that does nothing but fail the same operation over and over, consuming CPU, holding the partition, and never making progress. The poison message has weaponized your own resilience.

There is a deeper point here about the *signature* of a poison message versus a transient one, and it is the key to detection later. A transient failure has a falling failure rate over time: you fail, you wait, you retry, and increasingly often the retry succeeds, because the transient condition clears. A poison message has a *flat* failure rate: attempt one fails, attempt two fails identically, attempt one hundred fails identically. If you plot "did this specific message succeed yet?" against attempt number, a transient failure eventually flips to success and a poison message never does. That difference — bounded versus unbounded repetition of the *identical* failure — is exactly what an automated detector keys on. Hold that thought; we make it concrete in section 3.

One more property makes poison messages especially nasty in log-based and partitioned systems: they cannot simply be *skipped* without consequence, the way a thread pool can drop a bad task and move on. In a queue with per-message acknowledgement (RabbitMQ, SQS), you can negatively-acknowledge a poison message and let the broker route it elsewhere while the rest of the queue keeps draining. But in a log-based system with ordered offsets (Kafka, Pulsar, Kinesis), the consumer commits a single monotonically advancing offset per partition. You cannot commit offset 1,000,002 while leaving 1,000,001 — the poison message — unprocessed, because the offset is a single number that says "everything up to here is done." To get past the poison you must either process it (impossible, it is poison) or make a deliberate decision to abandon it and advance the offset anyway. That decision — abandon and advance — is the heart of containment in log systems, and getting it wrong in either direction (abandoning too eagerly and losing good data, or never abandoning and blocking forever) is where teams get hurt.

## 2. Head-of-line blocking on an ordered partition

Now we get to the structural reason a single poison message is not a localized nuisance but a partition-wide outage. The mechanism is called **head-of-line blocking**, and once you see it you will see it everywhere — in TCP, in HTTP/1.1 pipelining, in single-lane traffic, and right here in your ordered message partition.

An ordered partition is, by design, a strict sequence. Messages are appended in order and must be consumed in order, because the ordering *is the guarantee* — it is the whole reason you chose a partitioned log over a fan-out queue. (If you want a refresher on why partitions give you ordering and what that costs, the [message ordering and partitioning guarantees](/blog/software-development/message-queue/message-ordering-and-partitioning-guarantees) post in this series covers it.) The consumer reads the message at the head of its current position, processes it, and only then advances to the next. The single committed offset enforces this: it is a high-water mark meaning "I have successfully handled everything strictly before this point." There is no facility to handle message N+1 before message N, because that would violate the very ordering you are paying for.

So what happens when message N is poison? The consumer reads N, tries to process it, fails. It does not advance the offset, because it has not successfully handled N. It retries N. It fails again. The offset stays pinned at N. And every message from N+1 onward — N+1, N+2, all the way to the most recent message in the partition — sits behind N, fully durable, fully available on the broker, completely unprocessed, because the consumer is structurally incapable of reaching them while N is stuck. One bad record at the head of the line stops the entire line. That is head-of-line blocking, and on an ordered partition it converts a single-message defect into a partition-wide stall.

This is the moment to be precise about *blast radius*, because it is what makes head-of-line blocking either a footnote or a catastrophe. The blast radius is exactly one partition. The other partitions of the same topic are entirely independent — they have their own offsets, their own consumers (or their own assignments within a consumer group), their own progress. A poison message on partition 7 does not touch partitions 0 through 6 or 8 through N. That is the good news. The bad news is twofold. First, *one* partition can be a huge fraction of your traffic if your partition count is low or your partition key is skewed — if a single hot tenant maps to one partition, that tenant is fully down. Second, and more insidiously, the messages stuck behind the poison are not lost but they *age*, and if they age past your retention period before you clear the blockage, they are deleted unprocessed. You can turn a recoverable stall into permanent data loss just by being slow to respond.

### Why ordered partitions and not queues

It is worth contrasting against a per-message-ack queue to see that head-of-line blocking is a *consequence of ordering*, not a universal law. In RabbitMQ with a single queue and multiple consumers and no ordering requirement, a poison message can be negatively acknowledged (`basic.nack` with `requeue=false`) and routed to a dead-letter exchange, while the other consumers happily pull and process every other message in the queue. There is no single offset; each message is acknowledged independently. The poison message does not block its neighbors because there *is* no "line" in the ordered sense — there is a bag of messages, and one bad one can be set aside. (See [RabbitMQ acks, confirms, and dead-letter routing](/blog/software-development/message-queue/rabbitmq-acks-confirms-durability-quorum-queues) for the mechanics.)

The instant you require ordering, though, even RabbitMQ gives you head-of-line blocking, because ordering means a single consumer processing one message at a time in sequence — which is exactly the structure that lets one stuck message block the rest. There is no free lunch: ordering and head-of-line resilience are in fundamental tension, and any system that gives you strict ordering hands you head-of-line blocking as the price. The containment job, then, is not to eliminate head-of-line blocking — you cannot, while keeping ordering — but to *bound how long* a poison message is allowed to block the line before you forcibly evict it to a dead-letter queue and advance.

#### Worked example: one poison message on a 50k backlog

Let us put numbers on the stall, because the abstract "it blocks the partition" undersells how bad it is. Consider a Kafka topic partition that normally carries 2,000 messages per second. Your consumer processes each message in about 5 milliseconds, so one consumer thread can comfortably handle 200 messages per second of headroom above the arrival rate — wait, that is the wrong way around; at 2,000 msg/s arrival and 5 ms each, you need the consumer to process at least 2,000/s, which a single thread doing 5 ms each (200/s) cannot, so in practice this partition is served by a consumer that batches and parallelizes within the partition's ordering constraints, or the partition is sized so a single thread keeps up. To keep the arithmetic clean, say the consumer sustains exactly 2,000 msg/s in steady state and the partition is in balance: arrivals equal departures, lag hovers near zero.

Now at time T a poison message arrives and lands at offset X. The consumer reaches X, fails, and enters its retry loop. From this instant, departures drop to *zero* — the offset is pinned, nothing behind X is processed — while arrivals continue at 2,000 msg/s. The backlog grows linearly: after 1 minute, 120,000 messages are stuck behind the poison; after 5 minutes, 600,000; after the 25 minutes it took the on-call engineer to get paged, log in, find the stack trace, and realize it is a poison message, the backlog is 3,000,000 messages on that single partition. If your retention is 6 hours and your replay rate after fixing it is, say, 6,000 msg/s (three times normal, because the consumer can run hot to catch up), then draining a 3,000,000-message backlog at a *net* catch-up rate of 6,000 − 2,000 = 4,000 msg/s takes 3,000,000 / 4,000 = 750 seconds, or 12.5 minutes of catch-up after the 25 minutes of stall — a 37-minute total partition outage from one bad sixteen-kilobyte record.

Now the fix, quantified. Suppose instead you had configured the consumer to give up on a message after 5 failed attempts with short backoff — say 5 attempts over roughly 10 seconds total — and then route it to a DLQ and advance the offset. The poison message blocks the partition for *10 seconds*, not 25 minutes. In 10 seconds, 20,000 messages queue up behind it (at 2,000 msg/s). After the poison is evicted to the DLQ, the consumer drains those 20,000 at the 4,000 msg/s net catch-up rate in 5 seconds. Total partition impact: about 15 seconds of elevated lag that never exceeds 20,000 messages and self-heals before anyone is paged. The poison message still went to the DLQ for a human to inspect later — but on a Tuesday afternoon, not at 3 a.m., and with the partition fully healthy the whole time. The entire difference between a 37-minute outage and a 15-second blip is one config: *bound the retries and evict to DLQ instead of looping forever.* The figure at the top of this post is exactly that contrast.

## 3. Detecting a poison message

You cannot contain what you cannot detect, and the detection problem is more subtle than it first appears, because you must distinguish a poison message (give up, quarantine it) from a transient failure (keep retrying, it will clear). Get this discrimination wrong in the "too eager" direction and you dead-letter perfectly good messages during a brief downstream outage, losing data you should have processed. Get it wrong in the "too patient" direction and you sit in a head-of-line stall for 25 minutes. The whole art of detection is drawing that line well.

The single most reliable signal is the **retry count crossing a threshold**. Attach a counter to each message — in Kafka, a header that you increment and re-emit; in RabbitMQ, the `x-death` header that the broker maintains automatically when a message is dead-lettered and re-queued; in SQS, the `ApproximateReceiveCount` attribute the service tracks for you. Each time the message fails and is redelivered, the count goes up. When the count exceeds a threshold — 3, 5, 25, depending on how transient-tolerant you want to be — you declare the message poison and quarantine it. This is exactly the detection-pipeline in the figure below: a failure increments the count, the count is checked against a threshold, and on exceeding it the message is quarantined to the DLQ and the offset advances so the partition unblocks.

![A linear pipeline showing poison detection where a processing failure increments a retry counter, the counter is compared against a threshold of five, and on exceeding it the message is quarantined to a dead-letter queue and the offset advances to unblock the partition](/imgs/blogs/poison-messages-and-retry-storms-containment-4.webp)

The retry-count threshold is simple and it works, but it has a blind spot: it cannot tell you *why* the message failed, so it treats a poison message and a message that failed five times due to a five-minute downstream outage identically. Both hit the threshold; both get quarantined. During a transient outage that lasts longer than your retry budget, you will dead-letter good messages. That is usually an acceptable trade — better to quarantine a few recoverable messages to a DLQ where they can be replayed than to block the partition — but you can do better by combining the retry count with a second signal.

That second signal is the **repeated identical failure**. A poison message fails the *same way* every time: same exception type, same message, ideally same stack frame. A transient failure tends to fail in *varied* ways or to eventually succeed — a timeout here, a connection-reset there, then success. So a stronger detector fingerprints the failure (exception class plus a hash of the normalized message) and asks: has this *exact* failure recurred N times for this *exact* message? If yes, it is almost certainly poison — the determinism is the tell. If the failures vary, lean toward "transient, keep retrying within budget." Combining "retry count high" AND "failure signature identical across attempts" gives you a detector with far fewer false positives than retry count alone.

Here is a Kafka consumer that implements retry-count-plus-signature detection with an in-process retry budget before falling back to a DLQ:

```java
// Detect poison by retry count AND identical failure signature.
// On exceeding the budget, route to a dead-letter topic and ADVANCE the offset.
public void handleRecord(ConsumerRecord<String, byte[]> record) {
    int attempt = retryCountFromHeader(record);      // read "x-retry-count" header
    String lastSig = failureSigFromHeader(record);   // hash of last exception+message
    try {
        process(record);                             // business logic
        // success: offset will commit normally on the next poll cycle
    } catch (Exception e) {
        String sig = signatureOf(e);                 // exceptionClass + normalized msg hash
        boolean identical = sig.equals(lastSig);     // same failure as last time?
        int next = attempt + 1;
        boolean poison = next >= MAX_ATTEMPTS && identical;   // 5 retries, same failure
        boolean budgetExhausted = next >= MAX_ATTEMPTS;       // hard cap regardless

        if (poison || budgetExhausted) {
            sendToDeadLetter(record, e, next);       // quarantine: full payload + cause
            // do NOT rethrow: let the offset advance past the poison message
            log.warn("Quarantined offset {} after {} attempts, sig={}",
                     record.offset(), next, sig);
        } else {
            // transient: re-emit to a retry topic with backoff, bump count + signature
            sendToRetryTopic(record, next, sig, backoffMillis(next));
            // offset still advances; the retry topic carries the work forward
        }
    }
}
```

Two design choices in that code are worth dwelling on. First, even the retry attempts go to a *separate retry topic*, not back to the head of the same partition — this is the **retry-topic pattern**, and it is the single most important structural fix for head-of-line blocking on Kafka. By moving the failing message off the main partition and onto a delayed retry topic, you let the main partition keep advancing while the retry happens out of band. The main partition never blocks, even for transient failures, because the moment a message fails it leaves the hot path. Confluent's own reference architecture and the popular `spring-kafka` `DeadLetterPublishingRecoverer` both implement exactly this. Second, the quarantine path *does not rethrow* — it logs, dead-letters, and returns normally so the offset advances. Rethrowing would re-pin the offset and recreate the block. The discipline of "on give-up, swallow and advance" is the whole point.

The detection thresholds you choose are a tuning problem with real consequences. A low threshold (3 attempts, short backoff) keeps head-of-line stalls short but dead-letters more good messages during transient outages. A high threshold (25 attempts over an hour) tolerates long outages without losing messages but allows long stalls if you are *not* using the retry-topic pattern. The combination that most production teams converge on: retry-topic pattern so the main partition *never* blocks, a moderate retry budget (5 to 10 attempts) with exponential backoff on the retry topic, and a DLQ at the end. With the retry-topic pattern in place, even a generous retry budget costs you nothing in head-of-line blocking, because the failing message is never on the hot path while it retries.

## 4. Retry storms and the thundering herd

We now switch failure families entirely. A poison message is a *content* problem — one bad record. A retry storm is a *load* problem — many good records, a degraded dependency, and retry logic that amplifies instead of dampens. The two are unrelated in cause but related in cure, because both are contained by the same family of tactics, and both are made catastrophic by the same enemy: naive retries.

Start with the mechanism. A **retry storm** begins when a downstream dependency degrades — gets slow, starts erroring, or briefly goes away. The consumers calling it start failing. Each consumer, following good resilience practice, retries the failed call. But now the dependency is serving *both* the fresh, first-attempt traffic *and* the retry traffic on top of it. If each request is retried up to three times, the offered load on the dependency can triple. The dependency, already struggling, now faces more load than it could handle even when healthy, so *more* requests fail, so *more* retries fire, and the offered load climbs further. The dependency is now firmly underwater and cannot surface, because every time it processes a request and frees a sliver of capacity, three retries are waiting to consume it. This is the storm, and the figure below shows the amplification: two hundred consumers, each retrying three times, multiplying offered load to six times baseline against a dependency that can serve only a fraction of it.

![A branching graph showing two hundred consumer instances and a growing queue backlog both feeding a retry layer that triples traffic into a six-times-baseline offered load, overwhelming a downstream dependency whose capacity is far below the offered request rate](/imgs/blogs/poison-messages-and-retry-storms-containment-3.webp)

The closely related **thundering herd** is the synchronized version of the same thing. The classic thundering herd is what happens when many clients are all waiting on the same event and all wake at once: a cache entry expires and a thousand requests that were being served from cache simultaneously miss and stampede the origin database; a downstream service that was down comes back up and every client that was backing off happens to retry at the same instant, slamming it the moment it recovers and knocking it down again. The "herd" is the synchronization — everyone moving together — and the synchronization is what turns a manageable trickle of retries into a wall. A retry storm is often a thundering herd in disguise, because naive retry timers are synchronized by the original failure event: everyone failed at the same time, so everyone's "retry after 1 second" fires at the same time, so the retries arrive in coordinated waves rather than spread out.

This is why **jitter** matters so much and why its absence is such a common root cause. If two hundred consumers all fail at T and all retry at exactly T+1s, T+2s, T+4s (synchronized exponential backoff), the dependency faces two hundred simultaneous requests in tight bursts with quiet gaps between — the worst possible shape, because the bursts exceed capacity while the gaps waste it. Adding randomized jitter to the backoff (each consumer waits a random duration in a range rather than a fixed value) de-synchronizes the herd: the same two hundred retries now spread across the backoff window instead of arriving in a single spike. AWS's well-known analysis of exponential backoff and jitter showed that "full jitter" — picking a uniform random delay between zero and the current backoff ceiling — dramatically reduces both the peak load and the time to drain a backlog of contending clients. Jitter does not reduce the *total* number of retries, but it spreads them out so the peak never exceeds capacity, and the peak is what kills you. The mechanics of backoff and jitter live in the [dead-letter queues, retries, and exponential backoff](/blog/software-development/message-queue/dead-letter-queues-retries-exponential-backoff) sibling; here the point is that *un-jittered* retries are a thundering-herd generator.

### Why retries amplify rather than dampen

It is worth stating plainly why retries are amplifying in the first place, because the intuition that "retrying a failed request is free, it would have failed anyway" is exactly backwards under load. When a dependency is *healthy and underutilized*, a retry is nearly free — the dependency had spare capacity, the retry uses a little of it, no harm done. But when a dependency is *saturated*, retries are the opposite of free: every retry consumes a connection, a thread, a slot in a bounded queue, CPU to parse the request and generate the error, and it does all of that *to produce another failure*. The retry traffic competes with the first-attempt traffic for the same scarce capacity, and because retries are generated *in proportion to the failure rate*, they grow precisely when the dependency can least afford them. The feedback is positive: more failures cause more retries cause more load cause more failures. That is the engine of the storm, and we examine it directly in the next section.

## 5. The feedback loop that prevents recovery

The reason a retry storm is so much worse than a simple overload is that it is *self-sustaining*. An ordinary traffic spike ends when the spike ends; the dependency was knocked down by an external surge, and when the surge passes, it recovers. A retry storm has no external surge to pass — the surge is generated internally, by the retries, which are generated by the failures, which are caused by the surge. The loop closes on itself. The system has entered a *stable bad equilibrium*: a state it will remain in indefinitely because the forces keeping it there are produced by the state itself. This is the single most important idea in this whole post, so let us trace the loop explicitly, with the timeline figure as our guide.

![A timeline of a retry storm showing downstream latency rising fivefold, timeouts beginning and retries starting, two hundred consumers retrying in synchrony, offered load reaching six times baseline as the dependency dies, a circuit breaker opening to fail fast, and a half-open probe finding recovery](/imgs/blogs/poison-messages-and-retry-storms-containment-2.webp)

At T+0 the downstream dependency degrades — say a slow query pushes its latency up fivefold. At T+10s the increased latency causes the first timeouts, and consumers begin retrying. At T+30s the retries have synchronized into a herd and two hundred consumers are retrying together, multiplying load. By T+60s the offered load is six times baseline and the dependency, far past saturation, is failing essentially every request — and critically, *it cannot recover on its own*, because the retry load does not depend on the dependency being healthy. Even if the original slow query is fixed at T+60s, the dependency is now buried under retry traffic that will keep it buried. The thing that triggered the storm is gone, but the storm continues, fed by its own output. Without intervention, this state persists until something external breaks the loop — an operator manually pauses the consumers, or the consumers crash from their own resource exhaustion and stop generating load (an ugly, accidental recovery), or a circuit breaker trips.

The phrase "stable bad equilibrium" is precise and worth unpacking. In a healthy system, the equilibrium is "offered load equals served load, latency low." That equilibrium is stable: a small perturbation (a brief load bump) gets corrected and the system returns to it. A retry storm creates a *second* stable equilibrium — "offered load far exceeds capacity, everything failing, retries pinning it there" — and this one is also stable in the bad sense: small perturbations toward recovery (a brief drop in load) get *erased* by the retry traffic rushing back in to re-saturate the dependency. The system has two stable states, and the storm flips it from the good one to the bad one, and it cannot flip back on its own because the bad state defends itself. Breaking out requires a force that *cuts the load* faster than the retries can replace it — which is exactly what a circuit breaker does, and why the breaker, not jitter, is the tool that actually ends a storm in progress.

There is a humbling corollary here for anyone who has ever "fixed" a downstream outage and watched it immediately re-break. You restart the dependency, it comes up healthy, and within seconds it is down again — because the moment it accepts connections, the entire backlog of retrying consumers slams it simultaneously (a thundering herd on recovery) and re-saturates it before it can establish steady state. The dependency was never the problem after the first thirty seconds; the *retry load* was the problem, and restarting the dependency does nothing to the retry load. The correct recovery procedure is to *cut the load first* — open the breakers, pause or throttle the consumers — *then* let the dependency stabilize, *then* ramp the load back gradually with jitter so you do not recreate the herd. Fix the dependency without cutting the load and you are restarting into the teeth of the storm.

## 6. Circuit breakers: failing fast to recover

The circuit breaker is the single most important tool for ending a retry storm, because it is the only one of our tactics that directly *cuts the load on a failing dependency to zero*, which is exactly the force needed to break the self-sustaining loop. The name comes from electrical circuit breakers, and the analogy is apt: when current (load) exceeds a safe threshold, the breaker *opens* — physically disconnecting the circuit — to prevent the wire from melting (the dependency from dying), and it can be reset once the fault clears. A software circuit breaker sits between your consumer and a downstream dependency and watches the success and failure of calls. When the failure rate crosses a threshold, it *opens* and stops sending calls through, failing them instantly and locally instead. The figure below contrasts the two worlds: on the left, no breaker, where a slow downstream exhausts the caller's thread pool and the collapse cascades back up the call chain; on the right, the breaker open, failing in a millisecond and keeping the caller alive.

![A before-and-after diagram contrasting a system with no circuit breaker where a slow downstream exhausts the caller thread pool and the collapse spreads up the call chain against a system where the breaker opens on a fifty percent error rate and fails in one millisecond so the caller stays up and sheds load gracefully](/imgs/blogs/poison-messages-and-retry-storms-containment-6.webp)

A circuit breaker has three states and the transitions between them are the whole design. In the **closed** state — the normal state, despite "closed" sounding like "off," because a closed electrical circuit conducts — calls pass through to the dependency and the breaker tracks the rolling failure rate. If the failure rate over a recent window crosses the threshold (commonly 50% of calls failing over the last 20 calls, or the last 10 seconds), the breaker trips to **open**. In the **open** state, no calls pass through; every call fails immediately and locally, without touching the dependency, usually in microseconds. This is "failing fast," and it is the magic: by failing fast, the breaker (a) stops adding load to the struggling dependency, giving it room to recover, and (b) returns control to the caller immediately instead of making it wait on a doomed timeout, which prevents the caller's own resources (threads, connections) from piling up. After a cooldown period (say 30 seconds), the breaker moves to **half-open**: it lets a single trial call — or a small trickle — through to test whether the dependency has recovered. If the trial succeeds, the breaker closes and normal traffic resumes. If it fails, the breaker re-opens and waits another cooldown. This half-open probe is how the breaker recovers automatically without a thundering herd: it sends *one* request to test the waters, not two hundred.

The reason the breaker is uniquely able to end a storm is precisely that, when open, it cuts the load to *zero* — faster than the retries can replace it, because there are no retries when the breaker is open; the calls do not even reach the retry logic, they fail at the breaker. This is the force that flips the system out of the stable bad equilibrium. While the breaker is open, the dependency receives no traffic at all, recovers fully, and then the half-open probe confirms recovery and traffic resumes gradually. Contrast this with jitter, which merely *spreads out* the retry load — useful for preventing a storm from forming, useless for ending one already in progress, because spread-out retries still keep the dependency saturated. The breaker is the off switch; jitter is the soft-start. You want both, but only the breaker ends a storm.

Here is a circuit breaker wrapping a downstream call in a consumer, using a config-driven library (the example uses Resilience4j-style semantics, which map closely to most languages' breaker libraries):

```java
// Circuit breaker config: open at 50% failures over a rolling window of 20 calls,
// stay open for 30s, then admit 3 trial calls in half-open before deciding.
CircuitBreakerConfig config = CircuitBreakerConfig.custom()
    .failureRateThreshold(50)                          // open at 50% failures
    .slidingWindowSize(20)                             // over the last 20 calls
    .waitDurationInOpenState(Duration.ofSeconds(30))   // cooldown before half-open
    .permittedNumberOfCallsInHalfOpenState(3)          // trial calls before closing
    .slowCallRateThreshold(80)                         // also trip on 80% slow calls
    .slowCallDurationThreshold(Duration.ofSeconds(2))  // "slow" means > 2s
    .build();

CircuitBreaker breaker = CircuitBreaker.of("payment-gateway", config);

public PaymentResult charge(Order order) {
    return breaker.executeSupplier(() -> {
        // Only runs when CLOSED or HALF_OPEN. When OPEN this throws
        // CallNotPermittedException immediately (microseconds), no network call.
        return paymentGateway.charge(order);
    });
    // Caller catches CallNotPermittedException and sheds gracefully:
    // park the message, do NOT retry into an open breaker.
}
```

Two subtleties make or break a breaker in production. First, the breaker must trip on **slow calls**, not just failed ones — note the `slowCallRateThreshold` above. A dependency that is degrading often does not *error*; it gets *slow*, holding your threads in long timeouts. If your breaker only counts hard errors, it will sit closed while every one of your threads is blocked for two seconds on a dependency that is technically "succeeding" but at a latency that is destroying you. Tripping on slow-call rate catches the degrade-before-error case that is far more common than a clean outage. Second — and this is the integration point that ties the breaker to everything else — **when the breaker is open, do not retry into it.** A naive retry layer wrapped *around* a breaker will catch the `CallNotPermittedException` and retry, which is pointless (the breaker will reject the retry too) and wasteful. The correct composition is: breaker innermost, retry outermost, and the retry policy must treat "breaker open" as a *non-retryable* terminal condition — park the message, apply backpressure, but do not hammer an open breaker.

#### Worked example: a circuit breaker collapses a storm

Let us quantify the breaker's effect on the retry storm from section 4. Baseline: 200 consumer instances, each making calls to a payment gateway whose healthy capacity is 10,000 requests per second. Normal first-attempt load is 8,000 req/s — comfortably under capacity. Now the gateway degrades and its effective capacity drops to 3,000 req/s. With a naive retry policy of up to 3 attempts and no breaker, here is the load math. First attempts: 8,000 req/s. The gateway can serve only 3,000, so roughly 5,000 req/s fail and each failed request retries. Retry attempts (second tries): about 5,000 req/s. Of those, the gateway again serves only a fraction, so roughly 4,000 fail and retry a third time: about 4,000 req/s of third attempts. Total offered load: 8,000 + 5,000 + 4,000 = 17,000 req/s against a degraded capacity of 3,000 — nearly 6x the capacity the gateway can actually serve. The gateway is now serving almost nothing useful (most of its 3,000 served slots go to requests that will be considered failed by the caller because of the latency) and cannot recover. This is the figure 3 amplification made numeric.

Now add the breaker. The 200 consumers' calls flow through breakers that trip at a 50% failure rate over a 20-call window. Within a few seconds of the degrade, every breaker sees well over 50% failures and *opens*. The instant a breaker opens, its consumer's calls stop reaching the gateway — they fail locally in microseconds and the message is parked (backpressure, not retried). Offered load to the gateway drops from 17,000 req/s to *near zero* — only the trickle of half-open probe calls, one or a few per breaker per 30-second cooldown, perhaps a few hundred req/s of probes across all 200 consumers, *far* below the 3,000 the degraded gateway can serve. With essentially no load, the gateway recovers fully within its 30-second cooldown. At the next half-open window, the probe calls succeed, breakers begin closing, and traffic ramps back. To avoid a thundering herd on recovery, the breakers' cooldowns are jittered so they do not all close at the same instant. Total impact: roughly 30 to 60 seconds of fast-failed (parked) traffic, no gateway death, full automatic recovery — versus, without the breaker, an indefinite outage that ends only when a human pauses the consumers. The breaker turned a multi-hour incident into a one-minute blip, and the mechanism was simply *cutting the load to zero so the dependency could breathe*.

## 7. Rate limiting, load shedding, and backpressure

The circuit breaker is a binary, reactive tool — it is fully closed or fully open and it reacts after failures pile up. The next family of tools is continuous and proactive: instead of waiting for failure and then slamming the door, you *cap the load in the first place* so failure never starts. Three related tactics — rate limiting, load shedding, and backpressure — share that goal but apply it at different points, and confusing them is a common source of muddled designs.

**Rate limiting** caps the rate at which you *send* requests to a dependency, regardless of how much work is queued. You decide that the payment gateway gets at most, say, 8,000 req/s from your fleet, and you enforce that with a token bucket or a leaky bucket: requests acquire a token before proceeding, tokens refill at the limit rate, and when the bucket is empty, requests wait or are rejected. Rate limiting is *prophylactic* — it prevents you from ever offering more load than the dependency can take, which means it prevents the storm from forming rather than ending one after it forms. It pairs beautifully with the breaker: the rate limiter keeps you under capacity in normal operation, and the breaker catches the case where the dependency's capacity *drops* below your rate limit. A well-tuned rate limit also makes you a good citizen toward shared dependencies — you cannot accidentally DoS a service you share with other teams.

**Load shedding** is the decision to *drop* work you cannot handle, deliberately and immediately, rather than queue it and fail slowly. When offered load exceeds what you can serve, you have three choices: serve everything slowly (latency explodes, queues grow unbounded, eventually everything times out and you serve *nothing* well), queue everything (memory grows until you OOM), or *shed* — reject the excess fast and serve the rest well. Load shedding chooses the third: it sacrifices some requests to keep the rest healthy. The crucial insight is that *shedding fast is better than failing slow*. A request you are going to drop should be dropped in microseconds at the front door, not after it has consumed a thread, a connection, and two seconds of a downstream timeout. Shedding the right requests — and *which* ones is a real design question, often by priority (shed low-priority work first) or by age (shed requests that have already waited so long they are about to time out anyway) — keeps your goodput high while offered load is impossible. The brutal but correct mindset: when you cannot serve everyone, serving 70% well beats serving 100% terribly.

**Backpressure** is the most elegant of the three because it does not drop anything — it pushes the slowdown *backward* through the pipeline to the source, so the source slows down to match the slowest stage. In a pull-based consumer (Kafka, SQS), backpressure is almost automatic: if your consumer cannot keep up because a downstream is slow, it simply polls less often, the unprocessed messages stay durably on the broker, and the *broker* absorbs the backlog instead of your consumer's memory. The slow downstream does not cause your consumer to pile up requests; it causes your consumer to *read more slowly*, which is exactly the right response — you are not generating retry load, you are simply not advancing until the downstream can take more. This is why pull-based consumption is so much more resilient to downstream slowdowns than push-based: pull has natural backpressure built in. (The [push vs pull](/blog/software-development/message-queue/push-vs-pull-acknowledgements-how-consumers-read) sibling covers exactly this contrast.) The matrix below maps each containment tactic to the failure mode it actually addresses — the point being that no single tactic covers everything, which is why robust pipelines layer all of them.

![A matrix mapping four containment tactics across the columns against four failure modes down the rows, showing that circuit breakers best contain retry storms and slow downstreams, dead-letter queues best contain poison messages, and bulkheads best contain noisy tenants, with no single tactic covering every mode](/imgs/blogs/poison-messages-and-retry-storms-containment-5.webp)

Read the matrix carefully, because its central message is the thesis of the whole containment story: each tactic targets a *different* failure cause, and the failure modes do not overlap, so you genuinely need all four. The dead-letter queue contains the poison message — but does nothing for a retry storm, where every message is fine. The circuit breaker contains the retry storm and the slow downstream — but does nothing for a poison message, which fails deterministically regardless of any breaker state. The bulkhead contains the noisy tenant — limiting blast radius — but only partially helps with a storm. Rate limiting helps prevent storms but is no defense against poison. A pipeline with only a DLQ survives poison messages and dies in retry storms; a pipeline with only a breaker survives storms and blocks forever on poison. You ship all of them.

#### Worked example: backpressure versus retry under a slow downstream

Compare two consumer designs against the same event: a downstream that slows from 5 ms to 500 ms per call (a 100x latency increase, no errors, just slow). Design A is a push-based consumer with a synchronous retry loop: it receives messages as fast as the broker pushes them, calls the downstream, and on the 500 ms latency it does not error but blocks each handler thread for 500 ms. With a fixed thread pool of 50, throughput collapses to 50 / 0.5s = 100 msg/s, but the broker keeps pushing at the arrival rate of 2,000 msg/s, so 1,900 msg/s pile up *inside the consumer's memory* as in-flight or buffered messages. Within seconds the consumer's heap fills, GC thrashes, and it OOMs — and on restart it re-reads the un-acked messages and OOMs again. A slow downstream killed the consumer through unbounded internal buffering. Design B is a pull-based consumer with a bounded in-flight limit (`max.poll.records` capped, processing fully before the next poll). When the downstream slows to 500 ms, the consumer simply polls less frequently — it processes its bounded batch, which now takes 100x longer, and only *then* polls again. Throughput drops to the same 100 msg/s, but the un-processed 1,900 msg/s of backlog stays *on the broker*, durably, costing the consumer nothing in memory. Lag climbs (visibly, alarmably — which is good, you get paged on lag), but the consumer stays healthy and recovers fully the instant the downstream speeds up. Same downstream event; one design dies, the other degrades gracefully. The difference is bounded in-flight work plus pull-based backpressure: never accept more work than you can hold.

## 8. Bulkheading: isolating blast radius

The final containment tactic is the most architectural and the one teams discover last, usually after an incident where one bad actor took down everything. **Bulkheading** takes its name from a ship's hull: a ship is divided into watertight compartments so that a breach in one compartment floods only that compartment, not the whole hull, and the ship stays afloat. In software, a bulkhead is an *isolation boundary* — a separate pool of resources (threads, connections, queues, consumer instances) dedicated to one tenant, one priority class, or one partition — so that exhaustion of one pool cannot starve the others. The blast radius of a failure is capped at one bulkhead. The grid figure below shows the pattern at its simplest: three tenants, each with its own queue and its own dedicated thread pool, so that when tenant C goes hot and saturates pool C, pools A and B remain entirely healthy.

![A grid showing three tenants each with a dedicated queue and a dedicated thread pool, where tenant C is hot and saturates only its own pool C while pools A and B for the other tenants remain healthy and fully isolated](/imgs/blogs/poison-messages-and-retry-storms-containment-9.webp)

The failure bulkheading prevents is the **noisy neighbor**, and it is endemic to any shared-resource system. Without bulkheading, all tenants share one thread pool, one connection pool, one queue. When tenant C generates a flood — a burst of traffic, a wave of slow requests, or a stream of poison messages each consuming retries — it consumes shared resources, and tenants A and B, who did nothing wrong, are starved: their messages wait behind C's in the shared queue, their requests cannot get a thread because C is holding them all, their calls cannot get a connection because C exhausted the pool. One tenant's problem becomes everyone's problem. Bulkheading walls C off: C gets its own pool of 20 threads, and when C exhausts those 20 threads, *only* C's processing degrades; A and B have their own 20 threads each, untouched. The ship floods one compartment and stays afloat.

Bulkheading maps onto message-queue architecture at several granularities, and choosing the right one is a real design decision. At the coarsest, you give each tenant its *own queue or topic* — full physical isolation, strongest containment, highest operational cost (more queues to manage, more consumers to run). At a middle granularity, tenants share a topic but you partition by tenant and assign partitions to dedicated consumer instances, so a hot partition saturates only its assigned consumer. At the finest, all tenants share topic and consumers but you give each tenant a *bounded concurrency budget* — a semaphore that caps how many in-flight operations one tenant can hold — so one tenant cannot grab more than its share of a shared pool. The semaphore version is cheap and surprisingly effective; here is the shape:

```java
// Per-tenant concurrency bulkhead: each tenant gets at most N in-flight slots.
// A flood from one tenant exhausts only that tenant's semaphore, not the shared pool.
Map<String, Semaphore> tenantSlots = new ConcurrentHashMap<>();
int SLOTS_PER_TENANT = 20;   // bulkhead size per tenant

void handle(Message msg) {
    Semaphore slots = tenantSlots.computeIfAbsent(
        msg.tenantId(), t -> new Semaphore(SLOTS_PER_TENANT));

    if (!slots.tryAcquire()) {
        // This tenant is at capacity. Shed or defer THIS tenant's message —
        // crucially, other tenants are unaffected because they have their own semaphores.
        deferOrShed(msg);     // backpressure scoped to the noisy tenant only
        return;
    }
    try {
        process(msg);
    } finally {
        slots.release();      // return the slot to THIS tenant's bulkhead
    }
}
```

There is an important interaction between bulkheading and the poison-message problem from the first half of this post: bulkheading is also what limits the blast radius of head-of-line blocking. Recall that a poison message blocks exactly one partition. If your partitioning *is* your tenant bulkhead — one tenant per partition (or per set of partitions) — then a poison message from tenant C blocks only tenant C's partition, and tenants A and B are unaffected. But if your partition key is something orthogonal to tenant (say, by entity ID, with tenants spread across all partitions), then a single tenant's poison message blocks a partition that *also* carries other tenants' messages, and the noisy neighbor strikes again — tenant C's bad record stalls tenant A's messages that happen to share the partition. The lesson: align your partition key with your isolation boundary when tenant isolation matters, so that a per-partition failure is also a per-tenant failure and never crosses tenants. This is a partitioning decision (see [partitioning and sharding](/blog/software-development/database/database-partitioning-and-sharding)) with direct blast-radius consequences.

The cost of bulkheading is real and you must weigh it. Dedicated pools mean *more* total resources, because you cannot share — if tenant A is idle, its 20 threads sit unused while tenant C is throttled at its own 20, whereas a shared pool of 40 would let C borrow A's idle capacity. Bulkheading trades *efficiency* (peak utilization of shared resources) for *isolation* (bounded blast radius). For a system where one tenant's failure cannot be allowed to affect another — a multi-tenant SaaS with SLAs per customer, a payment system where one merchant's flood cannot delay another's settlements — that trade is obviously worth it. For a homogeneous internal pipeline where all the work is yours and equally important, full bulkheading is over-engineering, and a single shared pool with good rate limiting and a breaker is plenty. As always, isolation is not free, and you buy exactly as much as your blast-radius requirements demand.

## 9. A containment playbook

We have nine sections of mechanism; now we assemble them into a layered defense you can adopt incrementally. The organizing principle is **defense in depth**: each layer catches a class of failure the layer above lets through, and the last layer is a quarantine of last resort so that *nothing* — no poison message, no storm, no flood — can take the whole pipeline down. The stack figure below shows the layers in order, top to bottom, from the cheapest and most specific to the most general.

![A layered stack showing defense in depth for pipeline containment, from poison detection with a retry-count cap at the top, through a circuit breaker that fails fast, backpressure with rate limiting, per-tenant bulkhead isolation, and finally a dead-letter-queue quarantine as the last resort at the bottom](/imgs/blogs/poison-messages-and-retry-storms-containment-7.webp)

**Layer 1 — poison detection.** Every consumer caps retries per message and tracks the failure signature, so a deterministically-failing message is detected (retry count plus identical signature) and removed from the hot path rather than looped on forever. On Kafka, prefer the retry-topic pattern so the main partition never blocks even during the retries. This layer contains the poison message.

**Layer 2 — circuit breaker.** Every call to a downstream dependency goes through a breaker that trips on failure rate *and* slow-call rate, fails fast when open, and probes with a single half-open call. This layer contains the retry storm by cutting load to zero so the dependency can recover. The retry policy must treat "breaker open" as non-retryable — never retry into an open breaker.

**Layer 3 — backpressure and rate limiting.** Consumers use bounded in-flight work and pull-based consumption so a slow downstream pushes the backlog onto the broker (durable, cheap) rather than into consumer memory (fatal). Outbound calls to shared dependencies are rate-limited with a token bucket so you never offer more than the dependency's capacity. This layer prevents storms from forming and prevents slow downstreams from OOMing consumers.

**Layer 4 — bulkhead isolation.** Tenants, priority classes, or partitions get isolated resource pools (dedicated queues, dedicated consumers, or per-tenant concurrency semaphores) so one bad actor's failure is capped at its own bulkhead. Align partition keys with isolation boundaries so per-partition failures stay per-tenant. This layer contains blast radius.

**Layer 5 — DLQ quarantine.** Anything that exhausts its retry budget — poison or persistently-failing — lands in a dead-letter queue with full payload and failure cause attached, off the hot path, for a human to inspect, fix, and replay on a Tuesday afternoon. This is the quarantine of last resort, and it guarantees that no single message can ever block the pipeline indefinitely. The DLQ machinery — including alerting on DLQ depth, which should be a hard alert because a growing DLQ means something systematic is wrong — is covered in the [dead-letter queues sibling](/blog/software-development/message-queue/dead-letter-queues-retries-exponential-backoff).

The taxonomy figure below ties the failure side of the playbook together: the two roots of every pipeline cascade — a single bad message, and a degraded dependency under naive retry — branch into the four named modes (poison message and schema drift on the message side; retry storm, thundering herd, and cascading failure on the dependency side). Knowing which root you are facing tells you which layer is doing the containing.

![A taxonomy tree rooted at pipeline cascade, branching into a single bad message and a degraded dependency, with the message branch splitting into poison message head-of-line blocking and schema drift, and the dependency branch splitting into retry storm self-amplification, thundering herd synchronized wake, and cascading failure from pool exhaustion](/imgs/blogs/poison-messages-and-retry-storms-containment-8.webp)

The adoption order matters because the layers have wildly different cost-to-value ratios. Start with **layer 1 and layer 5 together** — bounded retries plus a DLQ — because they are cheap (a retry-count header and a dead-letter topic), they directly prevent the most common 3 a.m. page (the head-of-line stall), and they require no coordination with downstream teams. Add **layer 2, the circuit breaker**, next, because retry storms are the second most common and most catastrophic failure, and a breaker is a well-understood, library-provided component you wrap around existing calls. Add **layer 3, backpressure and rate limiting**, as you scale and start sharing dependencies and hitting OOMs under slow downstreams. Reach for **layer 4, bulkheading**, last and only when multi-tenancy or hard per-class SLAs make blast radius a first-class concern — it is the most architecturally invasive and the easiest to over-build. A pipeline with layers 1, 2, 3, and 5 in place is already dramatically more survivable than the median production system; layer 4 is the multi-tenant capstone.

## Case studies and war stories

**The one-record outage at a logistics platform.** A shipping-events pipeline consumed Kafka topics keyed by warehouse, processing `package.scanned` events into a tracking database. One afternoon a firmware bug in a single handheld scanner emitted events with a timestamp field set to a value that overflowed the downstream database's `timestamp` column — every insert for that record threw, the consumer retried forever, and the partition for that warehouse stalled. Because the retry policy was "retry indefinitely with 1-second backoff," the partition never advanced, and within twenty minutes that warehouse's tracking data was twenty minutes stale while every other warehouse was fine. The on-call engineer initially suspected a database problem (why is only one warehouse failing?) and lost half an hour before spotting the identical stack trace repeating in the logs — the signature of poison. The fix was immediate (skip the offset manually) and the permanent fix was layer 1 plus layer 5: cap retries at 5, route the poison record to a DLQ, and alert on DLQ depth. The lesson the team wrote into its runbook: *a single partition stalled while its siblings are healthy is almost always a poison message; look for the repeating identical exception first.*

**The retry storm that restarting could not fix.** A payments company ran two hundred consumer instances charging a third-party payment gateway. A gateway-side index migration tripled the gateway's p99 latency, consumers began timing out at their 2-second deadline, and the naive 3-attempt retry policy with *no jitter and no breaker* tripled the offered load in synchronized waves. The gateway, already slow, went fully down under the retry load. The team's first instinct — restart the gateway connection pool and the gateway itself — made things worse: each restart brought the gateway up into the teeth of two hundred consumers' synchronized retries, which re-killed it in seconds (a textbook thundering herd on recovery). They were stuck for over an hour, restarting into the storm. The break came only when an engineer realized the retries were the problem and *paused all consumers* — cutting load to zero — let the gateway stabilize for two minutes, then ramped consumers back in three staggered waves. The permanent fix was a circuit breaker (open at 50% failures, half-open probe) plus full jitter on the backoff. The breaker, they noted afterward, would have ended the original incident automatically in under a minute by doing exactly what the manual pause did — cut the load so the dependency could breathe.

**The schema rollout that poisoned a whole topic.** A team evolved an event schema, adding a required field, and deployed the new producers before updating the consumers — a textbook ordering mistake. Every message in the new format was schema-incompatible poison to the old consumers, so it was not *one* poison message but a continuous *stream* of them. The retry-count detection worked perfectly — every new-format message exhausted its retries and went to the DLQ — but the DLQ filled at the full topic throughput, thousands of messages a second, and the DLQ-depth alert (which the team did have, from a prior incident) fired within seconds and made the cause obvious: a schema mismatch, not a one-off bad record. They rolled back the producers, drained the DLQ by replaying it through the new consumers once those were deployed, and adopted a schema-registry compatibility check in CI so a producer could never ship an incompatible schema ahead of its consumers. The lesson: poison detection and a DLQ contained the blast (the pipeline never blocked), but a *flood* of poison is a signal of a systematic problem, and the DLQ-depth alert is what turns that flood from a silent data-quality disaster into a paged, diagnosable incident.

**The noisy tenant that starved everyone.** A multi-tenant analytics ingestion pipeline shared one consumer pool across all customers. One enterprise customer ran a backfill that dumped fifty million events in an hour — a 50x spike over their normal rate. With a shared pool and a shared queue, those fifty million events crowded out every other tenant's events: small customers whose data normally appeared in seconds now waited twenty minutes behind the backfill. No tenant had done anything *wrong* — the big customer was within their contractual limits, the small customers were sending normal traffic — but the lack of isolation meant one tenant's legitimate burst became everyone's latency problem. The fix was layer 4: partition by tenant, assign a bounded concurrency budget per tenant with a semaphore, and shed-or-defer a tenant's own overflow without touching others. After the change, the same backfill throttled *only* that customer's ingestion (which was fine — backfills are not latency-sensitive) while every other tenant kept their sub-second latency. The lesson: in a shared system, one tenant's success is every other tenant's outage unless you bulkhead.

## When to reach for each tactic (and when not to)

The honest answer to "which of these do I need?" is "it depends on which failures can actually hurt you," and over-applying every tactic to a simple pipeline is its own failure mode — complexity you will pay for in debugging and operations. Here is the decision framework.

**Always do layer 1 (bounded retries) and layer 5 (DLQ).** There is no pipeline so simple that an unbounded retry loop on a poison message is acceptable. The cost is trivial — a retry-count cap and a dead-letter destination — and the failure it prevents (an indefinite head-of-line stall) is universal and severe. If you do nothing else from this post, bound your retries and add a DLQ. This is non-negotiable table stakes.

**Add a circuit breaker (layer 2) the moment you call any downstream dependency from a consumer.** If your consumer's processing involves a call to a database, a cache, an external API, or any other service that can degrade independently, you are exposed to retry storms, and a breaker is the containment. The exception is a consumer whose processing is purely local (transform and write to the same broker, no external call) — there is no downstream to storm, so no breaker is needed. But the moment there is an external call, wrap it.

**Add backpressure and rate limiting (layer 3) when you scale or share.** Bounded in-flight work is good hygiene always, but you *need* it when a slow downstream can OOM your consumer — which happens once your throughput is high enough that unbounded buffering fills memory in seconds. Rate limiting becomes important when you share a dependency with other teams (be a good citizen) or when the dependency has a hard capacity you must respect. A small, low-throughput pipeline with a generously-sized downstream may not need explicit rate limiting, though it should still bound in-flight work.

**Reach for bulkheading (layer 4) only when blast radius across tenants or priority classes is a real concern.** If you run a multi-tenant system with per-customer SLAs, or you mix critical and best-effort work in one pipeline, bulkheading is essential — one tenant or one priority class must not be allowed to sink the rest. But for a single-tenant or homogeneous internal pipeline where all work is equally important and yours, full bulkheading is over-engineering; the resource inefficiency of dedicated pools is not worth it. Start with a shared pool plus good rate limiting and a breaker, and add bulkheads only when an incident or an SLA forces the issue.

A final caution against over-containment: every layer adds latency, code, configuration, and failure modes of its own. A circuit breaker that is mis-tuned (too sensitive) trips during normal jitter and *causes* outages. A rate limiter set too low becomes the bottleneck. A bulkhead sized too small throttles legitimate load. Containment mechanisms are themselves things that can fail, and a pipeline drowning in half-tuned resilience widgets is harder to operate than one with a few well-understood ones. Add each layer deliberately, in response to a real or clearly-anticipated failure, tune it with load tests, and monitor it — a containment mechanism you cannot observe is a containment mechanism you cannot trust.

## Key takeaways

- **A poison message always fails** — deterministically, every retry — because the fault is in the message (unparseable, schema-incompatible, references a deleted entity), not in any transient condition. Naive retry, which assumes faults are transient, turns lethal against it.
- **On an ordered partition, one poison message at the head blocks every message behind it.** Head-of-line blocking is the price of ordering; you cannot eliminate it without giving up ordering, so instead you *bound* how long it blocks before evicting to a DLQ.
- **Detect poison by retry count crossing a threshold, ideally combined with an identical failure signature.** The poison signature is a flat failure rate (same error, every attempt); a transient signature eventually clears. On Kafka, the retry-topic pattern keeps the main partition unblocked even during retries.
- **A retry storm is a positive feedback loop:** a degraded dependency causes failures, failures cause retries, retries amplify load, amplified load deepens the degradation. It is self-sustaining and will not recover on its own.
- **The thundering herd is the synchronized version of the storm.** Un-jittered backoff synchronizes retries into waves that exceed capacity; full jitter spreads them out so the peak never exceeds capacity. Jitter prevents storms but does not end one in progress.
- **A circuit breaker is the only tactic that ends a storm in progress,** because opening cuts load on the dependency to zero — faster than retries can replace it — letting it recover, then probing with a single half-open call. Trip on slow-call rate, not just errors, and never retry into an open breaker.
- **Backpressure beats retries under a slow downstream:** pull-based consumption with bounded in-flight work pushes the backlog onto the durable broker instead of into consumer memory, so the consumer degrades gracefully instead of OOMing.
- **Load shedding fast beats failing slow.** When you cannot serve everyone, drop the excess at the front door in microseconds and serve the rest well; serving 70% well beats serving 100% terribly.
- **Bulkheading caps blast radius:** dedicated pools or per-tenant concurrency budgets ensure one tenant, partition, or priority class cannot starve the others. Align partition keys with isolation boundaries so a per-partition stall stays per-tenant.
- **No single tactic contains every failure** — DLQ for poison, breaker for storms, backpressure for slow downstreams, bulkhead for noisy tenants — so robust pipelines layer all of them as defense in depth, adopting layers 1 and 5 always, the breaker whenever there is a downstream, and bulkheads only when blast radius is a real concern.
